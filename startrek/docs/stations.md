# Final Frontier — Bridge Stations

**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Status:** Skeleton — scenario types and station-specific mechanics to be detailed

---

## Overview

The bridge is the game's central operational frame. Every scenario is resolved through
one or more stations. The player's role at any given station depends on their current
rank — early career, you ARE the station officer; later, you are assigning your crew
to the station and making the call based on their capabilities and your own judgment.

There are six primary bridge stations plus the command chair. Each station has:
- A primary stat that governs its base resolution rolls
- A secondary stat that applies in specific scenarios
- A set of scenario types it is most relevant to
- A set of scenario types where it provides supporting bonuses to other stations

## The Stations

### HELM — Navigation and Piloting

**Primary stat:** Tactical
**Secondary stat:** Engineering
**Officer archetype:** Cool under pressure, spatial reasoning, instinct over deliberation

**What Helm does:**
Controls the ship's movement — course setting, evasive maneuvers, approach vectors,
emergency navigation, pursuit and escape. In combat scenarios, Helm determines
positioning (which affects Tactical rolls). In exploration scenarios, Helm determines
approach quality (which affects first-contact tone). In crisis scenarios, Helm is often
the escape route or the difference between arriving in time and arriving too late.

**Scenario types (primary):**
- Ship-to-ship pursuit / escape
- Navigating hazardous space (nebulae, asteroid fields, gravitational anomalies)
- Emergency course change under fire
- Precision approach (disabled ship, narrow corridor, debris field)

**Scenario types (support):**
- Combat positioning (bonus to Tactical resolution)
- Emergency evacuation (bonus to timeline)

**Key decision examples:**
> "The Klingon vessel is gaining. You have three options: push the engines to warp 9.2
> (risk of burnout), drop to sublight and hide in the dust cloud (you'll lose them but
> add 6 hours to your mission), or stand and fight. Helm station — what do you do?"

> "Debris from the destroyed freighter is filling the corridor. You have 90 seconds to
> thread through it before the shockwave hits. The navigator says it's possible. The
> engineer says it isn't."

### TACTICAL — Weapons and Defense

**Primary stat:** Tactical
**Secondary stat:** Command
**Officer archetype:** Precise, controlled, thinks in threat vectors

**What Tactical does:**
Weapons targeting, shield management, threat assessment. In combat, Tactical is the
primary resolution station. But Tactical also governs threat assessment in non-combat
scenarios — reading a sensor contact, evaluating whether a vessel's posture is
aggressive or defensive, determining the appropriate show of force in a tense encounter.

**Scenario types (primary):**
- Ship-to-ship combat (targeting, shield rotation, weapons management)
- Threat assessment (is this vessel hostile? is this reading what it appears to be?)
- Show of force decisions (raise shields? charge weapons? stand down?)
- Boarding defense

**Scenario types (support):**
- Any combat-adjacent scenario (provides threat context to other station resolutions)
- Diplomatic standoffs (tactical read on opponent's actual posture vs. stated posture)

**Key decision examples:**
> "You have a firing solution. The Romulan vessel's shields are at 40%. One more hit
> will disable them. But their captain is hailing — they say they want to negotiate.
> Your tactical officer says they're charging weapons. What do you do?"

> "Three contacts on sensors. Unknown vessel class. They're not responding to hails.
> Tactical gives you three reads: peaceful survey vessel running silent, automated
> probe misidentifying you as a threat, or a trap. You have 30 seconds."

### SCIENCE — Sensors and Analysis

**Primary stat:** Science
**Secondary stat:** Medicine (for biological phenomena)
**Officer archetype:** Methodical, curious, comfortable with ambiguity

**What Science does:**
Sensor analysis, anomaly investigation, unknown phenomenon interpretation. Science
doesn't resolve the crisis — it defines what the crisis actually is, which changes what
options are available. A high Science resolution before a scenario might reveal that
the "hostile alien vessel" is a derelict running on automated defense protocols — that
changes every subsequent decision. Weak Science leaves you deciding with incomplete
information.

**Scenario types (primary):**
- Unknown anomaly investigation (nebula, signal, spatial phenomenon)
- First contact species analysis (biology, technology level, intent signals)
- Disease or biological threat identification
- Technical mystery (what's wrong with the ship/station/planet?)

**Scenario types (support):**
- Pre-resolution information gathering (used before the primary station's roll)
- Diplomatic scenarios (cultural context, species history)

**Key decision examples:**
> "The signal is repeating every 43 seconds. Your science officer has two theories: a
> natural pulsar with an unusual harmonic, or a distress beacon from a species using
> frequency bands we've never catalogued. Investigating means diverting 4 hours from
> your primary mission. The signal could be nothing. Or it could be a civilization."

> "The away team has returned. Three of them are showing neurological symptoms no one
> has seen before. Science can run a full analysis in 12 hours or a fast scan in 2.
> The fast scan might miss something. The full analysis delays the mission. And the
> symptoms are progressing."

### ENGINEERING — Ship Systems and Improvisation

**Primary stat:** Engineering
**Secondary stat:** Science
**Officer archetype:** Pragmatic, inventive, deeply familiar with the ship as a living system

**What Engineering does:**
Keeps the ship alive. Repairs damage. Improvises solutions when the textbook answer
isn't available. Engineering is the station that gets called when everything else has
failed — "can we reroute power from the secondary EPS conduits to keep shields up for
one more minute?" That question is an Engineering roll.

Engineering also governs *preparation* — a player who invests free time in Engineering
study and keeps ship condition high has more options in every crisis. The ship at 95%
condition and the ship at 55% condition face the same scenarios very differently.

**Scenario types (primary):**
- Ship damage repair (battle damage, system failure, cascade risk)
- Technical improvisation (jury-rigging a solution with available parts)
- Resource management (power allocation, oxygen, fuel)
- Sabotage response
- Hostile environment survival (radiation, extreme temperature, unstable space)

**Scenario types (support):**
- Combat (determines what the ship can actually do vs. what Tactical wants it to do)
- Medical (life support maintenance, surgical bay power, etc.)
- Every scenario where ship condition matters (which is most of them)

**Key decision examples:**
> "The warp core breach is contained but the containment is holding at 34%. Your chief
> engineer says she can stabilize it but will need to take propulsion offline for 6
> hours. You're 2 hours from the rendezvous. The Cardassian patrol passes every 4 hours."

> "The transporter is damaged. You can get the away team back but there's a 12% signal
> degradation — your engineer says probably fine, possibly catastrophic. The alternative
> is a shuttle, which takes 40 minutes you may not have."

### MEDICAL — Crew Health and Biological Threat

**Primary stat:** Medicine
**Secondary stat:** Science
**Officer archetype:** Calm in crisis, unwilling to accept loss, ethically grounded

**What Medical does:**
Keeps the crew alive and functional. In the short term, Medical manages injuries from
combat, away missions, and hostile environments. In the medium term, Medical manages
the crew's health status — which affects their performance at every station. In the
long term, Medical is the station that faces the ethical scenarios: triage decisions,
treatment allocation under scarcity, alien biology that doesn't respond to known medicine.

Medical is also a source of crew loyalty. A captain who takes crew health seriously —
who doesn't burn people out, who ensures Medical has what it needs — earns loyalty that
pays off in the hard moments.

**Scenario types (primary):**
- Combat casualties and triage
- Unknown disease or biological threat
- Away mission injury
- Crew mental health crisis (fatigue, trauma, morale collapse)
- Alien medical emergency (do you help? can you?)

**Scenario types (support):**
- Crew condition assessment before high-stakes scenarios
- Long-mission fatigue management

**Key decision examples:**
> "You have enough of the antidote for 12. There are 17 crew members infected.
> Five will die regardless of who gets treated first. Your CMO presents the triage list.
> Three names on the list are senior officers you can't afford to lose."

> "The alien ambassador is dying. Your CMO has never seen this physiology before.
> A full autopsy of the alien who died last week would give her the information she
> needs — but the alien cultural attaché has said their dead are sacred and cannot be
> touched. The ambassador has 6 hours."

### COMMUNICATIONS — Hails, Diplomacy, and Information

**Primary stat:** Diplomacy
**Secondary stat:** Science (for translation)
**Officer archetype:** Patient, precise with language, reads subtext

**What Communications does:**
Manages all contact with other entities — alien species, Starfleet command, distress
signals, encrypted transmissions, and the hundred ambiguous things in between. A good
Communications officer doesn't just transmit and receive — they translate, interpret
tone and subtext, and tell you what the other party *actually* means as opposed to what
they said.

Communications is the station most directly connected to the ethical and diplomatic
scenarios that define Star Trek as a setting. It is also the station that determines
your relationship with Starfleet brass — how you communicate your decisions upward
affects your career as much as the decisions themselves.

**Scenario types (primary):**
- First contact (initial communication with unknown species)
- Diplomatic negotiation (treaties, disputes, standoffs)
- Distress signal response and prioritization
- Encrypted transmission decoding
- Starfleet command relations (reporting in, requesting support, receiving orders)

**Scenario types (support):**
- Any scenario with a talking-your-way-out option (most scenarios have one)
- Crew morale crisis (addressing the crew)
- Tactical standoffs (de-escalation before weapons are necessary)

**Key decision examples:**
> "The hail comes in on a frequency you've never seen. No universal translator match.
> Your communications officer has a partial decode — she thinks it's a greeting, but it
> could also be a warning to back away. You have three minutes before they close to
> weapons range."

> "Starfleet Command is ordering you to stand down and withdraw. Your science officer
> believes the colony on Veridian III has 4 hours before the stellar phenomenon kills
> everyone on it. Command doesn't have your sensor data. Do you follow the order?"

## The Command Chair

The Command position is not a station — it is the role that oversees all of them. As
an Ensign, you don't have it. As a Captain, it's all you do.

**What Command does:**
At any given scenario, the commanding officer makes three decisions:
1. **Station assignment** — which officer sits at which station (affects which stats
   apply to the roll)
2. **Response selection** — which of the available options to pursue
3. **Resource commitment** — how many of your limited assets (crew focus, ship power,
   time) to put behind the resolution

The Command position does not roll. It decides. The roll belongs to the station officer.
The outcome belongs to the commander.

**At lower ranks (pre-Captain):** The player IS the station officer. They make the
resolution roll. Their captain (an NPC) makes the assignment and response decisions
above them — sometimes correctly, sometimes in ways that set the player up to fail.
Learning to perform well inside a bad command decision is part of career growth.

**At Commander rank:** The player manages the bridge but has a captain above them. The
player makes station assignments; the captain makes the final call. The player's
recommendations affect outcomes.

**At Captain rank:** Full control. The weight of all three decisions sits entirely
with the player. This is the design.

## Station Assignment Strategy

*[To be detailed — this is the core tactical layer of the endgame]*

When the player reaches Captain, every scenario begins with a station assignment phase:
- 6 senior officers; 6 stations
- Each officer has a stat profile
- Each scenario has primary and secondary station requirements
- The "optimal" assignment is not always obvious (is this a Tactical or Science problem?)
- Officers have relationships with each other that affect multi-station resolutions
- An officer assigned to a station outside their strength incurs a penalty
- An officer assigned to their strength station but exhausted also incurs a penalty

The assignment phase is where preparation (knowing your crew) pays off.
