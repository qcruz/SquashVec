# DED Red Team Scenario: Varroa-X Catastrophic Honeybee Colony Collapse

**Scenario ID:** RT-003
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Primary DED Scenarios Activated:** Drought (food supply analog), Shipping Blockade (food import),
  Oil Restriction (agricultural input cost), Financial Fragmentation (commodity leverage)
**Estimated Economic Impact:** $200–400B over 5 years; permanent restructuring of U.S. agriculture
**DED Readiness Assessment:** Moderate-Low — drought/food programs partially applicable; no DED
  instruments specifically address biological threats to agricultural infrastructure
**Unusual Feature:** Unlike most DED scenarios, this one unfolds over 2–3 years, not weeks or months;
  the economic damage is diffuse, compounding, and arrives before the cause is well-understood

---

## 1. Situation

### Background

Commercial beekeeping is the invisible infrastructure of American agriculture. Approximately
one-third of U.S. food supply — $15–17B in annual crop value, 90+ commercial crops — depends
on pollination by managed honeybee colonies. The relationship is so embedded that it is largely
invisible until it fails:

- **Almonds:** 100% dependent on commercial bee pollination; California almond crop is the
  single largest use of commercial bees in the world; 80%+ of global almond supply
- **Blueberries, cherries, avocados, apples:** 60–90% dependent on bee pollination
- **Canola, sunflower (seed oil):** Pollination-dependent; inputs to cooking oils, biofuels,
  processed food
- **Clover and alfalfa seed:** Pollination-dependent; inputs to dairy and beef feed chains

The U.S. commercial beekeeping industry consists of approximately 2.8 million managed colonies
operated by ~17,000 commercial beekeepers. These colonies are trucked across the country on
a seasonal schedule — California almonds (February), Pacific Northwest apples and cherries
(April-May), blueberries (May-June), Midwest clover (July-August) — before being returned to
overwintering sites in the South.

### Existing Vulnerability

Honeybee populations have been declining for 15+ years under the pressure of:
- **Varroa destructor mite:** A parasitic mite that feeds on bee fat bodies and transmits
  bee viruses; the dominant pathogen in managed colonies worldwide
- **Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD):** Mysterious abandonment of hives; attributed to
  Varroa + pesticide + nutritional deficiency interaction
- **Annual winter loss rates:** Averaging 37–44% since 2010 (vs. a sustainable ~15%);
  beekeepers compensate by splitting surviving colonies in spring, but this limits growth

The industry has been running on a treadmill: losing 37–44% of colonies every winter,
rebuilding them from splits and package bees, and barely maintaining total colony count.
Any significant additional mortality vector could tip the system from managed decline to collapse.

### The Triggering Event

**October 2026.** A new Varroa mite biotype — initially named "Varroa destructor strain X7"
by USDA-ARS researchers who first identify it, quickly shortened to "Varroa-X" in industry
press — is identified in commercial apiaries in California's Central Valley.

Varroa-X is initially assumed to be a standard Varroa infestation. By November, it becomes
clear something is different:

1. **Acaricide resistance:** Varroa-X carries a mutation conferring complete resistance to
   oxalic acid (the primary organic treatment) and amitraz (the dominant synthetic acaricide).
   Both treatments, when applied, kill near-zero mites. No registered treatment is effective.

2. **Accelerated reproduction:** Varroa-X reproduces in worker brood (normal Varroa primarily
   reproduces in drone brood); this doubles or triples the reproduction rate, causing colony
   populations to crash within 4–8 weeks of infestation rather than the typical 6–18 months.

3. **Horizontal spread:** The new biotype spreads via robbing behavior and drone drift at
   3–5× the rate of standard Varroa.

By January 2027, Varroa-X has been confirmed in apiaries in 11 states. The February 2027
California almond pollination season — which requires 1.8 million of the nation's 2.8 million
colonies to converge on 1.4 million acres of almond orchards — occurs with 40% of the
arriving colonies already collapsed or collapsing. Almond pollination rates reach only 45–55%
of the levels needed for full crop set.

By summer 2027, Varroa-X has spread to 38 states. Annual colony losses reach 72% (vs.
historic 37–44%). The commercial beekeeping industry — already financially fragile — begins
collapsing faster than it can rebuild.

## 2. Cascade Timeline

### Season 1 (Winter 2026–27): Detection and Initial Failure
- Varroa-X identified in California; rapid spread through Central Valley apiaries
- 2027 almond pollination: severe shortfall; almond crop set at 45–55% of normal
- Almond prices spike 60–90% at the farm gate within 3 months of crop failure announcement
- California almond industry loss: $4–6B (vs. $6B normal crop value at reduced set)
- USDA APHIS emergency declaration; research emergency at USDA-ARS Beltsville

### Season 2 (2027–28): National Spread and First Cascade
- 72% winter colony loss nationally; industry cannot rebuild fast enough
- 2028 cherry and apple crops in Washington, Oregon, Michigan: pollination shortfall of
  30–50%; crop losses $2–4B combined
- Blueberry crop in Maine, Michigan, New Jersey: 35–60% shortfall; $1.5–2.5B loss
- **The feed grain cascade begins:** Alfalfa and clover seed production falls 40%; hay prices
  rise as alfalfa seed scarcity constrains new planting. Dairy and beef feed cost increases.
  Milk prices rise 15–25%; beef prices rise 10–18%.
- **The cooking oil cascade:** Canola and sunflower seed yields fall 20–35%; cooking oil
  prices rise 30–50%; affects processed food production costs across entire food industry.
- Commercial beekeeping industry: ~30% of operations declare bankruptcy or cease commercial
  operations; 850,000–1M colonies permanently lost from the commercial fleet

### Season 3 (2028–29): Structural Food System Damage
- Available commercial colonies: 1.4–1.8M (down from 2.8M); nowhere near sufficient for
  normal pollination demand
- **Almond crisis reaches peak:** Full almond crop requires 1.8M colonies; only 1.2M
  available nationwide. California almond industry faces a structural question about whether
  current acreage is sustainable. Growers begin removing trees; 30–40 year orchards are
  abandoned. The almond contraction is not recoverable within 5 years even if bees recover.
- **Food import demand rises:** U.S. almond, cherry, blueberry, and apple shortfalls
  drive import demand from Chile, Australia, South Africa. Shipping costs rise; logistics
  chains adjust; some substitution occurs but quality/variety constraints limit it.
- **Grocery prices:** Affected categories see 20–60% price increases; broadly propagates
  through CPI as food prices are a major component

### Year 3–5: Economic Tail
- Without treatment solution, the commercial beekeeping fleet is functionally halved
- Food production geography permanently shifts: crops with lower bee dependency gain acreage
- Agricultural land values in almond/specialty crop regions fall 20–40%
- Rural communities (Kern County CA, Hood River OR, Traverse City MI) that built economic
  identities around pollination-dependent crops face structural decline
- Import dependence for nuts, berries, stone fruits permanently increased

## 4. DED Program Mapping

### What Activates From Existing Programs

**Drought Phase 1 Instruments (Partial Analog):**
The drought scenario instruments are designed for water-driven agricultural supply disruption,
but the economic effects of Varroa-X are parallel — farm income collapses, food prices spike,
rural communities contract. The instruments transfer:

- **Inverse crop yield bonds:** Almond, cherry, and apple inverse yield bonds pay out as
  yields collapse; this is exactly the trigger condition. DED should issue bonds tied to
  USDA NASS pollination-dependent crop yield indices. These were not pre-positioned for
  this event but the mechanism (outcome-linked-bonds.md) applies immediately.
- **Agricultural Bridge Loans (BSL variant):** Farm operators in USDA-declared pollinator
  emergency counties receive ABL-structure loans — fixed cost coverage during the yield
  shortfall period, secured by future crop insurance settlements. Priority for specialty
  crop operators whose entire revenue base is pollination-dependent.
- **CRHP deployment:** Beekeepers are a skilled agricultural workforce at risk of permanent
  exit from the industry. CRHP enrollment for commercial beekeepers provides income support
  and keeps skilled labor in the industry during the crisis, accelerating recovery.

**Oil Restriction / Food Price Instruments:**
Cooking oil price spikes (canola/sunflower) directly activate oil restriction Phase 1
demand management instruments:
- Gas tax / fuel surcharge revenue recycling to lower-income households (Fuel Security Dividend)
  applies to cooking oil price shocks — the DED's consumer rebate logic transfers to food
- The TRAL crisis activation provides income floor for households facing elevated food prices

**Shipping Blockade Phase 1 (Import Substitution):**
Increased import demand for almonds, berries, stone fruits engages shipping blockade instruments:
- Allied freight rerouting coordination (Chile, Australia, South Africa as primary alternative
  almond and stone fruit suppliers)
- Air freight priority contracts for the highest-value perishable imports
- Type C contracts with allied producers for guaranteed supply at floor prices

### New DED Actions Required

**Emergency Beekeeping Industry Survival Program (EBISP):**
The commercial beekeeping industry is the critical infrastructure being destroyed. There is
no DED instrument designed to preserve a biological production system — this is a gap.

Proposed program (Type A + Type B hybrid):
- **Survival payments (Type A):** Annual payments to commercial beekeepers per surviving
  colony during the crisis period ($300–500/colony/year; at 1.5M surviving colonies = $450–750M/year)
  — keeps experienced operators solvent and in the industry
- **Research emergency contracts (Type B):** USDA-ARS and university accelerators for
  Varroa-X treatment development; goal is a registered acaricide effective against the
  resistant biotype within 24 months. Cost: $300–500M
- **International treatment sharing:** If any foreign research program develops a treatment
  (Australia has active Varroa-resistant bee development; New Zealand Varroa management
  is advanced), DED IP licensing framework activates in reverse — DED pays for access to
  foreign treatment IP rather than licensing U.S. IP outward
- **Resistant bee strain Accelerator:** USDA-ARS has been developing Varroa-resistant bee
  strains (Mite-Biter, VSH — Varroa Sensitive Hygiene) for two decades. These have 30–50%
  lower Varroa load without treatment. DED Accelerator Type D contracts to scale production
  and distribution of resistant queens: $200–400M over 3 years to put resistant genetics
  into 40–50% of commercial colonies

**Pollinator Infrastructure Reserve Contracts:**
Analogous to the strategic transformer reserve in grid cyberattack, DED should pre-position
contracts preserving the minimum viable pollinator infrastructure:
- Type A contracts with the 500 largest commercial beekeeping operations (those operating
  >10,000 colonies) guaranteeing survival payments of $300/colony during any declared
  pollinator emergency
- These 500 operations represent ~60% of total commercial colony capacity
- Annual cost (peacetime): $0 (no colonies in emergency status)
- Annual cost (activated): $300M–500M (500k × $300–500 × 12 months)
- This is the minimum colony fleet needed to sustain almond pollination; if this floor
  collapses, the almond industry restructuring becomes permanent

**Crop Diversification Transition Payments:**
Specialty crop farmers (almonds, cherries, apples) facing permanent pollination shortfalls
should not simply wait for their orchards to fail. DED transition payments encourage managed
transitions to less pollination-dependent crops:
- Type A contracts: farmers who remove pollination-dependent orchards and transition to
  drought-tolerant, lower-pollination-dependency crops receive 8-year income support at
  70% of prior 5-year average net income
- Applies to: almond acreage in regions where commercial colony availability falls below
  50% of pollination requirement for 2+ consecutive seasons
- This prevents the chaotic land value collapse that follows disorderly crop failure; instead
  of forced abandonment, it's managed transition with income support
- Cost: $500M–1.5B/year for years 3–8 of the scenario

**Alternative Pollinator Development Accelerator:**
Honeybees are not the only pollinators. Alternatives exist but are not at commercial scale:
- **Mason bees (Osmia species):** Highly efficient pollinators (one mason bee = 120 honeybees
  for apple pollination); already used commercially in Pacific Northwest orchards; not scalable
  to almond acreage yet
- **Bumblebee (Bombus impatiens):** Commercial colonies exist; primary use is greenhouse
  tomato; outdoor application limited
- **Mechanical pollination:** Japanese apple orchards use hand-pollination and mechanical
  spreaders for pollen; labor-intensive but viable for high-value crops
- **AI-guided drone pollination:** Emerging technology; MIT and Japanese universities have
  demonstrated proof-of-concept; not close to commercial outdoor scale

DED Accelerator (Type D) targeting mason bee production scale-up and AI-guided drone
pollination:
- $300–500M over 5 years
- Mason bee production: target 5× current commercial production capacity (from 50M to
  250M bees/year); addresses mid-value crops
- Drone pollination: $100M for demonstration projects at almond scale; if viable, Type B
  contracts with manufacturers for commercial deployment in years 4–6

## 5. Optimal Program Combination

### Year 1: Stop the Industry Collapse ($1–2B)

The first-year priority is preventing the commercial beekeeping industry from falling below
the minimum viable fleet. Once skilled beekeepers exit and colonies are destroyed, they do
not return for 5–10 years. The recovery window collapses.

| Action | Cost (Year 1) | Purpose |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| EBISP survival payments (1.5M colonies × $400) | $600M | Keep operators solvent |
| USDA-ARS emergency research contracts | $300M | Acaricide treatment pipeline |
| Agricultural Bridge Loans for specialty crop farmers | $800M–1.5B | Income bridge during crop shortfall |
| Inverse yield bonds payout (pre-positioned) | Contingent | Farmer and insurer hedge |
| CRHP enrollment for displaced beekeepers | $100M | Skills retention in industry |

**Year 1 total: $1.8–2.5B**

### Year 2: Close the Supply Gap ($2–3B)

Year 2 priority: food price stabilization and alternative supply development.

| Action | Cost (Year 2) | Purpose |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| EBISP survival payments (continued) | $600M | Colony fleet preservation |
| Resistant queen Accelerator (ramp-up) | $150M | Genetics pipeline to resistant colonies |
| Alternative pollinator Accelerator launch | $150M | Mason bee + drone pollination |
| Type C allied sourcing (Chile, Australia almonds) | $400M | Import gap contracts |
| BSL for beekeeping operations with debt restructuring | $500M–1B | Prevent operator bankruptcy cascade |
| TRAL + stimulus activation for food price impact on low-income | $80–120B (short duration) | Consumer food price relief |

**Year 2 total: $2–4B (excluding TRAL stimulus which is self-funding/broader-purpose)**

### Year 3–5: Structural Transition ($3–5B/year)

| Action | Cost | Purpose |
|--------|------|---------|
| Crop diversification transition payments | $500M–1.5B/year | Managed orchard transition in high-risk regions |
| Resistant bee genetics deployment (Type B) | $300M/year | 40–50% resistant-genetic colonies by year 5 |
| Alternative pollinator commercialization | $200M/year | Mason bee and drone scale-up |
| Acaricide treatment deployment (if developed) | $100–300M | Distribution of new treatment to all operators |

**Years 3–5 total: ~$4–7B over 3 years**

### Total Program Cost vs. Damage

| | Cost |
|--|------|
| Total optimal DED investment (5 years) | $12–18B |
| Estimated damage prevented vs. no response | $100–200B |
| Unavoidable damage (season 1–2 before programs mature) | $60–80B |
| **Net benefit** | **$80–180B** |
| **Benefit/cost ratio** | **6–12:1** |

The ratio is lower than most DED scenarios because this event cannot be prevented once it
begins — the biology of the cascade is largely autonomous. DED value is in preventing the
*permanent* structural damage (industry collapse, orchard removal, land value crash) rather
than the immediate crop loss, which is largely unavoidable in seasons 1–2.

## 6. What the DED Wouldn't Have

**Critical Gap 1 — No biological agricultural infrastructure monitoring:**
NEID tracks crop prices, yield forecasts, and drought indices. It does not track the
health of agricultural support infrastructure — commercial colony count, Varroa infestation
rates, beekeeping operator solvency. By the time this scenario shows up in NEID's crop
yield data, the industry has already been collapsing for 6–12 months.

**Pre-build:** NEID Pollinator Infrastructure Index — quarterly USDA NASS survey of commercial
colony counts, winter loss rates, acaricide resistance reports, and operator solvency signals.
Alert: if winter loss rate exceeds 55% in any USDA region, or if a novel resistance report
is confirmed, Phase 1 assessment begins immediately. Cost: $5–10M/year.

**Critical Gap 2 — No pre-positioned beekeeping industry reserve contracts:**
There is no equivalent of the strategic transformer reserve for pollinator infrastructure.
Pre-building the 500-operator reserve contract structure (Type A, $300/colony activation)
before a crisis would cost near-zero in peacetime and prevent the most damaging cascade.

**Critical Gap 3 — No framework for biological threats to non-food supply chains:**
The DED considers biological threats in the pandemic scenario, but only as human pathogens.
Agricultural biological threats (crop diseases, pollinator pathogens, livestock diseases)
are structurally similar — they spread exponentially, they are hard to contain once established,
and the economic damage is compounding — but they are not covered by any existing DED program.

**Pre-build:** Expand NEID to include a standing agricultural biological threat dashboard
monitoring: Varroa resistance reports, avian influenza prevalence, crop rust and fungal
pathogen spread, and citrus greening. These are the biological analog to the energy price
and supply chain monitors that are NEID's core function.

**Critical Gap 4 — No polyphagous crisis — DED programs are designed for single scenarios:**
This scenario simultaneously activates drought analog programs, oil restriction programs,
shipping blockade programs, and financial fragmentation programs. The DED's scenario library
is designed around single-primary-scenario activation. A multi-cascade event like Varroa-X
(food, input costs, rural economy, import demand) requires a DED response coordinator with
authority to activate programs from multiple scenario libraries simultaneously.

**Pre-build:** DED General Response Authority — a standing authorization allowing the DED
Secretary to activate instruments from any scenario library when NEID indicators from
multiple scenarios exceed alert thresholds simultaneously, without requiring the event to
be classified under a specific named scenario.

## 7. Lessons for Pre-Positioning

1. **NEID needs biological infrastructure monitoring.** The biggest gap this scenario exposes
   is that NEID cannot see a biological cascade developing until it shows up in market prices —
   too late for Phase 1 prevention. Agricultural biological threat monitoring (pollinator health,
   livestock disease, crop pathogen) belongs in NEID as standing infrastructure.

2. **Pre-position beekeeping industry reserve contracts.** The 500-operator Type A contract
   ($0 peacetime cost) converts a potential catastrophic industry collapse into a managed
   disruption. This is the highest-leverage pre-build for this scenario: zero peacetime cost,
   enormous crisis value.

3. **The resistant bee genetics program should be ongoing, not crisis-activated.** USDA-ARS
   has been developing Varroa-resistant bee genetics for 20+ years without adequate funding
   to achieve commercial-scale deployment. A $50M/year standing DED Type B contract to
   deploy resistant queen genetics would reduce Varroa mortality in commercial beekeeping
   by 30–40% — not as crisis response, but as peacetime resilience building that makes
   Varroa-X less catastrophic when it arrives.

4. **Alternative pollinators as insurance, not replacement.** Mason bee production and
   drone pollination cannot replace honeybees at current scale. But a 5× increase in mason
   bee commercial availability is a $30–50M investment that provides meaningful insurance
   against partial colony loss for mid-value crops. Pre-build at low cost; activate in crisis.

5. **Agricultural biological threats need a dedicated DED scenario.** This red team exercise
   demonstrates that crop disease, livestock disease, and pollinator failure create economic
   cascades indistinguishable in structure from the DED's named scenarios — but are not
   covered. A new scenario category ("Agricultural Biological Threat") should be added to
   the DED scenario library, with its own overview, phase files, and pre-positioned instruments.
