*The case for an institution that prepares for the next one*

## Central Argument

The United States has a consistent pattern: it builds the institution after the disaster.
The Strategic Petroleum Reserve was created after the 1973 oil embargo. The Consumer
Financial Protection Bureau was created after the 2008 financial crisis. The Strategic
National Stockpile was rebuilt after COVID-19. In each case, the cost of improvising
during the crisis exceeded — by orders of magnitude — what the pre-built institution
would have cost. The Department of Economic Defense applies the logic of the Federal
Reserve and DARPA — standing institutions with focused mandates — to the full range of
economic security threats.

---

## Key Evidence and Numbers to Build From

- COVID-19 economic cost: $16 trillion in GDP loss (Federal Reserve Bank of New York);
  $5.2 trillion in federal emergency spending — all reactive, all improvised
- 2021 semiconductor shortage: $500 billion in global economic damage from a 10–15%
  supply imbalance. No government saw it coming. No institution had a plan.
- 2010 rare earth embargo: China restricted exports to Japan; prices spiked 13–103× in
  14 months. No strategic stockpile. No domestic processing capacity. No warning system.
- The Federal Reserve analogy: created in 1913 not in response to a crisis underway but
  to prevent the next one. Annual cost: ~$7B. Benefit: financial stability across a century.
- DARPA analogy: created in 1958 after Sputnik. Annual budget: ~$4B. Output: the internet,
  GPS, stealth technology, the foundational investments in autonomous vehicles.
- Proposed Department of Economic Defense annual budget: $60–80 billion — roughly the
  cost of the federal highway system, maintained permanently

## Tone Notes

This article should be written with controlled urgency — not alarmism, not complacency.
The reader should finish it thinking "this is obviously correct and obviously not yet
built." The Federal Reserve and DARPA comparisons are the load-bearing analogies.
Both were controversial when proposed. Both are now so obviously correct that no serious
person would suggest eliminating them. The Department of Economic Defense deserves to
be in that category.
