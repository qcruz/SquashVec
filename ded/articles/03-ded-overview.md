# We Always Prepare for the Last Crisis

*The case for an institution that prepares for the next one*

---

The Strategic Petroleum Reserve was created in 1975. The oil embargo that made it necessary
happened in 1973. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau was signed into law in 2010.
The financial crisis that made it necessary began in 2007. The Strategic National Stockpile
was rebuilt and expanded beginning in 2020. The pandemic that exposed its inadequacy
arrived in February of that same year.

This is not a coincidence. It is a pattern — one so consistent across American history
that it should probably be regarded as a law of institutional behavior rather than a
recurring oversight. The United States builds the institution after the disaster. It
improvises during the crisis at enormous cost, survives, and then — in the relative calm
that follows — constructs the agency that would have limited the damage if it had existed
before.

The improvisation is always expensive. COVID-19 cost the United States an estimated $16
trillion in GDP loss, according to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. The federal
government's emergency response — passed in a matter of weeks, designed in real time with
no pre-built framework — totaled $5.2 trillion in additional spending. For comparison, the
entire federal discretionary budget in 2019 was $1.3 trillion.

The 2021 global semiconductor shortage cost the world economy approximately $500 billion in
output — from a supply imbalance of 10 to 15 percent. Automakers idled factories. Electronics
manufacturers missed quarters. Medical device companies rationed components. The shortage
was entirely foreseeable: semiconductor production had been concentrating in Taiwan and South
Korea for two decades, the trend was documented, and no government agency had a plan for
what to do when the concentration became a problem. No government agency even had a mandate
to notice.

This article is about why the pattern persists, what it costs, and what the institution
that would break it looks like.

---

## Why We Always Arrive Late

The pattern is not a failure of intelligence. The 1972 CIA had analysts who understood
America's oil import dependency. The 2006 Federal Reserve had economists who understood
the risks accumulating in the mortgage market. The 2019 Department of Health and Human
Services had public health experts who had run pandemic simulations and understood the
inadequacy of the Strategic National Stockpile.

The problem is structural. Institutions are funded by annual appropriations. Annual
appropriations are controlled by legislators on two-year election cycles. Legislators
respond to constituent concerns. Constituent concerns are driven by present problems, not
future ones.

Pre-positioning for a crisis that has not yet happened requires spending money now for
benefits that accrue in the future, possibly to a future government with different politics.
It requires maintaining capabilities that may never be used — and whose non-use will be
cited as evidence of waste. It requires building institutions that will be criticized until
the moment they are desperately needed, at which point they will be praised for not being
built sooner.

There are two exceptions to this pattern in American institutional history. Both are
instructive.

The Federal Reserve was created in 1913 — not in response to a financial crisis underway,
but to prevent the next one. The Panic of 1907 was the motivating event, but by 1913 that
panic was six years in the past. Congress nonetheless created a standing institution with
a specific mandate, a defined set of tools, professional independence from the political
cycle, and a funding structure that did not depend on annual appropriations. The Federal
Reserve has been controversial at every moment of its existence. It has also prevented
financial collapses that would otherwise have been catastrophic, and it operates today with
so little political opposition that proposals to eliminate it are treated as fringe positions
regardless of party.

DARPA was created in 1958 — in direct response to Sputnik, but it was designed not to
manage the Sputnik crisis but to ensure that no future technological surprise would catch
the United States without an institutional response. Its annual budget is approximately
$4 billion. Its outputs include the internet, GPS, stealth technology, and the foundational
research that underlies autonomous vehicles, mRNA vaccine platforms, and modern
machine learning. The return on that $4 billion per year is not measurable in any
conventional accounting framework.

Both institutions share a design: professional staff insulated from political cycles, a
clear mandate that does not change with each administration, funding structures that survive
budget negotiations, and oversight frameworks that provide accountability without
micromanagement. Both were controversial when proposed. Both are now so obviously correct
that their elimination is unthinkable.

The Department of Economic Defense applies the same design logic to a different domain.

## What the Department of Economic Defense Is

The Department of Economic Defense is a cabinet-level agency with a single mandate: prepare
for economic disruptions before they happen, using economic instruments — not during them,
using improvised emergency powers.

Every word of that mandate is load-bearing.

**Cabinet-level** means it has a seat at every national security conversation. Economic
threats are not treated as footnotes to military strategy. The Secretary of Economic Defense
chairs the National Economic Security Council — the interagency body that coordinates
responses across Treasury, Commerce, Defense, Agriculture, Energy, and State. In 2021,
when the semiconductor shortage hit, there was no such council. There was no single person
whose job was to coordinate the federal response across industries. There was improvisation.

**Economic instruments** means the department operates through contracts, investment, tax
incentives, and financial guarantees — not rationing, not price controls, not emergency
mandates to private companies. This distinction is fundamental. The department does not
tell companies what to produce. It creates conditions in which the desired production is
economically rational. It signs long-term contracts that guarantee a market for domestic
semiconductor production before that capacity exists — so that private investors have a
revenue commitment against which to borrow and build. It does not build the fabs. It does
not nationalize the fabs. It creates the financial conditions in which private capital
builds them.

**Before they happen** means the department's entire purpose is anticipatory. Its National
Economic Intelligence Division monitors leading indicators across all identified risk
categories and publishes quarterly reports on commodity price trajectories and supply chain
stress. Its Office of Scenario Analysis maintains a library of pre-built response packages
for identified disruption categories — packages that have been designed, reviewed, debated,
and refined in peacetime, so that when a crisis arrives the government is not drafting
policy under pressure.

---

## What the Department of Economic Defense Is Not

The mandate comes with explicit prohibitions, because the failure modes of emergency-authority
agencies are well-documented and the department must be designed against them.

The Department of Economic Defense has no authority to ration goods. That authority stays
with FEMA, USDA, and state governments. It has no authority to set prices. It has no
authority to compel private sector action outside of contract terms. Participation in its
programs is voluntary — the incentives are designed to make participation economically
rational, not mandatory. It has no authority to conduct surveillance or compel data
disclosure. And it has no authority to operate critical infrastructure. Its role in the
energy grid, water systems, and transportation networks is limited to financial contracts
and advisory functions.

These prohibitions are not rhetorical. They are statutory — written into the enabling
legislation with the same legal weight as the grants of authority. An agency with a
broad mandate to protect economic security and unlimited authority to pursue it is not a
solution to the problem. It is a different problem. The Department of Economic Defense
is designed to be powerful within a narrow, clearly defined lane — and to have no
authority beyond it.

## The Nine Scenarios

The department's scenario library — the catalog of disruptions it pre-positions for — is
public. Every contract the department signs must link to a scenario in that library. The
library cannot be changed by political appointees without oversight approval. And the
library is designed to cover the range of threats that no single existing agency has a
mandate to address.

The nine identified scenarios are not predictions. They are categories of disruption that
historical evidence suggests are plausible within any given decade, that would impose
severe economic costs if they arrived without preparation, and that have no adequate
institutional response under the current structure. They cover:

**Energy shock.** An oil import restriction, natural gas supply disruption, or grid
cyberattack that causes energy prices to spike and cascade into every sector of the
economy that uses energy — which is every sector of the economy.

**Rare earth and critical mineral denial.** A foreign government restricts exports of
materials that U.S. defense, technology, and clean energy industries depend on. In 2010,
China's rare earth embargo drove prices up 13 to 103 times their prior levels in fourteen
months. There was no U.S. strategic stockpile, no domestic processing capacity, and no
pre-built response. There still isn't.

**Semiconductor collapse.** A concentration of global advanced chip production in a
geopolitically exposed region becomes a supply crisis. The 2021 version was mild — a
cyclical imbalance, not a deliberate embargo. A Taiwan Strait crisis would be a different
category of event entirely.

**Agricultural drought.** A multi-year drought in the American Midwest affects 40% of
global corn and soybean production. The 2012 single-season drought caused $77 billion in
economic damage. A multi-year event affecting multiple growing regions would be five to
ten times larger. The Colorado River's current flow is 20% below its 2000 level and
declining.

**Pandemic and biological threat.** The COVID-19 version was not the worst-case scenario.
A pathogen with higher transmissibility, higher lethality, or resistance to rapid vaccine
development would impose costs that dwarf the $16 trillion figure.

**Artificial intelligence unemployment.** A rapid deployment of AI systems displaces
workers across multiple sectors faster than the labor market can absorb. The question is
not whether AI will create new jobs — it likely will — but whether the adjustment period
is managed or improvised.

**Financial system fragmentation.** A disruption to dollar-denominated global trade —
whether from sanctions blowback, digital currency displacement, or deliberate decoupling
by adversarial economies — disrupts the international financial infrastructure on which
U.S. economic power depends.

**Cyberattack on critical infrastructure.** A sophisticated attack on the electrical grid,
water systems, or financial clearing infrastructure. Unlike the scenarios above, this one
arrives without warning and is designed to overwhelm improvised response.

**Pharmaceutical and medical supply disruption.** 80% of active pharmaceutical ingredients
for the U.S. market are manufactured in China and India. A supply disruption — through
trade conflict, political decision, or natural disaster — affects medications that Americans
depend on daily.

The point is not that all nine will happen. The point is that the probability of at least
one significant event from this list within any ten-year period is high — and that the
cost of arriving at that event without preparation is, in each case, orders of magnitude
larger than the cost of preparation.

No single existing agency has a mandate to think across all nine simultaneously. Commerce
tracks trade data but does not maintain pre-built crisis response packages. FEMA manages
disasters but is reactive by design and has no economic instruments. Treasury manages
financial stability but not supply chain risk. The Department of Agriculture manages food
programs but has no mandate to pre-position for a multi-year drought that hasn't arrived.
The Department of Economic Defense occupies the intersection of all of these — the space
where none of the existing agencies has jurisdiction.

## The Investment Case

The department's steady-state annual budget is $60 to $80 billion — roughly the cost of
the federal highway system, maintained permanently.

The highway system does not prevent pandemics, semiconductor shortages, or oil embargos.

To make the investment case concrete: COVID-19 cost the United States $16 trillion in GDP
loss and $5.2 trillion in emergency federal spending. The Department of Economic Defense's
full ten-year budget, at the gross level, would be approximately $680 billion. If the
department had existed and performed at even moderate effectiveness, it would not have
prevented COVID-19 — no institution can prevent a novel virus. But it would have entered
the crisis with pre-built response packages, pre-positioned financial instruments, and
a Strategic National Stockpile sized for actual demand rather than budget negotiations.
The conservative estimate of what that preparation would have been worth — in avoided
emergency spending, faster economic recovery, and reduced mortality from supply failures —
exceeds the department's ten-year budget at the gross level.

That is a comparison based on a single event that has already happened.

The expected value case is more systematic. At any point in time, the probability that
some event from the nine-scenario list will impose more than 0.5% of GDP in losses within
the next decade is not low. The semiconductor shortage alone cost $500 billion globally in
2021, and it was a mild version of what a deliberate supply interruption could produce.
A single rare earth embargo at the 2010 price-spike magnitude would impose tens of billions
in costs across defense manufacturing, consumer electronics, and clean energy production.
A two-year agricultural drought in the Corn Belt — an event that becomes more probable
each decade — would cost hundreds of billions and affect global food markets.

The question is not whether the expected value of preparation exceeds its cost. It
clearly does. The question is why, given that it clearly does, the institution does
not exist.

The answer is the same pattern described above. Congress operates on a two-year cycle.
Preparation pays off in the future. The people who appropriate money now will not be in
office when the crisis they prevented fails to materialize.

The Federal Reserve's founding legislation solved this problem by creating an institution
with its own funding mechanism and a mandate that survives administrations. The Department
of Economic Defense solves it the same way: dedicated revenue streams, multi-decade
contract terms that new administrations cannot casually unwind, and a public scenario
library that makes the mandate visible and accountable.

## The Anti-Capture Design

The most common objection to a powerful economic security agency is not philosophical.
It is practical: government agencies are captured by the industries they are designed
to regulate or support. The defense procurement system — the department's closest
institutional relative — is the cautionary example. The Department of Defense budget
exceeds $800 billion per year and is shaped at least as much by Congressional district
employment politics and prime contractor relationships as by actual defense requirements.
A Department of Economic Defense with similar dynamics would be a very expensive way to
funnel money to politically connected industries dressed in national security language.

The department's founding charter addresses this directly with a set of structural
provisions that are worth understanding, because they are the difference between an
institution that works and one that becomes the problem it was meant to solve.

Every contract the department signs must link to a published scenario in the public
scenario library. Scenarios cannot be retroactively added to justify contracts already
negotiated. This requirement prevents the most common failure mode: a project with no
genuine strategic rationale wrapped in national security language after the fact.

The contract portfolio is subject to geographic distribution caps: no more than 8% in
any single congressional district, no more than 20% in any single state, no more than
35% in any single economic sector. These caps do not prevent strategic investment —
they prevent the department from becoming a regional development slush fund directed by
whoever holds the most seats on the relevant oversight committees.

Revolving-door restrictions are aggressive: the Secretary and Under Secretaries cannot
join any contracted company or lobbying firm representing those companies for five years
after leaving. Contract managers cannot join companies they personally oversaw within
three years. These are not token provisions — they are longer and more stringent than
standard government ethics rules.

The Joint Committee on Economic Defense — a standing bipartisan congressional committee —
has approval authority over all contracts above $2 billion and all emergency declarations
above Tier 1. The committee cannot direct specific investments, but it can block ones
that fall outside the published mandate. This creates an accountability structure that
is robust without crossing into micromanagement.

And the career staff — the analysts, scenario specialists, and contract managers who
carry the institutional knowledge — are civil service employees protected across
administrations. The 14 political appointees set direction. The 3,500 career staff
maintain the portfolio. A new administration can change priorities at the margin. It
cannot dismantle decades of accumulated scenario analysis and contract relationships
in an afternoon.

## The Institution America Keeps Almost Building

The Council on Competitiveness. The National Economic Council. The Supply Chain
Disruptions Task Force. The Office of Manufacturing Policy. Over the past thirty years,
the United States has created and dissolved a succession of advisory bodies, interagency
working groups, and White House offices that gesture at exactly the mandate the Department
of Economic Defense would hold.

Each of them shared two characteristics: they had no independent budget, and they had no
durable authority. When the administration that created them left office, they disappeared.
Their work — scenario analyses, strategic assessments, policy frameworks — disappeared with
them. The next administration began roughly where the previous one had started, often
without access to the prior work.

This is the institutional equivalent of rebuilding a burned house with the same wood in
the same location and being surprised when it burns again.

The Federal Reserve does not disappear with each administration. DARPA does not disappear
with each administration. The Department of Transportation does not disappear with each
administration. These institutions persist because they were created by statute, funded
by dedicated mechanisms, and given mandates that survive political transitions. The
Department of Economic Defense must be built the same way — not as a White House office,
not as an advisory council, but as a permanent cabinet department with statutory authority
and a mandate written in law.

---

## What This Looks Like After the Crisis

There will be a moment — possibly in the next decade, possibly sooner — when the United
States faces a disruption from the nine-scenario list and there is no pre-built response.
There will be emergency legislation passed in weeks instead of months, cobbled together
from provisions that were not designed to work together, costing orders of magnitude more
than preparation would have cost. The Congressional hearings afterward will ask why no
institution had a plan.

And then, in the relative calm that follows, Congress will create one.

The Strategic Petroleum Reserve was created in 1975. The Consumer Financial Protection
Bureau was created in 2010. The Strategic National Stockpile rebuild began in 2020.

The Department of Economic Defense could be created now — before the next crisis, rather
than after it. The analysis is done. The scenarios are documented. The institutional
design is worked out. The expected value case is not ambiguous.

The only question is whether, this time, we build it first.
