# The Ownership Economy

*What if every American owned a piece of the country they built?*

---

Every October, the state of Alaska mails a check to every resident who has lived there for
at least one year. In 2022, that check was for $3,284. In a lean year, it might be $1,000.
The program has been running since 1982, and in forty years, no serious Alaska politician —
Republican or Democrat — has proposed eliminating it. Alaskans across every political
identity defend it with remarkable intensity, even when they disagree about virtually
everything else their government does.

The Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend is not welfare. It does not phase out when you earn
more money. It does not require an application, a caseworker, or proof of need. It goes
to every qualifying resident: the oil worker and the schoolteacher, the CEO of a Juneau
law firm and the retired fisherman on Kodiak Island. It is a dividend on a natural resource
that Alaskans collectively own — the oil beneath the public land of their state — returned
to them in the form of an annual check.

The question this article asks is simple: why doesn't every American have one?

Not just a rebate or a stimulus payment. A permanent, growing, annually distributed dividend
on the national wealth that Americans collectively generate, own, and have never been paid
for.

The proposal is the American Sovereign Fund. It is not a utopian idea. It is the obvious
extension of something that already exists — scaled to the country that could have built
it first and chose, repeatedly, not to.

---

## The Missed Moments

America has been here before. The moments when a national wealth fund could have been built
are not hypothetical — they are historical, and the reasons they weren't built are
instructive.

**1973.** The OPEC oil embargo reveals, overnight, that the United States is dangerously
dependent on imported petroleum. The country responds with a flurry of policy. The Strategic
Petroleum Reserve is created. The Department of Energy is established. What is not created
is a national fund to capture the revenue from the domestic oil production that the
United States ramps up in response. That revenue — billions annually from federal land
leases and royalties — flows into the general fund and is spent.

Alaska watches this and draws a different lesson. In 1976, by constitutional amendment,
Alaska dedicates 25% of all oil and mineral royalties from state lands to a permanent fund.
The rest, as they say, is history. Alaska's fund now stands at $80 billion for a state of
730,000 people.

**1998.** The United States runs its first federal budget surplus in thirty years. The
question of what to do with the surplus produces a genuine national debate. Al Gore proposes
putting the surplus in a "lockbox" for Social Security. Congressional Republicans want to
return it as tax cuts. What no one seriously proposes is a national sovereign wealth fund —
a permanently capitalized investment vehicle that would have begun compounding in 1999 and
would stand at over $3 trillion today.

The surplus was used to pay down debt. Fine — debt reduction has value. But it was not
transformed into permanent national capital. A dollar of debt repaid is gone. A dollar
invested in a sovereign wealth fund compounds for decades.

**The pattern.** Every time the United States has had surplus revenue, the incentive
structure of annual appropriations has won. Congress operates on a two-year election cycle.
Spending money now is visible to voters. Saving it in a fund that pays dividends in fifteen
years is not. The long-horizon investment always loses to the immediate expenditure — not
because the long-horizon investment is wrong, but because the people who make budget
decisions will not be in office when it pays off.

This is the structural problem the American Sovereign Fund is designed to solve. It
takes the decision to save out of the annual appropriations process entirely.

## How the Fund Works

The American Sovereign Fund is capitalized by three dedicated revenue streams — revenue
that currently flows into the general fund with no particular purpose, or that exists
as a policy lever without a mechanism to convert it into lasting national capital.

**Revenue stream one: arms sales.**

The United States is the world's largest arms exporter, by a significant margin. In fiscal
year 2024, U.S. defense exports — including both government-to-government sales and
direct commercial sales — totaled approximately $265 billion. These sales are made possible
by the U.S. defense industrial base: the factories, engineers, supply chains, and
technological infrastructure that American taxpayers have funded for eight decades. The
workers who build those weapons are American citizens. The research that makes them
competitive was largely government-funded. The military credibility that makes allies want
to buy American equipment is a public good produced by every American who has ever served
or paid taxes.

And yet the proceeds of those sales flow to the companies that manufacture the equipment —
and the portion that flows to the government (export licensing fees, minor administrative
charges) is absorbed into discretionary spending with no dedicated purpose.

A 7.5% levy on all defense export contracts — built into pricing with allied governments
at the point of sale — generates approximately $20 billion per year at current volumes.
Over the next decade, as U.S. allies increase defense spending in response to a changed
security environment, that baseline is likely to rise. The levy does not reduce U.S.
competitiveness in the global arms market; it is an operational cost that is priced into
contracts, similar to the way every government contract incorporates overhead and profit
margins.

**Revenue stream two: tariff revenue.**

The United States collects between $80 billion and $300 billion per year in customs and
tariff revenue, depending on the trade regime in effect. In the elevated tariff environment
that has persisted since 2018 and accelerated since 2025, the upper end of that range is
increasingly the baseline.

Tariffs are a form of national decision-making about trade. When the United States imposes
tariffs on Chinese semiconductors, it is saying: the domestic cost of dependency on this
supply chain exceeds the consumer savings from cheap imports. It is a judgment that
national resilience has a value that market prices do not capture. That judgment is
correct. But if that judgment is correct, then the revenue generated by tariffs should
fund the domestic alternative — not disappear into general spending.

Twenty percent of tariff revenue, deposited automatically into the American Sovereign Fund
before any appropriation process, generates $16 to $60 billion per year. The exact number
tracks trade policy: in high-tariff years, more capital accumulates. In low-tariff years,
less does. This is not a flaw — it is a feature. The fund grows fastest precisely when the
case for domestic investment is most urgent.

**Revenue stream three: dedicated taxes.**

The third stream consists of three targeted levies on sectors that have most benefited from
the public investments the fund is meant to repay:

- A 0.5% excise on enterprise software licensing revenue for companies above $1 billion
  in annual revenue — approximately $4 billion per year and growing at 12% annually as
  software markets expand.
- A 0.01% surcharge (one basis point) on U.S. equity and derivatives trades above $1 million
  notional — calibrated to be invisible to retail investors and to absorb into institutional
  bid-ask spreads. At current trading volumes, this generates approximately $17 to $20
  billion per year.
- An additional 3% royalty on oil, gas, and mineral extraction on federal lands, deposited
  directly to the fund. This is the national version of what Alaska did: public land
  generates public capital, not just current government revenue. Approximately $6 billion
  per year.

**Combined, the three streams generate $60 to $137 billion per year** — $60 billion in the
conservative base case, higher as arms sales grow and trade policy elevates tariff revenue.

## What the Fund Invests In

The fund operates in two tranches, and the distinction between them is important.

**The Commercial Portfolio** — 75% of assets under management — is invested for maximum
risk-adjusted return using standard institutional asset management practices. Professional
asset managers are selected by competitive process. Performance is benchmarked against
the S&P 500 and peer sovereign wealth funds. The target return is 6 to 7.5% annually,
consistent with what Norway's Government Pension Fund Global has averaged since inception.
No government agency directs these investments. No congressional member can steer them.
The mandate is return.

**The Strategic Reserve** — 25% of assets — is invested in sectors that the Department
of Economic Defense identifies as critical national vulnerabilities: domestic semiconductor
manufacturing, rare earth processing, pharmaceutical production, energy infrastructure,
advanced materials. These investments earn below-market returns (3 to 4.5%) because they
are not pure commercial bets — they are insurance. The fund accepts a lower financial
return in exchange for the resilience value of having domestic capacity in sectors where
a foreign government could otherwise cut off supply overnight.

The two-tranche design is not new. Singapore has operated a nearly identical structure for
decades. The Government Investment Corporation manages Singapore's foreign reserves for
maximum commercial return. Temasek Holdings manages strategic domestic investments in
Singapore Airlines, port infrastructure, telecommunications. Singapore Airlines is not a
nationalized laggard — it is one of the most profitable airlines in the world. The
Temasek model demonstrates that government equity in strategic industries, managed with
commercial governance and return requirements, produces better outcomes than either
pure privatization or pure state ownership.

The blended return on the full fund — 75% at 6 to 7.5%, 25% at 3 to 4.5% — is
approximately 5.5% annually. At that return, with $60 billion in annual contributions,
the fund reaches $850 billion in ten years and $5.5 trillion in thirty years. Norway's
fund, for reference, reached $1.6 trillion in thirty years on annual contributions that
were larger but came from a smaller, single-commodity source.

## The Alaska Lesson

Alaska's experience with the Permanent Fund Dividend is forty-three years of real-world
data on what a universal citizen dividend does — politically, economically, and socially.
The lessons are more nuanced than either proponents or critics of basic income tend to
acknowledge.

**What Alaska got right.**

The constitutional mandate worked. For forty years, the fund was protected from exactly
the kind of political raiding that kills every other long-horizon government savings
program. When the state faced budget shortfalls, the legislature found other ways to
close the gap — or raised revenue — rather than raiding the fund. The constitutional
protection forced the political system to solve problems at the source rather than
borrowing from the future.

The dividend created a constituency of overwhelming political force. Every Alaskan has
a direct financial stake in the fund's health. Every candidate who has ever proposed
touching the fund has learned, quickly and painfully, that this is not a winning strategy.
The political durability of a universal benefit that writes people annual checks is a
structural fact, not a contingent one. It works the same way Social Security works: the
check makes the program real in people's lives, and people defend what is real.

The fund also demonstrated that a professionally managed, politically insulated investment
vehicle can produce market-rate returns. The Alaska Permanent Fund Corporation — the
entity that manages the fund — has consistently outperformed when managed by investment
professionals and underperformed when subjected to political interference. The governance
lesson is clear.

**What Alaska got wrong — and what the American Sovereign Fund fixes.**

Alaska began distributing dividends in 1982, just six years after the fund was established.
As a result, the fund is smaller than it would have been if it had spent another decade
compounding before the first distribution. The American Sovereign Fund spends its first
ten years in pure accumulation — no dividends paid, all returns reinvested. This produces
a dramatically larger base and a dramatically larger eventual dividend.

Alaska's eligibility is based on residency — one year in the state is sufficient. This
creates gaming incentives (people move to Alaska specifically for the dividend) and doesn't
reflect any deeper relationship to the wealth being distributed. The American Sovereign Fund
ties eligibility to citizenship held for eighteen years. This is not arbitrary. It mirrors
the logic of a pension: you accumulate a stake over time, and you draw on it after
sufficient investment in the enterprise. A citizen who has lived and worked and paid taxes
in the United States for eighteen years has contributed to the national wealth. A tourist
who happened to obtain citizenship two years ago has not.

Alaska's fund is also dependent on a single revenue source — oil royalties. When oil prices
collapsed, the fund's income collapsed. When the legislature raided the fund in 2016 to
close a budget gap, the dividend was cut in half. The American Sovereign Fund's revenue
diversification — arms sales, tariffs, technology taxes, financial transaction surcharges,
resource royalties — means that no single market disruption cuts the fund's inflows
dramatically. The streams are designed to be uncorrelated: technology market slowdowns
don't affect arms sales; trade tensions that reduce tariff revenue also tend to increase
domestic manufacturing investment, which increases the technology tax base.

## The Arms Sales Loop

The most counterintuitive part of the fund's design is the arms sales levy — and it is
worth examining carefully, because the intuition that makes it seem controversial is wrong.

The criticism, stated plainly: is it appropriate for the United States to build national
wealth on the proceeds of weapons sales?

The answer is that the United States is already building private wealth on those proceeds.
Lockheed Martin's shareholders receive dividends from F-35 sales. Raytheon's shareholders
receive dividends from Patriot missile contracts. The defense industrial base — built on
public R&D, public infrastructure, and a workforce trained by public institutions — generates
enormous private returns that flow to shareholders, not to the citizens whose tax dollars
funded the underlying capability.

The 7.5% levy does not make U.S. weapons sales more or less moral. It does not change
which countries the United States sells to, or under what conditions — those decisions
remain with the State Department and Congress, as they always have been. What the levy
does is ensure that some portion of the proceeds from national security assets flows back
to the national treasury, and ultimately to every eligible citizen.

The second-order effect is genuinely constructive. The levy funds a Strategic Reserve that
invests in the domestic defense industrial base — in the Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers
who make components for those weapons and who are chronically undercapitalized. The
Strategic Reserve investment reduces the cost of producing those components. Lower component
costs reduce the unit cost of the finished weapon. A cheaper F-35 is a more competitive
F-35. More competitive weapons generate more sales. More sales generate more levy revenue.

The loop: arms sales fund domestic industrial investment that makes arms cheaper to produce,
which makes them more competitive globally, which increases sales, which increases the levy.
Citizens who bear the costs of the defense industrial base — through taxes and through the
public goods they contribute to — receive a share of its revenues for the first time.

## The Dividend

The first American Sovereign Fund dividend is paid in Year 15. At that point, the fund
holds approximately $1.6 trillion in assets — equivalent to Norway's entire sovereign
wealth fund today. Thirty percent of the year's returns are distributed. The dividend:
approximately $108 per eligible American.

That is not a lot of money. It is not designed to be, at Year 15. It is designed to be
real — a check that arrives annually, that every eligible American receives regardless
of income or political affiliation, that begins to create the constituency that protects
the fund for the decades ahead.

By Year 30, the fund holds $5.5 trillion. Sixty percent of annual returns are distributed.
The dividend: $720 per eligible American per year. By Year 40, $1,289. By Year 50, $2,174
in the base case — approaching $4,000 in the mid case, and over $5,000 in the high case.

These are not transformative amounts in isolation. Combined with the Negative Income Tax
(the income floor described in the previous article in this series), they become a
meaningful permanent income supplement — the difference between precarity and stability
for tens of millions of Americans.

**Who receives it.** Every U.S. citizen who has held citizenship for eighteen or more years,
including diaspora citizens living abroad (at a reduced rate for long-term non-residents).
Natural-born citizens become eligible at 18. Naturalized citizens become eligible eighteen
years after the date of naturalization — which means they must clear the naturalization
process (five years minimum) and then wait another eighteen years. The design is not
exclusionary toward immigrants; it reflects the same logic as Social Security's work-credit
requirement. You build a stake over time. You draw on it after you have built it.

The dividend is taxable as ordinary income for recipients above the standard deduction
threshold. For low-income recipients, it falls below the threshold and is effectively
tax-free. Middle-income recipients pay their marginal rate on it. The dividend itself is
not means-tested, but the tax system creates a mild progressive structure without the
political toxicity of eligibility cutoffs.

## The Political Economy of the Long Game

Social Security was enacted in 1935. Its first monthly payment — $22.54 — went to Ida May
Fuller of Brattleboro, Vermont, in January 1940. For decades, it was a modest supplement.
Today it is the most politically durable program in the federal government — a program
that legislators approach with such caution that "cutting Social Security" has become a
shorthand for political suicide regardless of party.

The durability is not an accident. It is the result of a design that made the benefit
universal, automatic, and tied to a clear claim. Social Security beneficiaries are not
receiving welfare. They are receiving a return on contributions they made throughout their
working lives. That framing — you earned this — is the source of the program's political
invulnerability.

The American Sovereign Fund dividend is designed on the same logic. You are not receiving
a government transfer. You are receiving a dividend on national assets that you own as a
citizen — assets that your taxes, your labor, and your participation in the national
enterprise have helped to create and maintain. The arms sales that fund the levy were made
possible by the defense industrial base you have collectively supported. The tariff revenue
reflects trade policy decisions made in your name. The technology taxes fall on industries
that grew on the foundation of publicly funded research — the internet, GPS, semiconductor
manufacturing processes developed in federal labs and funded by the Department of Defense.

By Year 15, when the first dividends are paid, more than 235 million eligible Americans
will receive an annual check. By Year 30, the political constituency for the fund's
continued growth will be indistinguishable from the constituency for Social Security. No
politician will propose eliminating it. No budget negotiation will seriously threaten it.
The annual dividend check — visible, universal, growing — will have become part of what
it means to be an American citizen.

That is the intended outcome. The dividend is not just an economic instrument. It is a
political instrument, designed to protect the fund and the Department of Economic Defense
programs it supports against the same short-term political pressures that have killed every
other long-horizon investment program in U.S. history.

## The Governance Question

The most common objection to a national sovereign wealth fund is not philosophical. It is
practical: governments are bad at managing money. The Saudi Public Investment Fund, directed
by political priorities, has produced below-market returns and notoriously questionable
investments. If the American Sovereign Fund is just another government program subject
to congressional earmarks and political direction, it will fail in exactly the same ways.

The governance design of the American Sovereign Fund is built around a single principle:
insulation. The same principle that makes the Federal Reserve function — professional
management, political independence, a clear mandate, and transparent reporting — is built
into the fund's charter at every level.

The Board of Trustees is structured to prevent single-party control: no more than five of
nine trustees from the same party at any time. Trustees cannot have been registered
lobbyists or senior government officials within four years of appointment. The Chief
Investment Officer is a professional appointment — not a political one — removable only
for cause by a supermajority board vote. The Investment Committee is composed of investment
professionals drawn from pension funds, endowments, and peer sovereign wealth funds. No
trustee sits on the Investment Committee — governance decisions and investment decisions
are separated.

Congressional oversight is robust (annual reporting, triennial Government Accountability
Office audits, embedded Inspector General) but does not extend to directing individual
investments. The legislature may audit; it may not instruct. All fund holdings above
$100 million are publicly disclosed quarterly — making any politically motivated investment
visible to the press and public immediately.

The Strategic Reserve's investment decisions are not political designations. They are
triggered by the Department of Economic Defense's Concentration Risk Score analysis —
a formula that measures supply chain vulnerability across standardized inputs. When a
sector's vulnerability score exceeds the threshold, the fund is required to deploy capital
in it. This mechanizes the strategic investment decision: sectors qualify by measured
risk, not congressional earmark.

The constitutional protection mechanism — a supermajority requirement to amend the fund's
core structure — makes the 2016 Alaska raid impossible at the federal level. A bare
majority in Congress cannot redirect the fund. It would take 67% of both chambers to change
the eligibility rules, the distribution formula, or the revenue dedication. That bar
protects the fund against exactly the kind of budget-crisis opportunism that periodically
threatens every large federal savings vehicle.

## What the Fund Becomes

At Year 30, the American Sovereign Fund holds approximately $5.5 trillion in assets —
roughly twice the current size of the Social Security Trust Fund, and larger than any
sovereign wealth fund that exists today. Twenty-five percent of that — $1.4 trillion —
is deployed in U.S. strategic sectors: $280 billion in domestic semiconductor
manufacturing, $210 billion in critical minerals processing, $140 billion in pharmaceutical
manufacturing, $280 billion in energy infrastructure. These are not government subsidies.
They are equity positions that generate returns and maintain the industrial capacity that
the country needs regardless of what any foreign government decides to do with its supply
chains.

The remaining $4.1 trillion in the Commercial Portfolio earns market-rate returns on a
diversified portfolio of U.S. and global assets. At 6 to 7.5% annually, it generates $250
to $300 billion in annual returns. Sixty percent of that — $150 to $180 billion — is
distributed to eligible citizens. The dividend rises every year as the fund grows.

The Strategic Reserve's equity positions in domestic semiconductor fabs mean that when
a future administration needs to negotiate with Taiwan, or China, or Korea about
advanced chip access, it does so from a position of partial self-sufficiency rather than
complete dependency. The equity positions in rare earth processing mean that the next rare
earth embargo — and there will be a next one — triggers the fund's emergency capital
deployment rather than a national crisis.

The dividend means that when citizens ask what they personally get from a government that
invests in semiconductor fabs and rare earth processing and pharmaceutical manufacturing —
the answer is concrete, annual, and deposited directly to their bank account.

That is a different country than the one we have. Not because the economics are
fundamentally different — the same wealth exists now. But because the ownership structure
is different. Right now, the wealth generated by national assets flows primarily to
shareholders of the companies that extract and export it. The American Sovereign Fund
redirects a defined share of that flow — not all of it, not most of it, but enough to
matter — to the citizens whose collective labor, taxes, and national standing made
those assets valuable in the first place.

## A Final Note on Alaska

Sarah Palin, who is not generally associated with expanding government programs, increased
Alaska's Permanent Fund Dividend in 2008. She added a $1,200 energy rebate on top of the
regular $2,069 dividend, for a total payout of $3,269 per Alaskan. She did this because
the dividend is popular, because it is defensible as a return on public assets, and because
it works.

The American Sovereign Fund is not a left-wing proposal and it is not a right-wing
proposal. It is a mechanism for ensuring that national wealth — generated by defense
exports, trade policy, resource extraction, and technology markets — is partially returned
to the citizens who made it possible, rather than entirely absorbed by the general fund
or distributed to private shareholders.

Alaska figured this out in 1976. Every year since 1982, the state has mailed a check to
every qualifying resident, and no serious politician has proposed stopping.

The only question is why it took the rest of the country this long to notice.
