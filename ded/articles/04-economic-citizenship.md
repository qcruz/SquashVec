# The Contract We Forgot We Signed

*Citizenship has always come with economic obligations — on both sides*

---

In 1944, the United States government made a deal.

It told eighteen million returning service members: you gave us your years, your bodies, and for 400,000 of you, your lives. In exchange, we owe you something real. Not a parade. Not a handshake. Something you can use. Education. A mortgage. Healthcare. A stake in the country you defended.

Nobody called the GI Bill welfare. Nobody called it redistribution. Nobody accused the 1944 Congress of socialism for providing $51 billion in benefits over its first decade. It was the return on an obligation already fulfilled. The country asked something enormous of its citizens. The citizens delivered. The contract closed.

Eighty years later, the economy asks different things. It asks workers to accept wages that have not kept pace with productivity gains for fifty years. It asks caregivers — mostly women — to provide $470 billion per year in unpaid labor that the healthcare system would otherwise have to supply at ten times the cost. It asks every citizen to absorb the economic consequences of disruptions they had no hand in creating — pandemics, supply chain collapses, automation waves — with whatever personal savings they happened to have.

The contract is not closing.

This article is about the other side of that ledger. Not about what citizens owe — Americans have always been clear on that part — but about what the economy owes back. Four specific things. An income floor. An opportunity floor. A health floor. A wealth floor. Each one is the return on an obligation already fulfilled. Each one is the second half of a deal the economy has been collecting on without paying.

---

## What Citizenship Has Always Required

The American social contract has never been abstract.

The founders understood citizenship as an active status with specific obligations. Civic participation. Legal accountability. Military service when called. These were not optional. The republic required contribution, and in exchange it granted standing — the right to own property, to vote, to petition for redress, to accumulate wealth in a system of enforceable law.

The Civil War amendments renegotiated the contract. They extended formal citizenship to formerly enslaved Americans and, in doing so, had to articulate what citizenship actually meant. The Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection clause is the founding document of modern economic citizenship: the state cannot deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process. The contract runs in both directions.

The New Deal renegotiated it again. Faced with mass unemployment and systemic economic failure, the Roosevelt administration argued that citizens who had fulfilled all of their obligations — who had worked, paid taxes, contributed — were being destroyed by forces they did not cause and could not control. The Social Security Act, the minimum wage, the right to organize: each was framed as the government honoring obligations the market had failed to honor on its behalf.

The GI Bill renegotiated it a third time, most explicitly. The explicit logic was: you fulfilled an obligation that no individual should have to fulfill voluntarily; the country therefore owes you a proportional return.

Each renegotiation of the American social contract followed the same structure. The economy expanded what it asked of citizens — larger armies, more complex labor markets, more education required to participate, more geographic mobility required to find work — and the contract adjusted to give citizens a proportional return.

The question for this moment is: what has the economy asked, and what does the contract require in return?

---

## What the Economy Has Been Asking

Three things the modern economy extracts from citizens without paying for:

**First: the productivity surplus.** Between 1979 and 2020, productivity in the United States grew by 61.8 percent. Compensation for workers grew by 17.5 percent. The gap between those two numbers — approximately 44 percentage points of economic output — represents value that workers created and did not receive. This is not a contested claim. It appears in the Economic Policy Institute's data, the Federal Reserve's research, and the CBO's long-term budget outlook.

The economy asked workers to participate in producing that surplus. Workers delivered. The return did not follow. Something went wrong with the contract.

**Second: the unpaid care economy.** There are approximately 53 million unpaid caregivers in America today. They care for aging parents, disabled children, chronically ill spouses, family members in recovery. If that care were not provided informally — if the people providing it decided that they would not do it for free anymore — the formal healthcare system would need to absorb it at an estimated cost of $470 billion per year.

The economy relies on this labor. It prices its services as if this labor will always be provided. The caregivers themselves receive no wages, limited Social Security credit, and frequently sacrifice their own career earnings to perform work that the economy treats as invisible.

The contract is collecting from 53 million people and delivering nothing back.

**Third: the collective risk.** When a pandemic emerges in a distant city, when a semiconductor factory in Taiwan burns down, when a cyberattack disables a power grid, citizens bear consequences they did not cause, could not have individually prevented, and cannot individually insure against. The expected annual cost of supply chain disruption to the American economy is estimated at $56-190 billion — risk that every citizen absorbs in the form of higher prices, lost jobs, and reduced economic opportunity.

The contract asked citizens to accept a fully integrated national economy in which any disruption anywhere cascades everywhere. It asked them to accept global supply chains, domestic deindustrialization, and labor market exposure to forces operating on the other side of the planet. It did not provide corresponding protection.

## The Four Floors

A contract that extracts and does not return is not a contract. It is a one-sided arrangement that will eventually collapse.

The return, expressed as a set of economic floors that follow from each obligation:

### The Income Floor

Milton Friedman proposed it in 1962. The Negative Income Tax: a simple mechanism in which the tax code — instead of taxing income above a threshold — would support income below a threshold. You file your taxes. If your income falls below the floor, the IRS sends you money rather than the other way around. No caseworker. No means test. No application for multiple overlapping programs with incompatible eligibility rules. Just arithmetic.

Friedman was not a progressive. He was the most influential conservative economist of the twentieth century. He proposed the income floor precisely because he thought the existing welfare bureaucracy was inefficient, paternalistic, and distorting. A cash floor, he argued, gives people the dignity of making their own economic decisions. It is more efficient than the alternative. It is more respectful of individual agency.

The Earned Income Tax Credit — which 23 million American households receive every year, which nearly every Republican and Democratic Congress has voted to expand, which nobody campaigns against — is a partial implementation of exactly this idea. The income floor is not a radical concept. It is already here. It just has an eligibility cliff, an application process, and a phase-out that creates precisely the work disincentives Friedman was trying to avoid.

The full income floor removes the cliff. If you are working part-time, you are not suddenly ineligible. If your income rises, the floor phases out gradually at 30 cents per dollar of additional earnings. You always have an incentive to earn more. You never fall off a cliff. The floor is there precisely because the productivity gap between what workers produce and what they receive is real, documented, and not going away.

This is not charity. It is the return on fifty years of productivity growth that went elsewhere.

---

### The Opportunity Floor

The Workforce Development Obligation is a simple employer requirement: companies above a minimum size must provide one paid experience slot — internship, apprenticeship, rotational program, any structure that produces real work — per fifty employees per year.

Germany has operated this model, in various forms, since the Weimar Republic. German manufacturers have the most productive blue-collar workforce in the world. German youth unemployment is the lowest in Europe. German companies do not face the skills gaps that American companies describe in every labor market survey, because they have been building the pipeline for a hundred years rather than outsourcing that function to someone else.

The opportunity floor is not a government jobs program. The federal government pays nothing — employers pay wages. It is not a regulation of what employers must produce — they can design the experience however they like. It is the economy acknowledging that the pipeline from school to career is a shared infrastructure that shared investment must maintain.

The economy has been free-riding on public education for decades while simultaneously arguing that public education produces graduates who cannot do the work. The Workforce Development Obligation is the contract response: if you benefit from the labor force, you contribute to building it.

---

### The Health Floor

The Civilian Reserve is modeled on military reserve service. You train. You remain available for deployment to national projects. In exchange, you receive a paycheck and health coverage through the Federal Employees Health Benefits program — the same coverage that every member of Congress, every federal judge, and every Cabinet secretary receives.

It is not welfare. It requires active enrollment. It requires training and availability. It is the explicit application of the military analogy to civilian life: if the country can ask soldiers to make themselves available and provide full benefits in return, the same logic applies to civilians whose labor is needed for infrastructure, emergency preparedness, and supply chain maintenance.

The health floor is not universal healthcare in the conventional sense. It is earned healthcare — coverage that follows from contribution, exactly as military healthcare does. The political durability of veterans' healthcare benefits is instructive: they have survived fifty years of budget debates because they are universally understood as earned, not given.

No one calls VA healthcare welfare. The Civilian Reserve makes that same logic available to civilians.

### The Wealth Floor

Alaska has been paying every resident a share of the state's oil revenue since 1982.

The Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend is not controversial in Alaska. It has been paid every year for forty-three years. Republicans support it. Democrats support it. The Palin administration raised it. The Dunleavy administration protected it. The dividend is currently approximately $1,300 per resident per year. It is the state's own sovereign wealth distribution — a portion of revenue from commonly owned natural resources returned to the common owners.

The American Sovereign Fund applies the same logic to national assets: a portion of revenue from arms sales (national security is a national asset), tariff collections (market access is a national asset), and financial transactions (the dollar's reserve currency status is a national asset) flows into a national investment fund. The fund invests commercially. The returns, compounding for eighteen years after enrollment, begin paying dividends.

At Year 30, the projected dividend is $720+ per enrolled American per year. At Year 50, it exceeds $2,000. It grows every year because the corpus grows every year.

The wealth floor is the return on citizenship itself — the acknowledgment that national assets belong to all citizens and that citizens are entitled to a share of the returns those assets generate. You have been a citizen. The national economy has been generating returns on assets that belong to all of you. The fund pays you your portion.

## Why This Is Neither Left Nor Right

The conservative case for economic floors is not difficult to make. It requires only taking conservative principles seriously.

Conservatives believe that contribution should be rewarded. The income floor rewards workers who have contributed to a productivity surge that did not return to them. Conservatives believe in efficient government. The Negative Income Tax is more efficient than the patchwork of means-tested programs it replaces — this was Friedman's argument, not a progressive one. Conservatives believe in personal responsibility. A cash floor that phases out gradually at 30 cents per dollar of additional earnings never punishes you for working more — it is the opposite of the dependency trap that conservatives correctly criticize in the existing system.

Conservatives believe in national security. The Civilian Reserve is modeled on military reserve service and requires active participation. Conservatives believe that what you earn is yours. The American Sovereign Fund pays a dividend on your share of national assets — no one is redistributing your neighbor's income to you; the fund invests commercially and pays returns.

The progressive case is equally straightforward. Economic floors address structural inequality without requiring continuous political coalitions. They do not depend on annual appropriations or the goodwill of future Congresses. They are embedded in law in ways that create constituencies large enough to be politically permanent. The care economy credit finally counts what caregivers — disproportionately women, disproportionately working-class — have always contributed.

The consensus case is simpler than either. Both traditions believe that contribution should be rewarded. They disagree about what counts as contribution and what form the reward should take. The economic citizenship framework makes this concrete: here is what the economy asks of you; here is what it owes in return. Contribution is defined broadly enough to include caregiving, military service, labor market participation, and civic membership. The reward is proportional and accumulated over time.

## The Graduated Model

One feature of the American Sovereign Fund distinguishes it from every existing welfare program and most existing social programs: it accumulates.

The longer you are enrolled, the larger your stake. A citizen enrolled from birth — an American child — has an eighteen-year head start on the dividend clock by the time they reach adulthood. A naturalized citizen who became an American at forty will receive a smaller dividend than someone enrolled from birth, but a dividend nonetheless, growing every year.

This is pension logic, not welfare logic. You earn your claim over time. The stake is yours to accumulate or to defer. You cannot transfer it to someone else. You cannot cash it out ahead of schedule. But it is yours, in the same way that a pension accrual is yours — earned through years of enrollment in the national economic community.

No American pension disappears if you leave a job. No American Social Security claim disappears if you become unemployed. The national wealth stake operates the same way: once you have enrolled, it accrues. The years count even if your income fluctuates. The floor holds.

---

## The Close

None of this is new. The elements have been present in American economic history for a hundred years.

The income floor is the EITC without the cliff, and Milton Friedman's 1962 design without the political timidity of its congressional implementations. The opportunity floor is the German apprenticeship model, which American manufacturers have been envying for three decades without successfully replicating. The health floor is the Veterans Administration model, extended through a civilian reserve that makes the military analogy explicit. The wealth floor is the Alaska Permanent Fund, applied at national scale.

What is new is the framing. Not left, not right — contractarian. The economy asks something specific of citizens. Citizens have been delivering. The contract is overdue for the other side of the ledger to be settled.

The GI Bill worked because it was not framed as welfare. It was framed as the return on a debt already owed. The men and women who received it did not feel diminished by it. They felt that the country had kept its word.

That is the frame. The country has been asking. Citizens have been delivering. It is time for the country to keep its word.
