# The Contract We Forgot We Signed

*Citizenship has always come with economic obligations — on both sides*

## Central Argument

Citizenship has never been purely a legal status. It has always been a contract —
obligations running in both directions between the individual and the collective. The
modern American social contract has been honest about what citizens owe (taxes, jury
duty, military service when called) and increasingly vague about what the economy owes
citizens in return. This article makes that second half of the contract explicit: four
specific economic floors — income, opportunity, health, and wealth — that follow from
the obligations citizens already fulfill, expressed as a non-ideological contractarian
argument that neither political party can own or dismiss.

---

## Key Evidence and Numbers to Build From

- The GI Bill analogy: soldiers were asked to risk their lives; the nation provided
  education, housing loans, and healthcare. No one called this welfare. It was the
  return side of an obligation already fulfilled.
- 53 million caregivers providing $470B in annual economic value — the clearest
  example of citizens fulfilling an obligation the economy has not acknowledged
- The Earned Income Tax Credit: already a de facto partial implementation of the
  income floor, reaching 23 million households and largely uncontroversial
- Milton Friedman's Negative Income Tax (1962): the conservative origin of the idea;
  provides the right-wing intellectual credibility that allows the article to claim
  non-ideological ground
- The Alaska Permanent Fund: a wealth floor in practice for forty years, defended
  by conservatives and progressives alike — the proof that universal wealth return
  is not politically categorizable

## Tone Notes

This is the most philosophical article in the series. It should be written with
intellectual confidence but without condescension. The reader should feel that they
are being offered a framework that clarifies something they already half-believed.
The GI Bill example is the emotional entry point: no American of any political
persuasion thinks it was wrong to give returning soldiers education and housing.
The article's job is to show that the same logic applies more broadly.
