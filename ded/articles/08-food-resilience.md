# The Next Famine Won't Look Like the Last One

*How drought, water politics, and protein technology are rewriting the global food equation*

**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

In the summer of 2012, a drought settled over the American Midwest. It was not the worst
drought in recorded history. It lasted one season. It produced $77 billion in economic
damage.

The drought of 2012 was a single bad year. The country absorbed it.

The question that deserves more attention is what happens during a multi-year drought —
two or three consecutive dry years across the Corn Belt, compounded by water table
depletion in the aquifers underneath it. The 2012 figure was $77 billion for one season.
A sustained multi-year event would not simply multiply that number. It would cascade:
into food price inflation, into municipal water rationing in cities that depend on the
same river systems, into geopolitical disruption as the world's largest grain exporter
reduces its exports to manage domestic supply. The damage estimate would be measured in
trillions, not billions.

There is currently no institutional infrastructure in the United States designed to
manage those consequences before they become catastrophic. There is a Federal Emergency
Management Agency for disasters. There is a Department of Agriculture for normal
conditions. There is nothing in between — no agency with a mandate to anticipate,
pre-position, and respond to the economic consequences of a major water and food
system disruption before it arrives.

This article is about what is coming and what is already available to address it.

---

## The Water Math

The Colorado River is the water supply for the American West.

Seven states depend on it: Colorado, Wyoming, Utah, New Mexico, Arizona, Nevada, and
California. Mexico depends on it. The river supplies drinking water for Los Angeles,
Phoenix, Las Vegas, Tucson, Denver, Salt Lake City, and Albuquerque — cities with a
combined population of roughly 40 million people. It irrigates 5.5 million acres of
agricultural land producing vegetables, fruits, and cotton that supply grocery stores
across the country year-round. It powers Hoover Dam and Glen Canyon Dam.

The river is overallocated by 20 to 30 percent relative to its current average flow.
This means that the legal agreements governing how much water each state and municipality
is entitled to withdraw were written based on flow measurements from the early twentieth
century — measurements taken during a period that subsequent climate research has
identified as anomalously wet. The river's "normal" level, over the past thousand years,
is lower than the level at which the law was written.

Lake Mead, the reservoir created by Hoover Dam and the largest reservoir in the United
States by volume, dropped to 27% of its capacity in 2022 — the lowest level since it
was filled in the 1930s. Lake Powell, upstream, reached similar lows. Both were on a
trajectory toward "dead pool" — the level at which water can no longer flow through the
dams' outlets — that would have ended hydroelectric generation and made water delivery
to downstream states impossible. Emergency cutbacks were negotiated; the crisis was
deferred, not resolved.

Beneath the Corn Belt, in Kansas and Nebraska and Oklahoma, lies the High Plains
Aquifer — the Ogallala — which irrigates approximately 30% of all U.S. groundwater-
irrigated cropland. The aquifer took thousands of years to accumulate. In the past
century of intensive agriculture, it has been depleted faster than it recharges. In
parts of Kansas, the water table has dropped by more than 150 feet. The depletion
is not reversible on any human timescale. When it is gone, the agriculture that depends
on it goes with it.

These are not future threats. They are current processes. The question is not whether
they will become a crisis but when — and whether the institutional response will be
ready when they do.

## What the Corn Belt Produces

The American Midwest is the most productive agricultural region in human history.

The Corn Belt — roughly Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, and parts of the surrounding
states — produces approximately 40% of global corn and 35% of global soybeans. The
Great Plains states produce the majority of U.S. wheat. The combined agricultural
output of the American interior feeds a significant fraction of the world's population,
both directly and through the feed grain that supports global livestock production.

A sustained disruption to Corn Belt agriculture — through multi-year drought, aquifer
depletion, or the combination of both — would be a global food security event, not just
an American one. Countries that import American grain have no comparable alternative
supplier. The substitution options are slower, more expensive, and geographically
concentrated in ways that introduce their own supply chain risks.

The economic consequences would extend far beyond food prices. Food price inflation is
a leading driver of political instability in developing countries. The 2010-2011 global
food price spike — partly driven by Russian drought and U.S. crop diversion to ethanol —
contributed to the economic conditions preceding the Arab Spring uprisings. A sustained
disruption to American grain production would produce global food price increases of a
different magnitude entirely.

## What the Department of Economic Defense Plans For

The drought scenario is not a one-time emergency to be improvised. It is a recurring
risk category with a predictable structure. The economic response follows three phases.

**Phase 1 — Economic instruments activated before the agricultural system collapses.**
The point of early intervention is that the same outcome — crop diversification, water
efficiency, agricultural adjustment — costs far less when done before the crisis than
during it. When drought conditions are detected by the National Economic Intelligence
Dashboard's agricultural monitoring system (the same system that tracks commodity
concentration risk), the Department of Economic Defense activates pre-built instruments:
irrigation efficiency tax credits that pay for the switch from flood to drip irrigation;
crop transition payments that cover the cost of moving from high-water-demand corn to
lower-demand sorghum or drought-resistant varieties; water futures contracts that give
farmers and municipalities price certainty during supply uncertainty. These instruments
are designed and ready. They activate on a trigger, not on emergency legislation.

**Phase 2 — Structural water efficiency investment.** If Phase 1 instruments are
insufficient — if the drought persists into a second or third year — Phase 2 activates:
mandatory drip irrigation conversion for large agricultural users, with federal financing
at below-market rates; expanded desalination contracting along coastal areas to reduce
freshwater demand from inland sources; crop transition support for permanent shifts to
drought-adapted agriculture in affected regions. The structural changes are more expensive
than Phase 1 but prevent the cascading failure that comes from absorbing a second or
third drought year without adaptation.

**Phase 3 — Long-run agricultural restructuring.** The programs that take decades to
mature cannot wait for a crisis to begin. Direct lithium extraction, drought-resistant
crop genetics, water-efficient protein sources, regional food system diversification:
these are started in Phase 3 of the drought scenario but designed to be running before
the scenario activates. The long horizon means the decision to invest must be made
before the urgency is undeniable.

## The Technology That Changes the Equation

Precision fermentation is not the technology you have seen on the news.

It is not lab-grown meat — the process of growing animal muscle tissue from stem cells.
That technology is real and advancing but not yet at commercial cost parity. Precision
fermentation is different: using microorganisms (yeast, bacteria, fungi) to produce
specific proteins through a fermentation process, the same basic technology used to
produce insulin, enzymes for cheese-making, and B-vitamins. The organisms are programmed
to produce the target protein; they ferment in bioreactor tanks; the protein is extracted
and processed.

The results are already commercially available. Perfect Day uses precision fermentation
to produce whey protein — the same protein found in cow's milk — without cows. The
product is FDA-approved, commercially available, and incorporated into products sold
at Whole Foods and other retailers. Several additional products have cleared FDA review.
The technology works. The question is cost.

In 2000, precision fermentation protein cost approximately $1 million per kilogram.
In 2026, it costs approximately $20 per kilogram. The trajectory — driven by bioreactor
efficiency gains, process optimization, and scale — points toward below $10 per kilogram
within a decade at commercial production volume.

For comparison: conventional beef protein costs approximately $15 to $25 per kilogram
at retail, and its production uses 99% more land and 82 to 96% more water per gram
of protein than precision fermentation. The technology is approaching cost parity with
the most resource-intensive conventional protein source while using a fraction of the
inputs.

The reason it is not already at scale is not technical feasibility. It is the first-mover
investment problem. Building the bioreactor infrastructure to reach commercial cost
parity requires billions of dollars of capital investment before the cost trajectory
is proven at scale. Private investors face a timing problem: the investment must be made
years before the return is certain. The incumbent food industry has every incentive to
prevent the transition. No single investor will fund the full scale-up, because the
benefits — cheaper protein for everyone, reduced dependence on water and land — flow
to the public as much as to the investor.

This is the standard pattern for technologies that require public goods infrastructure
to reach their social value. The internet required ARPANET. GPS required military
satellite investment. mRNA vaccine manufacturing required Operation Warp Speed.
Precision fermentation requires an anchor buyer.

## The Anchor Buyer Mechanism

The federal government buys approximately $4 billion per year in food — for military
installations, Bureau of Prisons facilities, Veterans Administration hospitals, federal
employee cafeterias, school lunch programs. This is not a trivial purchasing volume.
It is large enough to function as an anchor commitment.

The Synthetic Protein Program proposes a 10-year advance purchase commitment: the
Department of Defense and affiliated federal purchasers will buy a defined volume of
precision fermentation protein products at a price that covers production cost plus
a margin, for ten years, regardless of whether the price falls below the contracted
level in the interim (the producer keeps the upside; the government gets the supply
security).

This commitment does two things. First, it provides the revenue certainty that allows
private capital to fund the bioreactor build-out. A bankable federal purchase commitment
converts a speculative investment into a secured one. Second, it creates a domestic
protein supply that does not depend on weather in the Corn Belt, water from the
Colorado River, or the supply chain stability of any single agricultural region.

At scale — if precision fermentation protein reaches below $10 per kilogram and
commercial food manufacturers adopt it widely — the agriculture that currently uses
40% of all U.S. freshwater withdrawals for livestock production would face a competitive
alternative that requires essentially no land and a fraction of the water. The water
freed by reduced livestock production is not available tomorrow. It becomes available
over decades as the transition occurs. But it represents the largest available lever
for reducing water consumption in the American West — larger than any efficiency program
aimed at existing uses, because it reduces the total demand rather than improving the
efficiency of existing demand.

## Why This Is a National Security Issue

The Department of Economic Defense's mandate is economic security. Food is an economic
security issue in the clearest possible terms: the cost of grain is the cost of bread,
the cost of meat, and the cost of feeding the military that defends the country.

The Department of Defense spends approximately $4 billion per year on food. A drought
that doubles commodity grain prices — a plausible consequence of a multi-year Corn Belt
disruption — doubles that food budget. The money comes from the same defense
appropriation that funds readiness. Military food costs and military readiness are not
unrelated budget lines. They compete for the same dollars.

Beyond the defense budget, food price inflation is one of the most politically destabilizing
forces in modern history. A country whose domestic food costs rise 40% over two years
has a different politics than one where they rise 5%. The economic stress concentrates
in the households that spend the highest fraction of their income on food — which are
the households at the bottom of the income distribution, the same households that DED's
income floor programs are designed to support. A food price shock and an inadequate
income floor produce a compounding crisis; a food price shock and a functioning income
floor produce a manageable adjustment.

The systems interact. Investing in food system resilience reduces the cost of income
floor programs during a food crisis. Investing in precision fermentation reduces the
vulnerability of the food system to water constraints. Investing in water efficiency
extends the timeline before the constraints become binding. None of these investments
are expensive relative to the $77 billion that a single drought year produced in damage,
let alone the multi-trillion dollar disruption that a sustained event would generate.

The estimated cost of inaction on Colorado River management over 15 years is $50 to
$200 billion in reduced agricultural output, infrastructure damage, and municipal water
system adaptation costs. The Synthetic Protein Program costs $6 to $9 billion over
five years. The math is not complicated.

## What Is Not Being Proposed

This is not a proposal to replace conventional agriculture. American farmers grow food
efficiently and feed the world at a scale that no alternative currently matches.
The precision fermentation program is not designed to compete with agriculture — it is
designed to reduce agriculture's dependence on inputs that are becoming constrained,
freeing agricultural production to focus on what land and weather do well rather than
straining the entire system through the production of protein that bioreactors can produce
more efficiently.

This is not a proposal to ration water. It is a proposal to price water more accurately
through market mechanisms — futures contracts, efficiency credits, transition payments —
so that decisions about water use incorporate the actual cost of the resource rather
than treating it as free until the aquifer is gone.

This is not a claim that the drought scenario is inevitable. It is a recognition that
the probability is high enough, the cost of the event is large enough, and the cost
of preparation is low enough that not preparing is fiscally irrational. The same logic
that justifies fire insurance for a house that probably won't burn down justifies food
system resilience investment for a food system that faces genuinely elevated risk.
