## Central Argument

The global food system is built on three increasingly fragile pillars: water (the
Colorado River has declined 20% in flow since 2000), geography (the Corn Belt produces
40% of the world's corn and soybeans from a region entering a decades-long drying
trend), and protein sources (beef uses 99% more land and 90% more water per gram of
protein than precision fermentation alternatives that are already commercially viable).
A multi-year drought affecting the American Midwest is not a tail-risk scenario. It is
a probability that increases every decade. And there is currently no institutional
infrastructure for managing its economic consequences before they become catastrophic.
This article addresses both the near-term policy response and the long-horizon
technological transition that could permanently reduce food system fragility.

---

## Key Evidence and Numbers to Build From

- 2012 U.S. drought: $77 billion in economic damage from a single season.
  A multi-year, multi-region drought would be 5–10× worse.
- Colorado River basin: Lake Mead dropped to 27% capacity in 2022 (lowest since
  the reservoir was filled). Seven states and Mexico depend on it for municipal water,
  agriculture, and hydropower.
- The Corn Belt produces 40% of global corn, 35% of global soybeans. Groundwater
  depletion in the High Plains Aquifer (which underlies much of it) has accelerated
  significantly since 2000.
- Precision fermentation protein: current production cost $20/kg and falling toward
  <$10/kg. Uses 99% less land, 82–96% less water than conventional beef per gram
  of protein. Five products already FDA-approved.
- A 10-year contract for anchor purchasing of domestic precision fermentation output
  (Department of Defense, Bureau of Prisons, VA hospitals) could fund the scale-up
  that drives costs below conventional protein within 8–12 years.
- The estimated cost of inaction on Colorado River management over 15 years: $50–200B

## Tone Notes

This article should feel urgent without being alarmist. The water situation should
be described with precision — specific numbers, specific timelines — rather than
apocalyptic language. The synthetic protein section should lead with curiosity rather
than advocacy: "here is what is already happening commercially" before "here is what
the government should do." The reader should arrive at the proposal having already
concluded that the problem is real and the technology is viable.
