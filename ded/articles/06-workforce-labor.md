# What If the Economy Trained You

*The free-rider problem in workforce development — and the proposal to end it*

**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

Every few years, a survey comes out showing that American employers cannot find workers
with the skills they need. The surveys generate hand-wringing op-eds. Business groups
testify before Congress. Someone proposes another job training program. Nothing changes.

The skills gap has been the oldest complaint in American business history — and it has
never been resolved.

There is a reason for that. The complaint identifies the symptom without acknowledging
the cause.

The symptom: workers don't have the skills employers need. The cause: employers stopped
training them. Between 1996 and 2020, employer-provided training per worker declined by
approximately 25% in real terms, even as the skills required for most jobs grew more
specialized. The jobs became harder. The training disappeared. And then employers
discovered they couldn't find trained workers — and blamed the workers.

This is not a moral failing. It is a structural problem with a name: the free-rider problem.
Understanding it is the first step to fixing it. And fixing it requires more than another
training program. It requires changing the incentive that created the gap in the first place.

---

## Why Employers Don't Train

Here is the economics of workforce training, as experienced by a rational employer.

Training a worker costs money — in instructor time, in productivity lost during the
training period, in wages paid for work not yet performed at full proficiency. That cost
is real and immediate. The return on that investment is uncertain and delayed. But there
is a deeper problem than uncertainty.

If Employer A trains a worker and Employer B recruits that worker away at a slightly
higher salary, Employer A has paid the cost and Employer B has received the benefit.
The trained worker takes their skills with them. Employer A learned an expensive lesson;
Employer B received a free employee.

This is the free-rider problem in its cleanest form. Each employer knows that if they
train workers, some of those workers will be poached by competitors who did not pay for
the training. The rational response for each individual employer is to reduce training
and wait for someone else to do it. But if every employer follows this logic — which
they do, because the incentive is real — no employer trains anyone. The market reaches
an equilibrium where everyone underinvests in workforce development, the skills gap
widens, and everyone loses.

This is not a failure of individual employers. The incentive structure makes this outcome
nearly inevitable. You cannot blame individual companies for making rational decisions
under the rules of the game they're playing. The solution is to change the rules.

## What Other Countries Figured Out

Germany figured this out decades ago. Its dual apprenticeship system — *Berufsausbildung* —
is the reason German manufacturing still dominates global markets in precision machinery,
automotive, and chemicals despite paying wages that would be considered high by American
standards.

Here is how it works. More than 400,000 German companies participate in the apprenticeship
system, covering approximately 330 recognized occupational categories. Young workers split
their time between classroom education (provided by vocational schools, publicly funded)
and structured workplace training (provided by employer-partners, primarily employer-funded).
An apprenticeship lasts two to three and a half years. At the end, the worker has a
nationally recognized credential and demonstrated competency. The employer has a trained
worker who already knows the specific practices of their industry.

Approximately 1.3 million Germans enter apprenticeships each year. For perspective: the
United States, with a population four times the size of Germany's, produces approximately
600,000 apprenticeship starts per year — about half as many in absolute terms, and about
one-eighth as many per capita.

The German system works not because German employers are more generous than American ones,
but because the system changes the incentive structure. Training costs are partially shared
through the vocational school system. Credentials are portable across employers in the
same sector, which reduces the poaching premium — a trained machinist at Company A can
move to Company B, but Company B doesn't gain a unique competitive advantage, because
every machinist in the country trained to the same standard. The collective cost and
collective benefit are roughly aligned. Individual defection — refusing to train while
your competitors train — becomes less rational because the system provides trained workers
to everyone, not just to those who invest in them.

Singapore took a different approach. Its SkillsFuture program provides every adult citizen
a skills development credit, usable for approved training courses throughout their working
life. The government doesn't tell workers what skills to develop. It gives them the money
and the marketplace to make that decision themselves. Take-up among workers aged 40 to 60
is above 90% — the cohort that conventional labor market theory would predict to be least
likely to retrain, because they have the least working time remaining over which to recover
the investment. They retrain anyway, because the credit makes training affordable and the
marketplace makes it relevant.

The United Kingdom's apprenticeship levy is a more direct mechanism: employers with an
annual payroll above £3 million pay a 0.5% levy, deposited into a digital training account
that can only be spent on apprenticeships. Companies that run apprenticeship programs draw
down their account. Companies that don't lose the money to a general fund supporting
smaller employers. The incentive is blunt: either train someone or pay for someone else's
training. Non-participation has a cost.

The common thread is that none of these systems rely on the voluntary goodwill of
individual employers. They change the underlying game. They either share the cost
collectively, provide a floor of public investment, or impose a levy that makes
non-participation carry a price. The market failure is corrected not by appealing to
employers' better nature, but by restructuring the incentives until training makes sense.

The United States has not done this. It has mostly appealed to better nature.

## The Catch-22 at the Bottom

Before getting to what the proposed system looks like, it is worth pausing on who
suffers most from the current failure.

The free-rider problem in training hits every part of the labor market. But it hits
hardest at the bottom: the workers who have no experience yet and need a first job
to get experience.

The entry-level job is not merely a paycheck. It is the mechanism through which young
workers acquire the professional credentials, references, networks, and demonstrated
competencies that determine their trajectory across an entire working life. The first
job is the first rung of the mobility ladder. And it is disappearing.

Three forces are converging to remove it. AI is eliminating the routine cognitive tasks
that previously occupied entry-level positions — data entry, basic analysis, customer
correspondence, document review. Employers have responded not by replacing those workers
with different entry-level workers, but by eliminating the entry-level positions entirely.
The credential spiral has accelerated: employers now require degrees for work that
previously required a diploma, and experience for jobs that previously developed it.
And where paid entry-level positions have disappeared, they have been partially replaced
by unpaid internships accessible only to students who can afford to work for free —
disproportionately from high-income families in high-cost cities.

The National Association of Colleges and Employers has documented that paid internship
experience translates directly to higher starting salaries and employment rates. Unpaid
experience shows minimal wage benefit. The unpaid internship system does not develop
workers. It selects for financial privilege and reproduces inequality.

The result is a catch-22 that operates with remarkable precision: you cannot get the
entry-level job without experience, and you cannot get experience without the entry-level
job. This catch-22 is not random in its effects. It falls hardest on young workers from
families without professional networks — the families that cannot call a family friend to
arrange an internship, cannot float their child through six months of unpaid work in an
expensive city, cannot cushion a false start. Workers from those families hit the catch-22
with nothing to absorb the impact.

This is worth saying plainly: the current system allocates first-rung access by family
wealth and network. It does not do this explicitly or by design. It does it structurally,
through the combination of eliminated paid positions, unpaid alternatives, and credential
requirements that serve as proxies for class. The result is an economy that wastes the
productive potential of the majority of its workforce before those workers get a chance
to demonstrate it.

That is both a moral failure and an economic security failure. An economy that reserves
its on-ramps for workers with the right family background is an economy that systematically
underperforms its potential.

## The Historical Baseline

We know what large-scale workforce investment produces, because we have done it before.

The Civilian Conservation Corps, launched in 1933, enrolled 3 million young men over
nine years — roughly one-third of all men aged 17 to 24 in the United States at the
time. Participants received housing, food, vocational training, literacy education, and
a $30 monthly wage (approximately $650 in today's dollars), of which $25 was sent
directly to their families. The work product included 800 state parks, 13,000 miles of
trails, 125,000 miles of roads, and 3 billion trees planted. A 2024 study in the
*Quarterly Journal of Economics* using newly linked administrative records found that
program participants experienced significantly higher lifetime earnings compared to
comparable non-participants — not just during the program, but across their entire
working lives.

The National Youth Administration enrolled 4.5 million young people in part-time
paid work while they remained in school, providing skills training and work experience
that kept youth attached to both education and the labor market simultaneously.

The GI Bill funded the education and training of 8 million veterans after World War II.
Congressional analysis estimated a 7:1 return to the national economy — for every dollar
spent, seven came back in the form of increased productivity, taxes, and economic growth.
The program produced 450,000 engineers, 238,000 teachers, 91,000 scientists, and 67,000
doctors — the professional workforce that built the American middle class and sustained
U.S. economic dominance for three decades.

The lesson is not subtle. Large-scale investment in workforce development produces
measurable, long-term returns. The question is not whether it works. The question is
what structure makes it work in the current economy — with current employers, current
technologies, and a labor market that looks nothing like 1944.

## The Proposed Structure: Two Floors and a Parallel Path

The proposal has three components. They address different parts of the same problem.

### Floor One: The Employer Development Obligation

Every private employer with more than 250 employees must provide paid training
experiences equivalent to 1% of their workforce per year. These are not internships
specifically — they are any structured developmental position with a defined learning
objective, a designated supervisor or mentor, and compensation at or above a defined
minimum. The form is flexible. The floor is mandatory.

At scale, this produces approximately 1 million paid training experiences per year —
funded by employers, at employer-determined wages averaging $7,000 to $8,500 per
position in direct employer cost. The federal cost is enforcement infrastructure and
compliance monitoring.

The return on this investment is estimated at 35 to 40:1 in GDP productivity gains
over a 10-year horizon — workers who receive structured early training earn more,
pay more in taxes, and are less likely to draw on public support systems across their
working lives. The program does not cost the federal government money at the scale the
benefit represents. It costs employers money to correct an externality they have been
passing to the public.

The Employer Development Obligation targets a specific inefficiency: large employers
benefit most from an educated, trained workforce and also benefit most from not paying
for it. They hire workers trained by public schools, public universities, and public
workforce programs. They retain productivity gains when that training pays off. They
externalize the cost of the training that made their workforce valuable. The 1%
obligation reprices that externality.

### Floor Two: The Mandatory Internship Floor

The Employer Development Obligation reaches employed workers and young workers entering
the formal economy through larger employers. But it does not reach the hardest part of
the problem: workers with no current employer relationship and no network pathway into one.

The Mandatory Internship Floor addresses this directly. Any private employer with 100 or
more full-time-equivalent employees must maintain at minimum one qualifying paid internship
position for every 100 employees. The positions must pay at least $17 per hour (indexed
annually), last at least 10 weeks at 20 or more hours per week, include a defined learning
objective and a designated supervisor, and be open to workers with no prior relationship
to the company.

At the 100-employee threshold, this produces approximately 390,000 net new paid entry-level
positions per year — positions that currently do not exist, or exist only in unpaid form.
The compliance incentive is calibrated precisely: the non-compliance penalty is $6,000 per
unfilled required slot per year. At 100 employees, a company owes 1 slot. Non-compliance
costs $6,000. Two qualifying internship positions cost approximately $6,800 to $8,500 in
direct employer wages. Compliance is marginally cheaper than non-compliance — and compliance
develops a worker.

Companies that cannot absorb interns directly can instead contribute to an industry-managed
intern pool, which places workers at smaller or more specialized employers in the same sector.
The floor is enforced through the existing EEO-1 filing infrastructure — no new reporting
bureaucracy required.

The Mandatory Internship Floor specifically targets the catch-22. It does not require
employers to lower their standards or hire unqualified workers. It requires them to create
positions through which workers can become qualified — positions that are currently
disappearing faster than market forces create them.

### The Parallel Path: Civilian Reserve as Career Infrastructure

Both floors operate through employer relationships. They reach workers who are either
currently employed or close enough to the formal labor market to access a qualifying
position. But there is a population for whom neither floor is accessible: workers who
have been out of the labor market long enough, or were never in it, or are in regions
or sectors where large employers are scarce, or face barriers that employment alone
cannot resolve.

For those workers, the Civilian Reserve and Health Program offers a parallel pathway.

Enrollment in the Civilian Reserve provides a non-combat reserve designation, full
healthcare coverage equivalent to military TRICARE, a training obligation of approximately
13 days per year, and eligibility for emergency mobilization during national emergencies.
In exchange, the government provides the healthcare coverage, compensation for training
days, and — critically — a structured training pipeline that functions as workforce
development for workers the market has excluded.

The Civilian Reserve training program is not designed to produce generic workers.
It produces workers with specific skills tied to identified national needs: infrastructure
construction and maintenance, emergency medical response, cybersecurity operations,
supply chain logistics, agricultural production. These are not skills that employers
are currently training workers to develop in sufficient numbers. They are skills that
every scenario in the DED portfolio identifies as bottlenecks — the places where a crisis
reveals that the labor supply is inadequate to the task of response.

The Civilian Reserve is not a jobs program for workers who couldn't find jobs elsewhere.
It is a strategic labor reserve for a country that has chronically underinvested in the
workforce capacity it will need when things go wrong. The fact that it also solves a
workforce development problem for the workers who enroll is not a side effect. It is
the design.

## The Return on Investment

The GI Bill returned $7 for every $1 invested. The Civilian Conservation Corps produced
measurably higher lifetime earnings for every participant. Germany's dual apprenticeship
system supports the highest-wage manufacturing workforce in the world. These are not
coincidences or accidents of history. Structured investment in workforce development
produces returns — in taxes, in productivity, in reduced dependence on public support
systems, in the political stability that accompanies a perception of open economic
mobility.

The Workforce Development Floor, Mandatory Internship Floor, and Civilian Reserve
together represent a coordinated attack on the free-rider problem that has made
large-scale employer training irrational for individual firms. They do not eliminate
the free-rider problem by making employers altruistic. They eliminate it by changing
the rules so that non-participation carries a cost, so that every worker who enters
the labor market has a viable pathway, and so that the workers the market has excluded
have access to a parallel system that develops them toward national needs.

The skills gap is not a mystery. The solution is not another voluntary program that
employers participate in when it suits them and exit when it doesn't. It is a floor.
A mandatory minimum. The recognition that workforce development is a public good —
like roads, like schools, like basic research — that markets reliably underprovide
when left to their own devices.

Every employer who has ever complained about not being able to find trained workers
has been describing the outcome of a system they collectively created by refusing to
train the workers. The proposal does not ask them to feel differently about that. It
asks them to behave differently — and it gives them a structure in which behaving
differently is rational.

## What This Requires

The Employer Development Obligation requires legislation — specifically, a federal
mandate attached to existing EEO-1 reporting infrastructure, enforceable through the
Department of Labor's Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs. For federal
contractors, executive action is sufficient for Phase 1 implementation: conditioning
federal contracts above a defined threshold on employer participation. Phase 1 reaches
approximately 40% of large private employers immediately.

The Mandatory Internship Floor operates through the same mechanism. EEO-1 filers
already report workforce composition. An annual supplement adds internship
reporting. Enforcement uses existing DOL penalty structures. No new agency required.

The Civilian Reserve requires DoD and DED coordination. It is the most complex of the
three, but also the most studied: the military reserve system already provides the
administrative model. The 13-day annual training obligation, the TRICARE coverage
mechanism, the emergency mobilization framework — all of these have operational
precedents in the existing National Guard and Reserve system. The Civilian Reserve
extends those precedents to non-combat roles.

The total federal cost is modest. The employer obligations fund themselves. The
Civilian Reserve costs are partially offset by the training days (compensated at
military pay rates already appropriated in defense budgets) and the healthcare
enrollment fees ($600/year per enrollee, reduced for low-income households).

The employers who will resist this proposal are the same employers who have been
complaining about the skills gap for thirty years. They will argue that mandatory
training requirements are too expensive, that government mandates are too inflexible,
that the market will solve this on its own. The market has had thirty years to solve
this on its own. The skills gap is wider than it was.

The time for voluntary programs has passed.
