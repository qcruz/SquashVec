# What If the Economy Trained You

*The free-rider problem in workforce development — and the proposal to end it*

## Central Argument

Every employer who hires a trained worker is benefiting from twelve or more years of
public investment — in education, public health, transportation infrastructure, and
the social stability that makes workforce participation possible. Employers pay wages for
the worker's time. They do not pay for the investment that made the worker employable.
This is not a moral criticism. It is a description of a coordination failure: no individual
employer can profitably train a workforce it does not yet employ. The result is chronic
underinvestment in workforce development, skills gaps that persist for decades, and a
generation of young people entering an economy with no structured on-ramp. This article
proposes the institutional solution.

---

## Key Evidence and Numbers to Build From

- The United States spends approximately $28 billion per year on federal workforce
  development programs. Germany's apprenticeship system — serving a country one-quarter
  the size — produces 1.3 million apprentices per year with employer participation rates
  above 20%. The United States produces approximately 600,000 apprenticeship starts per year.
- Employer-provided training per worker has declined 25% since the 1990s, even as the
  skills required for most jobs have become more specialized (Bureau of Labor Statistics)
- Singapore's SkillsFuture program: lifelong training credits available to every adult;
  90%+ take-up among workers aged 40–60; measurable GDP contribution through productivity growth
- The Workforce Development Floor's Employer Development Obligation: requires large
  employers to provide paid training experiences equivalent to 1% of their workforce
  per year. At scale: approximately 1 million training experiences per year,
  generating $7–8.5 billion in employer-funded wages
- The Mandatory Internship Floor: 390,000 net new paid entry-level positions per year
  for young workers who currently have no structured pathway into the formal economy
- Estimated return on investment of the Workforce Development Floor: 35–40:1
  (GDP productivity gains from trained workforce vs. program cost)

### The Free-Rider Structure

*TODO: Why individual employers cannot solve this collectively even when they want to.
If Employer A trains a worker and Employer B poaches that worker, Employer A has funded
Employer B's workforce development. The rational response is for every employer to stop
training and wait for others to do it. Every employer does this. No one trains anyone.
This is a classic collective action failure — individually rational, collectively
catastrophic. It requires a mandatory solution.*

## Tone Notes

This article should feel like the reader is being shown a mechanism that was broken
all along, not a new problem. The employer complaints about skills gaps should appear
early — because readers will recognize them — and then be reframed as a collective
action failure rather than a worker failure. The international comparisons should not
be triumphalist ("Europe does it better") but analytical ("here is why those systems
work and ours doesn't"). The internship floor section should carry some moral weight:
the catch-22 of "no experience, no job" falls hardest on young people from families
without professional networks.
