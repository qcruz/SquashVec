## Central Argument

When China restricted rare earth exports to Japan in 2010, prices for some materials
spiked 100-fold in fourteen months. When COVID-19 hit in 2020, N95 mask prices jumped
30-fold in weeks. When Russia cut gas to Europe in 2022, energy prices rose 20 times.
In every case, the market had priced the risk of disruption at approximately zero —
for years before the disruption occurred. This is not bad luck. It is a structural
market failure: geopolitical supply risk cannot be priced by individual buyers competing
in spot markets. It requires an institution that holds a portfolio view of national
exposure and pre-positions before the crisis. This article explains what that exposure
actually costs — measured in expected annual loss, not crisis hindsight — and what
it would take to insure against it.

## Key Evidence and Numbers to Build From

- China controls 85% of rare earth processing. The Concentration Risk Score for rare
  earths: 0.60 — the highest in the critical materials inventory
- 2010 rare earth embargo: cerium oxide went from $1.50/kg to $157/kg (103× increase)
  in 14 months. Markets had embedded zero disruption risk in 2009 prices.
- 2021 semiconductor shortage: 10–15% supply imbalance caused $500B in global economic
  damage. The shortage was caused by demand fluctuation alone — no adversarial action.
- 2020 N95 crisis: 12 million masks in the Strategic National Stockpile against a
  300 million mask healthcare worker requirement. Price spiked 30× in weeks.
- Total unpriced supply chain concentration risk in U.S. critical material imports:
  estimated $56–190 billion per year in Expected Annual Loss — the actuarially correct
  cost of the risk the market prices at zero
- The insurance cost ratio: pre-positioning stockpiles and alternative capacity for
  rare earths costs approximately $1.3B/year against $12–30B/year in expected loss
  prevented — 4 to 11 cents per dollar of coverage

## Tone Notes

This article should feel like a reveal — the reader should finish it feeling like they
have just been shown a risk they were carrying without knowing it. The four historical
case studies are the emotional core: each one is a moment where the market said "this
cannot happen" and then it happened. The surprise is not that the disruptions occurred
but that the prices before the disruptions embedded zero probability of them occurring.
That is the scandal. The Department of Economic Defense is the institutional response
to it.
