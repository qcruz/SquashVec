# The Price of Everything, the Value of Nothing

*How America prices its most dangerous dependencies at zero — and what that costs*

---

In September 2010, China reduced its rare earth export quota to Japan by approximately 40 percent. No formal announcement. No declared embargo. Shipments simply slowed, then stopped at Japanese ports. The two countries were in a territorial dispute over the Senkaku Islands, and Beijing had decided to make a point about who controlled the materials that made Japanese electronics possible.

Before that decision, cerium oxide — a rare earth compound used in glass polishing, semiconductor manufacturing, and catalytic converters — traded at roughly $1.50 per kilogram. Fourteen months later, it traded at $157 per kilogram. A 103-fold increase.

Here is the part that deserves attention: in 2009, the price of cerium oxide embedded essentially zero probability that this would ever happen. Not low probability. Not "unlikely but possible." Zero. The price was set by individual buyers in a spot market, each making rational purchasing decisions, and none of them was pricing in the risk that the single country controlling 97 percent of global rare earth processing might one day use that control as a weapon.

They were not being irrational. They were being individually rational in a market that cannot, by its structure, price collective geopolitical risk.

That is the problem this article is about.

---

## What Markets Cannot Price

Spot markets are very good at setting prices when risk is private, independent, and short-term. If I am one of a hundred buyers for a commodity, and the risk that my specific supplier fails is idiosyncratic — not correlated with every other buyer's supplier — then competitive market pricing will eventually reflect that risk. Insurance markets exist for exactly this: actuarially independent risks that diversify across a large pool.

Geopolitical supply risk is none of those things.

It is not private — when China restricts rare earth exports, every buyer in every importing country faces the same disruption simultaneously. There is no portfolio diversification. There is no risk-pooling. When the event occurs, it occurs for everyone at once.

It is not independent — the risk comes from a single actor whose choices affect every supply chain that depends on that actor. The 2022 Russian gas crisis did not affect only one European utility. It affected every European economy simultaneously, because they had all optimized for the same supplier.

It is not short-term — the solution to geopolitical supply concentration requires building alternative supply chains, processing capacity, or strategic reserves. These take ten to twenty years to develop. No individual corporate planning cycle extends that far, and no corporate treasury will pay for a twenty-year option that benefits the entire industry rather than the company bearing the cost.

This is a public goods problem in the technical economic sense. The benefit of pre-positioning — of having domestic processing capacity or a strategic stockpile — is non-excludable (if a rare earth shortage is avoided, every manufacturer benefits, not just the ones who paid) and non-rival (one manufacturer using the strategic reserve doesn't prevent another from using it). Private markets systematically underproduce public goods. That is why we have governments.

The market prices geopolitical supply risk at zero not because it is zero but because the market cannot price it at all. The mechanism does not exist. The incentive does not exist. The planning horizon does not exist. Individual rationality produces collective vulnerability, and collective vulnerability is repriced, catastrophically, when the event occurs.

## Four Moments the Market Said It Couldn't Happen

### The Rare Earth Embargo (2010)

China controls approximately 85 percent of global rare earth processing. This is not primarily a geological fact — rare earth deposits exist on every continent — but a consequence of thirty years of deliberate industrial policy. China invested heavily in rare earth processing capacity beginning in the 1980s when prices were low and Western producers could not compete. American rare earth mines closed. Processing facilities shuttered. By 2010, there was essentially no non-Chinese rare earth processing capacity capable of serving global demand at scale.

The market knew this. It was publicly documented. Congressional testimony in the early 2000s described the dependency in precise terms. Defense Department assessments flagged rare earths as a strategic vulnerability. None of this moved the price of cerium oxide by a dollar.

Then the September 2010 quota cut happened. Prices moved 103-fold in fourteen months.

The lesson is not that China behaved unexpectedly. The lesson is that the market embedded zero probability of expected behavior from a state that had demonstrated, repeatedly, its willingness to use economic instruments as geopolitical levers. The 2010 disruption was predictable. The price before it was not.

### The Semiconductor Shortage (2021)

In early 2020, automotive manufacturers looked at collapsing consumer demand, concluded that car sales were going to fall sharply during the pandemic, and cut their semiconductor orders. It was a reasonable forecast. It was wrong.

Consumer demand for automobiles rebounded sharply in late 2020. The chip foundries that had been producing automotive semiconductors had reallocated their capacity to consumer electronics — laptops, tablets, gaming consoles — products that people stuck at home were buying in record quantities. By the time automakers tried to reorder chips, there were none available. Not for months. Eventually not for over a year.

A 10 to 15 percent supply imbalance — that is all it was — caused an estimated $500 billion in global economic damage. Automotive plants idled. New vehicle inventory fell to record lows. Used car prices increased 40 percent in a single year, contributing measurably to overall inflation. The military procurement implications were real: defense systems that depend on the same commercial chip architectures faced delivery delays.

And critically: none of this was caused by adversarial action. China did not restrict semiconductor exports. Taiwan did not suffer a natural disaster. TSMC did not fail. The disruption was caused by ordinary demand forecasting error under uncertainty — the kind of error that happens in every industry in every recession. In an industry with two years of lead time and near-zero surge capacity, ordinary demand error produced extraordinary economic damage.

The market had priced in zero risk of a semiconductor shortage severe enough to idle automotive plants for twelve consecutive months. The shortage happened anyway.

### The N95 Crisis (2020)

In January 2020, the United States Strategic National Stockpile contained approximately 12 million N95 respirator masks. The estimated requirement for healthcare workers during a severe respiratory pandemic — a requirement that had been modeled, published, and submitted to Congress in pandemic preparedness reports since the 2009 H1N1 outbreak — was approximately 300 million masks.

The gap was known. It had been known for a decade. The 2009 pandemic preparedness review recommended rebuilding the stockpile. The Obama administration requested funding. The funding was partially appropriated and partially spent on other priorities. The gap remained.

When COVID-19 arrived in force in March 2020, the United States had 4 percent of the masks it needed. The domestic manufacturing capacity to produce N95 masks — the specialized equipment, the trained workforce, the supply chains for the raw materials — had been offshored over the preceding two decades to Chinese manufacturers who could produce them at lower cost.

In the weeks following the declaration of a national emergency, mask prices increased thirty-fold. State governments bid against each other for inadequate supply. Federal agencies outbid states. Healthcare workers reused masks designed for single use. Some improvised.

The market had optimized perfectly. It had offshored production to the lowest-cost supplier and eliminated domestic capacity that could not compete. It had produced the maximum efficiency outcome in a world without disruptions. In a world with disruptions — in the world we actually live in — it had produced 4 percent of what was needed.

### The Russian Gas Crisis (2022)

For twenty-five years, Europe optimized its energy supply for the cheapest available source. Russian natural gas, delivered via Nord Stream and a network of pipelines crossing Ukraine, was cheap, reliable, and abundant. European utilities signed long-term contracts. Domestic gas fields were allowed to deplete without replacement investment. Liquefied natural gas import terminals were not built because Russian pipeline gas was always cheaper.

The market was rational. Given the prices, given the contracts, given the apparent reliability, the optimal decision was clear. No individual European utility could justify paying more for Norwegian gas or building an LNG terminal on the chance that Russian gas might one day be unavailable. The premium was too high, the probability appeared too low, and the benefit — avoiding a disruption — would accrue to every European economy regardless of who paid for it.

In the twelve months following Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine and the subsequent curtailment of gas flows, European natural gas prices rose approximately twenty-fold. Germany, which had structured its industrial economy around reliable cheap Russian gas, faced deindustrialization pressure in energy-intensive sectors. Aluminum smelters and fertilizer plants closed. European governments paid approximately $800 billion in energy subsidies to prevent the crisis from becoming an economic catastrophe.

Twenty-five years of optimization for efficiency had produced perfect efficiency in a world where Russia remained a reliable supplier. In a world where it did not, it had produced a $800 billion crisis bill.

## Measuring the Risk You Are Already Carrying

There is a number that should appear somewhere in every government budget document, every corporate supply chain report, and every trade policy analysis. It almost never does.

It is called Expected Annual Loss: the probability of a supply disruption in a given year multiplied by the economic damage that disruption would cause. It is how actuaries price insurance. It is how pension funds model liabilities. It is how every rational institution managing concentrated risk should be thinking about its exposure.

For U.S. critical material supply chains, the aggregate Expected Annual Loss — across rare earths, semiconductors, pharmaceutical ingredients, battery materials, and other concentrated import categories — is estimated at $56 to $190 billion per year.

That is the cost you are already paying, probabilistically, every year. You just do not see it as a line item because it is distributed across the economy as occasional catastrophic events rather than annual premiums. The 2020 N95 crisis was not a surprise expense. It was the Expected Annual Loss for healthcare supply concentration, collected in a single event after years of zero-premium carrying.

The $56 to $190 billion range is wide because the probability and damage estimates involve genuine uncertainty. The inputs are: the Concentration Risk Score for each commodity (a measure combining geographic concentration, substitutability, import dependency, and historical disruption frequency); the annual probability of a disruption event exceeding a damage threshold; and the economic damage at various disruption severities.

For rare earths, the Concentration Risk Score is 0.60 — the highest in the critical materials inventory. For semiconductors, it is 0.52. For pharmaceutical active ingredients, it is 0.48. Each of these commodities has a high concentration score because one or two countries control a disproportionate share of supply, the commodity is difficult to substitute in the short run, and the United States has high import dependency with limited domestic alternatives.

The Expected Annual Loss is not a hypothetical. It is the actuarially correct price of the risk the market embeds at zero.

## What Pre-Positioning Actually Costs

The question the Expected Annual Loss number raises is obvious: if the uninsured risk is $56 to $190 billion per year, what does it cost to insure it?

For rare earths specifically: pre-positioning a 90-day strategic reserve and funding allied processing capacity in Australia, Canada, and the European Union costs approximately $1.3 billion per year. The expected loss prevented is $12 to $30 billion per year. The cost ratio is 4 to 11 cents per dollar of coverage.

That is extraordinarily cheap insurance. The reason it is cheap is that pre-positioning avoids the crisis response premium. The cost of a 90-day strategic reserve is the cost of building and maintaining storage and the carrying cost of the inventory. The cost of responding to an actual rare earth shortage — emergency sourcing, production disruption, military procurement impact, downstream industry losses — is measured in tens of billions per event.

The problem is that no private market will offer this insurance at any price. The risk is non-diversifiable: a Chinese rare earth restriction affects every U.S. manufacturer simultaneously, so an insurer cannot pool the risk across a large population of independent events. The risk is adversarially manipulated: the probability of disruption depends on geopolitical decisions that an adversary can adjust in response to the existence of the insurance, making actuarial pricing unstable. And the solution requires coordination across the entire industry — every manufacturer benefits from the strategic reserve, whether or not they paid for it.

Private markets do not solve public goods problems. This is not an opinion. It is the defining characteristic of a public good.

The Department of Economic Defense is the institution that can hold the portfolio view, pay the actuarially correct premium, and provide the coverage that no private insurer can offer. At 4 to 11 cents per dollar of coverage, the economics are not marginal. They are overwhelming.

## The Option You Did Not Know You Had

There is one more way to think about strategic industrial capacity that changes the evaluation.

A domestic rare earth processing facility, viewed in isolation, may not appear economically competitive. Chinese processing is cheaper. If you model the facility as a widget that must compete in the spot market against Chinese alternatives every year, you will conclude it is not worth building.

That is the wrong model.

A domestic processing facility is not a widget. It is a real option: the right, but not the obligation, to produce domestically at a guaranteed cost, at any time in the future, regardless of what Chinese export policy does in the interim. Options have value even when not exercised. A call option on a stock is valuable even if you never exercise it, as long as there is a nonzero probability that you will want to in the future.

The Black-Scholes framework, adapted for strategic industrial capacity, gives a net present value of $31 to $68 billion for domestic rare earth processing capacity, even assuming a 15 percent premium over Chinese production costs. The option value comes from the volatility of Chinese supply policy and the catastrophic downside if that option is not available when needed.

The market does not price this option. It prices the commodity at the current spot rate and ignores the value of the flexibility that domestic capacity provides. The Department of Economic Defense exists to price the option correctly — to hold strategic capacity as a portfolio of real options against disruption scenarios that individual buyers cannot rationally prepare for.

## The Institution We Keep Almost Building

After every supply disruption, the post-mortem is the same. Congressional hearings. Expert testimony. Reports recommending strategic reserves, alternative capacity, allied supply chain investment. Partial funding. The crisis fades. The recommendations are implemented partially or not at all. The strategic stockpiles that were rebuilt after one crisis are allowed to deplete before the next one.

The Strategic Petroleum Reserve: created after the 1973 oil embargo, allowed to reach historic lows before the COVID-era drawdown in 2022. The Strategic National Stockpile: reviewed and found inadequate after H1N1 in 2009, still inadequate when COVID arrived in 2020. Rare earth policy: flagged as a critical vulnerability in the early 2000s, still 85 percent dependent on Chinese processing in 2024.

The pattern is consistent because the incentive structure is consistent. Pre-positioning requires spending money before the crisis. Crises are politically salient; preparation is not. The Congress that funds a strategic reserve gets no credit when the reserve prevents a disruption — prevented disruptions are invisible. The Congress that fails to fund a reserve gets blamed only if the specific disruption that reserve would have prevented occurs on its watch. The optimal political strategy, individually, is to underfund preparation. The optimal outcome, collectively, is to overfund it.

This is the same public goods problem, expressed in political terms.

The Department of Economic Defense is the institutional solution: a permanent agency with a statutory mandate to maintain portfolio exposure analysis and pre-positioning capacity across all critical supply chain categories, with a funding mechanism that does not depend on annual Congressional discretion to pass before the crisis becomes obvious. The DARPA model, applied to economic security: technical expertise, long planning horizons, and institutional authority to act before political consensus has formed.

The price of the risk we are carrying is $56 to $190 billion per year. The cost of insuring it is $8 to $12 billion per year across all critical categories combined. The insurance is cheap. The institution that can buy it has to be built.

Every year we wait, we pay the premium without getting the coverage.
