*Automation is the most powerful productivity engine in human history. The question
is who receives the gains.*

## Central Argument

Every previous wave of automation — mechanized agriculture, industrial machinery,
computing — displaced workers in some occupations while creating new ones in others.
The standard argument is that this time will be the same: AI will displace some jobs
and create others, and the labor market will adjust. That argument may be correct.
But "may be correct" and "the labor market will adjust on its own at an acceptable
pace without institutional support" are different claims. The current pace of large
language model capability development, robotic dexterity improvement, and autonomous
agent deployment suggests that the adjustment speed required may exceed the labor
market's historical self-correction rate. This article examines what that would mean
economically, what institutional response is proportionate, and — most importantly
— what automation owes the workers whose productivity it is built on.

---

## Key Evidence and Numbers to Build From

- McKinsey Global Institute (2023): 30% of current work hours in the U.S. could be
  automated by 2030. Goldman Sachs (2023): 300 million jobs globally exposed to
  automation. These are displacement-exposed estimates, not displacement predictions —
  the actual displacement rate depends on adoption speed and institutional response.
- Historical comparison: the mechanization of U.S. agriculture displaced 40% of the
  agricultural workforce between 1900 and 1960 — 60 years for a massive structural
  shift. The current AI transition may need to accomplish comparable displacement
  absorption in 15–20 years.
- Labor market adjustment is not painless: the communities displaced by manufacturing
  automation in the 1980s and 1990s experienced 20–30-year economic declines. "The
  market adjusts" is true in aggregate and catastrophic at the local and individual level.
- The Automation Tax Credit: an 18% excise on documented labor cost savings from AI
  automation generates $70–110B/year at full deployment. This is the mechanism that
  connects automation productivity gains to the workers those gains displaced.
- The projected $5.9 million to 9.8 million new jobs created in AI-adjacent categories
  by 2035 (infrastructure, oversight, service expansion, research, physical-world
  integration) — the new jobs exist, but they require training that the current system
  does not provide

### What Makes AI Different — and What Doesn't

*TODO: The case for "this time is different" and the honest case against it. What is
different: AI automates cognitive tasks, not just physical ones, meaning the class of
workers previously protected from automation (white-collar, credentialed, professional)
is now exposed. What is not different: the historical pattern of new jobs emerging from
new technology has held through every previous wave; there is no strong reason to believe
it will not hold again. The honest conclusion: we don't know the pace or distribution
of displacement in advance, which is exactly why institutional pre-positioning is the
correct response — not because catastrophe is certain, but because the cost of having
the infrastructure and not needing it is much lower than the cost of needing it and
not having it.*

### The Automation Tax Credit — Making the Machine Pay

*TODO: The central policy innovation. When a company uses AI to do work that previously
required human employees — saving X dollars in labor costs — it pays an 18% excise on
those savings into the Automation Tax Credit fund. That fund flows to the Negative
Income Tax (the income floor for displaced workers) and to retraining programs. The
logic: the productivity gain from automation is partly a return on the public investment
in the workers, the infrastructure, and the social stability that made the AI development
environment possible. The Automation Tax Credit recaptures a fraction of that gain and
returns it to the public base that generated it.*

### What Automation Owes

*TODO: The moral and economic argument together. Automation is built on publicly funded
research (the internet, GPS, and the foundational AI research were all government-funded).
It is deployed in a market structured by public law. It benefits from a workforce trained
by public institutions. When it displaces that workforce, the workers whose productivity
it was built on have a legitimate claim on the gains. The Automation Tax Credit is not

## Tone Notes

This article must be balanced between urgency and honest uncertainty. The reader
should not finish it thinking the AI apocalypse is certain — that is not the argument.
The reader should finish it thinking that the uncertainty itself is the reason to
pre-build the institutional response. The analogy to financial regulation is useful:
we regulate banks not because every bank will fail but because the cost of the failure
when it occurs justifies the cost of regulation in advance. The Automation Tax Credit
and the Negative Income Tax are the insurance and the adjustment infrastructure — not
a prediction that displacement will be catastrophic, but a preparation for the scenario
in which it is.
