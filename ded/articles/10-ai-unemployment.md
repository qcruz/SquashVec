# What AI Owes You

*Automation is the most powerful productivity engine in human history. The question
is who receives the gains.*

**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

In 1900, 41% of the American workforce worked in agriculture. By 1960, that figure was
7%. In six decades, the agricultural sector shed approximately 34% of the national
workforce — tens of millions of people whose livelihoods depended on work that machines
learned to do.

The standard account of this transition is optimistic: workers left the farm, moved to
the factory, wages rose, living standards improved, and the country became dramatically
richer. All of that is true in aggregate and over the full 60-year timeline.

The standard account does not tell you what it was like in 1930 in Oklahoma when
mechanized combines displaced sharecroppers. It does not tell you about the Dust Bowl
migration — 3.5 million people leaving the Southern Plains, many of whom never
recovered economically. It does not tell you about the labor strife of the 1930s or
the political movements that emerged from concentrated economic displacement. The
aggregate outcome was broadly positive. The path through was, for many specific people
in specific places, catastrophic.

The factory automation of the 1980s and 1990s followed the same pattern. Steel towns in
Pennsylvania. Auto plants in Michigan. Textile mills across the South. Communities that
had built their economic identity around a specific kind of work found that work
disappearing — not slowly, through attrition, but through plant closures and
outsourcing decisions that restructured the regional economy within a decade. Economists
studying those communities found persistent effects: lower wages, higher mortality,
higher opioid use, 20 to 30 years after the initial displacement. "The market adjusted"
was eventually true at the aggregate level. At the community level, the adjustment looked
more like a wound that did not fully heal.

The relevant question about the AI transition is not whether it will happen — it will —
and not whether it will eventually produce economic benefits — it will. The relevant
question is whether the path through is managed or unmanaged, and who bears the cost
of an unmanaged transition.

## The Scale of What Is Coming

McKinsey Global Institute estimates that 30% of current U.S. work hours could be automated
by 2030. Goldman Sachs estimates 300 million jobs globally are exposed to automation.
These are exposure estimates, not certainties — the actual displacement rate depends on
adoption speed, the emergence of new job categories, and the effectiveness of
institutional response.

But the exposure is real. And it is qualitatively different from previous automation
waves in one important respect.

Agricultural mechanization displaced physical labor — the muscle work of planting,
cultivating, and harvesting. Factory automation displaced physical labor in manufacturing.
Both left cognitive work relatively untouched. The workers displaced from agricultural
and factory jobs could find new employment in cognitive roles — clerks, bookkeepers,
customer service representatives, analysts — that machines could not yet perform.

AI automates cognitive work.

The occupations most exposed to large language model substitution are not factory
workers. They are: paralegals, junior analysts, customer service agents, data entry
workers, basic coders, document reviewers, content writers, and entry-level
administrative roles. These are predominantly office occupations, service occupations,
and white-collar roles — precisely the job categories that absorbed workers displaced
from manufacturing and agriculture in previous decades.

When the cognitive jobs that served as the safety net for manual displacement are
themselves subject to automation, the safety net changes. Workers displaced from
cognitive roles cannot move to a higher cognitive tier that machines cannot yet
perform — not all of them, and perhaps not most of them, on a timeline that matches
the displacement speed.

This does not mean mass unemployment is inevitable. It means the adjustment is harder,
faster, and affects a broader class of workers than previous transitions. It means the
question of institutional preparation is more urgent.

## What Is Not Different

The case for catastrophe is not as strong as some of its advocates suggest.

Every previous wave of automation produced predictions of permanent technological
unemployment. The handloom weavers of 1810 predicted that power looms would end
employment. The economists of 1930 predicted that farm mechanization would create a
permanent unemployed class. The industrial automation of the 1970s generated serious
academic literature on the end of work.

In each case, new technologies created new job categories that no one had anticipated.
The power loom created factory workers, factory managers, and the supply chains that
served them. Farm mechanization created agricultural equipment manufacturers, rural
electrification workers, and the suburban economy that absorbed displaced agricultural
labor. Industrial automation created programming, network administration, and the
information economy.

There is no strong theoretical reason to believe AI is different in this fundamental
sense. New technologies create new demands, and new demands create new work. The 5G
network created tower technicians. Social media created content moderators. Cloud
computing created cloud architects. AI itself is creating AI trainers, AI auditors, AI
integration specialists, and entirely new professional categories that did not exist
five years ago.

The honest answer is that we do not know whether the AI transition will produce
structural unemployment or structural adjustment. The historical record supports
adjustment. The theoretical arguments for structural unemployment are stronger this time
than in previous cycles, but not conclusive.

This uncertainty is itself the policy argument. If we knew the transition would be
smooth, we could rely on the labor market to manage it. If we knew it would be
catastrophic, we would mobilize emergency resources. We know neither. The rational
response to genuine uncertainty about a high-consequence outcome is insurance — building
the institutional capacity to manage the bad scenario without betting the country's
economic stability on the good scenario materializing.

## The Automation Tax Credit: Making the Machine Pay

Here is the mechanism.

A company deploys AI that handles customer service inquiries previously managed by 200
employees. The annual labor cost savings are real and documentable: wages, benefits,
payroll taxes, and overhead. The company calculates those savings accurately — it has
every incentive to do so, because the savings are why it deployed the AI.

An 18% Automation Tax Credit excise applies to documented labor cost savings from AI
automation. The company that saved $20 million in labor costs pays $3.6 million into
the Automation Tax Credit fund. It retains $16.4 million in net savings — a strong
positive return on its AI investment. The excise does not eliminate the incentive to
automate. It reduces the after-tax return slightly while creating the fund that supports
the workers displaced by that automation.

At full deployment — when AI automation has reached substantial penetration across the
U.S. economy — the Automation Tax Credit generates an estimated $70 to $110 billion
per year. That revenue funds the Negative Income Tax income floor for displaced workers
and the retraining programs that move them toward new employment.

The logic is not punitive. It is accounting.

AI is built on publicly funded research. The foundational models — the large language
models and neural network architectures underlying commercial AI — descend from decades
of academic research funded primarily by government grants and defense R&D. The
infrastructure AI runs on — the internet, GPS, cloud computing — was built primarily
with public investment. The workforce that trained the AI systems, created the data
they were trained on, and built the companies that deploy them was educated at public
institutions.

When a company uses AI to capture productivity gains, it is capturing the return on
a public investment. The Automation Tax Credit is not a punishment for efficiency.
It is the billing mechanism for a service — the trained workforce, the research base,
the stable society — that automation consumed and that the companies who benefited
from it should contribute to restoring.

## The Three-Phase Response

The institutional response to AI-driven displacement is not a single program. It is a
sequenced architecture.

**Phase 1: Triage.** For workers displaced by AI automation before retraining programs
exist at scale, the immediate response is stabilization. Extended unemployment insurance
— beyond the standard 26 weeks — for workers in documented AI-displacement occupations.
Immediate $2,500 payments to workers who lose jobs in displacement-flagged categories.
$25,000 in tax forgiveness for retraining expenses, available immediately and
repayable only if retraining results in income above the pre-displacement level.
Student loan suspension for workers enrolled in retraining. Healthcare continuity
through the Civilian Reserve enrollment option.

These instruments are not generous by any expansive definition. They are the minimum
stabilization needed to prevent the acute financial pressure that makes good retraining
decisions impossible. A worker who needs to make rent next month cannot make a two-year
retraining investment this month. Stabilization buys the time that retraining requires.

**Phase 2: Transition.** The Workforce Development Floor and the AI Retraining Flywheel
constitute the transition infrastructure. The Flywheel is a personalized retraining
system using AI tools themselves — the same technology driving displacement — to identify
each displaced worker's transferable skills, match them to emerging job categories,
design a learning path calibrated to their background, and connect them to employers
who need the skills at the end of training. AI that displaces workers can also be the
most efficient tool for retraining them.

The five categories of AI-created employment — infrastructure build-out, AI oversight
and auditing, service expansion in sectors made affordable by AI efficiency,
research and alignment, and physical-world integration for robotic systems — each
require skills that current training pipelines are not producing at scale. The retraining
program is designed to fill this gap, not by predicting exactly which jobs will exist
in five years but by developing the underlying capabilities — technical fluency,
adaptive problem-solving, human-AI collaboration — that make workers valuable in
AI-adjacent roles as those roles emerge.

**Phase 3: Normalization.** The Negative Income Tax and the American Sovereign Fund
dividend are the steady-state instruments — not the emergency response, but the
permanent floor and the permanent stake. In a fully automated economy — wherever
that endpoint lies — a significant fraction of economic value is generated by capital
(AI systems, factories) rather than labor. Workers whose skills are no longer in
demand still need to participate in the economy. The Negative Income Tax provides the
income floor. The American Sovereign Fund dividend provides a return on the national
capital stock that citizens collectively built and own.

The three phases are not alternative responses. They are sequential stages of a
transition architecture: stabilize the immediate impact, support the medium-term
adjustment, establish the long-run equilibrium.

## The Jobs That AI Creates

The argument that AI creates new employment is often dismissed as wishful thinking
or historical analogy that won't apply this time. The dismissal is premature.

The Department of Economic Defense's AI employment analysis identified five categories
of AI-created work with documented, near-term demand:

**Infrastructure.** Building, maintaining, and operating AI systems requires physical
infrastructure: data centers, power systems, cooling systems, network equipment, and
the construction workers who install them. Every major AI model requires warehouses full
of chips drawing enormous amounts of power. The Sovereign Compute Reserve program is
already identifying the workforce gaps in this category. The jobs are physical,
require skilled trades training, and cannot be offshored.

**Oversight and auditing.** Regulated industries — healthcare, financial services, legal,
government — cannot operate AI systems without human oversight. Radiologists reviewing
AI-flagged medical images. Compliance officers auditing AI-driven lending decisions.
Safety engineers reviewing autonomous vehicle decision-making. These are not jobs that
disappear when AI improves; they are jobs that grow with AI deployment, because the
regulatory requirements grow with the risk exposure.

**Service expansion.** AI reduces the cost of providing certain services, enabling
demand that didn't previously exist. Online therapy sessions become affordable at AI-
assisted rates; this creates demand for human therapists at scale that didn't exist before.
Personalized tutoring becomes affordable; this creates demand for human educators in
a monitoring and relationship role. Elder care becomes assistable by AI systems; this
creates demand for human care workers who supervise and connect with clients that
machines cannot fully replace. The service expansion category is the most difficult
to predict but historically has been the largest source of new employment after
productivity gains in adjacent sectors.

**Research and alignment.** Making AI systems more capable, more reliable, and safer
is a research enterprise that employs tens of thousands of people and will employ more
as deployment expands. AI alignment — the technical problem of ensuring AI systems
behave as intended — is unsolved and requires the kinds of creative, interdisciplinary
research that AI itself cannot yet perform.

**Physical-world integration.** Robots are advancing rapidly. They are not yet reliable
enough to operate without human supervision in most unstructured environments. The gap
between AI capability in software and AI capability in the physical world creates
sustained demand for humans who can operate, supervise, repair, and adapt robotic
systems in complex real-world settings. This gap is measured in years, not decades.

Across these five categories, the AI unemployment analysis projects 5.9 million to
9.8 million new positions in the United States by 2035. These positions do not
self-populate — they require training pipelines that currently do not exist at the
required scale. The retraining program is what makes those projections achievable.

## What Automation Owes

There is a version of this argument that is purely economic: automation creates a
fiscal case for institutional support because the Negative Income Tax and retraining
programs, properly designed, generate returns that exceed their costs. That argument
is correct and is documented elsewhere.

But there is also a version of this argument that is not primarily economic.

The people who will be displaced by AI — the customer service workers, the paralegals,
the data entry clerks, the junior analysts — did not cause the displacement. They did
not make the investment decisions. They did not capture the productivity gains. They
provided labor that was valuable until technology made it less so, and they did so in
a system that gave them no stake in the technology that replaced them.

The productivity gains from AI will be enormous. They will flow, in the current
institutional arrangement, primarily to the owners of AI systems and to the shareholders
of the companies that deploy them. Some will flow to consumers in the form of lower
prices and better products. Very little, absent institutional intervention, will flow
to the workers whose displacement generated those gains or whose labor trained the
models that are now more productive than they are.

The Automation Tax Credit is the correction to this distribution. It is not charity
toward displaced workers. It is the acknowledgment that automation is a collective
achievement — built on public research, public infrastructure, and the labor of workers
who are entitled to a return on the productivity their work made possible.

The workers who trained the data that trained the models. The workers who built the
data centers that run the models. The workers who provided the services that AI now
replaces. They contributed to the foundation on which automation stands.

What automation owes them is not guaranteed employment in jobs that no longer exist.
It is the income floor that keeps them solvent during the transition. The retraining
program that opens the door to new work. The dividend on the national wealth that their
labor helped create. The healthcare that doesn't depend on the employer relationship
that automation just ended.

These are not luxuries. They are the terms of a fair transition.
