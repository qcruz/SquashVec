# Mining the Moon to Secure the Earth

*Why the next rare earth solution is not in Australia or Canada*

**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

The United States imports approximately 80% of its rare earth elements from China.

This fact has been known for twenty years. The policy response has been consistent:
encourage domestic mining, negotiate with allied nations, build alternative supply chains.
These responses are correct and necessary. They are also, in the long run, insufficient.

The reason is geology. Rare earth elements are not rare in the periodic table sense —
they are present in the Earth's crust in moderate abundance. What is rare is the
concentration. High-grade rare earth deposits require specific geological processes
to form, and those processes tend to produce deposits in a limited number of places.
Mountain Pass in California is the premier domestic deposit. Round Top in Texas is
viable. Bear Lodge in Wyoming is viable. Lynas in Australia is the most significant
allied processor.

Building out those deposits reduces the concentration risk substantially. It buys the
United States 20 to 30 years of reduced dependence on Chinese rare earth processing,
which is exactly what the near-term rare earth programs are designed to do.

But it does not solve the underlying constraint. Terrestrial rare earth deposits are
genuinely finite, genuinely concentrated, and genuinely subject to geopolitical control
by whoever controls the territory they sit on. The 20 to 30 years that domestic and
allied development buys is time — valuable time — not a permanent solution.

The permanent solution is not on Earth.

---

## What Is Actually Out There

The asteroid 16 Psyche is a metallic body approximately 280 kilometers in diameter,
orbiting the Sun between Mars and Jupiter. NASA's Psyche mission, launched in 2023,
is currently en route to it. Early spectroscopic analysis suggests 16 Psyche is
primarily composed of iron, nickel, gold, and platinum group metals. The face-value
estimates of its mineral content — widely quoted as $10,000 quadrillion — are not
meaningful as a market prediction; bringing that quantity to Earth would collapse the
market for every metal it contained. But the number illustrates something important:
the constraint that makes rare earth supply geopolitically dangerous on Earth simply
does not exist in the solar system. The materials are there in quantities that
dwarf anything available terrestrially.

More practically: near-Earth asteroid (469219) Kamo'oalewa is estimated to contain
more platinum group metals — platinum, palladium, rhodium, iridium — than have been
extracted from the Earth's crust in all of recorded human history. It orbits at a
distance that makes it accessible to robotic spacecraft with current propulsion technology.
It has no geopolitical owner. No adversarial government can restrict access to it.
No environmental permitting process governs extraction from it.

Rare earth elements are present in significant concentrations in lunar regolith —
the material covering the Moon's surface. The lunar surface has been surveyed
extensively. Neodymium, dysprosium, and other rare earth elements critical to permanent
magnet production have been identified in concentrations that warrant commercial interest.
The Moon is roughly 384,000 kilometers away. That is close enough that round-trip
communication delays are measured in seconds rather than minutes, and that robotic
operations can be controlled with meaningful real-time supervision.

The question is not whether these resources exist. They do. The question is whether
the technology to extract them commercially is ready, and if not, what would make it
ready faster.

## What Is Actually Being Built

The commercial space resource industry is not speculative. It is small, early, and
moving faster than most people outside the industry realize.

AstroForge, a U.S. company founded in 2022, launched its first mission in 2023 — a
rideshare payload that demonstrated asteroid material processing technology in orbit.
Its second mission is a deep-space prospecting flight to a specific metallic near-Earth
asteroid. The company has raised institutional venture capital from investors who
have reviewed the technology and the market.

ispace, a Japanese company with U.S. operations, launched a lunar landing mission in
2023. The landing attempt failed — the spacecraft made contact with the surface but
could not confirm a successful landing. The company identified the failure cause,
is building a second mission, and continues to attract investment. Failed first
attempts are not unusual in new industries; they are normal.

Karman+, Redwire, and a handful of other companies are developing in-situ resource
utilization technology — the systems that would extract, process, and either store
or transmit materials from asteroid or lunar environments. The technology stack is
not complete, but the components are in active development, not theoretical design.

The commercial space resource market was valued at approximately $2.05 billion in
2024 and is growing at roughly 21% annually. By the early 2030s, at current trajectory,
the first commercial demonstrations of asteroid material processing in space are plausible.
Scaled commercial operations — the kind that would supply materials at volumes relevant
to industrial demand — are a 2040 to 2050 story.

That is the honest timeline. It is also the relevant one for policy: the investments
made in the next five to ten years determine whether 2040 arrives with a functioning
commercial space resource industry or with a nascent one still waiting for its first
demonstration.

## The First-Mover Problem

Space resource companies face the same investment problem that precision fermentation
companies face and that semiconductor fabs faced in the 1980s: the capital required to
reach commercial viability is large, the timeline to return on investment is long, and
the revenue certainty that private investors need to fund the scale-up does not exist
until the scale-up has happened.

Private venture capital can fund early-stage technology development. It cannot fund
the multi-billion dollar infrastructure investment required to move from demonstrated
technology to commercial operations, on a 10 to 20 year timeline, in a new industry
with no comparable precedent. The risk-adjusted return does not work for private capital
at that scale and horizon.

The mechanism that breaks this problem is the advance purchase commitment.

Operation Warp Speed committed the U.S. government to purchasing hundreds of millions
of doses of COVID-19 vaccines before those vaccines had completed clinical trials —
before it was certain they would work, before manufacturing processes were validated,
before regulatory approval was granted. That commitment converted a speculative
investment into a bankable revenue stream. Private manufacturers mobilized capital
that would otherwise have waited for risk reduction that would have taken years. The
vaccines were ready in months instead of years. The advance purchase commitment
accelerated development by an estimated 18 months.

The same mechanism applies to space resource development. The Department of Economic
Defense commits to purchasing a defined quantity of specific materials — platinum group
metals, rare earth concentrates, nickel — at 90% of the prevailing terrestrial spot
price, for a defined period, when commercially delivered from space-based extraction.
The commitment is contingent on delivery — no payment until material is received. But
it is legally binding once material begins arriving, and it is announced before development
begins.

That announcement changes the investment calculus. A company building toward space
resource extraction can now show investors a bankable revenue commitment from the U.S.
government. The risk profile of the investment changes. Capital mobilizes. The commercial
timeline accelerates.

The cost of the commitment, if space resources arrive on schedule, is approximately
10% of the terrestrial price — the discount the government accepts for committing early.
Against the expected annual loss from critical mineral concentration risk — estimated at
$56 to $190 billion per year in unpriced expected disruption cost — a 10% price discount
on future mineral supply is an extremely cheap form of insurance.

The cost of the commitment, if space resources never arrive, is zero. Advance purchase
commitments are contingent on delivery.

## The International Framework Question

The United States is not the only country pursuing space resources. China has an active
lunar program with an explicit resource utilization objective. The Chang'e series of
missions has been systematically mapping lunar mineral deposits. China's 2030 lunar
program includes a permanent research station designed, among other functions, to support
in-situ resource utilization.

Luxembourg passed a national space resources law in 2017 establishing that Luxembourg
companies can own materials they extract in space. The United States passed the SPACE
Act in 2015, granting U.S. citizens and companies the right to own space resources they
extract. Japan, the UAE, and India have active space resource programs. Several
international frameworks for space resource governance are in various stages of
discussion.

The country that establishes the first working legal and commercial framework for space
resource rights — analogous to international maritime law for seabed mining — will have
structural advantages in the emerging space economy comparable to the advantages the
United States gained from establishing the GPS standard, the internet protocol standards,
or the dollar as the world's reserve currency. Standards set early are hard to displace.
First movers who establish the legal architecture tend to write rules that favor their
own commercial structures.

The United States is currently well-positioned. The SPACE Act provides the legal
foundation. The commercial industry has the most advanced technology base. The military
space program provides unmatched launch and orbital operations capability.

That window will not stay open indefinitely. China's pace in lunar and deep-space
capability development is accelerating. Luxembourg has attracted several major space
resource companies through its early legal clarity. If the United States does not
actively shape the international framework in the next five to ten years, it will be
negotiating into a framework shaped by others.

The Department of Economic Defense's role is not to run the diplomacy — that is State
and USTR — but to provide the analytical foundation: the supply chain concentration
risk data, the advance purchase commitment structure, and the strategic reserve framework
that make U.S. participation in space resource governance credible and commercially
grounded.

## Why This Belongs to the Department of Economic Defense

The Department of Economic Defense's mandate is to reduce the concentration risk in
materials critical to the U.S. economy and defense. The Concentration Risk Score for
rare earth processing — measuring geographic concentration, substitutability, import
dependency, and disruption history — sits at approximately 0.60, classified as Critical.

Terrestrial diversification programs bring that score down to 0.40 or 0.45 on a 10 to
15 year horizon, assuming the domestic and allied processing investments perform as
designed. That is meaningful progress but not resolution — a score of 0.40 still
represents a significant concentration risk for materials that appear in every
guided missile, every advanced radar system, and every electric vehicle motor.

Space-derived supply would bring the Concentration Risk Score for those materials to
effectively 0.0. No adversarial government controls the asteroid belt. No single country
owns the Moon's surface. No supply restriction policy can be applied to resources
extracted in the commons of outer space. The geopolitical supply risk that makes rare
earths a national security concern is eliminated permanently, not managed indefinitely.

The Department of Economic Defense's mandate calls for identifying the lowest-cost path
to eliminating a given concentration risk, accounting for all timeframes. The lowest-cost
path to permanently eliminating rare earth concentration risk is space resource
development, enabled by advance purchase commitments that fund the commercial scale-up
on a realistic timeline.

Terrestrial diversification is the short-run solution. Space mining is the long-run one.
The programs are complementary, not competing. The question is whether the long-run
investment begins in time for the long run to arrive when it is needed.
