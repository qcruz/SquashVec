# The Machine That Pays for Itself

*How the individual programs connect into a single self-reinforcing economic system*

**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

Consider two ways to design a government program.

The first way: identify a problem, allocate money to address it, and repeat the process
every year in the budget negotiation. The program costs something. It addresses the
problem at some level. If the political will persists, it continues. If the political
will evaporates — in a recession, a midterm election, a change in administration — the
program is cut, and the problem returns. This is how most social programs work. They are
as durable as the political coalition that supports them, which is to say, not very.

The second way: design programs so that solving the problem generates the revenue that
funds solving the problem. The program is not a cost center that requires continuous
political defense. It is a machine that produces output — and the output includes the
fuel to keep running. This is how good infrastructure works. The interstate highway system
generates the economic activity that produces the tax revenue that funds highway maintenance.
Social Security generates the payroll tax contributions that fund Social Security benefits.
The machine runs because the outputs include the inputs.

The set of programs in this series — the Automation Tax Credit, the Negative Income Tax,
the American Sovereign Fund, the Civilian Reserve, the Workforce Development Floor — were
not designed independently. They were designed as a system, with explicit feedback loops
between them. The system is not a collection of programs. It is a machine. And machines
designed correctly generate more output than the sum of their parts.

This article explains how the machine works.

---

## The Problem With Programs

Lists of programs fail. They fail politically because each program has opponents, and
opponents can pick them off individually in budget negotiations — the income floor is
cut one year, the training program the next, the workforce reserve the year after. They
fail analytically because programs designed in isolation create interaction effects that
undermine each other: benefit cliffs where earning a dollar of income triggers the loss
of two dollars in benefits; eligibility conflicts where qualifying for one program
disqualifies a person from another; perverse incentives where the rational response to
the benefit structure is to reduce work, not increase it.

The programs in this series were designed to avoid these failures. The feedback loops
are explicit. The phase-out rates are calibrated. The funding mechanisms are connected.
The political durability comes not from ideology but from architecture: a machine that
generates its own fuel is harder to defund than a program that requires external
appropriation every year.

## Cycle 1: Productivity to Ownership

This is the central loop — the one that connects automation to economic security for
the people automation displaces.

A company deploys AI systems that handle work previously done by employees. The labor
cost savings are real and documentable. An 18% Automation Tax Credit excise on those
savings flows into a dedicated fund. At full deployment — when AI automation has achieved
a substantial share of its projected penetration — that fund generates $70 to $110 billion
per year.

That revenue has two destinations. Sixty percent flows to the Negative Income Tax:
the income floor that ensures displaced workers can meet basic expenses while retraining.
A worker whose job was automated receives a guaranteed income floor of $6,200 per year
per individual — not a temporary unemployment payment, but a permanent floor that phases
out as they earn more. The floor exists whether or not they find work quickly. It gives
them time to retrain without the desperation that makes good retraining decisions
impossible.

The remaining forty percent flows to the American Sovereign Fund: the national wealth
fund that invests in domestic strategic industries, builds the infrastructure the country
needs, and returns dividends to every eligible citizen.

The retrained workers from the Negative Income Tax pipeline enter the sectors the
American Sovereign Fund has built — semiconductor manufacturing, rare earth processing,
clean energy infrastructure, pharmaceutical production. Those sectors generate taxable
income. That income expands the Automation Tax Credit tax base. The fund grows.

The loop closes. Automation generates productivity gains. The gains fund an income floor.
The floor enables retraining. Retraining fills the jobs in strategic sectors. Those
sectors generate revenue. The revenue expands the system that supported the transition.

The machine is not perpetual motion — it requires an initial investment and ongoing
governance. But it is designed so that success in each stage funds the next, rather than
requiring fresh political authorization every year.

## Cycle 2: Defense to Capital

This loop is less intuitive but equally important.

The United States is the world's largest arms exporter — approximately $238 billion in
foreign military sales in 2023. The revenue from those sales currently flows largely
to private defense contractors and their shareholders. The workers who build the systems
receive wages. The citizens who live in the communities where the factories operate
receive some local economic benefit. But there is no mechanism by which the national
defense industrial capacity — built with decades of public research investment, sustained
by public contracts, and dependent on public infrastructure — returns any ownership stake
to the public.

The American Sovereign Fund changes this. A 7.5% levy on documented foreign military
sales flows into the Fund's Strategic Reserve tranche. This is not a tax on defense
exports in the sense of making them more expensive — it is a participation fee, paid
by the fund of arms sale revenue, that earns the public a stake in the industrial
assets that generate those sales.

The Strategic Reserve tranche invests in domestic defense-adjacent industrial capacity:
the titanium supply chain, the specialized electronics manufacturing, the propellant
chemistry, the shipbuilding infrastructure. These investments make the defense supply
chain more resilient and more domestic. A more resilient and domestic supply chain
produces defense systems at lower cost. Lower-cost systems are more competitive on
the export market. More export sales generate more levy revenue. The fund grows.

The citizens who live in the communities where the defense industrial base operates
receive an annual dividend — not from charity or redistribution, but from the returns
on the industrial assets the American Sovereign Fund owns. The defense industrial
complex, which has always extracted public subsidy and returned private profit, begins
returning public dividends on public investment. The cycle is not ideological. It is
the correct accounting for whose money built what.

## The Build-Own-Benefit Loop

The most concrete expression of how the system works is not an economic model. It is
a story about a specific person.

Maria is 28 years old. She enrolled in the Civilian Reserve at 26, when the company
where she worked began replacing her position with automated systems. The Negative Income
Tax gave her a $6,200 income floor — enough to pay rent while she figured out what came
next. The Civilian Reserve gave her healthcare, a training schedule, and two years of
structured work in infrastructure construction: installing grid modernization equipment
at federal facilities in her region.

At 28, she completes her Civilian Reserve training in electrical systems. She takes a
job with a domestic battery storage manufacturer — one of the companies the American
Sovereign Fund Strategic Reserve invested in four years earlier. Her starting wage is
$52,000, higher than her previous job. The Negative Income Tax phases out completely.
She pays federal and state income taxes. Her payroll taxes go toward Social Security and
Medicare. Her consumer spending flows into the local economy and generates multiplier
activity that produces additional tax revenue.

At 45, Maria receives her first American Sovereign Fund dividend. The fund has been
compounding for decades. The dividend is not large yet — $400 that year. But it is a
return on the assets she helped build: the grid equipment she installed at 26, the battery
manufacturing facility her employer operates, the rare earth processing plant that her
company's supply chain depends on. She built those assets. The American Sovereign Fund
owns them. She is a beneficiary of the fund. She receives a dividend.

At 65, she retires. Her American Sovereign Fund dividend has grown with the fund's
returns. Her Social Security benefit reflects 37 years of payroll contributions from
her manufacturing career. The care economy program she will rely on in her later years —
staffed partly by Civilian Reserve members providing community health services — is
funded partly by the Automation Tax Credit that has been running for decades.

The loop is concrete, not abstract. Labor builds capital. Capital generates income.
Income flows back to labor. The same person who did the work receives the dividend from
the work. This is a different economy than the one we have — where the person who does
the work receives a wage, and the person who owns the capital receives the return on
the capital, and these are rarely the same person.

## What the Steady State Looks Like

At Year 30, if the programs perform as designed:

The American Sovereign Fund holds approximately $5.5 trillion in assets — split between
the Strategic Reserve (domestic industrial and infrastructure equity) and the Commercial
Portfolio (diversified global investments). The annual dividend is approximately $720 per
eligible citizen per year, growing toward $2,000 or more by Year 50. This is not a
living wage. It is a stake — a recognition that the citizens of this country own something
real in the economy they built.

The Negative Income Tax is fully funded by Automation Tax Credit revenue. The net federal
cost of the income floor — which was approximately $90 billion per year at launch, offset
by consolidation of programs it replaced — has declined as employment has risen and the
phase-out provision has reduced payments. The program runs without an annual appropriation
fight because its funding source is statutory and automatic.

The Civilian Reserve has trained approximately three million workers over thirty years in
skills tied to national strategic needs. The labor shortages in critical infrastructure
sectors — the bottlenecks that every crisis scenario identifies — have been reduced
because the reserve training pipeline anticipates them rather than responding to them
after the fact.

The scenario response infrastructure is pre-positioned. The Strategic Reserve holds
sufficient stockpiles and contracted capacity across nine identified disruption scenarios.
A drought, a semiconductor shortage, a pandemic, a supply chain disruption: each has a
pre-built institutional response that activates automatically based on trigger thresholds.
No emergency legislation required. No improvised response at enormous cost. The machine
runs.

Total system inflow at Year 30: approximately $190 to $540 billion per year (Automation
Tax Credit, arms levy, tariff revenue, American Sovereign Fund returns, resource royalties).
Total citizen benefit: approximately $470 to $570 billion per year — in the form of income
support, healthcare coverage, training access, dividends, and the crisis insurance that
prevents the catastrophic losses that crises without institutional response produce.

The system is approximately self-financing by Year 25. After that, it generates surplus.

## The Political Durability Argument

Every program in this system has an enrolled constituency. American Sovereign Fund
dividend recipients will defend their dividend. Negative Income Tax recipients will defend
their floor. Civilian Reserve members will defend their healthcare. Automation Tax Credit
revenue recipients — the displaced workers and the communities where strategic investments
flow — will defend the system that supports them.

This is not an accident. It is the design. The Alaska Permanent Fund has survived forty
years of fiscal crises, ideological shifts, and political turbulence because every Alaskan
has a personal financial interest in its continuation. No governor has successfully cut it.
No legislature has seriously tried. The machine protects itself because the people it
benefits understand what they would lose.

The Department of Economic Defense system is designed with the same logic at the
national scale. Each program generates a constituency. Each constituency defends the
program. The programs reinforce each other, so cutting one damages the others, raising
the political cost of any single cut.

This is not political cynicism. It is political realism. Programs that work and that
people understand they depend on are hard to eliminate. Programs that generate visible
dividends and measurable income floors and documented crisis responses are harder to
cut than programs that work in the background on problems people do not yet experience
as their own.

The machine is designed to run. And it is designed so that running it is in everyone's
interest.
