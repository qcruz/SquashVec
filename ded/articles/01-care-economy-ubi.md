# 53 Million Unpaid Workers

*How America's largest workforce stays invisible — and a proposal to change that*

---

Elena is 58 years old. She spent her career as an administrative professional, earning
$55,000 a year, paying into Social Security, saving incrementally for retirement. Then
her husband Robert was diagnosed with stage IV pancreatic cancer.

They made a decision together, the way couples do when they've been married long enough
to know what matters. He would come home. She would be there. He would spend his remaining
months surrounded by family rather than in an institution.

Elena left her job.

For the next fourteen months, she managed his pain medication — opioids that require
careful, consistent dosing. She prepared food adapted to his restricted diet. She helped
him bathe. She helped him move when he could no longer move himself. She coordinated with
the hospice team that visited three times a week and handled everything they didn't cover
the other four days. She was on call, always, including every night.

The professional alternative to what Elena was doing — inpatient hospice care — costs
between $1,100 and $1,500 per day. Over fourteen months, that is somewhere between
$462,000 and $630,000. The home aide option, which would have covered only 40 of the 80+
hours of weekly care Elena was providing, would have cost $52,000 to $79,000 per year.

Elena saved the healthcare system hundreds of thousands of dollars. She provided better
care than an institution would have. She honored a commitment to her husband that no
professional service could replicate. She did everything right.

When Robert died, Elena had no job to return to. She had no health insurance — she had lost
her employer-sponsored plan when she left, and COBRA had cost her $900 a month to maintain.
She was 59, with a 14-month resume gap, in a job market that is not kind to 59-year-old
women returning after a gap. And those 14 months now appeared in her Social Security
earnings record as zeroes — permanently reducing the retirement benefit she would receive
for the rest of her life.

The United States government gave Elena nothing for any of this. Not recognition, not
income, not healthcare, not retirement credit. She performed an act of enormous economic
and social value, and the economy treated it as if it had not happened.

Elena is not unusual. She is one of 53 million Americans.

## The Economy We Don't Count

There is an economy running parallel to the one measured in GDP. It does not appear in
official statistics. It does not generate taxable income. It is not discussed in Federal
Reserve policy meetings or tracked by the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

It is the economy of care.

Fifty-three million Americans — roughly one in six — are currently providing unpaid care
to a family member. Caring for parents with dementia or Alzheimer's disease. Caring for
children with serious disabilities. Caring for spouses with chronic illness or terminal
cancer. Caring for siblings with severe mental illness. Caring for the community around
them in ways that no government agency and no private business is funded to do.

The AARP Public Policy Institute puts the annual economic value of this unpaid care at
$470 billion. Other estimates go higher. For comparison, the entire U.S. prescription drug
industry — one of the most profitable sectors in the American economy — generates roughly
$550 billion in annual revenue. The unpaid care economy is nearly as large, and it is
staffed entirely by people who receive nothing for it.

Of those 53 million caregivers, 22 million have reduced their paid work hours to provide
care. Six million have left paid employment entirely. These are not people who chose
not to work. They are people who made a rational, loving, economically significant
decision to perform labor that the country depends on — and found that the country's
economic system treats that decision as an absence rather than a contribution.

Every zero that appears in Elena's Social Security record is a year of genuine, valuable
work that the system simply cannot see.

## What Happens When the System Can't See You

The consequences of this invisibility compound over time. They are not evenly distributed.

### The Retirement Penalty

Social Security calculates your benefit based on your 35 highest-earning years. Every year
you spend providing unpaid care appears in that calculation as a zero. If you provided care
for five years, your benefit is calculated on 30 earning years instead of 35 — the five
zero years drag down your lifetime average.

The National Women's Law Center has studied this extensively. Women who spent years
providing care receive Social Security benefits that are 20 to 30 percent lower than
those of men with equivalent education and work capacity, specifically because caregiving
years appear as zeroes in their earnings record. In dollar terms, this represents tens of
thousands of dollars in lost retirement income, spread across the decades of retirement.

This is not a small or marginal effect. For a woman who spent five years as a full-time
caregiver in her 50s — the peak earning years, when her Social Security record should
be accumulating its highest values — the lifetime retirement income penalty can exceed
$100,000.

The penalty falls disproportionately on women. Roughly 60 percent of unpaid caregivers
are women. The Social Security system was designed in 1935, when it was assumed that
most women would not have earnings records of their own. That assumption has not been
corrected in the intervening ninety years. Women who stepped out of the workforce to
raise children and then stepped out again to care for aging parents are being penalized
twice by a system that still, structurally, treats their care work as nothing.

### The Healthcare Gap

Employer-sponsored health insurance ends when employment ends. COBRA — the federal program
that allows you to continue your employer's coverage — costs an average of $700 to $1,400
per month for an individual. For someone who has just left paid employment to provide
full-time care, $700 to $1,400 per month is money they frequently do not have.

The result: caregivers — people who are spending their days managing someone else's
complex medical situation — frequently go without healthcare coverage themselves. The
irony is both personal and systemic. Personal because the caregiver's own health
deteriorates during intensive care episodes; studies consistently show elevated rates
of depression, anxiety, hypertension, and immune dysfunction among full-time caregivers.
Systemic because a caregiver whose health breaks down cannot continue providing care —
and when informal care collapses, the formal healthcare system absorbs the cost at
many times the price.

### The Young Caregiver Trap

The caregiving crisis reaches backward into the next generation in ways that are not
widely understood.

The American Academy of Pediatrics estimates that 5.4 million Americans under 18 are
regularly providing care for a family member. They get siblings to school in the morning
when their parent cannot. They manage medication schedules. They handle household
logistics that a healthy family distributes across two adults. They do this while
attending — or trying to attend — school.

Young caregivers have significantly higher school absenteeism rates, lower graduation
rates, and dramatically lower college enrollment rates compared to their peers. The
caregiving responsibility that begins in childhood follows them into adulthood as a
career gap, a credential gap, and an earnings gap. The child who stayed home to care
for a parent becomes the adult who was never able to build the economic foundation
that peers without that responsibility could build.

This is the mechanism by which economic precarity reproduces itself across generations.
It is invisible in every policy discussion about educational inequality.

## The Proposal

The argument so far is a description of a problem. Here is the proposed solution. It has
three components, and they work together.

### Component 1: The Negative Income Tax — An Income Floor for Everyone

A Negative Income Tax is simpler than its name suggests. Here is how it works:

When you file your taxes, the government calculates your income. If your income is above
a threshold, you pay taxes — as you do now. If your income is below a threshold, instead
of paying taxes, the government pays you. The payment is the "negative" tax — the
government taking a negative amount from you, which is to say, giving you something.

The payment phases out as your income rises. At a 30 percent phase-out rate and a $6,200
annual guarantee for an individual, the math looks like this:

- $0 in income: you receive $6,200
- $10,000 in income: you receive $3,200
- $20,000 in income: you receive $200
- $20,667 in income: the payment reaches zero (the crossover point)
- Above $20,667: you owe taxes in the normal way

The critical property — the one that makes this different from every existing welfare
program — is that you always benefit from earning more. There is no cliff. There is no
point at which earning an additional dollar causes you to lose two dollars in benefits.
Every dollar you earn improves your situation by 70 cents even when you are receiving
the maximum guarantee.

This matters because the existing welfare system is riddled with cliffs. A family
receiving food assistance, housing assistance, childcare assistance, and Medicaid can
face a situation where a $500 raise at work triggers the loss of thousands of dollars
in benefits. The rational response — and many people in this situation make it — is
not to take the raise. The Negative Income Tax eliminates this trap structurally.

Milton Friedman proposed a version of this in 1962. Richard Nixon nearly got it through
Congress in 1969 — it passed the House, failed in the Senate. The Earned Income Tax
Credit, which currently benefits 23 million households, is a partial version of it:
a refundable tax credit that phases out as income rises. The Negative Income Tax extends
that logic to everyone, including those who are not currently employed.

The cost, net of the programs it replaces — food assistance, some cash assistance
programs, and the bureaucratic overhead of administering means-tested eligibility — is
approximately $89 billion per year at initial implementation. That is roughly 0.3
percent of GDP. For comparison, the United States spent $5.2 trillion on emergency
COVID relief over two years — a crisis the Negative Income Tax, had it existed, would
have managed automatically without requiring a single piece of emergency legislation.

### Component 2: The Care Provider Track — Recognizing the Work Already Being Done

The Negative Income Tax addresses income. But for many caregivers, the income floor
alone is not what they most need. They need healthcare coverage when they lose their
job to provide care. They need their retirement record to reflect what they spent those
years doing. They need training that converts their care experience into credentials
the labor market recognizes. They need, in some cases, permission — a formal societal
acknowledgment that what they are doing is work, and that choosing to do it is not a
mistake.

This is where the Care Provider designation within the Civilian Reserve and Health
Program comes in. It works like this:

Any American who provides regular, substantive care for a family member — parent,
child, sibling, spouse, or in some cases a close community member — can register
formally as a care provider. Registration is simple: it requires documentation of
the care relationship and a basic assessment of care intensity. It does not require
the caregiver to be poor. It does not require them to have left paid employment.
It is a service recognition track, not an income support track.

Registration activates a package of supports that address the specific vulnerabilities
caregivers face:

**Healthcare coverage.** Registered care providers receive healthcare coverage through
the Civilian Reserve program. This closes the gap that Elena faced — the COBRA
problem, the insurance cliff that greets caregivers the moment they leave employment.

**A service recognition stipend.** Between $3,500 and $6,000 per year, calibrated
to care intensity, with higher payments for terminal care and care for children with
severe disabilities. This is not a replacement for the income caregivers give up —
it is a recognition that what they are doing has economic value, and that the economy
should say so explicitly.

**Training and credentials.** Caregivers who register gain access to training programs
that convert their experience into portable credentials. The caregiver who has spent
three years managing a parent's Alzheimer's progression has skills that are directly
applicable to paid positions in memory care facilities, home health agencies, and
elder care services. The training formalizes that expertise and creates an exit
pathway back to the paid economy — with a credential, not just a resume gap.

**Social Security caregiving credit.** This is the most valuable provision in the long
run. Registered care provider years are credited in the Social Security earnings record
at the national median wage, replacing the zeroes that currently appear there.

For Elena, that last provision means the difference between a retirement benefit penalized
by 14 months of zeroes and a benefit that accurately reflects a working life that included
14 months of intensive, economically valuable care. Over 20 years of retirement, that
difference can be $50,000 to $100,000.

### Component 3: The Growing Dividend — Long-Term Wealth Building for Everyone

The Negative Income Tax addresses immediate income. The Care Provider designation addresses
the caregiving gap. But there is a third component that addresses something neither of
those reaches: the long-run wealth-building capacity of ordinary Americans.

The American Sovereign Fund is a national investment fund — funded by a share of arms
sales revenue, tariff receipts, and dedicated tax streams — that invests in American
companies and infrastructure and distributes the returns as an annual dividend to every
American citizen who has held citizenship for 18 or more years.

The dividend starts small. In Year 15 after the fund is established, it pays approximately
$108 per eligible citizen per year. In Year 30, approximately $720. In Year 50, somewhere
above $2,000. These are not life-changing sums in isolation. But they are permanent,
growing, and universal — every eligible American receives them, not based on need, not
based on work history, but as a return on nationally owned assets that every citizen
has a stake in.

The Alaska Permanent Fund has operated on this model since 1982, funded by oil royalties
and paying between $1,000 and $3,000 per Alaskan per year. It is the most popular
government program in Alaska by a considerable margin. Politicians across the political
spectrum have defended it fiercely for four decades, because the constituency defending
it is everyone who receives it — which is everyone.

The American Sovereign Fund scales this nationally, funded not by a single resource
but by the ongoing revenues of American economic power: the proceeds of defense exports
to allies, the tariff revenue from trade policy, and the productivity surplus captured
from automation.

Over fifty years, as the dividend grows, it becomes the dominant income floor — not
a welfare program, not a redistribution mechanism, but a return on the national
assets that every citizen collectively owns. By the time the dividend reaches $2,000
per year, the Negative Income Tax's role has changed: it is no longer the primary floor,
it is the safety net beneath the dividend. The two programs together create an income
architecture that is structurally more durable than either alone.

## Who This Helps, in Concrete Terms

Abstract proposals become real when you can see the specific lives they change.

**Marcus, 26**, is keeping his brother Anthony alive. Anthony has severe depression
and post-traumatic stress following a workplace accident. He cannot be alone for
extended periods. Marcus works part-time as a barista — 16 hours a week, $10,000
a year — to be available for Anthony. Without the Negative Income Tax, Marcus has
no income support and no healthcare. With it, he receives $3,200 a year in monthly
payments and, through the Care Provider designation, a peer recovery specialist
certification. That certification is the beginning of a career in mental health
support. Marcus is not just being helped; he is being trained into a workforce the
country desperately needs. The informal crisis management he is providing prevents
an estimated two psychiatric hospitalizations per year at $20,000 each. The program
spends roughly $7,000 on Marcus; the healthcare system avoids $40,000 in hospitalization
costs.

**Dorothy, 62**, is retired, living on $14,400 a year in Social Security. Her daughter
Keisha is a registered nurse earning $68,000. Keisha's two children — ages 2 and 4 —
would cost $28,000 to $34,000 a year in commercial childcare. Dorothy provides that
care for free, keeping Keisha in the nursing workforce and keeping the family financially
stable. Dorothy is above the Negative Income Tax income threshold; she receives nothing.
The Care Provider designation changes this: she receives a $3,500 annual service stipend,
a childcare certification that could grow into a small licensed home-based operation,
and — most importantly — Social Security caregiving credit that partially compensates
for the retirement income penalty that decades of caregiving have imposed on her earnings
record. The program recognizes, for the first time, that Dorothy is doing something.

**Destiny, 17**, is one of 5.4 million young caregivers. Her mother was injured in a
workplace accident and has limited mobility. Her father is not present. Destiny manages
the morning routine for her two younger siblings, handles much of the household, and
provides direct personal care for her mother on the hardest days. She is at serious
risk of dropping out of school at 18 to work full-time. The programs, through a minor
track in the Care Provider designation, provide school accommodation coordination,
respite support that gives Destiny relief from caregiving responsibilities, and a
mentorship and college pathway program that positions her for post-secondary education
after graduating. The lifetime income difference for Destiny between graduating and
not graduating — between the care economy trap reproducing itself in the next
generation and being interrupted — is $300,000 to $500,000.

## How This Pays For Itself

The standard objection to any income support proposal is cost. It deserves a serious
answer.

The net new cost of the Negative Income Tax — after replacing existing means-tested
programs and eliminating their administrative overhead — is approximately $89 billion
per year. That is a real number. It is also a number that needs to be set against
what the current system costs when it fails.

The annual cost of informal caregiver breakdown — when care collapses and people
enter institutional settings earlier than they would have with sustained home support —
is estimated at over $200 billion in additional Medicaid and Medicare expenditure.
A psychiatric hospitalization that Marcus's informal support prevents costs $20,000
to $30,000. A memory care facility placement that Maria's caregiving delays by two
years saves $96,000 to $192,000. Elena's home hospice care, across fourteen months,
saved the healthcare system $462,000 to $630,000 compared to inpatient alternatives.

These are not exotic cases. They are the normal outcome when informal care collapses
because caregivers cannot sustain it economically. Every caregiver who leaves the care
role because they cannot afford to continue is a healthcare system expenditure waiting
to happen — and the healthcare system is a much more expensive provider than a
supported family member.

The $89 billion cost of the Negative Income Tax is partially offset by the Automation
Tax Credit — a levy on the productivity gains from large-scale AI deployment — which is
projected to generate $70 to $110 billion per year at full deployment. The logic is
deliberate: as automation replaces human labor at scale, some portion of the productivity
surplus it generates should flow back to the income floor that supports the workers
displaced by it. The program captures the gain and recirculates it as the guarantee.

This is the same logic as Social Security, funded by payroll taxes on the wages it
eventually replaces. It is the same logic as the highway trust fund, funded by the
gas tax on the driving that wears the roads. The funding mechanism is not a trick —
it is the correct structural response to the market failure it addresses.

The American Sovereign Fund dividend requires no net government expenditure after its
initial capitalization period, because it distributes investment returns rather than
appropriated funds. The dividend grows from national assets — it does not come from
taxes on individual incomes.

## The Political Innovation

Every previous attempt to establish a guaranteed income in the United States has failed
on politics before it reached economics. It has been attacked from the right as welfare
for the undeserving, and it has been attacked from the left as insufficient. These attacks
land because they are framing the proposal correctly within their own terms.

The care economy reframes everything.

Asking "should we pay people who don't work" is a question designed to fail politically.
It invokes the welfare recipient, the free rider, the undeserving poor.

Asking "should we recognize 53 million Americans who are doing economically essential
work that the market has failed to compensate" is a different question entirely. The
answer to that question is not ideological. Every political tradition in America, from
conservative to progressive, claims to value work and family. Every political tradition
claims to believe that people who contribute should receive something in return.

The 53 million caregivers are contributing. They are doing work that the healthcare
system depends on, that the labor market depends on, that the family structure depends
on. They are doing it for free, at significant personal cost, and the economy is penalizing
them for it in the most concrete possible terms: lower retirement income, lost healthcare,
interrupted careers, transferred poverty to their children.

Making the Negative Income Tax the income floor that enables care — rather than the
welfare program for the nonworking poor — changes who is defending it and who is
attacking it. Instead of a program defended only by its recipients (who are a minority,
and a stigmatized one), it becomes a program defended by every person in America who
has cared for a family member, who expects to, or who knows that without informal care
the healthcare system would collapse under its own cost.

That is not a minority. That is most of America.

There is also a conservative argument here that goes beyond the political calculation.
The Negative Income Tax, in the specific design proposed, eliminates the poverty trap
that means-tested programs create. It replaces a bureaucratic apparatus of caseworkers,
eligibility determinations, and program-specific restrictions with a simple mechanism:
income below the threshold, payment issued. No caseworker. No application. No stigma.
No cliff. Milton Friedman designed this proposal. The most efficient income support
system is a universal one.

## The Fifty-Year Vision

The programs described here are not primarily emergency responses to present conditions.
They are infrastructure — designed to function across fifty years, during which the
economy will change in ways that cannot be fully anticipated.

The Negative Income Tax grows with inflation, automatically. The American Sovereign Fund
dividend grows with national investment returns, compounding. The Care Provider designation
captures new categories of care work as they emerge — today it is Alzheimer's caregiving
and pandemic support; in thirty years it may be something the current moment cannot yet name.

The fifty-year trajectory looks like this. In Year 15, the American Sovereign Fund begins
paying dividends — small, around $100 per eligible citizen per year, but real. In Year 30,
the dividend has grown to roughly $720 per person. In Year 40, above $1,200. The dividend
supplements the Negative Income Tax for low-income recipients and supplements retirement
savings for middle-income ones. In Year 50, the income floor is no longer primarily a
government support program. It is primarily the return on a national investment fund that
every citizen owns a stake in. The framing has changed completely: from welfare to ownership.

This transition matters because ownership is politically indestructible in a way that
welfare is not. Social Security has survived ninety years of hostile political environments
because every working American has "paid into" it and perceives a personal claim on it.
The American Sovereign Fund dividend will be perceived the same way — not as a government
gift but as a return on an investment every citizen holds by virtue of citizenship itself.

The care economy makes the near-term case. The ownership economy makes the long-term case.
Together they are not a welfare program. They are the infrastructure of an economy that
can see all of its workers.

## What Would Have Been Different for Elena

Elena cared for her husband for fourteen months. She saved the healthcare system somewhere
between $462,000 and $630,000. She provided him with a death that was what he chose:
at home, surrounded by his family, cared for by someone who loved him.

In return, the economy gave her zeroes in her Social Security record, a COBRA bill she
could barely afford, and a job market that was not interested in a 59-year-old returning
after a gap.

With the programs proposed here, the story ends differently. Not dramatically differently —
she still leaves her job, she is still exhausted and grieving, Robert still dies. The care
is not made easy. But she has healthcare through the Care Provider registration. She has
a small stipend that prevents her from going into debt during the months when she is most
financially exposed. Her Social Security record credits the fourteen months at the national
median wage rather than at zero. When Robert dies, there is a bereavement transition period
before her obligations under the program end — six months of continued support while she
finds her footing.

And in Year 15, after the American Sovereign Fund has been operating for a decade and a
half, she receives a dividend check. Not large — about $100 that first year. But it is
addressed to her, and it is a return on the national economy that she spent her whole
working life contributing to. Including the fourteen months that the current system
cannot see.

The economy will not be able to thank Elena for what she did. But it can stop pretending
she didn't do it.
