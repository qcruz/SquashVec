# DED Asset Profile: Mississippi River System

**DED Score: 10 / Tier S**
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

| Field | Value |

## Why This Asset Scores 10

The Mississippi River system is not just important in one scenario — it is a structural dependency that appears in five separate DED scenarios. Its failure does not just reduce efficiency; it eliminates a mode of transport with no near-term substitute at equivalent scale.

**Freight moved on the Mississippi river system cannot be absorbed by road or rail:**
- Moving 500M tons/year via truck would require ~25 million additional truck trips/year
- Moving it via rail would require doubling Class I rail capacity in the affected corridors
- Neither is achievable in under 5 years; neither is close to the economics

This makes the river a **single point of failure for the bulk freight economy** — one of the few physical systems where disruption is both severe and unsubstitutable at short notice.

## Scenario-Specific Roles

### Drought
The river itself is the drought vulnerability. Low water levels — caused by reduced precipitation, aquifer depletion, and climate variability — directly reduce barge draft clearances, limiting cargo per trip and eventually halting navigation entirely.

**Reference events:**
- 2012 drought: Mississippi reached near-record low levels; barge traffic restricted for weeks; grain movement to export terminals slowed, contributing to global grain price spikes
- 2022 drought: Low water at Memphis and Vicksburg forced load reductions; marine insurers required partial-loads; per-bushel freight cost increased 3-4x
- Future risk: Climate models project more frequent and severe low-water events

**DED Response Role:**
- River water level monitoring as early warning indicator for food security risk
- Emergency dredging authority (USACE already has this; expand to increase response speed)
- Barge load factor standards under drought declaration

---

### Shipping Blockade
If ocean shipping lanes are disrupted (Suez, Panama Canal, or malicious blockade of U.S. coastal ports), the inland waterway system becomes the primary alternative for moving bulk commodities to export terminals or redistributing domestic supply.

**However:** This requires Gulf Coast ports (New Orleans, Baton Rouge, Memphis terminals) to remain operational. A shipping blockade combined with Gulf port disruption could completely sever bulk freight flow.

**DED Response Role:**
- Pre-plan domestic reallocation of export-bound grain to domestic processing in blockade scenario
- Identify and pre-position emergency barge capacity (contract options with private operators)
- Inland port activation plan: riverside terminals currently used only for export should have domestic distribution protocols

---

### Oil Restriction
Barge transport is the most oil-efficient freight mode per ton-mile (~500 ton-miles per gallon of fuel, vs. ~130 for rail and ~60 for truck). In an oil restriction, the river system's efficiency advantage becomes a strategic asset — it is the freight mode that should be maximized.

**DED Response Role:**
- Fuel allocation priority: towboat operators receive Tier 2 fuel allocation (essential freight)
- Modal shift incentives: temporary freight tax advantages for moving cargo to barge from truck
- Capacity expansion: emergency fast-track for new barge construction (steel availability permitting)

---

### Pandemic
River freight does not require large concentrations of workers at any single point (unlike port container terminals or food processing plants). Towboat crews are already isolated during multi-week trips. This makes river freight **more pandemic-resistant** than most alternatives.

**DED Response Role:**
- Early declaration of towboat crew as Tier 2 essential workers
- Crew isolation protocols (already largely in practice on river boats)
- Terminal automation to reduce dock worker concentration

---

### Financial Fragmentation
U.S. grain exports are priced and settled in USD via CME grain futures. If the dollar's reserve status is under pressure, maintaining grain export volume via the Mississippi system supports petro/agro-dollar demand.

**DED Response Role:**
- Grain export capacity maintenance as dollar-support mechanism
- Bilateral grain supply agreements with allied nations priced in USD

## Infrastructure Fragility Points

### Lock and Dam System
The Mississippi River navigation channel is maintained by 29 locks and dams on the upper river (Pool to Minneapolis) and free-flowing on the lower river (below St. Louis, maintained by dredging alone).

**Critical failure points:**

| Structure | Location | Risk | Failure Impact |
|-----------|----------|------|---------------|
| Lock and Dam 26 (Melvin Price) | Alton, IL | Age (1990 replacement; most-trafficked lock in world) | Severs upper/lower Mississippi connection; highest single-point risk |
| Lock and Dam 19 | Keokuk, IA | Age (original 1913); high traffic | Severs upper river navigation |
| Old River Control Structure | Simmesport, LA | Structural; if failed, Mississippi diverts to Atchafalaya River | Catastrophic: moves river channel away from New Orleans and Baton Rouge permanently |
| Bonnet Carré Spillway | St. Charles Parish, LA | Flood management structure | Failure or misoperation could flood New Orleans industrial corridor |

**The Old River Control Structure** deserves special attention. The Mississippi River has been naturally migrating toward the Atchafalaya course for decades. The USACE Old River Control Structure prevents this capture. If it were to fail (it nearly failed in the 1973 flood), the Mississippi would permanently shift its course, leaving New Orleans and Baton Rouge without river navigation. This would be the single most catastrophic permanent infrastructure failure in U.S. history.

### Dredging Dependency
The lower Mississippi (below St. Louis) has no locks — navigation depth is maintained entirely by continuous dredging. The USACE maintains a 45-foot navigation channel. Insufficient dredging budget leads to shoaling and reduced draft capacity.

**Current status:** USACE has a multi-year dredging maintenance backlog; dredge fleet is aging. This is a slow-moving fragility that compounds during drought years.

## DED Optimization Plan

### Current Use
- Commercial bulk freight: grain, fertilizer, coal, petroleum, steel
- Hydroelectric power (some dams)
- Recreation and tourism

### DED-Optimal Additions

1. **Strategic Grain Reserve Integration**
   - Contract with riverside grain elevator operators (see `industrial-capacity.md`) to maintain reserve stockpiles accessible to barge loading within 24 hours
   - Establish DED River Freight Coordinator role at Memphis as operational hub

2. **Emergency Fuel Depot Network**
   - Pre-designate 12 riverside locations (every ~100 miles on Mississippi/Ohio/Illinois) as emergency fuel depots
   - Federal reserve fuel contracts with riverside terminal operators

3. **Infrastructure Investment Mandate**
   - Old River Control Structure: $1-2B upgrade to extend operational life to 2100
   - Lock and Dam 26 redundancy: pre-design second lock chamber to eliminate single-lock constraint
   - Dredge fleet modernization: $500M investment in 4 new trailing-suction hopper dredges

4. **Low Water Early Warning Protocol**
   - River stage monitoring triggers DED Level 1 alert at 45-day rolling low; Level 2 at near-record low
   - Alert triggers automatic activation of cargo load-planning review and emergency dredging authorization

## Investment Case

| Investment | Cost | DED Benefit |
|-----------|------|------------|
| Old River Control Structure upgrade | $1.5B | Prevents catastrophic permanent course change; protects entire lower river indefinitely |
| Lock & Dam 26 redundant chamber | $800M | Eliminates single-point choke on busiest lock; maintains upper-lower connection |
| Dredge fleet modernization | $500M | Reduces low-water vulnerability; maintains navigation during drought years |
| Emergency fuel depot network | $200M | Tier 2 fuel access for river freight in oil restriction scenario |
| Strategic reserve integration contracts | $100M/year | Grain and freight buffer for drought, pandemic, blockade scenarios |
| **Total** | **~$3.1B + $100M/year** | **Protects ~$180B/year in freight value across 5 major scenarios** |

Return on investment: protecting $180B/year in freight value for $3.1B in infrastructure investment is a ratio better than almost any other DED infrastructure program.
