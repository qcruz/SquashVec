## Why This Asset Scores 10

The national laboratory network is the only DED asset that is relevant to every scenario in the registry. Its value is not just in what it can do today but in its **institutional capacity to develop solutions that do not yet exist**.

When a scenario produces a technical problem that the private sector cannot solve within the required timeline — because the risk is too high, the return is too low, or the knowledge base is classified — the national labs are the institution that fills the gap. They have done this repeatedly:

- Developed nuclear weapons and reactor designs (Manhattan Project, naval nuclear)
- Created the internet (ARPANET originated from DARPA; network research at national labs)
- Developed GPS (DARPA and labs)
- Produced the first lithium-ion battery improvements that enabled commercial EVs (Argonne)
- Developed mRNA vaccine stabilization chemistry foundational to COVID vaccines (NIH + national labs)
- Developed the photovoltaic efficiency improvements underlying modern solar panels (NREL)

The labs represent **accumulated human capital and physical infrastructure that cannot be rebuilt on any relevant timeline.** If ORNL's Materials Science & Technology Division were disbanded, it would take 20-30 years to reconstitute that knowledge base. This irreversibility is the core of the labs' strategic value.

## Key Lab Capabilities by DED Scenario

### Oil Restriction

**ORNL — Transportation Decarbonization:**
- Manufactures prototype carbon fiber and advanced composite materials for lightweight vehicles
- Houses the only U.S. facility for direct manufacturing of battery components at pilot scale
- Manufacturing Demonstration Facility: tests new production processes before industrial deployment

**NREL — Renewable Energy:**
- Full solar testing facility (outdoor and indoor); wind turbine blade testing
- Grid integration modeling; can simulate the entire U.S. grid under any generation mix
- Biofuel research: algae, cellulosic ethanol, sustainable aviation fuel

**Argonne — Battery Technology:**
- Joint Center for Energy Storage Research (JCESR): consortium of 5 labs + universities
- Developed NMC cathode chemistry now used in most EV batteries
- ANL-20, ANL-32 cell formats are industry standard references

**INL (Idaho National Laboratory) — Nuclear Energy:**
- Only U.S. facility authorized to test advanced reactor designs at full scale
- TREAT (Transient Reactor Test Facility): tests fuel behavior under accident conditions
- Advanced Test Reactor: breeds and tests fuel for next-generation reactors

**DED Use in Oil Restriction:** Fast-track NREL grid integration research to support emergency permitting; Argonne battery research accelerated under JCESR emergency program; ORNL manufacturing expertise applied to rapid factory conversion

---

### Pandemic

**NIH / BARDA partnership (not strictly DOE labs but federal R&D):**
- mRNA platform: NIH held key stabilization patents; Moderna licenses enabled rapid COVID vaccine
- BARDA: manages biodefense countermeasure development pipeline

**LLNL — Biosecurity:**
- Biodefense research: protein modeling, pathogen characterization
- NIF (National Ignition Facility): not directly relevant, but LLNL's modeling capability (high-performance computing for physical simulations) applies to epidemiological modeling
- LLNL Sierra supercomputer: second-fastest in U.S.; used for drug interaction modeling

**PNNL — Biosurveillance:**
- Environmental monitoring technology: air, water, soil sampling for biological agents
- Laboratory Response Network support: analytical methods for pathogen identification

**Argonne — Drug Characterization:**
- Advanced Photon Source: X-ray synchrotron used to determine 3D structure of proteins, viruses, drug targets
- During COVID-19, APS worked 24/7 to characterize SARS-CoV-2 protein structures — directly enabling drug development

**DED Use in Pandemic:** Argonne APS as emergency drug characterization facility; PNNL biosurveillance methods for enhanced wastewater monitoring; LLNL modeling for epidemic spread simulation and drug interaction screening

---

### Grid Cyberattack

**PNNL — Grid Cybersecurity (Primary):**
- Leads all DOE OE (Office of Electricity) cybersecurity research
- Developed VOLTTRON (open-source distributed energy management platform)
- Cyber range: simulated utility control environments for attack testing and defense
- Embedded analysts at utilities during high-threat periods (critical infrastructure information sharing)

**Sandia — Infrastructure Security:**
- Red team / blue team exercises on physical + cyber infrastructure
- Developed security standards for industrial control systems
- EMP and electromagnetic vulnerability research

**ORNL — Grid Modeling:**
- Real-time grid simulation (HELICS — Hierarchical Engine for Large-scale Infrastructure Co-Simulation)
- Advanced power electronics research; can model grid behavior under cyberattack scenarios

**INL — Critical Infrastructure Protection:**
- Idaho National Lab is the lead lab for critical infrastructure security research (DHS partnership)
- Operational Technology security for SCADA systems

**DED Use in Grid Cyberattack:** PNNL and Sandia deploy red teams to utilities pre-crisis; ORNL models grid recovery routing; INL provides SCADA defense protocols; combined rapid response team deployed within 24 hours of attack declaration

### Semiconductor Collapse

**Sandia — Microelectronics:**
- Advanced microelectronics research: alternative fabrication methods, radiation-hardened chips for defense
- Microsystems Engineering, Science and Applications (MESA): complete on-site fab for low-volume specialty chips
- This is the only U.S. facility that can produce some classes of defense microelectronics domestically — it is not scalable but it is a non-zero domestic fallback

**LLNL — Materials Fabrication:**
- Precision manufacturing at micro and nanoscale
- High-power laser systems that enable new deposition and patterning methods

**Argonne — Materials Characterization:**
- APS is essential for understanding semiconductor defect structures and new materials
- In a semiconductor collapse, Argonne becomes the primary diagnostic facility for understanding failure modes in remaining chips and developing alternative materials

**ORNL — Advanced Manufacturing:**
- Additive manufacturing of electronics at small scale
- Materials synthesis for semiconductor alternatives

**DED Use in Semiconductor Collapse:** Sandia MESA as emergency domestic production for defense-critical chips; Argonne APS for materials characterization; ORNL for alternative materials synthesis; combined program to identify which chip functions can be replaced with less-advanced domestic fabrication

---

### Rare Earth Denial

**Ames National Laboratory (Primary — unique):**
- The only national laboratory focused primarily on rare earth and critical materials research
- Developed the materials science of neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB) magnets — the most important rare earth application
- Currently researching rare-earth-free motor designs and magnet alternatives

**ORNL and Argonne — Material Substitution:**
- Critical Materials Institute (CMI): consortium led by Ames, with ORNL, Argonne, PNNL, and universities — specifically designed to solve rare earth dependency
- Developed processes for rare earth separation and purification that could be scaled domestically

**PNNL — Separation Chemistry:**
- Advanced separations research: solvent extraction processes for rare earth purification
- Could provide the technical basis for a domestic rare earth processing facility in 2-3 years vs. 10+ years to develop from scratch

**DED Use in Rare Earth Denial:** CMI immediately re-prioritized to emergency materials substitution; Ames rare-earth-free motor designs fast-tracked to manufacturing; PNNL separation chemistry licensed to Mountain Pass for domestic processing

## The Knowledge Preservation Problem

National labs face a structural problem that directly threatens their DED value: **the irreplaceable knowledge base retires with the workforce.**

Many national lab capabilities depend on individual scientists and engineers who have spent 20-40 years developing expertise in highly specialized domains. When they retire, the knowledge partially retires with them — institutional memory is imperfect.

**High-risk knowledge domains (near-term retirement wave):**
- Nuclear reactor design (SL-1 accident analysis; historical test reactor data)
- Chemical weapons defense (aging specialists from Cold War era)
- High-altitude nuclear effects (atmospheric test data analysis)
- Certain rare earth chemistry (first-generation CMI researchers)

**DED Action — Knowledge Preservation Program:**
- Systematic knowledge capture: video interviews, technical documentation, apprenticeship requirements before retirement
- Budget: $200M/year across all 17 labs for structured knowledge preservation and transfer
- Incentive: retirement bonuses for lab staff who complete structured knowledge transfer protocols
- Target: zero loss of critical technical capability to attrition

## DED Optimization: From Research to Response

The labs' biggest structural weakness as a DED asset is **the gap between research and deployment.** Labs develop solutions but often lack the authority and funding to push them to adoption at scale. NREL has developed the world's most efficient solar cells — but manufacturing technology transfer to industry takes 5-10 years through normal channels.

**DED Fast-Transfer Program:**
- DED R&D Deployment Officer embedded at each of 8 highest-priority labs
- Authority to issue emergency IP licenses to commercial manufacturers during declared scenarios
- Co-investment budget: $500M/year for lab-to-industry transfer on DED-prioritized technologies
- Target: time from lab demonstration to commercial-scale deployment reduced from 7-10 years to 2-4 years for DED-critical technologies
