## Why This Asset Scores 9

The SPR is the DED's most direct model and its most important physical analogue. It is the
proof of concept that **government-held physical commodity reserves can materially buffer supply
disruptions** — the same logic the DED applies to large power transformers, rare earth
stockpiles, semiconductors, and (prospectively) space-extracted platinum group metals.

The SPR is not primarily an energy tool — it is a **time-buying instrument**. It does not solve
an oil restriction. It creates a window of 30–90+ days during which the economy can adapt:
contracts can be renegotiated, alternative suppliers engaged, conservation measures deployed,
and demand reduced before the full cost of the disruption is felt.

The DED's instrument design borrows directly from the SPR model:
- Pre-positioned physical stockpile → applied to transformers, rare earths, chips, protein
- Government anchor buyer driving domestic production → applied to semiconductors, cultivated meat
- Release triggered by declared emergency → applied to all DED strategic reserves
- IEA coordinated release mechanism → applied to DED allied sharing frameworks

**Why it scores 9 and not 10:**
- Relevant to 4 scenarios (not all 9) — lower scenario breadth than National Labs or Mississippi River
- Optimization gap exists but is partially addressable (inventory replenishment is politically
  contested, not technically blocked)

## Physical Configuration

### Salt Cavern Storage

The SPR uses natural salt formations — the most secure, lowest-cost bulk liquid storage
technology available. Key properties:

- **Self-sealing:** Salt flows plastically under pressure, healing micro-fractures
- **Impermeable:** Crude oil cannot escape; no liner required
- **Inert:** Salt does not react with petroleum hydrocarbons
- **Deep:** Caverns at 2,000–4,000 feet depth; immune to surface disruption
- **Large:** Individual caverns hold 6–35 million barrels; effectively unlimited within the salt
  formation

Salt cavern construction cost: approximately $2–3/barrel of capacity — the cheapest large-scale
storage solution in existence. Replacement cost of the 4 existing sites is estimated at
$20–25B.

### Drawdown and Distribution Infrastructure

- Pipeline connections to the major Gulf Coast refining complexes
- Maximum drawdown rate: 4.4 million barrels/day
- **Limitation:** Distribution is geographically concentrated. The SPR is connected to Gulf
  Coast pipelines and refineries — it serves regions connected to that grid effectively, but
  a disruption to Gulf Coast pipeline infrastructure simultaneously with an oil supply
  disruption could impair release logistics

## Scenario-Specific Roles

### Oil Restriction (Primary Scenario)

The SPR is the Phase 1 response instrument for oil restriction. On declaration, DOE is
authorized to release up to 30 million barrels under emergency authority (Energy Policy Act 2005),
or coordinate with IEA members for a coordinated release.

**Historical deployments:**
| Year | Event | Release Volume | Impact |
|------|-------|---------------|--------|
| 1991 | Gulf War / Operation Desert Storm | 17.3M bbl (sold); 33.8M bbl authorized | Crude prices fell ~$10/bbl within days of announcement |
| 2005 | Hurricane Katrina | 11M bbl loan + 9.8M bbl sale | Buffered Gulf Coast refinery outage |
| 2011 | Libya disruption (IEA coordination) | 30M bbl (60M bbl IEA total) | Offset ~3 months of Libyan production loss |
| 2022 | Russia-Ukraine; inflation response | 180M bbl over 6 months (largest in history) | Contributed to ~$20/bbl price reduction; inventory fell to 40-year low |

**Key lesson from 2022:** Large releases reduce price pressure effectively but at the cost of
inventory. The SPR fell from ~600M bbl to ~365M bbl — reducing the buffer window from ~90 days
of U.S. import coverage to ~55 days. Replenishment has been slow due to political contestation
over when oil prices are "low enough" to justify buying.

**DED Phase 1 role:**
- Coordinated IEA release on Tier 1 declaration
- Domestic allocation contracts to priority sectors (Phase 1 triage)
- Price cap on released crude to prevent speculative arbitrage
- DED NEID tracks SPR inventory level as a leading indicator of Phase 1 response capacity

### Financial Fragmentation

When dollar-denominated trade breaks down, the SPR provides:
- **Commodity-backed payment alternative:** SPR crude can be used as collateral or direct
  payment in bilateral energy agreements — converting the reserve into a diplomatic instrument
- **Price buffer:** Coordinated release depresses oil prices during a period when rising energy
  costs could accelerate the economic fragmentation
- **Allied credibility:** U.S. commitment to release SPR under IEA coordination framework
  strengthens allied confidence in U.S. as economic partner during financial system stress

### Shipping Blockade

A Strait of Hormuz or Red Sea blockade affecting energy shipping creates a physical oil supply
disruption even when financial systems function normally:
- SPR provides the same time-buying function as in the oil restriction scenario
- DED coordinates with DOE on drawdown rate that matches the pace of alternative supply development
- Pipeline and Jones Act coastal shipping serve as the domestic distribution fallback if
  Gulf of Mexico supply is disrupted

### Grid Cyberattack

An extended grid outage creates a fuel crisis — diesel and natural gas generation increases
dramatically, and diesel distribution relies on functional fuel distribution infrastructure
that itself requires power:
- SPR crude must be converted to diesel and gasoline at refineries that may themselves be
  operating on emergency power
- DED pre-positions priority refinery contracts for emergency SPR crude processing
- Diesel allocation to power generation (hospitals, water treatment, emergency services)
  is the immediate Phase 1 consumption priority

## Current Vulnerabilities

### Inventory Depletion

The 2022–23 drawdown left the SPR at its lowest level since 1983. At ~365 million barrels:
- Maximum drawdown provides approximately 55 days of import coverage (vs. 90+ days at peak)
- The buffer against a sustained Tier 2 oil restriction is materially reduced
- **DED position:** The SPR is an underinvested asset. The "buy low" window — when oil prices
  are depressed and replenishment is cheap — has repeatedly been missed due to political
  pressure to keep gasoline prices low now rather than rebuild the strategic reserve.

**Replenishment cost analysis:**
At $70/bbl (2025 approximate WTI), restoring 200M barrels = ~$14B
The expected value of that reserve against a single Tier 2 oil restriction event (estimated
$200–500B economic cost) is strongly positive — the replenishment math is compelling at
any oil price below ~$120/bbl.

**DED recommendation:** Establish an **SPR Automated Replenishment Rule** — mandatory
incremental purchases when WTI falls below a 5-year moving average, funded by a dedicated
appropriation outside the annual budget process (same logic as the Social Security trust fund
or the Highway Trust Fund). Removes the political economy obstacle to buying at the right time.

### Geographic Concentration

All 4 SPR sites are in the Gulf Coast. A scenario involving:
- Major hurricane (Katrina-class) destroying Gulf Coast infrastructure
- Coordinated attack on Gulf Coast energy infrastructure
- Regulatory change disrupting Gulf Coast refining

...simultaneously degrades both the SPR distribution infrastructure and alternative supply sources.

**DED recommendation:** Evaluate a **Distributed SPR Expansion** — 2 additional sites in
mid-continent and Northeast locations, with pipeline connections to those regions' refinery
complexes. Would cost an estimated $5–10B for 100M barrels of additional capacity and would
materially reduce geographic concentration risk.

### Crude Quality Mix

The SPR holds primarily sour crude (high sulfur). The U.S. refining system has shifted
increasingly toward lighter, sweeter crude. In a supply disruption, the SPR's sour crude
inventory may not be directly substitutable for the light crude that Gulf Coast facilities
are now optimized to process.

**DED recommendation:** Diversify SPR crude mix toward light sweet crude over time through
routine inventory management; does not require large capital investment.

## DED Pre-Positioned Instruments

### Existing Authority
- **Emergency drawdown:** DOE authorized up to 30M bbl without congressional action
- **IEA coordination:** Standing agreement for coordinated release with IEA member nations
- **Exchange authority:** Can loan crude to refiners facing temporary supply shortfalls (repaid in kind)
- **Test sale authority:** Up to 5M bbl/year for operational testing

### DED Enhancements Proposed
1. **SPR Automated Replenishment Rule:** Mandatory purchase when WTI < 5-year moving average;
   $3–5B annual authorization; removes political obstacle to buying at efficient prices
2. **Distributed SPR expansion:** 2 new sites (mid-continent, Northeast); 100M bbl additional
   capacity; $5–10B capital; reduces geographic concentration
3. **Priority allocation contracts (Type A):** Pre-committed framework for Phase 1 triage
   allocation of released crude to priority refinery → priority sector pipeline; prevents
   released crude from being captured by financial speculators
4. **Commodity-backed payment framework integration:** SPR crude as collateral in DED bilateral
   payment agreements (Financial Fragmentation scenario Phase 1)
5. **Emergency refinery contracts:** Pre-committed refinery capacity for rapid SPR crude
   processing during grid or infrastructure disruption; prevents bottleneck at the crude→product
   conversion step

## The SPR as DED Design Template

The SPR is not just an asset — it is the proof of concept for the DED's core approach to
supply chain resilience. The arguments that justified the SPR in 1975 (after the 1973 Arab
oil embargo) are identical to the arguments that justify DED strategic reserves for rare earths,
large power transformers, semiconductors, and pharmaceuticals:

1. **The supply chain for a critical input is geopolitically controlled by a potential adversary**
2. **Domestic production cannot be ramped on a timeline that prevents crisis-level damage**
3. **A pre-positioned reserve buys the time needed for alternatives to come online**
4. **The cost of maintaining the reserve is small relative to the expected cost of the crisis
   it buffers**

The U.S. built the SPR and then used it repeatedly, successfully, to buffer oil supply disruptions.
The DED applies the same logic — now well-validated — to the 21st century's critical inputs:
critical minerals, semiconductor chips, precision manufacturing equipment, large power transformers.

The SPR is the DED's founding precedent.
