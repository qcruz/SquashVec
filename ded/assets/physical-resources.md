## Freshwater Systems

### Ogallala Aquifer
**Location:** 8 states (TX, OK, KS, NE, CO, WY, SD, NM) | **Type:** Groundwater system

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Drought, oil restriction (agriculture), pandemic (food), AI unemployment (food security) |
| Criticality | 3 | Feeds ~30% of U.S. groundwater irrigation; no substitute at this scale |
| Adaptability | 1 | Fixed physical resource; limited repurposing |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Extraction rate 3-5x recharge rate; depletion is irreversible; DED-optimal = managed drawdown |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**Current Use:** Irrigates ~15M acres of cropland; primary water source for corn, wheat, cotton, sorghum in High Plains
**DED Concern:** At current depletion rates, large portions will become economically unviable within 25-50 years — this is a slow-moving crisis that accelerates catastrophically under drought scenario
**Optimization Gap:** No federal management authority exists over state groundwater rights; DED needs aquifer management law, metered extraction, recharge investment programs
**Scenario Intersections:** Drought (primary), oil restriction (fertilizer → food), pandemic (food supply stability)

### Great Lakes System
**Location:** IL, IN, MI, MN, OH, WI, NY, PA (+ Canada) | **Type:** Surface freshwater

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Drought, pandemic (water treatment), grid cyberattack (cooling water for plants), industrial |
| Criticality | 2 | Contains 21% of world's surface freshwater; large buffer but regional dependency is extreme |
| Adaptability | 2 | Water, shipping, industrial cooling, potential desalination source for regional distribution |
| Optimization Gap | 1 | Great Lakes Compact (2008) provides some governance; gap is in long-term climate modeling and withdrawal limits |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |

**Current Use:** Drinking water for 30M+ people; industrial cooling; Great Lakes shipping corridor
**Optimization Gap:** Compact doesn't fully account for climate-driven level variability; DED needs contingency withdrawal protocols and industrial cooling alternatives pre-planned

### Colorado River System
**Location:** CO, UT, WY, NV, AZ, CA, NM | **Type:** Surface water / reservoir system

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Drought (primary), oil restriction (agriculture), AI unemployment (Southwest food) |
| Criticality | 3 | Lake Mead and Lake Powell at historically low levels; compact over-allocates water relative to actual flow |
| Adaptability | 2 | Hydroelectric generation + water supply; can shift allocation under emergency authority |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | 1922 Colorado River Compact allocates 17.5 MAF/year; actual average flow ~12-14 MAF; structural deficit with no binding resolution mechanism |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

**Current Use:** Water for 40M people across 7 states and Mexico; 4.5M acres irrigated; 4,200 MW hydropower
**DED Concern:** Compact renegotiation and agricultural-to-municipal reallocation authority must be pre-built before crisis
**Optimization Gap:** No emergency federal allocation override authority; current interstate compact framework will collapse under severe drought without pre-negotiated contingency

## Mineral Resources

### Rare Earth Deposits (Mountain Pass, CA + Federal Lands)
**Location:** San Bernardino County, CA (primary); also IA, WY, ID federal land deposits | **Type:** Critical minerals

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Rare earth denial, semiconductor collapse, grid cyberattack (grid hardware), defense/AI |
| Criticality | 3 | Mountain Pass is only operating U.S. rare earth mine; ore goes to China for processing — circular dependency |
| Adaptability | 1 | Fixed geological resource; value is in building domestic processing, not repurposing the mine |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Mine produces ore but sends to China for separation; no domestic rare earth separation capacity at commercial scale |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**Current Use:** Mountain Pass produces ~15% of global rare earth ore (mostly cerium, lanthanum); no domestic separation
**Key Assets:** MP Materials (Mountain Pass operator); USGS identified deposits in WY, ID, MT with no active mining
**Optimization Gap:** $2-5B federal investment in domestic separation and processing would eliminate the circular dependency; DoD has begun small-scale programs but far below needed scale

### Lithium Deposits (Nevada / Thacker Pass)
**Location:** Humboldt County, NV (primary); also Clayton Valley, NV; Kings Mountain, NC deposit | **Type:** Battery mineral

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Oil restriction (EV transition), grid cyberattack (grid storage), rare earth denial, AI (data center UPS) |
| Criticality | 2 | U.S. has large lithium reserves but processing is nascent; world supply is not scarce but U.S. domestic supply is underdeveloped |
| Adaptability | 1 | Fixed resource; value is in extraction and processing infrastructure |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Thacker Pass permitted (Lithium Americas); Kings Mountain (Albemarle) being developed; far short of domestic EV demand |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |

**Current Use:** Minimal domestic production; ~95% of U.S. lithium imported
**Optimization Gap:** Federal mining permitting reform + offtake guarantees needed to accelerate domestic lithium to 20-30% of domestic demand by 2030

### Appalachian Coal Reserves
**Location:** WV, KY, VA, PA | **Type:** Fossil fuel / industrial feedstock

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Oil restriction (coal-to-liquid synthesis), grid cyberattack (backup generation), steel production |
| Criticality | 1 | Substitutable via other fossil fuels and renewables for most uses; CTL is the unique DED value |
| Adaptability | 2 | Coal-to-liquid (CTL) technology can produce diesel, jet fuel; existing mines can be rapidly ramped |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | CTL infrastructure essentially nonexistent in U.S.; value is in pre-building pilot-scale CTL capacity as oil restriction bridge fuel |
| **DED Score** | **7** | **Tier A** |

**Current Use:** ~500M tons/year coal production; primarily thermal (power generation) and metallurgical (steel)
**DED-Optimal:** Maintain metallurgical coal as steel feedstock; develop CTL pilot capacity (1-3 facilities) as oil restriction bridge; do not expand thermal coal (faster renewable substitution path)

### Phosphate Deposits (Florida / Idaho)
**Location:** Central Florida (Bone Valley); SE Idaho (Phosphoria Formation) | **Type:** Agricultural mineral

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Drought (fertilizer under water stress), oil restriction (fertilizer for food supply), pandemic (food security) |
| Criticality | 3 | U.S. is world's third largest phosphate producer; domestic agriculture depends on it entirely; no substitute for phosphorus in biology |
| Adaptability | 1 | Fixed resource; cannot be synthesized; recycling from wastewater is emerging but not at scale |
| Optimization Gap | 1 | Florida production is near-optimal; long-term concern is depletion (estimated 25-30 year reserves at current extraction) |
| **DED Score** | **7** | **Tier A** |

**DED Concern:** Phosphorus is genuinely irreplaceable in agriculture. U.S. is currently a net exporter — this is strategically sound. Long-term risk is reserve depletion; DED needs phosphorus recycling investment program and reserve management.
