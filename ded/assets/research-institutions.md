# DED Asset Registry: Research Institutions

**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Category:** Research Institutions — national laboratories, universities, federal R&D, private research

The strategic value of research institutions in a DED context is distinct from academic prestige rankings. The relevant question is: *when a scenario unfolds, which institutions have the knowledge, equipment, and human capital to develop solutions that cannot be improvised?*

---

## National Laboratory Network
**See also:** `profiles/national-labs.md`

The 17 DOE national laboratories are the most strategically concentrated research infrastructure in the world. They combine:
- Heavy physical infrastructure (nuclear reactors, particle accelerators, high-performance computing)
- Institutional knowledge accumulated over decades
- Security clearances enabling classified defense and infrastructure work
- Deep government relationships enabling rapid pivot to emergency priorities

### Individual Lab Profiles

---

#### Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL)
**Location:** Oak Ridge, TN | **Operator:** UT-Battelle for DOE

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Grid cyberattack (grid security research), oil restriction (advanced materials, nuclear), pandemic (biosurveillance), semiconductor (materials science) |
| Criticality | 3 | Only facility with Spallation Neutron Source + High Flux Isotope Reactor; world-leading in materials characterization |
| Adaptability | 3 | Broadest mission portfolio of any national lab; nuclear, computing, biology, materials, manufacturing |
| Optimization Gap | 1 | Well-funded and well-directed; gap is in faster technology transfer to industry |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

**Key capabilities:** Manufacturing Demonstration Facility (additive manufacturing, composites); Grid cybersecurity (ORNL is primary DOE partner for grid security); Isotope production (medical and industrial); Summit/Frontier supercomputers for simulation

## University Research Clusters

Universities are distinct from national labs in that their value is more diffuse — no single university is a critical single point of failure. But certain university concentrations represent irreplaceable knowledge nodes.

---

### MIT + Boston University + Harvard (Boston Cluster)
**Location:** Cambridge / Boston, MA

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Pandemic (biomedical), semiconductor (MIT materials, MIT Lincoln Lab), grid cyberattack (MIT CSAIL), oil restriction (MIT Energy Initiative) |
| Criticality | 2 | Important but distributed; MIT Lincoln Lab is the highest-criticality component (defense R&D) |
| Adaptability | 3 | Highest cross-domain adaptability of any U.S. university cluster |
| Optimization Gap | 1 | Well-connected to federal programs; gap is in harder industrial application partnerships |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**MIT Lincoln Laboratory** (unique asset within cluster): Federally-funded R&D center for DoD; radar, sensor, cybersecurity, communications; cannot be replicated without decades of institutional knowledge accumulation.

---

### Stanford + SLAC (Silicon Valley Cluster)
**Location:** Palo Alto / Menlo Park, CA

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Semiconductor collapse (Stanford engineering, SLAC synchrotron), grid cyberattack (Stanford cybersecurity), AI unemployment (AI safety research) |
| Criticality | 2 | SLAC National Accelerator is unique; Stanford semiconductor research is world-leading but not single-point |
| Adaptability | 3 | Deep industry integration; fastest translation of research to deployment of any cluster |
| Optimization Gap | 1 | Well-funded; gap is in national security prioritization |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |

---

### Big Ten Land Grant Universities (IA State, Purdue, Ohio State, Michigan State, Wisconsin)
**Location:** Midwest

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Drought (agricultural science), pandemic (food system + biosurveillance), oil restriction (crop science, biofuels), AI unemployment (rural workforce training) |
| Criticality | 2 | Critical agricultural research base; USDA agricultural research stations co-located; no single campus is irreplaceable but the cluster collectively is |
| Adaptability | 3 | Extension network reaches every county in their states; fastest research-to-farm-field translation in the world |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | DED should pre-contract emergency research programs with these institutions (e.g., drought-resistant crop development, disease-resistant livestock genetics); currently ad-hoc |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

**DED Value:** The agricultural university extension system is the most valuable knowledge-distribution infrastructure in U.S. agriculture. In a drought or pandemic affecting food supply, these institutions can deliver technical guidance to millions of farmers within weeks. No federal agency can replicate this.

## Federal R&D Agencies

### DARPA (Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency)
**Location:** Arlington, VA | **See also:** `profiles/federal-rd.md`

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | All scenarios — DARPA's mission is breakthrough solutions to seemingly intractable problems |
| Criticality | 3 | Created the internet, GPS, stealth, mRNA vaccine platform (partially), autonomous vehicles; irreplaceable institutional model |
| Adaptability | 3 | By design, DARPA can pivot to any domain within months |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Budget ~$4B/year; DED could benefit from DARPA program managers co-assigned to each DED scenario as rapid R&D leads; no current mechanism for this |
| **DED Score** | **11** | **Tier S** (adjusted cap: 10) |

---

### ARPA-E (Energy)
**Location:** Washington, DC

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Oil restriction (energy breakthrough), grid cyberattack (grid resilience tech) |
| Criticality | 2 | Small agency (~$450M/year) with outsized impact; has funded several technologies now entering commercial deployment |
| Adaptability | 3 | Modeled on DARPA; can spin up programs in any energy technology within a few months |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Budget is 1/10th of what independent assessments recommend for the energy challenge; Congress has repeatedly tried to eliminate it |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

---

### CDC / Public Health Infrastructure
**Location:** Atlanta, GA (CDC HQ) + state and local public health departments

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Pandemic (primary), rare chemical/industrial events |
| Criticality | 3 | The U.S. public health infrastructure at the state and local level is the first-responder system for pandemic; it was badly underfunded pre-2020 |
| Adaptability | 2 | CDC can redirect to biosurveillance, environmental monitoring, disaster health; state labs can process any biological sample |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | State and local public health funding was cut 20-30% in the decade before COVID; the infrastructure that failed in 2020 has only partially recovered |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |
