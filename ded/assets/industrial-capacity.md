## Energy Processing

### Gulf Coast Refinery Complex
**Location:** TX, LA (PADD 3) | **Type:** Petroleum refining | **See also:** `profiles/gulf-coast-refinery.md`

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Oil restriction (primary conversion node), industrial (petrochemical feedstock), shipping blockade (import disruption) |
| Criticality | 3 | ~55% of U.S. refining capacity; if Gulf Coast refining is disrupted, national fuel supply collapses within weeks |
| Adaptability | 2 | Refineries can adjust product slate; crude flexibility (can process domestic, Canadian, various grades); some can pivot to petrochemicals |
| Optimization Gap | 1 | Near DED-optimal for current function; gap is in crude-grade flexibility assessments and product-slate emergency protocols |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**Key Facilities:** Motiva Port Arthur (~600k bbl/day, largest in U.S.); ExxonMobil Beaumont; Valero Port Arthur; LyondellBasell Houston
**DED Concern:** Geographic concentration creates single-region catastrophic failure risk (hurricane, grid attack, labor strike). Pre-positioned mutual aid agreements and standby staffing are the main gap.

### Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR)
**Location:** Bryan Mound, Big Hill, West Hackberry, Bayou Choctaw (TX/LA) | **Type:** Crude oil storage
**See also:** `profiles/strategic-petroleum-reserve.md`

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Oil restriction (primary buffer), pandemic (economic stability), industrial (feedstock bridge) |
| Criticality | 3 | Only federal crude reserve; ~370M barrels; ~20-25 days of full U.S. consumption |
| Adaptability | 1 | Single-purpose storage; limited adaptability |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | SPR was drawn down to ~40-year lows 2021-2023; rebuilding slowly; optimal reserve is 700M+ barrels; current level is insufficient for a sustained Tier 2+ restriction |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**Optimization Gap:** At $70-80/barrel, rebuilding from 370M to 700M barrels costs ~$24B. Congress has repeatedly sold SPR reserves for budget offsets — structural policy fix needed.

### Nuclear Power Fleet
**Location:** 93 reactors at 54 sites, concentrated in Southeast, Midwest, Northeast | **Type:** Zero-carbon baseload electricity

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Oil restriction (electricity substitution), grid cyberattack (hardened baseload), pandemic (zero-fuel supply chain needed) |
| Criticality | 3 | Provides 19% of U.S. electricity; zero oil required; most reliable dispatchable power source (90%+ capacity factor) |
| Adaptability | 2 | High-temperature reactors can produce hydrogen; waste heat can power industrial processes; sites reusable for SMR deployment |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Several plants facing retirement due to economics, not safety; federal subsidy to retain fleet is extremely high-value per MW |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

**Key Assets:** Palo Verde (AZ, 3.9 GW — largest in U.S.); Peach Bottom (PA); Browns Ferry (AL); Braidwood (IL)
**Optimization Gap:** A single nuclear plant that retires permanently cannot be restarted — the loss is permanent. Every plant kept online during the energy transition is a DED asset preserved at very low marginal cost relative to replacement value.

## Food and Agricultural Processing

### Meatpacking Complex (Iowa / Nebraska / Kansas)
**Location:** IA, NE, KS, MN, TX (Panhandle) | **Type:** Protein processing

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Pandemic (labor vulnerability), oil restriction (transport + refrigeration energy) |
| Criticality | 3 | Top 5 facilities process ~25-30% of U.S. beef; top 4 companies control ~85% of U.S. beef packing |
| Adaptability | 1 | Highly specialized physical plant; limited repurposing |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Extreme concentration creates catastrophic single-point failure; DED-optimal = geographic distribution, automation, regional processing backup |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |

**Key Facilities:** JBS Greeley CO; Tyson Dakota City NE; National Beef Liberal KS; Cargill Schuyler NE
**DED Action:** Reserve contracts with 5 mid-scale regional processors in each geographic quadrant to surge capacity when a mega-plant is disrupted; automation grants (see pandemic/phase-2)

### Grain Elevator and Storage Network
**Location:** Nationwide; highest density in IA, IL, KS, NE, MN | **Type:** Grain storage and distribution

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Drought (crop storage + distribution buffer), pandemic (food supply stabilization), oil restriction (food supply chain) |
| Criticality | 2 | ~20B bushels of on-farm and commercial storage capacity; provides 6-12 month buffer for grain supply |
| Adaptability | 2 | Storage can hold multiple grain types; infrastructure can be repurposed for strategic food reserve |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Commercial storage is privately owned and commercially motivated; DED has no authority to mandate reserve maintenance or access during emergency |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**Optimization Gap:** USDA commodity programs can partially reserve storage, but there is no standing federal strategic grain reserve comparable to the SPR. Establishing reserve contracts with top 100 elevator operators is a high-value, low-cost intervention.

### Fertilizer Production (CF Industries, Mosaic, Nutrien U.S. Plants)
**Location:** IA (CF Industries), FL (Mosaic phosphate), multiple | **Type:** Agricultural chemical

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Oil restriction (natural gas feedstock for nitrogen), drought (fertilizer efficiency under water stress) |
| Criticality | 3 | U.S. corn yield is directly dependent on nitrogen fertilizer; a 50% fertilizer supply reduction could cut yields by 30-40% within one growing season |
| Adaptability | 1 | Facilities are highly specialized; slow to repurpose |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Natural gas is the primary input for nitrogen fertilizers (Haber-Bosch); green hydrogen Haber-Bosch is the substitution path; no U.S. commercial green ammonia facility at scale yet |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |

**DED-Optimal:** Pre-build 2-3 green ammonia pilot facilities; establish 90-day strategic fertilizer reserve in conjunction with grain elevator network

## Defense and Strategic Manufacturing

### Shipbuilding Industrial Base
**Location:** Newport News VA (HII); Bath ME (BIW); Pascagoula MS (HII Ingalls); San Diego CA (NASSCO) | **Type:** Naval and commercial shipbuilding

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Shipping blockade (build response capacity), financial fragmentation (naval power projection), rare earth denial (defense supply chain) |
| Criticality | 3 | Only 4 facilities capable of building major combatants; 6-10 year lead times for destroyers/carriers; cannot surge quickly |
| Adaptability | 2 | Naval yards can produce commercial vessels, offshore wind infrastructure, barges, and maritime equipment |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Jones Act limits commercial fleet size; naval shipbuilding is chronically behind schedule; workforce pipeline is thin — 150,000 skilled trades workers needed, current pipeline adds ~10,000/year |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**Optimization Gap:** Dual-use investment: naval shipyard expansion also builds capacity for offshore wind installation vessels (currently nearly all Jones Act-exempt foreign vessels) and commercial shipbuilding capacity needed in blockade scenario

### Steel Production (Integrated Mills + EAF)
**Location:** Burns Harbor IN (Cleveland-Cliffs); Gary IN; Middletown OH; Nucor Charlotte NC; multiple EAF minimills | **Type:** Primary metals

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Shipping blockade (import substitute), grid cyberattack (grid hardware), rare earth denial (specialty steel alloys for defense) |
| Criticality | 2 | U.S. has significant domestic steel capacity (~80M tons/year); not a single-point failure but specialty grades (electrical steel, armor plate) have concentrated production |
| Adaptability | 2 | EAF minimills are highly flexible; can switch between rebar, structural, specialty grades with equipment investment |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Electrical steel (for EV motors and grid transformers) is heavily imported; only one domestic producer of grain-oriented electrical steel (Cleveland-Cliffs); this is a critical DED gap |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**Critical Gap:** Grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES) is essential for grid transformers and EV motors. U.S. has one domestic producer. In a grid cyberattack scenario requiring large transformer replacement, or an EV surge during oil restriction, this single-producer dependency is catastrophic.
**DED Action:** Federal loan guarantee to build a second GOES production facility; target output = 50% of projected demand by 2030

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Pandemic (drug supply), oil restriction (chemical feedstock), financial fragmentation (China supply cut-off) |
| Criticality | 3 | ~90% of generic APIs imported; domestic capacity for life-critical drugs is a national security gap |
