# DED Asset Registry: Human Capital

**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Category:** Human Capital — skilled workforce concentrations, training infrastructure, institutional knowledge

Human capital is the most adaptable and most fragile DED asset. Unlike a pipeline or a mine, skilled workers can retrain, relocate, and apply their expertise across scenarios. But institutional knowledge — the accumulated understanding of how a complex system actually works — can be permanently lost when a workforce generation retires or disperses.

The DED evaluates human capital along two axes: **concentration of rare skills** (where is the knowledge that cannot be quickly reproduced?) and **training infrastructure** (where can new essential workers be produced at scale and speed?).

## Rare Skills Concentrations

### Nuclear Operations Workforce
**Location:** 54 plant sites + DOE facilities + Navy nuclear program | **Count:** ~100,000 nuclear workers + 6,000+ Navy nuclear-trained personnel

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Oil restriction (nuclear power), grid cyberattack (plant resilience), pandemic (critical ops), financial fragmentation (energy independence) |
| Criticality | 3 | Nuclear plant operators require NRC licensing; 2-3 years to train; cannot be replaced with generalists; plant shutdown if staffing falls below NRC minimums |
| Adaptability | 2 | Navy nuclear veterans can transition to commercial plants; SMR deployment will need this workforce |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Workforce is aging (average age ~48); retirement wave imminent; university nuclear engineering programs are enrollment-flat; Navy pipeline is a partial offset |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

**DED Action:** Federal nuclear workforce pipeline program — scholarships for nuclear engineering + fast-track NRC licensing pathway + Navy-to-commercial transition incentives. Target: 20,000 new nuclear workers over 10 years.

### Grid Operations and Power Engineering
**Location:** 66 balancing authorities across U.S.; concentrated in ISO/RTO control rooms | **Count:** ~60,000 power engineers + ~30,000 licensed grid operators

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Grid cyberattack (primary responders), oil restriction (grid management under demand surge), pandemic (critical ops) |
| Criticality | 3 | Licensed power system operators cannot be replaced; grid control room staffing below minimum leads to mandatory generation curtailment |
| Adaptability | 1 | Highly specialized; limited cross-domain transfer |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Same aging demographic as nuclear; many experienced operators trained in analog systems retiring; transition to digital systems requires retraining |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |

### Long-Haul Truck Drivers (CDL Class A)
**Location:** Nationwide | **Count:** ~1.9M Class A CDL holders; ~900K actively driving long-haul

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Pandemic (freight continuity), oil restriction (fuel distribution), drought (food distribution), financial fragmentation (domestic supply chains) |
| Criticality | 3 | Virtually all manufactured goods in the U.S. touch a truck; driver shortage was already acute pre-pandemic |
| Adaptability | 2 | CDL skills transfer across cargo types; can operate tankers, flatbeds, refrigerated units with endorsement training |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Median age of long-haul driver is 55; chronic shortage of 60,000-80,000 drivers; industry turnover rate ~90%/year; pay and working conditions are the retention problem |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

**DED Action:** Federal CDL training grant program; mandatory minimum pay standards for long-haul drivers; driver retention incentive (federal match for carrier health insurance contribution); supervised autonomous convoy program to extend effective driver capacity

### Skilled Trades — Electricians, Pipefitters, Ironworkers
**Location:** Nationwide via union halls (IBEW, UA, Iron Workers) | **Count:** ~700K electricians, ~450K pipefitters, ~120K ironworkers

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Grid cyberattack (repair), oil restriction (grid buildout, renewable construction), pandemic (critical facility maintenance) |
| Criticality | 3 | No amount of engineering design matters if there are no electricians to install it; renewable energy buildout requires 2-3x current electrician workforce |
| Adaptability | 3 | Skilled trades knowledge transfers across sectors (residential → commercial → utility scale) |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Trade apprenticeship programs take 4-5 years; demand for electricians alone is forecast to exceed supply by 300,000+ by 2030 due to EV, renewable, and data center growth |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

**DED Action:** Emergency expansion of union apprenticeship programs with federal co-funding; compressed credentialing for helper-level work; military MOS integration (Army signal, Navy electricians → civilian credential bridge)

### Agricultural Extension Workers and Farm Operators
**Location:** Nationwide; 3,000+ county extension offices | **Count:** ~2M farm operators, ~10,000 extension agents

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Drought, pandemic, oil restriction (food supply) |
| Criticality | 2 | Extension agents are the critical knowledge-transfer link from research to field; farm operator institutional knowledge is irreplaceable per-farm but redundant at national scale |
| Adaptability | 3 | Extension network can deliver any information (pest management, water conservation, crop switching, emergency practices) to farm operators within weeks |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Extension workforce has declined ~40% since 1980 (state funding cuts); DED needs to re-invest and expand, especially in precision agriculture and drought-adaptive practices |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

### Medical and Public Health Workforce
**Location:** Nationwide; concentrated in major metros | **Count:** ~1M physicians, ~4.4M RNs, ~450K EMTs, ~300K public health workers

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Pandemic (primary), all scenarios (injury, illness, psychological support) |
| Criticality | 3 | Healthcare is the foundation that makes economic response possible; a pandemic that collapses healthcare has cascading effects on every other sector |
| Adaptability | 2 | Healthcare workers can be retrained across specialties; EMTs can support non-emergency medical transport |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Primary care and rural healthcare are chronically understaffed; psychiatry has a decade-long backlog; public health workforce (~300K) is far below recommended levels |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

### Semiconductor Process Engineers
**Location:** Hillsboro OR (Intel); Phoenix/Chandler AZ (TSMC/Intel); Albany NY (GlobalFoundries/IBM); Austin TX | **Count:** ~50,000 process engineers and technicians in U.S.

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Semiconductor collapse (primary), AI unemployment (context: AI infrastructure dependency) |
| Criticality | 3 | A semiconductor fab without qualified process engineers cannot operate; TSMC's reported reluctance to staff Arizona plants reflects this — the knowledge base is not portable |
| Adaptability | 1 | Highly specialized; limited transfer across industries |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | U.S. trains far fewer semiconductor engineers than South Korea, Taiwan, or China proportionally; visa and immigration policy restricts international talent pipeline |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |

## Training Infrastructure

### Community College Vocational Network
**Location:** ~1,000 institutions with vocational programs nationwide | **Type:** Workforce training infrastructure

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | All scenarios requiring rapid workforce retraining (pandemic, oil restriction, AI unemployment) |
| Criticality | 2 | Not irreplaceable per institution but collectively the largest vocational training infrastructure in the world |
| Adaptability | 3 | Can pivot curriculum within 6-12 months; wide geographic distribution; accessible to working adults |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Chronically underfunded; often lack modern equipment for high-skill trades training; DED emergency training programs should route through this network rather than building parallel infrastructure |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

**DED Use:** Community colleges are the fastest path to scaling essential worker training in any scenario. A federal DED training grant that pre-funds expanded capacity and curriculum for the top 20 essential skills is a one-time investment that pays across every scenario.

### Union Apprenticeship Programs (AFL-CIO / Building Trades)
**Location:** Nationwide via local union halls | **Type:** Skilled trades training

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | Oil restriction (construction workforce), grid cyberattack (repair workforce), pandemic (essential trades maintenance) |
| Criticality | 3 | Apprenticeship programs are the primary pathway for electricians, pipefitters, plumbers, ironworkers, operating engineers; these cannot be replaced by non-apprenticeship training on quality |
| Adaptability | 2 | Can add new modules (solar installation, EV charging, heat pump installation) to existing apprenticeships; 6-12 month curriculum integration |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | Apprenticeship completions are ~150,000/year; need to be ~300,000+/year by 2030 for renewable buildout alone |
| **DED Score** | **10** | **Tier S** |

---

### Military Veterans Transition Pipeline
**Location:** All military installations + VA systems | **Count:** ~200,000 veterans separating annually

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 3 | All scenarios — veterans represent cross-trained, disciplined, rapidly deployable human capital |
| Criticality | 2 | Not irreplaceable per individual but the pipeline is a consistent, high-quality labor supply |
| Adaptability | 3 | Military occupational specialties map directly to critical civilian roles: nuclear operators, electricians, mechanics, logistics, medical, IT |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | VA and DoD transition programs do not systematically steer veterans toward DED-critical occupations; better alignment would channel 20,000-40,000 veterans/year into nuclear, grid, healthcare, and skilled trades |
| **DED Score** | **9** | **Tier S** |

---

### Immigration and International Talent Pipeline
**Location:** U.S. visa and naturalization system

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Scenario Breadth | 2 | Semiconductor collapse (fab engineers), pandemic (healthcare workers), AI unemployment (counter: retraining for displaced workers) |
| Criticality | 2 | U.S. semiconductor fabs and hospitals depend significantly on immigrant engineers and workers; restrictive immigration reduces this supply |
| Adaptability | 2 | Policy-adjustable relatively quickly compared to physical infrastructure |
| Optimization Gap | 2 | EB-2/EB-3 visa backlogs of 5-10+ years for workers from India and China effectively eliminate this pipeline; DED-critical occupation exemptions would unlock high-value talent immediately |
| **DED Score** | **8** | **Tier A** |
