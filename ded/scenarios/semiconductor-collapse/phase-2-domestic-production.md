# Semiconductor Collapse — Phase 2: Domestic Production Build-Out

**Scenario:** Semiconductor Supply Collapse
**Phase:** 2 of 3
**Timeline:** 18 months – 7 years from declaration
**Trigger:** Phase 1 stabilization criteria met (see phase-1-triage-and-allocation.md);
  NSIVS aggregate inventory stabilized; mature node domestic capacity up ≥15% from pre-crisis
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 2 Objective

Phase 1 manages the allocation of constrained supply. Phase 2 eliminates the structural
vulnerability — near-total dependence on Taiwan for advanced chip production — by building
domestic capacity, coordinating allied industrial strategy, and funding the next-generation
lithography research that determines who controls semiconductor manufacturing after 2030.

The DED's role in Phase 2 is the same as its role elsewhere: pre-build the financial
conditions that make the right investment rational. It is not a fab operator. It is the
anchor buyer, the co-investor, the IP partner, and the workforce pipeline that makes
domestic semiconductor manufacturing economically viable without permanent subsidy.

**The timeline constraint:** Advanced node fabs take 4–5 years from groundbreaking to
production. High-NA EUV lithography machines cost $350–380M each and currently ship
at ~20/year from ASML's single production line. There is no short-term fix. Phase 2's
investment must begin in Phase 1's early months and run for 7–10 years to meaningfully
alter the supply landscape. Starting late is the only unrecoverable failure mode.

## Program 1: Advanced Node Domestic Investment Acceleration

### Building on the CHIPS Act Foundation

The CHIPS and Science Act (2022, $52B in awards) has established the first meaningful
domestic advanced node capacity since the 1990s. By late 2025:
- **TSMC Arizona Fab 1:** In production at 4nm/5nm, yield rates exceeding 92% — matching
  Taiwan facilities ahead of schedule
- **Intel Arizona (Fab 52):** 18A process in high-volume manufacturing — first U.S. facility
  surpassing the 2nm threshold
- **Samsung Texas:** 2026 target operational date (3nm class)
- **TSMC Arizona Fab 2:** 3nm production targeted 2027; Fab 3 equipment installation 2026

This represents the first 3–5% of U.S. advanced chip consumption from domestic sources —
significant progress from near-zero, but far short of supply security.

**The gap the DED must close:** Phase 2 target is **20% of U.S. advanced chip consumption
from domestic or allied fabs by 2035.** CHIPS Act alone is unlikely to reach this without
ongoing offtake commitment — the commercial economics of U.S. fab operation remain
challenging relative to Taiwan without government volume guarantees.

### DED Anchor Buyer Program

**Mechanism:** DED enters multi-year (10-year) volume commitment contracts with qualifying
domestic advanced node fabs, agreeing to purchase a defined annual volume of chips at a
defined at-production price — not at spot, not at below-cost subsidy, but at a price that
makes the fab's unit economics viable at planned scale.

These are not procurement contracts (DED does not consume chips). They are **offtake
guarantees**: DED commits to buy at the contract price; the fab commits to deliver.
If DED's designated priority sector buyers (DoD contractors, medical OEMs, grid equipment
manufacturers) purchase above the committed volume, DED's obligation reduces. If commercial
demand covers the volume, DED pays nothing. The guarantee reduces fab investment risk without
requiring a perpetual direct subsidy.

Structure modeled on: U.S. government's early semiconductor anchor buying in the 1960s
(DoD purchased >60% of U.S. chips, de-risking the entire industry's scale-up) and the
Operation Warp Speed advance purchase commitments that accelerated COVID vaccine manufacturing.

**DED Type B co-investment alongside fabs:**
In addition to offtake guarantees, DED enters Type B R&D contracts with each major fab
for process technology development at the next node:
- TSMC Arizona: DED co-investment in 2nm process development and transfer
- Intel: DED co-investment in Intel 14A (next-gen post-18A) development
- Samsung Texas: DED co-investment in 2nm-class process for Texas facility

IP developed under Type B contracts is jointly owned, with DED retaining a royalty-free
license and the IP entering the IP Asset Register as a strategic negotiating asset.

**Estimated DED commitment:**
- Offtake guarantees: $8–15B/year (contingent — activated only if commercial demand
  falls short; expected net cost below $2–4B/year as commercial demand fills most volume)
- Type B process R&D co-investment: $5–8B over 5 years across 3 major fabs
- Administrative and oversight: $200M/year

### Mature Node Domestic Continuation

Phase 1 activated mature node (28nm+) expansion via Type B contracts. Phase 2 deepens
this investment:
- DED extends Phase 1 mature node contracts for 5 additional years, locking in the
  capacity expansion
- Adds **Type C supply chain development contracts** for the specialized materials and
  equipment that domestic mature node fabs depend on: specialty gases, photomasks,
  CMP slurries, probe cards — most of which remain import-dependent despite U.S. fab
  construction

## Program 2: Allied Semiconductor Industrial Strategy

### The Concentration Problem Persists at Allied Level

Moving production from Taiwan to a mix of U.S., Japan, South Korea, and EU still creates
risks if those allied nations are not coordinating their investments. If Japan, South Korea,
and the U.S. all build advanced logic capacity but no allied nation builds advanced memory
(DRAM, HBM), or if all build different logic nodes with no specialization agreement, allied
production remains fragmented and commercially unviable against a reconstituted concentrated
competitor.

Phase 1 established the Semiconductor Access Agreement (SAA) framework for emergency
allocation sharing. Phase 2 builds on SAA into a **permanent Allied Semiconductor Industrial
Compact (ASIC)** — a formal coordination framework governing allied fab investment strategy,
node specialization, and joint R&D.

### ASIC Framework Design

**Member parties:** U.S., Japan, South Korea, Netherlands, EU (Belgium/Germany industrial policy).
United Kingdom optionally through AUKUS framework.

**Node specialization agreement:**
Allied nations negotiate a specialization matrix that prevents redundant investment and
ensures each critical chip category has production spread across geographies:

| Node Category | Primary Domestic (U.S.) | Allied Primary | Allied Secondary |
|---------------|------------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Advanced logic ≤3nm | U.S. (Intel, TSMC AZ, Samsung TX) | Taiwan (if available), Japan (Rapidus) | South Korea (Samsung home) |
| Advanced memory (HBM, DRAM) | Limited (building) | South Korea (SK Hynix, Samsung) | Japan (Kioxia for NAND) |
| Advanced analog/mixed-signal | U.S. (GlobalFoundries, TI) | Germany (Infineon, Bosch) | Japan |
| Mature logic 28nm–180nm | U.S. (multiple fabs) | EU (STMicro, NXP) | South Korea |
| Leading-edge packaging (CoWoS, HBM stack) | U.S. (building) | Taiwan (if available) | South Korea |

The specialization agreement does not prevent allied nations from building whatever they
choose. It governs DED co-investment and ASIC trade preference: DED co-invests in the
agreed U.S. nodes and extends ASIC trade concessions to allied nations that are filling
agreed specialization roles rather than duplicating covered capacity.

**Joint R&D pooling:**
ASIC members establish a **Joint Semiconductor R&D Fund** capitalized at $2B/year
collectively, with DED contributing $800M/year (U.S. share). The fund contracts with
IMEC (Belgium — the preeminent semiconductor research institute, already used by all
major chipmakers as neutral R&D ground) and participating national labs for:
- Post-3nm process research (gate-all-around transistors, 2D materials, nanotube FETs)
- Next-generation packaging and interconnect technology
- Novel memory architectures (neuromorphic, resistive RAM) as alternatives to DRAM dependency

IP from joint fund research is jointly owned by member nations under an agreed
commercialization framework — no member can exclusively license or export-control the IP
without ASIC majority approval.

## Program 3: Lithography Diversification Track

### The ASML Single Point of Failure

ASML holds a **complete monopoly on EUV lithography** — the equipment without which
advanced chips below 7nm cannot be manufactured anywhere on earth. This monopoly is
not just commercial; it is a physical capability that does not exist at any other company.
Canon and Nikon make deep-UV (DUV) lithography systems but have not developed EUV.

As of 2026:
- ASML's High-NA EUV machines (EXE:5200B) are shipping at approximately 20/year at
  $350–380M each — far below the demand from Intel, Samsung, SK Hynix, and eventually TSMC
- TSMC has declined High-NA EUV until at least 2029 (economics), using multi-patterning
  on standard Low-NA EUV to reach 2nm class nodes
- Intel is the lead adopter of High-NA EUV (14A process); Samsung beginning High-NA EUV
  production in 2026
- Next-generation Hyper-NA EUV is at early research stage — commercial deployment post-2032

Any disruption to ASML's production (political, physical, or via Dutch export control
reversal) would halt advanced chip manufacturing globally within 2–3 years as machines
require ongoing service and parts from ASML.

### DED Instruments

**U.S.-Netherlands Partnership Agreement (Phase 1 SAA extension):**
Phase 1 established a bilateral U.S.-Netherlands agreement. Phase 2 converts this from
an emergency access agreement into a long-term technology partnership:

- **DED Accelerator: Advanced Lithography** — joint program with ASML and U.S. national
  labs (LLNL, Sandia, NIST) for next-generation lithography research. DED contributes
  $1.5–2B over 5 years; ASML contributes proprietary process knowledge and researcher access.
  U.S. retains joint IP rights on discoveries made with DED funding.

- **ASML service center guarantee:** The Netherlands government (as condition of ASML-CHIPS
  Act export cooperation) commits to maintaining ASML service access for U.S. and allied
  fabs even in the event of export restrictions to other parties. Service access to existing
  machines is as critical as machine supply — EUV tools require ASML service engineers.

**Alternative lithography research program:**
DED funds a parallel research track through the Accelerator program and DARPA for
alternative patterning approaches that do not require EUV:

- **Multi-beam direct-write electron beam lithography (MBEBL):** Does not require
  EUV; research scale today, potentially manufacturable for specialty applications.
  Throughput is the current limitation — DARPA program targeting 10× throughput improvement.
- **Directed self-assembly (DSA):** Uses molecular chemistry to form features below
  conventional lithography limits without EUV. Complementary to DUV patterning.
  Already in pre-production research at IMEC and Intel Labs.
- **Nanoimprint lithography (NIL):** Canon has a commercial NIL system for mature nodes;
  DED Type B contract to accelerate NIL capability at leading edge for specialty chips
  (not a general EUV replacement, but viable for specific high-volume, cost-sensitive nodes)

These programs do not produce an EUV replacement in Phase 2 timelines. They generate the
research base that makes EUV diversification feasible in Phase 3 (7–15 years) and establish
U.S. IP in the lithography field that becomes a diplomatic and commercial asset.

**Domestic lithography equipment ecosystem:**
While EUV is ASML-monopolized, the adjacent equipment sector — etch, deposition, metrology,
CMP, wafer handling — is significantly more competitive and partially U.S.-based (Lam Research,
Applied Materials, KLA are global leaders). DED Type A contracts preserve and expand U.S.
leadership in this adjacent equipment base, ensuring that even if lithography supply chains
remain concentrated, the U.S. maintains leverage across the broader fab equipment ecosystem.

## Program 4: Semiconductor Workforce Pipeline

### The Critical Bottleneck

The most immediate constraint on domestic fab scale-up is not capital or permits — it
is workforce. Industry projections show:

- Global semiconductor industry needs **1 million+ additional workers by 2030**
- U.S. annual demand for semiconductor engineers rising from **9,000 to 17,000/year** in 2025
- U.S. annual demand for fab technicians rising from **7,000 to 14,000/year** in 2025
- The engineer shortage will **worsen until approximately 2028** when CHIPS Act-funded
  programs begin to meaningfully increase supply
- Existing training pipeline produces nowhere near these numbers

TSMC Arizona's initial ramp was slowed precisely by this: not enough qualified technicians
domestically. Intel's response — partnering with Maricopa Community Colleges on a 10-day
fab technician certificate program — is a model but operates at insufficient scale.

### DED Workforce Instruments

**DED-ERP Semiconductor Track:**
The Expert Reserve Program (ERP) is expanded to include a semiconductor-specific Accelerator:
- Recruit semiconductor engineers from U.S. companies, universities, and national labs into
  a reserve cohort available for surge deployment to domestic fabs experiencing ramp constraints
- ERP members maintain their primary employment; DED Type D contracts provide compensation
  for reserve availability and activation; fabs pay activation fees when ERP engineers deploy
- Target: 5,000 ERP-enrolled semiconductor engineers within 3 years

**CRHP Semiconductor Technician Track:**
The civilian reserve health program (CRHP) is extended to include a fab technician track:
- 6-month intensive training program at community colleges adjacent to CHIPS Act fab sites
  (Maricopa CC for TSMC Arizona; Columbus State CC for Intel Ohio; Collin College for Samsung Texas)
- Participants receive CRHP enrollment benefits (healthcare, stipend) during training; fab
  employers pay guaranteed placement fees upon hire
- Target: 20,000 new fab technicians trained annually at full program scale
- Interaction with AI Unemployment retraining: workers displaced from AI-exposed occupations
  in data entry, administrative, and back-office roles are specifically recruited for the
  fab technician track — precision-oriented, process-following work is a natural transition

**University semiconductor program investment:**
DED Type B contracts with 15–20 universities to establish or expand semiconductor
engineering degree programs. Contract requirements:
- Minimum 50 new semiconductor engineering graduates per year within 5 years
- CHIPS-adjacent location preferred (Arizona, Texas, Ohio, Oregon) but not required
- Curriculum includes hands-on fab access (DED co-funds a shared university fab or
  provides access agreements with commercial fabs for student training)
- Contract duration: 10 years, with 5-year review
- Total investment: ~$3–4B over 10 years across all university contracts

**K-12 semiconductor pipeline (long-lead):**
DED grants to state education agencies for semiconductor/microelectronics curriculum
development in high schools, particularly in CHIPS Act fab-adjacent communities.
This is a 10-year investment — the Phase 3 workforce — but must begin in Phase 2.
Budget: $200–300M in grants over 5 years.

## Program 5: Strategic Chip Reserve and Design Standards

### Strategic Chip Reserve

A physical stockpile of the highest-priority chip categories, maintained at 12–24 months
of U.S. demand for Tier 1–3 priority sectors, analogous to the Strategic Petroleum Reserve.

**Reserve categories (Tier 1 priority — defense and medical):**

| Chip Category | Primary Use | Reserve Target |
|---------------|-------------|---------------|
| High-reliability ASICs (MIL-SPEC) | Defense systems, avionics | 18 months DoD annual use |
| Medical-grade microcontrollers | Implantables, monitoring devices | 18 months FDA-registered device use |
| Power management ICs (industrial grade) | Grid infrastructure, defense power | 12 months |
| FPGA (military/space grade) | Defense systems, reconfigurability | 18 months |
| Automotive-grade MCUs (AEC-Q100) | Military ground vehicles, emergency services | 12 months |

**Reserve management:**
- Chips are purchased at contract price from NSIVS-enrolled distributors and domestic fabs
- Rotated on a FIFO basis as newer generations replace older (chips have finite shelf life;
  certain categories degrade over 5–10 years)
- Stored at 4–6 regional DED facilities with DoD security standards
- Estimated capital cost: $8–12B initial; $2–3B/year in rotation/refresh

### Chip-Minimal Design Standards for Critical Systems

DoD and DED jointly establish procurement standards requiring **design-for-resilience** across
all major defense acquisition programs and DED-priority civilian infrastructure:

**Standard requirements:**
1. All new defense systems rated for 15+ year operational life must be capable of
   operation using mature node chips (28nm or above) as a fallback if leading-edge chips
   become unavailable. This requires design documentation of the mature-node fallback
   configuration as part of the acquisition contract.

2. DED-priority civilian infrastructure (grid SCADA systems, water utility controls,
   medical life-critical systems) must similarly document a mature-node fallback architecture
   in any federal procurement over $10M.

3. Software-defined architectures: DED funds an ORNLC research program at national labs to
   develop software-defined versions of traditionally hardware-intensive chip functions —
   allowing critical systems to trade off performance for resilience when advanced chips
   are unavailable.

**DED Accelerator: Mature-Node Performance Optimization:**
A focused Accelerator program targeting the research problem of achieving advanced
performance on older process nodes — chiplet architectures, 3D stacking of mature-node
dies, advanced packaging (CoWoS, SoIC) as multipliers for mature-node performance.
Budget: $800M over 5 years. Output enters IP Asset Register.

diversification, and strategic reserve. The combined program reaches 20% domestic/allied
chip supply by 2035 — compared to the ~5% CHIPS Act alone would likely achieve.
