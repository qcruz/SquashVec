# Scenario: Semiconductor Supply Collapse

## Scenario Definition

A severe, sustained disruption to the global semiconductor supply chain — caused
by geopolitical conflict (Taiwan Strait), natural disaster (earthquake, typhoon
at TSMC fabs), coordinated export controls, or a combination — that reduces
availability of advanced chips by 50%+ for 1–5 years.

---

## Why Semiconductors Are a Critical Node

Semiconductors are the **oil of the digital economy** — a platform input
embedded in nearly every manufactured product and critical system.

| Sector | Dependency |
|---|---|
| Automotive | 1,000–3,000 chips per vehicle; modern EVs 3,000+ |
| Defense systems | Entirely dependent; no analog fallback |
| Medical devices | Pacemakers, ventilators, imaging equipment |
| Grid infrastructure | Smart inverters, SCADA systems, transformers |
| Consumer electronics | Phones, laptops, appliances |
| Industrial manufacturing | CNC machines, robotics, sensors |
| Telecommunications | Base stations, routers, data centers |
| Agriculture | GPS-guided equipment, sensor networks |

---

## The Concentration Problem

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| TSMC share of advanced chip manufacturing (<7nm) | ~90% |
| Taiwan share of global foundry revenue | ~64% |
| Dutch ASML monopoly on EUV lithography machines | ~100% |
| South Korea share of DRAM production | ~70%+ |
| U.S. domestic advanced fab share | <5% (pre-CHIPS Act) |

A Taiwan Strait conflict would instantly eliminate access to the vast majority
of the world's most advanced semiconductor production. There is no short-term substitute.

---

## Severity Tiers

| Tier | Disruption | Duration | Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | 15–30% reduction in advanced chips | 6–18 months | Single fab disaster, export control escalation |
| Tier 2 | 50–70% reduction in advanced chips | 2–5 years | Taiwan conflict, TSMC disruption |
| Tier 3 | 70–100% reduction in advanced chips | 5–10 years | Full Taiwan conflict, fab destruction |

---

## Cascade Sequence (Tier 2)

```
Month 1–3:   Existing inventory drawn down; spot prices spike 200-500%
Month 3–6:   Automotive production halts (lowest chip priority vs. margin)
Month 6–12:  Consumer electronics shortages; defense procurement disrupted
Year 1–2:    Industrial equipment, medical devices, grid equipment backlog
Year 2–5:    Structural economic contraction in chip-dependent sectors
```

---

## Phase Responses

### Phase 1: Triage and Allocation

- **Chip priority framework:** Defense → medical → grid → industrial → automotive → consumer
- **Inventory visibility mandate:** All large chip consumers report inventories to DED
- **Export control negotiations:** Coordinate with allies on mutual access agreements
- **Repair-over-replace mandate:** Extend life of existing chip-dependent equipment

### Phase 2: Domestic Production Build-Out

- **CHIPS Act acceleration:** DED coordinates with Commerce to fast-track fab construction
- **Mature node focus:** Advanced chips take 5–10 years; 28nm and above can be built faster
- **Design-for-resilience:** Government contracts require domestic chip availability
- **Allied fab agreements:** AUKUS, Japan, EU — coordinate fab build across allies

### Phase 3: Structural Resilience

- **Domestic advanced fab capability:** Target 20%+ of U.S. chip consumption by 2035
- **Chip-minimal defense systems:** R&D into designs that reduce advanced chip dependency
- **Stockpile standards:** Critical chip categories maintained at 12–24 month reserve
- **Multiple-node architecture:** Design critical systems to run on older, more available chips as fallback

## Key Data Point

The CHIPS and Science Act (2022) committed ~$52B to domestic semiconductor manufacturing.
First new fabs (TSMC Arizona, Samsung Taylor, Intel Ohio) are under construction as of 2025–2026.
**Lead time from groundbreaking to production:** 4–5 years minimum.
