# Oil Restriction — Transportation Supply Chain

## Dependency Map

Transportation is the **primary transmission mechanism** of an oil crisis.
It is both a victim of oil restriction and a cause of secondary cascades:
as transportation degrades, every supply chain that depends on it also degrades.

### Oil Dependency by Mode

| Mode | Oil Dependency | Electrification Status | Substitution Options |
|---|---|---|---|
| Personal vehicles | 100% liquid fuel | EV transition underway | Transit, e-bike, remote work |
| Light trucks / vans | 95%+ liquid fuel | EV transition underway | Route optimization, modal shift |
| Long-haul trucking | ~99% diesel | Early-stage EV, limited range | Rail (intermodal), platooning |
| Freight rail (diesel) | 70% diesel, 30% electric | Partial electrification exists | Electrification, already efficient |
| Aviation | 100% jet fuel | No near-term substitute | Reduced operations, SAF long-term |
| Marine shipping | 95%+ bunker fuel/diesel | Minimal | Methanol, wind assist, slow steaming |
| Construction/equipment | 99% diesel | Limited | Work prioritization |
| Emergency services | 99% diesel/gasoline | Some EV, limited | Priority allocation |

### Failure Sequence (Tier 2 Disruption)

```
Week 1–2:  Fuel price spike → long-haul trucking cost increase
Week 2–4:  Spot shortages in fuel-dependent regions
Month 1–2: Freight rate increases cascade into consumer goods prices
Month 2–3: Agricultural logistics disruption (planting/harvest season risk)
Month 3–6: Airlines reduce capacity, raise fares
Month 6+:  Structural freight shortfall without modal shift
```

## Substitution Analysis

### Personal Vehicle Demand (Largest Volume Target)

**Potential reductions with Phase 1 measures:**
- Remote work mandate: 5–12% personal vehicle fuel consumption
- Speed limit reduction (75→55 mph): 3–5%
- Transit expansion: 1–3% (constrained by existing capacity)
- E-bike substitution (urban): 1–2%

**Combined Phase 1 effect:** 10–22% reduction in personal vehicle consumption.

### Freight: Truck-to-Rail Modal Shift

**Current state:** ~70% of U.S. freight moves by truck; ~15% by rail.
Rail is 4x more fuel-efficient per ton-mile.

**Bottlenecks to rapid modal shift:**
1. Intermodal terminal capacity (limited transfer points)
2. Last-mile delivery (rail doesn't reach final destinations)
3. Time sensitivity (rail is slower than trucking for many goods)
4. Infrastructure constraints (specific corridors are at capacity)

**Pre-built requirement:** Catalog of bottleneck removal projects with cost,
timeline, and capacity impact. These projects exist; funding is the constraint.

## Strategic Pre-Positioning

### Emergency Bus Fleet

**Target:** 50,000 additional buses deployable within 6 months.
**Mechanism:** Defense Production Act manufacturing orders + depot contracts.
**Estimated fuel displacement:** 200–400 million gallons/year (at full utilization).

### Dedicated Emergency Lane Network

**Target:** Pre-identified bus/carpool lanes on 500+ highway corridors.
**Mechanism:** Restriping and temporary signage — no construction required.
**Lead time if pre-planned:** 2–4 weeks.

### Intermodal Expansion Priority List

**Target:** 30 highest-impact intermodal terminal expansions, pre-permitted.
**Mechanism:** DED maintains engineering-ready designs for each.
**Lead time if pre-planned:** 6–18 months (construction, not planning).
