# Energy Transition Programs — Oil Restriction Response

**Scenario:** Oil Restriction
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

Companion to: `demand-reduction-policy.md` (demand-side) | `phase-2-adaptation.md` (mid-term) | `phase-3-structural.md` (long-term)

## Purpose

This document covers the supply-side response: programs that build alternative energy capacity and accelerate adoption across the economy. While demand-reduction-policy.md focuses on reducing oil consumption now, energy-transition-programs.md focuses on replacing oil's structural role with permanent alternatives.

The logic is sequential but overlapping:
- **0-6 months:** demand reduction buys time
- **6 months-5 years:** transition programs replace oil's role in transportation, heat, and industry
- **5-20 years:** structural programs eliminate oil dependency from the national physical architecture

## Program 1: Emergency Permitting Reform

### The Problem
The biggest bottleneck to renewable energy in the U.S. is not technology or capital — it is permitting. A utility-scale solar project takes 3-5 years to permit. A wind farm can take 5-7 years. In a crisis, this timeline is incompatible with the response requirement.

### Federal Fast-Track Program
**Trigger:** Declared oil restriction emergency (Tier 2 or higher)

**18-Month Hard Cap:**
- All renewable energy projects (solar, wind, geothermal, transmission) with federal nexus receive an 18-month maximum permitting timeline from application to final permit
- Federal coordinating office assigns a single point of contact per project
- Environmental review compressed via concurrent (not sequential) agency review
- NEPA categorical exclusions pre-approved for: solar on disturbed land, offshore wind in pre-designated zones, transmission line upgrades on existing corridors

**Pre-Approved Zones:**
DED pre-maps and pre-clears high-potential zones before any crisis:
- Solar: degraded agricultural land, brownfields, highway corridors, federal land in Southwest
- Wind: offshore Atlantic/Pacific/Gulf, Great Plains and Mountain West federal land
- Geothermal: Western states, identified high-potential geology

Projects sited in pre-approved zones receive a 90-day permitting track rather than 18 months.

**Single Federal Permit:**
- Replace the current multi-agency permit stack (Army Corps, EPA, USFWS, BLM, state, local) with a single consolidated federal permit issued by a DED Emergency Energy Office
- State and local permits pre-empted under National Emergency authority for Tier 3 restrictions
- Binding on states: if a state fails to act within 60 days, federal permit becomes effective

**Estimated Impact:**
- 18-month track: accelerates ~300 GW of projects currently stuck in permitting queue
- 90-day track: unlocks new construction capacity in pre-approved zones within one year
- Combined effect: potential to add 100-150 GW of renewable capacity in 3 years vs. 7-10 under normal timelines

## Program 2: Solar Scale-Up

### Distributed Solar Emergency Deployment

**Residential Solar Surge:**
- Instant point-of-sale rebate (25-35% of system cost) — no waiting for tax filing
- Federal installer registry with training grants to expand workforce by 200%
- Low-income rooftop solar at zero cost: federal pays 100% of install for households below 200% of federal poverty line
- Community solar virtual net metering for renters and multi-family buildings: 200% of current capacity target in 3 years

**Commercial and Industrial Solar:**
- Emergency accelerated depreciation: full deduction in year 1 for commercial solar installs during restriction period
- Pre-approved design kits for standard commercial roof sizes (reduces engineering cost and time by 40-60%)
- Utility-scale solar on federal land: DED issues direct lease grants to developers in pre-approved zones, bypassing standard BLM leasing process

**Solar Manufacturing:**
- Emergency tariff suspension on solar panels and components from allied nations to accelerate deployment
- Domestic manufacturing capacity: emergency low-interest loans to expand U.S. panel manufacturing from current ~10 GW/yr capacity to 50 GW/yr over 5 years
- Critical mineral supply: stockpile silicon, silver contacts, and encapsulant materials for 12-month forward production

## Program 3: Wind Scale-Up

### Offshore Wind Emergency Acceleration

**Current Status (2025 baseline):**
- ~7 GW installed offshore (primarily Northeast)
- 30-40 GW in various stages of lease, permitting, or construction
- Target under normal planning: ~110 GW by 2035

**Emergency Program:**
- Fast-track the 30-40 GW pipeline to construction-ready status within 18 months using emergency permitting
- Pre-position transmission infrastructure (offshore cables, onshore grid interconnects) under separate emergency authorization
- Federal loan guarantees covering 80% of project financing to unlock private capital in uncertain environment
- Fixed-price power purchase agreements (PPAs) from DOE at guaranteed rate for 20 years — removes merchant risk and accelerates financial close

**Floating Offshore Wind (Deeper Waters):**
- Pacific coast (California, Oregon, Washington) has large potential but requires floating platforms
- Emergency R&D-to-deployment program: fund 5 GW pilot program in Pacific with 50% federal cost share
- Goal: first floating offshore wind commercial farm online within 36 months

**Onshore Wind:**
- Primary bottleneck is transmission (wind resource in Great Plains, load centers in coastal cities)
- Emergency transmission line authority: DED can designate National Wind Corridors — federal transmission right-of-way across states using eminent domain authority
- Target: 10,000 miles of new high-voltage transmission in Great Plains and Mountain West within 5 years

## Program 4: Nuclear Fast-Track

### Existing Fleet Preservation
Twenty-three U.S. reactors at 16 sites are currently licensed for operation. Nuclear provides ~19% of U.S. electricity — zero oil required for operation.

**Emergency Life Extension:**
- Fast-track license renewals: compress NRC review from 5-7 years to 18 months for plants with strong safety records
- Defer scheduled retirements: any plant planning to close within 5 years receives operating cost subsidy to extend (estimated $40-60B over 5 years across fleet)
- Fuel security: expand domestic uranium enrichment capacity; reduce dependence on Russian-supplied enriched uranium (Orano/Centrus expansion loans)

### Small Modular Reactors (SMRs)
SMRs are factory-built, standardized reactors with output of 50-300 MW (vs. 1,000+ MW for conventional plants). First commercial SMRs are targeting deployment in 2029-2032.

**Emergency Program:**
- NRC establishes SMR design certification fast-track: standardized designs approved once, then replicated without re-review at each site
- Federal reactor deployment contracts: DOE purchases output from first 20 SMR deployments at guaranteed rate, removing demand risk
- Site pre-approval: identify and pre-clear 100 sites for SMR deployment (federal land, retiring coal plants, industrial sites) before any design is finalized
- Workforce pipeline: partner with nuclear engineering programs and national labs to expand qualified workforce by 10,000 personnel over 5 years

**Timeline Targets:**
- 2027-2029: First NRC-certified SMR design approved
- 2029-2032: First commercial SMRs online at pre-approved sites
- 2032-2040: 50+ GW of SMR capacity deployed if crisis conditions sustain program

### Nuclear Hydrogen Production
High-temperature reactors (HTRs) can produce hydrogen via thermochemical splitting of water — no carbon, no natural gas required.

- Fund 3 demonstration HTR + hydrogen production facilities
- Hydrogen target use: fertilizer production (displacing natural gas Haber-Bosch), steel production, heavy transport fuel

## Program 5: Geothermal Expansion

Geothermal is currently ~0.4% of U.S. electricity — dramatically underutilized given the resource base.

**Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS):**
EGS uses oil/gas drilling techniques to create heat exchange systems in hot dry rock, vastly expanding the geographic range beyond traditional geothermal areas.

**Emergency Program:**
- Transfer oil and gas drilling expertise and equipment to EGS development: incentive payments to drillers who retrain and retool for geothermal
- DOE cost-share program: 50% federal cost share for first 5 GW of EGS projects
- Fast permitting: geothermal on federal land uses emergency 90-day permitting track
- Target: 10 GW of geothermal by 2035 (up from ~4 GW today), with potential for 100+ GW over longer horizon

## Program 6: Grid Hardening and Storage

Alternative energy generation is only as useful as the grid that delivers it. Oil restriction combined with grid fragility creates a compound crisis.

### Transmission Buildout
- **National Transmission Superhighways:** Emergency authority to build 3 major HVDC (high-voltage direct current) corridors:
  1. Great Plains Wind → Midwest/Southeast load centers (2,500 miles)
  2. Southwest Solar → Pacific Coast and Texas (1,800 miles)
  3. Atlantic Offshore Wind → Northeast and Mid-Atlantic cities (1,200 miles)
- HVDC lines carry power 2-3x more efficiently over long distances than AC; reduce transmission losses from ~6% to ~2%
- Estimated cost: $150-200B; payback via reduced energy losses and fossil fuel displacement in 8-12 years

### Grid-Scale Storage
- **Iron-air and flow batteries:** Emerging long-duration storage (8-100+ hours) suitable for balancing variable renewable supply
  - Emergency procurement: 10 GW of long-duration storage contracts by 2028
  - Manufacturing onshoring: loans to build U.S. iron-air battery manufacturing (Ambri, Form Energy)
- **Pumped hydro:** Largest form of existing grid storage (~22 GW in U.S.)
  - Closed-loop pumped hydro (no new dams, just reservoirs connected by pipe) has 35,000 MW of identified potential
  - Emergency permitting and 50% federal cost share for closed-loop projects
- **Distributed battery storage:** Pair with residential solar for 6-hour household backup; pre-positioned community battery storage in vulnerable grid regions

## Program 7: Building Electrification

Buildings consume ~40% of U.S. energy — primarily for space heating, water heating, and cooking. Oil and natural gas account for roughly 70% of building energy use today.

### Federal Building Standards
- Emergency mandate: all federally-owned and -leased buildings convert to electric HVAC within 5 years
- New construction standard: no new natural gas hookups in federal projects effective immediately
- Building code pre-emption: federal minimum standard banning gas in new construction applies in states during Tier 2/3 restriction

### Residential Conversion Program
- **Heat pump rebate:** $8,000-15,000 instant rebate at point of sale for electric heat pump replacing gas/oil furnace; covers ~60-80% of system cost for average home
- **Contractor workforce expansion:** 200,000 certified heat pump installers needed; emergency training grants through community colleges and trade schools
- **Oil heat emergency conversion:** Households heating with fuel oil receive priority access to heat pump program + free installation if income-qualified (see oil-restriction/supply-chains/energy.md: ~5 million homes)
- **Weatherization co-investment:** Heat pump program bundled with insulation and air sealing upgrades; reduces home heating load before conversion, right-sizing equipment

- See `supply-chains/industrial.md` → Process Heat section for industrial electrification
