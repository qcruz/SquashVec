# Oil Restriction — Demand Reduction Policy Programs

This document covers the economic and regulatory programs that reduce oil demand
during an oil restriction event. These are distinct from infrastructure programs
(EV conversion, rail expansion) — they operate on **behavior and price signals**
and can be activated within weeks rather than years.

---

## 1. Remote Work Mandate and Infrastructure

### The Opportunity

Personal vehicle commuting accounts for approximately **28% of U.S. oil consumption**.
A significant fraction of the workforce (estimates: 30–40% of jobs are remote-compatible)
does not need to commute at all for large portions of their work.

In 2020, COVID-era remote work reduced U.S. vehicle miles traveled by ~40% at peak.
This translated to an estimated **5–10% reduction in total oil consumption** — achieved
with no new infrastructure.

### Policy Framework

**Tier 1 (Voluntary + Incentive):**
- Federal employer tax credit for remote work infrastructure investment
- Waiver of commuter transportation benefits for remote workers
- Federal agency remote work targets (50%+ of eligible workforce)

**Tier 2 (Regulatory):**
- Mandatory remote work for all federal contractors with >50% remote-capable workforce
- SEC disclosure requirement: public companies must report % of remote-capable workforce and actual WFH rate
- States incentivized via highway funding formula to achieve WFH targets

**Tier 3 (Emergency Mandate):**
- Presidential emergency authority to mandate remote work for qualifying occupations
- Similar to WWII "Commuter Restriction Orders" but digitally enforceable
- Enforcement: employer reporting, spot audits, fines for non-compliance

### Sector Eligibility Analysis

| Sector | Remote-Capable Share | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Finance/insurance | ~75% | High margin; strong incentive |
| Professional services | ~70% | Already high adoption |
| Information/tech | ~65% | Demonstrated during COVID |
| Government (federal) | ~55% | Large employer, direct control |
| Education (admin) | ~40% | Teaching harder; admin easy |
| Healthcare (admin) | ~50% | Clinical staff excluded |
| Retail/hospitality | ~5% | Limited applicability |
| Manufacturing/construction | ~5% | Not applicable |

**Realistic aggregate:** A strong remote work mandate across eligible sectors
reduces commute trips by 15–20%, translating to **4–8% of total national oil consumption**.

### Pre-Built Requirements

- **Employer registry:** Database of all companies with >100 employees, classified by remote-work eligibility
- **Legal framework:** Pre-drafted executive order and regulatory guidance
- **Broadband baseline:** Current gap analysis for under-connected workers (rural, lower-income)
- **Technology access program:** Device and connectivity support for workers who lack home office equipment

## 2. Road Tolls and Congestion Pricing

### The Case for Congestion Pricing

Congestion pricing charges drivers for road use based on time, location, and
vehicle type. It directly prices the externality of driving and generates revenue
that can fund alternatives. It is the most economically efficient demand-reduction tool.

**Proven results:**
- Stockholm congestion charge (2006): 20% traffic reduction, sustained; significant mode shift to transit
- London Congestion Charge (2003): 30% traffic reduction in charging zone; sustained
- NYC Central Business District Tolling (2025): early results showing 10–15% traffic reduction

### DED Program Design

**Emergency Congestion Pricing Authority:**
- Pre-written legal framework for federal emergency congestion pricing on interstates and federal highways
- Normally, states control tolling; emergency authority allows federal override
- Revenue earmarked 100% for transit expansion and EV charging infrastructure

**National Toll Network (Medium-Term):**
- Transition key interstate corridors to tolled operation
- Variable pricing by congestion level and vehicle fuel efficiency
- EVs and high-occupancy vehicles receive discounts or exemptions
- Revenue directed to federal transportation trust fund (currently underfunded)

**Pricing Structure:**

| Vehicle Type | Peak Toll (example) | Off-Peak | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| ICE single-occupant | $0.15–0.25/mile | $0.05–0.10/mile | Full externality price |
| Hybrid | $0.08–0.12/mile | $0.03–0.06/mile | Partial discount |
| EV | $0.02–0.04/mile | $0.01–0.02/mile | Infrastructure cost only |
| Carpool (2+) | 50% discount any vehicle | — | Incentivize ridesharing |
| Commercial freight | Weight-adjusted rate | — | Already efficient per ton |

**Equity Considerations:**
Congestion pricing is regressive if revenue is not recycled. The DED framework
requires:
- Low-income exemption or rebate (means-tested, quarterly payment)
- Free or subsidized transit passes for displaced commuters
- Geographic equity: rural areas not subject to tolling
- Revenue transparency: annual public accounting of toll revenue and use

### Estimated Impact

At full national deployment on major corridors:
- 10–20% reduction in vehicle miles traveled
- $50–120 billion/year in revenue (depending on coverage)
- Equivalent to 2–5% national oil consumption reduction

## 3. Gas Tax and EV Buildout Funding

### Current Gas Tax Structure

The federal gas tax is **18.4 cents/gallon** — set in 1993 and **never raised for inflation**.
In real terms it has lost 60%+ of its purchasing power. The Highway Trust Fund is
chronically underfunded, requiring repeated general fund transfers.

At 18.4 cents/gallon, the gas tax raises ~$40 billion/year.
The U.S. spends ~$500 billion/year on surface transportation infrastructure.

### The DED Proposal: Fuel Security Surcharge

**Framing matters:** Rather than a "gas tax increase" (politically toxic), the DED
proposes a **Fuel Security Surcharge** — transparently dedicated to reducing the
nation's oil dependency and the economic risk it creates.

**Structure:**

| Component | Rate | Revenue (est.) | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base surcharge | +$0.25/gallon | ~$35B/year | EV charging infrastructure |
| Security surcharge | +$0.15/gallon | ~$21B/year | SPR maintenance and refill |
| Transition fund | +$0.10/gallon | ~$14B/year | Worker retraining, transit |
| **Total increase** | **+$0.50/gallon** | **~$70B/year** | — |

**Consumer impact:**
- At 12,000 miles/year, 30 mpg average: $200/year increase
- Low-income rebate (bottom 30% of earners): full rebate via tax credit
- EV drivers pay zero gas tax but contribute via road tolls (above)

**EV Charging Buildout (funded by surcharge):**

| Infrastructure Target | Investment | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| 500,000 public Level 2 chargers | ~$5B | 3 years |
| 100,000 DC Fast chargers | ~$15B | 5 years |
| Highway corridor charging (every 50 miles) | ~$8B | 3 years |
| Multi-unit dwelling charging | ~$10B | 5 years |
| Fleet depot charging (bus, delivery, municipal) | ~$12B | 4 years |

**Total charging infrastructure investment:** ~$50B over 5 years —
fully funded by one year of surcharge revenue.

**Escalating schedule:** Surcharge increases $0.05/gallon per year as EVs
are adopted, so the burden shifts to a shrinking base and the incentive to
switch grows. When EV share reaches 50%, surcharge converts fully to a mileage fee.

## 4. Economic Adoption Programs for Alternative Energy

### The Adoption Gap Problem

Alternative energy technology often exists at viable cost before adoption
reaches scale. The gap between "technically available" and "economically adopted"
is filled by:

1. Information barriers (consumers don't know the economics)
2. Upfront cost barriers (EVs cost more initially even if cheaper over time)
3. Infrastructure gaps (range anxiety, charging unavailability)
4. Coordination failures (no one builds charging until EVs are common; no one buys EVs until charging is common)

DED programs target each barrier specifically.

### Program: Instant Rebate at Point of Sale (EV)

Current federal EV tax credits are annual — consumers pay the full price and
wait for a refund. This is a significant barrier for middle-income buyers.

**DED program:** Convert to **instant rebate at dealership**, funded by IRS
advance to dealers, reconciled annually. Eliminates cash flow barrier.

**Enhancement:** Rebate scales inversely with income — higher rebate for lower
income buyers. Maximum rebate for lowest quintile; minimum for highest.

### Program: Guaranteed Buyback for ICE Vehicles

Uncertainty about resale value of ICE vehicles reduces willingness to buy EVs.

**DED program:** Federal guarantee to purchase any qualifying ICE vehicle at
a floor price (indexed to age and mileage) when the owner purchases an EV.
This de-risks the transition for middle-income buyers with significant asset
tied up in their current vehicle.

### Program: Utility EV Rate Tariffs (Federal Standard)

EV charging is cheapest at night (off-peak) but many utilities don't offer
favorable time-of-use rates, or make them difficult to access.

**DED program:** Federal standard requiring all utilities to offer a simple,
accessible EV charging rate at no more than 50% of peak residential rate
between 11pm–6am. Reduces charging cost ~40–50%, improving EV economics.

### Program: Community Solar + EV Charging Bundles

Low-income and renter households often cannot install rooftop solar or home
chargers. They get no benefit from either program.

**DED program:** Community solar subscriptions bundled with shared EV charging
access in apartment complexes and multi-family housing. Federal grant program
covers installation; residents access at cost. Targets the adoption gap in
the bottom half of the income distribution.

### Program: Industrial Electrification Grants (SMEs)

Small and medium manufacturers often lack capital for electrification upgrades
(electric forklifts, process heat, compressed air systems) even when the ROI
is positive within 3–5 years.

**DED program:** Direct grants (not loans) for small manufacturers (under 500
employees) to electrify industrial processes. Grants cover 40–60% of capital cost.
Conditioned on domestic equipment sourcing and energy reporting.

### Program: Alt Energy Fast-Track Permitting

Renewable energy projects currently face 5–10 year permitting timelines.
This is the single largest constraint on clean energy build-out.

**DED program:** For projects designated as "energy security infrastructure":
- Maximum 18-month federal permitting timeline (hard cap)
- Pre-approved zones for wind and solar (similar to military installation siting)
- Single federal permit covering all federal agency reviews (no sequential process)
- Streamlined NEPA process for energy security projects under 500MW
- Automatic approval if federal agencies fail to act within the timeline

**Estimated impact:** Fast-track permitting alone could double the rate of
renewable energy deployment within 5 years, based on analysis of current pipeline.
