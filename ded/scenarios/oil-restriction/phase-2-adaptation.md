# Phase 2: Medium-Term Adaptation (6 Months – 5 Years)

**Objective:** Structurally reduce oil dependence before reserves and
public tolerance erode. This is where industrial mobilization occurs.

---

## Transportation Transition

### Emergency EV Conversion Program

Not a subsidy program — an **industrial mobilization**.
The distinction matters: subsidies nudge markets; mobilization directs industry.

| Measure | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Government purchase guarantees | Guaranteed contracts reduce manufacturer risk |
| Idle factory conversion | Defense Production Act, WWII-style repurposing |
| National battery contracts | Long-term offtake agreements to fund expansion |
| Accelerated permitting | Emergency review timelines for EV infrastructure |
| Military logistics support | DOD assists in supply chain buildout |
| Fast-track apprenticeship programs | Labor pipeline for new manufacturing |

**Precedent:** WWII automobile manufacturers converted to aircraft and tank
production within months. The tooling, workforce, and organizational capacity
exist for analogous conversions today.

---

### Fleet-First Electrification

Consumer EV adoption is slow and uneven. Fleet electrification is faster,
more controllable, and displaces more fuel per dollar.

**Priority fleets:**

| Fleet | Why Priority |
|---|---|
| USPS | Predictable routes, centralized charging, federal control |
| Delivery fleets (UPS, FedEx, Amazon) | High daily mileage, urban concentration |
| Municipal vehicles | Government procurement leverage |
| Utility trucks | Controlled charging at depots |
| Corporate fleets | Large purchasers, contractual leverage |
| Buses | Single vehicle replaces ~40 private vehicles |
| Government vehicles (all agencies) | Direct federal control |

**Fuel displacement logic:** Fleet vehicles drive 3–5x more miles annually
than average consumer vehicles. A fleet EV displaces 3–5x more oil per unit.

---

### Hybridization as a Bridge

Full EV conversion requires years. Hybrid and plug-in hybrid conversion
displaces oil immediately while the EV infrastructure builds out.

**Options:**
- Hybrid mandate for new vehicle sales
- Retrofit kits for existing commercial vehicles
- Plug-in conversion programs for existing hybrids
- Electrified last-mile delivery (cargo bikes, electric cargo vehicles)
- E-bike substitution programs for short urban trips

**Metric:** Oil displacement per dollar per month — not long-term technological elegance.

---

### National E-Bike and Scooter Strategy

Often overlooked in large-scale energy planning.

The strategic observation: in dense urban areas, a significant fraction of
car trips are under 5 miles. These trips are easily replaced.

**Programs:**
- E-bike vouchers (similar to existing EV tax credits)
- Protected lane infrastructure (primarily paint and signage, low cost)
- Domestic e-bike and cargo-bike assembly
- Cargo-bike subsidies for last-mile delivery

**Scale:** Replacing 10% of urban car trips with e-bikes eliminates roughly
2–3% of personal vehicle fuel consumption nationally.

## Freight and Logistics

### Rail Renaissance Program

Freight rail is **4x more fuel-efficient** than trucking per ton-mile.
The U.S. has the largest freight rail network in the world — largely underutilized
for intermodal (truck-to-rail) conversion.

**Programs:**
- Emergency rail electrification of highest-volume corridors
- Intermodal terminal expansion (truck-to-rail transfer points)
- Priority freight corridor designation
- Bottleneck removal (identified but unfunded projects)

**Bottleneck catalog:** The DED maintains a prioritized list of infrastructure
constraints limiting freight rail throughput — pre-engineered, permitting-ready,
awaiting crisis funding.

---

### Strategic Inland Shipping Expansion

The U.S. dramatically underutilizes its water freight capacity relative to
its geography. Inland waterways are the most fuel-efficient freight mode.

**Opportunities:**
- Mississippi / Ohio / Missouri river system (existing but underinvested)
- Great Lakes freight (existing but underutilized)
- Coastal shipping (cabotage, currently restricted by Jones Act — waivable)

**Fuel comparison:** Waterway freight uses approximately 1/6 the fuel of trucking
per ton-mile.

---

### Trucking Efficiency Emergency

For freight that cannot shift modes:

| Measure | Fuel Reduction |
|---|---|
| Platooning technology (automated convoy) | 10–15% per truck |
| Aerodynamic retrofits (fairings, skirts) | 5–10% |
| Idle restrictions at rest stops | 2–4% |
| Speed governors (65 mph max) | 5–8% |
| Load optimization AI (fill trucks fully) | 5–15% |
| Shared freight exchanges (reduce empty miles) | 10–20% of total truck miles |

## Agriculture and Food Security

### Fertilizer Contingency Programs

Nitrogen fertilizer requires **natural gas** as a feedstock (Haber-Bosch process).
An oil crisis often affects natural gas supply and pricing simultaneously.

**Programs:**
- Strategic fertilizer reserve (6–18 month supply)
- Domestic ammonia production expansion (Defense Production Act contracts)
- Organic transition contingencies (pre-researched crop yield impacts)
- Emergency crop prioritization (shift inputs to highest-calorie, most-essential crops)

**Critical dependency:** Corn and soy production is extremely fertilizer-intensive.
A fertilizer shortage that reduces corn yields by 20% is also a livestock feed
crisis, an ethanol crisis, and a food price crisis simultaneously.

See: [Agriculture Supply Chain](supply-chains/agriculture.md)

---

### Crop Portfolio Shift

Under resource constraints, what crops are grown matters enormously.

**Shift away from:**
- Biofuel crops (corn ethanol, soy biodiesel) — compete with food
- Water-intensive luxury exports
- Feed crops for inefficient protein conversion (cattle feedlots)

**Shift toward:**
- Staple calorie crops (wheat, potatoes, legumes) — highest calories per acre
- Crops with lower fertilizer and fuel requirements
- Crops that can be grown domestically without long freight

---

### Regional Food Production

Long-distance food logistics become a vulnerability when fuel is constrained.

**Programs:**
- Controlled environment agriculture (greenhouses, vertical farms) near population centers
- Urban greenhouse networks in major metros
- Local food processing infrastructure (reduce need for long transport)
- Emergency farming incentive programs (bring fallow land into production)

## Energy System Diversification

### Nuclear Fast-Track

Nuclear does not directly solve transportation fuel needs in the short term.
It solves the grid problem: as electrification increases, grid capacity must grow.

**Pre-built requirements:**
- Pre-approved reactor sites (environmental review complete, permits ready)
- Standardized reactor designs (reduce engineering time per unit)
- Emergency licensing framework (shorten NRC approval timelines)
- Reactor manufacturing reserve (domestic supply chain for components)

---

### Grid Hardening for Electrification

Electrification without grid resilience is fragile — a cyberattack or storm
that would previously cause a regional inconvenience now disrupts transportation.

**Programs:**
- Transformer reserves (current lead times: 1–2 years; strategic stockpile needed)
- Domestic transformer manufacturing (currently 80%+ imported)
- Microgrid buildout (communities that can island from the main grid)
- Distributed storage (utility-scale batteries as grid buffer)
- Cybersecurity hardening of grid control systems
