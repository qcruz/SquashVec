# Grid Cyberattack Scenario — Phase 1: Immediate Response

**Scenario:** Electrical Grid Cyberattack
**Phase:** 1 of 3
**Timeline:** 0–90 days from attack detection
**Trigger:** Confirmed malicious intrusion causing equipment damage or sustained loss of
  grid control across one or more transmission zones; or detection of pre-positioned malware
  in critical grid control systems at imminent deployment stage
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 1 Objective

A grid cyberattack is not like other economic disruptions. It does not unfold over weeks
or months — it can cascade from regional outage to interconnect-level failure in hours,
and physical damage (particularly to large power transformers) cannot be reversed by
removing the threat. The Phase 1 window is measured in **hours to days**, not weeks.

Phase 1 objective: **Contain the cascade, maintain life-safety power, and prevent
physical damage from becoming permanent damage.**

The DED's role in Phase 1 is not operational — DED does not operate the grid, dispatch
repair crews, or manage emergency services. DED's role is to **pre-position the economic
instruments** that give grid operators, hospitals, and emergency services the resources
they need to sustain operations during the attack window, and to activate the pre-committed
contracts that move equipment and personnel faster than emergency procurement allows.

### The 72-Hour Window

The critical parameter in a Tier 2–3 grid cyberattack is the 72-hour threshold:
- **0–72 hours:** Backup generators can sustain critical facilities; fuel supply is adequate;
  water treatment can maintain operations; communications remain functional
- **72 hours – 2 weeks:** Generator fuel exhausts without resupply; water treatment begins
  to fail; food cold chain breaks; hospital operations become critical
- **2–4 weeks:** Civil order risk; heat/cold mortality if seasonal extremes; economic
  activity largely halted in affected regions

Every DED Phase 1 instrument is designed to extend the functional window beyond 72 hours
and prevent the cascade into civil disorder while physical repairs proceed.

## Program 1: Strategic Transformer Reserve Deployment

### Why This Is the Critical Variable

The single most dangerous consequence of a Tier 3 grid cyberattack is physical destruction
of **Large Power Transformers (LPTs)**. The attack sequence that causes the most lasting
damage is:
1. Attacker gains control of grid management systems
2. Attacker operates the grid outside of equipment tolerances — voltage spikes, frequency
   excursions, overloads — causing transformer insulation failure and winding damage
3. The transformer is destroyed, not just taken offline
4. Lead time for custom replacement: 12–24 months
5. The grid cannot be fully restored until the transformer is replaced

Without a pre-positioned reserve, a Tier 3 attack that destroys 20–30 strategically chosen
LPTs creates a grid outage measured in months to years, not weeks.

### The Pre-Positioned Reserve (Phase 2/3 Investment, Phase 1 Deployment)

The Strategic Transformer Reserve — built under DED Phase 2 contracts — is the primary
Phase 1 deployment instrument. On attack declaration:

**DED Action (Day 0–4):**
- DOE Emergency Order activating transformer reserve deployment
- DED coordinates transportation logistics: LPTs typically weigh 100–400 tons; require
  special rail cars (Schnabel cars) or heavy transport — DED pre-committed logistics
  contracts cover this; no emergency procurement required
- Priority list of substations activated from NEID critical node map (built during
  peacetime; identifies the 50–100 substations whose failure would cause cascading loss
  of the broadest load)
- Reserve deployed to highest-priority nodes first

**What DED pre-builds (not Phase 1 action — peacetime investment):**
- Type A contracts with LPT manufacturers for 50–100 units held in regional DED facilities
- Type A logistics contracts with heavy-haul rail and transport companies (Schnabel car
  availability confirmed annually)
- Pre-engineered foundation designs for the most common LPT configurations (reduces
  installation time from weeks to days at priority substations)
- CRHP electrician and transformer specialist roster: 2,000+ certified technicians
  enrolled in DED reserve; activated within 48 hours

**Realistic deployment timeline:**
Without reserve: 12–24 months for replacement LPT delivery
With DED reserve + pre-committed logistics: 10–30 days for priority substations

This is the difference between a Tier 3 multi-year outage and a Tier 2 multi-month outage.
The reserve doesn't prevent the attack — it prevents the attack from causing permanent damage.

## Program 2: Emergency Fuel Priority Contracts

### The Generator Fuel Problem

During an extended outage, the entire economy runs on stored fuel:
- Hospitals: diesel generators with 24–96 hour tanks (varies by facility)
- Water treatment plants: diesel or natural gas; 24–72 hours without resupply
- Emergency services (police, fire, 911): diesel vehicles and generators
- Communications infrastructure: cell towers typically have 8 hours of battery backup
  + generator capability
- Gas stations: require electricity to pump (pumps fail with power); also need resupply trucks

The cascade mechanism: fuel distribution requires functional roads + gas stations + dispatch
coordination. A major outage disrupts all three simultaneously. Without pre-committed
priority access, every critical facility is competing in the same degraded market.

### DED Pre-Committed Fuel Priority Contracts (Type A)

**Structure:**
- DED holds Type A contracts with major fuel distributors (Pilot/Flying J, Love's, regional
  distributors) committing to priority delivery tiers during declared grid emergency
- Tier 1 deliveries (first 24 hours): hospitals, water treatment plants, emergency dispatch,
  dialysis centers, nursing homes with life-safety power requirements
- Tier 2 deliveries (24–72 hours): communications infrastructure (cell towers, internet
  exchange points, cable headends), correctional facilities, National Guard armories
- Tier 3 deliveries (72+ hours): grocery cold storage, fuel stations on priority evacuation
  routes, public transit

**Fuel reserve positioning:**
DED pre-positioned contracts with regional fuel distributors maintain a 96-hour
pre-filled distribution buffer for Tier 1 facilities within each FEMA region — contracts
specify minimum on-hand fuel at 12 regional distribution hubs, at DED's cost during the
hold period.

**Activation:** DED Emergency Declaration automatically activates fuel priority contracts;
distributors begin Tier 1 deliveries within 6 hours of contract activation.

**Cost:** $200–400M/year for pre-positioned buffer maintenance; $800M–2B activation cost
for a 30-day Tier 2 event (fuel at above-contract prices, delivered against pre-committed
logistics).

## Program 3: CRHP Mobilization — Electricians and Grid Workers

### Workforce as the Critical Path

The physical restoration of a damaged grid requires skilled labor: linemen, substation
electricians, transformer installation specialists, SCADA technicians. Utilities maintain
mutual aid agreements (the Edison Electric Institute's Emergency Recovery program), but
these were designed for weather events — large numbers of linemen repairing downed lines,
not for specialized transformer installation or control system remediation under adversarial
conditions.

A Tier 2–3 attack simultaneously:
1. Increases labor demand dramatically (restoration work across a wide geographic area)
2. Reduces labor supply (workers in the affected region can't travel; communications are degraded)
3. Introduces security constraints (control systems may be actively compromised; only
   cleared technicians can work on them)
4. Requires specialized skills (LPT installation, high-voltage testing, SCADA forensics)
   that are in short supply nationally

### DED CRHP Deployment

The DED Civilian Reserve Human Program (CRHP) includes a **Grid Restoration Reserve Track**:
- 3,000+ journeyman and master electricians enrolled in DED reserve; grid restoration
  certified (NEMA and NERC standards)
- 500+ LPT installation specialists; trained on reserve unit configurations
- 200+ SCADA security technicians; DOE clearance-eligible
- 100+ power systems engineers; transmission planning and protection relay expertise

**Activation sequence:**
- Day 0: DED Emergency Declaration → CRHP mobilization order issued
- Day 0–4: CRHP members notified; travel logistics coordinated through pre-committed
  lodging and transportation contracts (Type A)
- Day 4–7: First wave CRHP deployed to priority restoration sites alongside utility crews
- Day 7–30: CRHP sustained deployment; 90-day maximum contract term

**Compensation:** CRHP members receive 1.5× their normal labor rate during deployment,
paid from DED emergency reserve. No new appropriation required for first 90 days.

## Program 4: Emergency Procurement Authority and Priority Manufacturing

### The Equipment Pipeline Problem

Beyond LPTs, a grid cyberattack requiring physical remediation needs a range of equipment:
- High-voltage circuit breakers (6–18 month lead time)
- Power cables and conductors (4–12 months for custom specifications)
- SCADA replacement hardware (varies; some components single-source)
- Substation control panels and protection relays (3–9 months)
- Emergency generator sets for critical facilities (2–6 months at scale)

Without pre-authority, government procurement of these items requires contracting processes
that take months to initiate — months the affected region does not have.

### DED Emergency Procurement Authority

**Pre-authorized under DED Enabling Act:**
- DED Secretary can invoke emergency procurement authority (simplified acquisition threshold
  raised to $50M; award within 72 hours) on Tier 2+ grid emergency declaration
- DPA Title III activation: DED coordinates with DOC to invoke Defense Production Act
  Title III priority ratings for grid restoration equipment; manufacturers required to
  prioritize DED orders over non-critical commercial orders
- Pre-positioned contracts (Type A) with major grid equipment manufacturers
  (ABB, Siemens Energy, GE Vernova, Hitachi Energy) for priority manufacturing queue
  commitments: DED pays a retainer to maintain a priority slot in the manufacturing
  schedule; no cost unless activated

**Pre-committed manufacturing retainers:**
- ABB, Siemens Energy, GE Vernova: 10 LPT manufacturing slots committed per quarter
  across their U.S. facilities; DED retainer ~$20M/year per manufacturer
- SE and similar: circuit breaker and control equipment priority queue

**Emergency generator reserve:**
DED maintains contracts with generator rental companies (Aggreko, Atlas Copco Power,
United Rentals) for priority deployment of large mobile generator sets to critical
facilities that exhaust their backup power:
- 500 MW equivalent of mobile generation on 48-hour deployment contracts
- Prioritized for: hospitals (Tier 1), water treatment (Tier 1), emergency services (Tier 1)

## Program 5: Attack Isolation and Control System Continuity

### The Adversarial Dimension

Unlike a hurricane, a grid cyberattack is ongoing during the response period. The attacker
may:
- Maintain persistent access to grid control systems during restoration efforts
- Use restoration activities as intelligence to identify remaining targets
- Interfere with remediation by re-attacking systems being restored
- Have pre-positioned malware at multiple nodes waiting for second-stage activation

This means restoration is not purely a physical/logistics problem — it is an active cyber
conflict being managed simultaneously with physical restoration.

### DED Coordination Role

**CISA and DOE primary; DED supporting:**
DED does not have cybersecurity operational authority. The roles are:
- CISA: incident response; adversary attribution; vulnerability remediation
- DOE CESER: grid-specific cybersecurity response; NERC coordination
- NERC: reliability coordination; interconnect management
- Utility operators: restoration decisions under NERC emergency operating procedures
- **DED: pre-positioned economic resources** (reserve, fuel contracts, CRHP, equipment)
  that support the operational response without duplicating it

**Air-gapped backup control systems:**
DED Phase 2 co-investment built air-gapped backup control systems at priority substations.
In Phase 1 emergency, DED activates the transition-to-backup-control contracts:
- Substation operators authorized to disconnect from digital network and operate on backup
  analog controls immediately on DED emergency declaration
- CRHP substation control specialists deployed to priority sites to operate backup systems
  until digital systems can be forensically cleared

**Isolation boundary contracts:**
DED pre-committed with grid operators to define and enforce **islanding boundaries** —
the geographic scope within which a damaged portion of the grid can be isolated and operated
independently, preventing cascade into adjacent regions. DED pays the grid operators to
maintain these boundaries in ready-to-activate status; activation is a control room decision
within the operator's existing authority.

| Program | Peacetime Cost (pre-build) | Phase 1 Activation Cost |
|---------|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| Strategic Transformer Reserve (pre-position) | $2–4B (Type A contracts; held units) | $0 additional (already held) |
| Transformer logistics (retainers) | $50–100M/year | $100–300M per event (transport) |
| Fuel priority contracts (buffer maintenance) | $200–400M/year | $800M–2B per 30-day event |
| CRHP Grid Track (enrollment and training) | $100–200M/year | $500M–1B per 90-day deployment |
| Equipment manufacturing retainers | $60–90M/year | $1–3B per Tier 2 event (activated orders) |
| Mobile generator reserve | $80–120M/year | $200–500M per 30-day deployment |
| **Total peacetime (annual)** | **~$500M–900M/year** | |
| **Total Phase 1 activation (30-day Tier 2)** | | **~$3–7B** |

**Comparison:** LLNL and DHS estimate a sustained Tier 3 grid attack causing LPT destruction
across 9 key substations would cost $1–2 trillion in economic damage and require 12–18 months
for full restoration. DED Phase 1 pre-positioning (~$500M/year ongoing + $3–7B activation)
is insurance against a $1–2T event — the expected value math is strongly positive even at
low probability of attack.

## Phase 1 → Phase 2 Transition Criteria

Phase 2 (Recovery and Hardening) begins when:
- Immediate life-safety threat (generator fuel, water treatment) stabilized; Tier 1 facilities
  have 30+ day fuel reserves secured
- Physical damage assessment complete; LPT replacement schedule established
- Affected grid region operating at Tier 2 islanded capacity (not full, but stable)
- Attack vector identified and initial isolation measures in place

Phase 2 objectives: accelerate physical restoration; harden surviving infrastructure
against follow-on attack; expand air-gapped control capability; domestic LPT manufacturing
surge.
