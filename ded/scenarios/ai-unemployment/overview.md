# Scenario: AI-Driven Structural Unemployment Shock

## Scenario Definition

Rapid AI and automation adoption displaces 15–30% of the workforce from their
current occupations within a 5–10 year window — faster than labor markets,
education systems, or social safety nets can adapt — causing sustained high
unemployment, political instability, and reduced consumer demand.

This is structurally different from other scenarios: there is no single disruption
to contain. The disruption is the transformation itself.

---

## Why This Belongs in DED

The department's mandate is not only acute disruptions but also **transitions
that overwhelm adaptation capacity**. AI displacement is slow-motion by comparison
to a blockade or pandemic, but its aggregate economic disruption potential is larger.

## Phase Responses

### Phase 1 (0–3 years): Safety Net Expansion

- **Unemployment insurance modernization:** Extend duration and coverage for AI-displaced workers
- **Retraining voucher system:** Portable, worker-controlled retraining accounts
- **Early warning system:** Track AI adoption rates by sector and occupation quarterly

### Phase 2 (3–10 years): Labor Market Transition

- **Federal transition jobs program:** Government as employer of last resort for transition period
- **Human-complementary sector investment:** Healthcare, education, skilled trades — areas where AI augments rather than replaces
- **Work-sharing programs:** Reduce hours per worker rather than eliminating jobs

### Phase 3 (Structural): Income and Work Redefinition

- **Productivity dividend policy:** Tax mechanisms to share AI productivity gains broadly
- **Care economy investment:** Paid caregiving, community work, recognizing non-market labor
- **Universal basic services:** Healthcare, education, housing as a floor regardless of employment
