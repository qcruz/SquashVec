# AI Unemployment Scenario — Phase 2: Structural Labor Market Transition

**Scenario:** AI-Driven Structural Unemployment
**Phase:** 2 of 3
**Timeline:** 24–120 months from Phase 1 trigger
**Trigger:** Phase 1 stabilization metrics met; retraining pipeline producing at scale;
  ATC revenue self-sustaining; displacement rate structural (no cyclical recovery expected)
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 2 Objective

Where Phase 1 maintains the economic floor during displacement, Phase 2 restructures
the underlying labor market so that the long-run economy captures the productivity gains
of AI while distributing the benefits broadly enough to maintain the consumer demand
base and tax revenue that sustain both the civilian economy and defense readiness.

The DED's Phase 2 instruments are longer-horizon fiscal and structural investments —
not emergency stabilization. The question changes from "how do we prevent catastrophic
income loss?" to "what does a post-transition labor market look like, and what economic
conditions does the DED need to pre-build to get there?"

## Key Programs (to be built out)

### 1. Human-Complementary Sector Investment

*TODO: Full investment case*

AI augments rather than replaces labor in domains requiring physical presence, judgment
in novel situations, emotional intelligence, and hands-on skill. These are the growth
sectors of a high-AI economy. The DED pre-positions the investment that builds them:

- **Skilled trades expansion:** Type B contracts with apprenticeship programs for
  electricians, plumbers, HVAC technicians, construction. The housing shortage and
  grid buildout require millions of skilled tradespeople; AI cannot build a house.
- **Care economy formalization:** Caregiving (elderly, disabled, pediatric) is AI-resistant
  and chronically underpaid because it is undervalued by market mechanisms. DED-adjacent
  fiscal instruments that raise the effective wage in care occupations via tax credits
  to employers of care workers, funded from ATC revenue.
- **Advanced manufacturing (co-bot operations):** The Phase 1 retraining pipeline
  produces workers who can operate AI-augmented manufacturing systems; Phase 2
  Type B contracts fund the facilities where those workers are employed.

### 2. AI Productivity Dividend Mechanism

*TODO: Full policy design*

The economic case for AI is that it increases total output per worker. Phase 2 requires
a fiscal mechanism that shares some of that productivity gain beyond capital holders:

- **ATC revenue deployment:** Phase 2 expands ATC-funded programs from retraining to
  broader worker support instruments — portable retirement accounts, reduced-hour work
  guarantees for workers over 55 in transition
- **Profit-sharing mandate for ATC payers:** Companies above a defined ATC threshold
  that do not offer profit-sharing programs face a higher ATC rate; companies with
  qualifying profit-sharing receive a reduction. Creates incentive for equity participation
  without mandating it.
- **Equity crowdfunding infrastructure:** DED pre-designs the legal and financial framework
  for worker equity participation in AI-deploying companies — allowing workers to hold
  a stake in the productivity gains from systems that replaced their previous roles

### 3. Work-Sharing and Hours Reduction Programs

*TODO: Full design*

Rather than layoffs, companies that reduce total labor hours due to AI efficiency gains
can deploy work-sharing: distribute the reduced hours across the workforce rather than
concentrating unemployment on specific workers.

- Federal work-sharing UI framework: workers on reduced hours collect partial UI for
  the reduction; companies maintain workforce attachment; government smooths the income
  transition at lower total cost than full unemployment claims
- Historical precedent: Germany's Kurzarbeit (short-work) program; maintained employment
  during 2008–2009 with <5% unemployment peak vs. 10% in the U.S.

### 4. Defense Industrial Workforce Guarantee

Phase 2 must ensure the defense manufacturing workforce is not lost to the AI transition.
DED and DoD jointly fund Type A readiness contracts with defense manufacturers that:
- Maintain minimum skilled workforce levels in precision manufacturing occupations
- Fund on-the-job AI-augmented manufacturing training
- Provide enhanced retention incentives for workers in ODRI Tier 2 occupations critical
  to the defense industrial base
