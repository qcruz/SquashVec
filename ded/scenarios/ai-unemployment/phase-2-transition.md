# AI Unemployment Scenario — Phase 2: Structural Labor Market Transition

**Scenario:** AI-Driven Structural Unemployment
**Phase:** 2 of 3
**Timeline:** 24–120 months from Phase 1 trigger
**Trigger:** Phase 1 stabilization criteria met: retraining pipeline operational at scale;
  ATC revenue self-sustaining at ≥$40B/year; ODRI stabilized (no new tier escalations for
  6+ consecutive months); net job creation in AI-created categories (see `ai-job-creation.md`)
  exceeding 500,000/year nationally
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 2 Objective

Phase 1 prevents catastrophic income collapse during the acute displacement period.
Phase 2 answers the harder question: **what labor market structure does a high-AI economy
need, and what does the DED build to get there?**

The frame shifts from emergency stabilization to structural investment. Three economic
realities define Phase 2's design:

**1. AI displacement is permanent, not cyclical.**
Unlike a recession — where workers return to the same jobs when demand recovers — AI
displacement removes job categories. The Phase 1 retraining pipeline addresses this at
the individual level. Phase 2 addresses it at the sector level: actively building the
sectors where AI is a complement to human labor rather than a substitute, so the economy
absorbs displaced workers into structurally growing roles rather than structurally declining ones.

**2. AI generates an enormous productivity surplus — which currently flows entirely to capital.**
A company that replaces 100 workers with AI systems and pays the ATC levy may still
capture $50–200M in annual productivity gains above the levy cost. Phase 2 designs the
fiscal and contractual mechanisms that redirect a portion of this surplus toward workers
and toward the AI-resistant sectors where labor demand is highest.

**3. The care economy and skilled trades are structurally short-staffed and AI-resistant.**
The U.S. has a simultaneous crisis of too many workers displaced from AI-exposed jobs and
too few workers in physically-present, judgment-intensive roles. The home care workforce
faces **6.1 million projected job openings by 2034** — the single largest labor demand
sector in the country. The construction trades need **439,000 new workers in 2025 alone**
with an 80,000/year electrician gap projected for a decade. Phase 2 channels ATC revenue
into closing this structural mismatch.

## Program 1: Human-Complementary Sector Investment

AI is not equally threatening across all occupations. Labor economics research consistently
identifies a cluster of roles where AI augments but does not replace human workers: those
requiring sustained physical presence, novel judgment in ambiguous situations, emotional
attunement, and manual dexterity in unstructured environments. These are not residual
jobs left after AI takes the rest — they are the structurally growing sectors of a
high-AI economy, and they are already severely undersupplied.

Phase 2 uses ATC revenue to build the three highest-priority human-complementary sectors.

### 1A. Care Economy Formalization and Wage Elevation

**The problem:**

The direct care workforce — home health aides, personal care workers, childcare workers,
disability support workers — is 3.2 million people (2024) and growing fast. It is the
sector with the largest projected job growth in the U.S. economy: 772,000 net new jobs
2024–2034, with 6.1 million total openings over that period when turnover is included.

These roles are almost entirely AI-resistant: they require physical presence in someone's
home, continuous judgment about individual needs in novel situations, and the kind of
trust built through sustained human relationship that no AI system can replicate.

They are also chronically and severely underpaid:
- Median wage: **$17.36/hour** ($34,900/year)
- Annual turnover rate: **nearly 80%** — among the highest of any occupation
- 36% of direct care workers live in or near poverty
- 49% rely on public assistance programs to make ends meet

The market failure is structural: care is primarily purchased by individuals and families
(who cannot afford high prices) and by Medicaid (whose reimbursement rates are set by
state budgets, not by labor market wages). The gap between what families and Medicaid
can pay and what it costs to provide quality care at a living wage is a persistent market
failure that no private mechanism will correct.

**DED instrument: Care Employer Tax Credit (CETC)**

A refundable employer-side tax credit funded from ATC revenue, targeting the gap between
current wages and a care-sector living wage floor:

| Qualifying Care Occupation | Current Median Hourly | Target Hourly | CETC Credit per Hour |
|---------------------------|----------------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Home health aide | $17.36 | $24.00 | $4.50 (partially offset by employer) |
| Personal care aide | $16.55 | $23.00 | $4.00 |
| Childcare worker | $15.82 | $22.00 | $4.00 |
| Disability support worker | $18.20 | $25.00 | $4.50 |

*Credit covers approximately 70% of the gap between current market wage and target; employer
contributes the remaining 30%, ensuring some market skin-in-the-game.*

**Fiscal cost and funding:**
- Approximately 3.2M care workers × $4.25/hr average credit × 1,800 hrs/year = **~$24.5B/year**
- Funded from ATC revenue (current $70–110B/year capacity; Phase 2 ATC allocations
  re-balanced to reflect reduced Phase 1 emergency demand as stabilization succeeds)

**Expected labor market outcomes:**
- Turnover reduction from ~80% to ~40% (wage increases of this magnitude have documented
  2:1 effect on care sector retention in state-level studies)
- Net new care workforce entrants: estimated 600,000–900,000 over 3 years as wages become
  competitive with adjacent service and retail occupations
- Reduction in public assistance reliance among care workers: estimated $8–12B/year in
  SNAP, Medicaid, housing subsidy savings as care worker household incomes rise above
  thresholds
- DoD readiness benefit: a stable care workforce reduces the number of military families
  who leave service due to inability to find reliable childcare or elder care

**Credentialing infrastructure:**
DED Phase 1 retraining system (AI Retraining Flywheel) is extended to include care
economy credential tracks: Certified Home Health Aide (CHHA), Certified Nursing Assistant
(CNA), and Early Childhood Educator credentials via AI-personalized curriculum.
Workers displaced from AI-exposed occupations can complete these tracks in 8–16 weeks
with CRHP enrollment option at completion.

### 1B. Skilled Trades Expansion Program

**The problem:**

The U.S. construction and skilled trades workforce faces a structural deficit that is
simultaneously a housing crisis, an infrastructure crisis, and an economic drag:

- **32% labor shortage** across residential construction in 2025
- Construction industry needs **439,000 new workers in 2025** to meet current demand
- **80,000 electrician openings per year** for the next decade; only 7,000 new electricians
  enter annually while 10,000 retire
- **22% of tradespeople are over 55** — workforce aging accelerates the deficit
- Housing starts down **18%**; project completion extended from 7 to 11 months
- Combined annual economic drag of construction labor shortage: **$10.8B** in carrying costs
  and lost housing production
- AI infrastructure buildout (data centers, grid expansion) requires an additional
  **500,000+ construction workers** over the next decade — demand is growing, not shrinking

These roles are AI-resistant by nature: building, wiring, plumbing, and HVAC work requires
physical dexterity in unstructured environments, judgment in novel on-site conditions, and
manual skill that no current or near-term AI system can replicate. Average construction
workers already earn **24% more than the average private-sector employee**.

**DED instrument: Trades Accelerator Program (TAP)**

Type B contracts with:
1. **Registered apprenticeship programs** in electrician, plumber, HVAC, carpenter,
   ironworker, and pipefitter trades — DED contracts fund expanded apprenticeship slots
   at union halls, community colleges, and trade schools; target: 300,000 new apprenticeship
   starts per year by Year 3 (up from ~200,000/year nationally)
2. **Accelerated pre-apprenticeship tracks** for displaced workers: 12-week intensive
   programs that prepare workers for immediate apprenticeship entry (Phase 1 retraining
   flywheel extended with hands-on lab components); DED CRHP participants completing
   pre-apprenticeship tracks receive automatic apprenticeship placement guarantee
3. **Vocational program capital grants** to secondary schools and community colleges:
   DED funds shop equipment, lab space, and instructor training to rebuild the vocational
   education infrastructure that was dismantled over the prior three decades
4. **Employer retention incentive** for apprenticeship completion: 15% tax credit to
   employers who retain an apprentice for 12+ months post-certification

**Target:** 1 million new skilled tradespeople credentialed within 5 years.

**Fiscal cost:**
- Apprenticeship program contracts: ~$3–5B/year
- Vocational capital grants: ~$2B over 3 years (one-time infrastructure)
- Accelerated pre-apprenticeship funding (for AI-displaced workers): drawn from Phase 1
  ATC retraining pipeline (already funded)
- Employer retention credit: ~$1–2B/year in revenue expenditure

**Interaction with WDF (Workforce Development Floor):** The internship floor
(`proposal/workforce-development-floor.md`) and the TAP apprenticeship tracks are
complementary: the floor creates entry-level on-ramps in large employers; TAP creates
the apprenticeship pipeline for the SME-dominated trades sector where the floor's
100-FTE threshold leaves most employers exempt.

### 1C. AI-Augmented Manufacturing (Co-Bot Operations)

**The problem and opportunity:**

Advanced manufacturing — precision machining, electronics assembly, specialty materials
production, pharmaceutical manufacturing, aerospace components — is not disappearing. It
is transforming: human workers operate alongside AI-guided robotic systems (co-bots)
that handle repetitive precision tasks while humans manage exceptions, quality judgment,
maintenance, and system supervision. This is the archetype of AI-complementary labor.

The challenge: workers displaced from AI-exposed occupations in back-office and data roles
do not naturally convert to manufacturing operations. The skills gap is real. And
manufacturing facilities require capital investment that won't happen without demand
certainty.

**DED instrument:**
- **Type B contracts** with advanced manufacturing facilities that are expanding co-bot
  operations and can absorb AI-displaced workers with defined training investment
- Contract structure: DED pays 50% of on-the-job training costs (capped at $15,000/worker)
  for workers entering from Phase 1 retraining or CRHP tracks; facility commits to
  retaining workers for 24+ months
- **Manufacturing Zone incentives:** In counties with ODRI Tier 1 or 2 displacement
  concentration, DED offers enhanced Type C contracts for manufacturers who locate or
  expand operations in those counties — directing investment to where the displaced
  workforce is, not where land is cheapest

## Program 2: Federal Work-Sharing Framework

### The Design Problem

When a company implements AI and reduces its required labor hours, the default market
response is layoffs: some workers are fully employed; others lose their jobs entirely.
The social cost (UI, displacement, retraining, household instability) concentrates on
the laid-off workers. The company captures the full productivity gain.

Work-sharing offers an alternative: reduce total hours across the workforce proportionally,
rather than concentrating unemployment on specific workers. Workers on reduced hours collect
partial unemployment insurance for the reduction. The company maintains workforce attachment.
The government absorbs a share of the income bridge at lower total cost than full unemployment.

### Historical Precedent

Germany's Kurzarbeit program during the 2008–2009 recession:
- Used by **1.4 million workers and 63,000 employers**
- Germany's unemployment rose from **7.2% to 7.6%** (0.4 percentage point increase)
- Comparison: U.S. unemployment rose from **5.8% to 9.2%** (3.4 pp increase) over the same period
- EU average: +1.9 pp
- Econometric analysis: **1 pp increase in short-time work take-up rate = 1 pp decrease
  in unemployment** — a near 1:1 relationship

The U.S. already has a statutory framework for this: **Short-Time Compensation (STC)**,
established under FUTA and available in ~29 states. It is severely underutilized because:
1. It is state-administered with inconsistent eligibility rules
2. Employers are unfamiliar with it
3. During AI-driven reduction (not a temporary recession), companies may not expect
   to restore hours — which is the opposite of STC's traditional assumption

### DED Program Design: AI Work-Sharing Initiative (AWSI)

**Eligibility:** Any employer that documents AI-driven hour reductions affecting 10+ workers
(a reduction of ≥10% of average weekly hours per worker, sustained for 90+ days).

**Benefit structure:**
- Workers on reduced hours collect partial UI equal to the UI benefit × the fraction of
  hours reduced. Example: 20% hour reduction = 20% of the worker's UI benefit as a supplement
- Government pays 75% of the benefit; employer pays 25% — creating employer skin-in-the-game
  while keeping participation economically rational compared to full layoff
- Maximum duration: 24 months per reduction event (AI-driven reductions are structural;
  AWSI provides a bridge, not a permanent supplement)

**Federal standardization:**
DED works with DOL to establish a federal STC floor standard — minimum eligibility,
minimum benefit formula, federal co-funding — that all states implement, ending the
patchwork that prevents national utilization. States with existing STC programs can
grandfather their rules if at least as generous as the federal floor.

**AWSI-to-retraining pipeline:**
Workers enrolled in AWSI for 12+ months are automatically offered Phase 1 retraining
tracks at the DED AI Retraining Flywheel — using the reduced-hours time for credentialing.
The employer maintains the employment relationship; the worker builds new skills. This is
the Kurzarbeit logic applied to structural AI transition rather than cyclical recession.

**Fiscal cost:**
- Estimated AWSI uptake: 2–4 million workers at any given time in peak transition period
- Average benefit supplement: ~$180/week × 50 weeks = ~$9,000/worker/year
- Government share (75%): ~$6,750/worker/year
- At 3M enrolled workers: **~$20B/year** peak; declining as retraining pipeline graduates workers
- Funded from ATC revenue; ATC payers who implement AWSI (rather than layoffs) receive a
  1.5 percentage point ATC rate reduction — aligning the levy with work-preserving behavior

## Program 3: AI Productivity Dividend Mechanism

### The Distribution Problem

The macroeconomic case for AI is productivity growth: AI increases output per worker, per
dollar of capital, per unit of time. If this productivity gain is broadly distributed,
a high-AI economy is wealthier across the income distribution. If it concentrates entirely
with capital holders, it is a displacement event for labor without compensation.

The ATC levy (Phase 1) is the first instrument: it taxes AI-driven labor cost savings and
redirects a share to affected workers. Phase 2 adds instruments that create ongoing equity
participation — so workers in AI-augmented workplaces have a stake in the productivity
upside, not just protection from the downside.

### 3A. ATC-Linked Profit-Sharing Incentive

**Design:** Companies above a defined annual ATC payment threshold ($5M/year) that offer
a qualifying profit-sharing or equity participation program to non-executive employees receive
a **2 percentage point ATC rate reduction** (from 18% to 16%). Companies without a qualifying
program face no change.

**Qualifying program criteria:**
- Covers all full-time non-executive employees
- Distributes at least 5% of pre-tax operating profit to employees annually, OR
- Grants equity (restricted stock or phantom equity equivalent) worth at least 0.5% of
  base salary per year
- Program must not be backloaded (cannot vest entirely at 10 years; at least 25% must
  vest within 3 years)

**Incentive logic:** A company paying $50M/year in ATC saves $5.6M/year at the reduced
rate. Setting up a qualifying profit-sharing program costs approximately $2–8M/year in
distributions at a 5% profit-share threshold (for a large company). The ATC reduction
covers the program cost, creating a cost-neutral or cost-positive case for adopting it.

**Expected reach:** Approximately 2,000–3,000 companies are likely above the $5M ATC
threshold; covering ~15–20M workers in AI-intensive industries. If even 40% adopt qualifying
programs, 6–8M workers gain equity participation in the AI productivity surplus.

### 3B. Worker Capital Account — Displacement Savings Account (DSA) Expansion

The Phase 1 DSA is a portable savings account matched by ATC revenue at $1:$1 up to
$2,000/year for workers in ODRI Tier 1 occupations. Phase 2 expands the DSA's function:

**DSA investment option:** DSA holders may direct up to 50% of their DSA balance into
a **DED-administered AI Economy Fund** — a low-cost index fund investing in publicly
traded AI infrastructure companies (AI chip manufacturers, cloud computing platforms,
automation hardware). Workers in AI-disrupted occupations can hold a financial stake in
the AI economy that displaced them.

The fund is administered by the DED OCES office at near-zero management cost (passive
index construction). Workers who cannot participate in direct employer equity (because
they work for private companies, or because employer programs have not yet adopted
profit-sharing) have a path to AI economy participation through the DSA.

## Program 4: Defense Industrial Workforce Guarantee

### The Readiness Problem

The AI transition poses a specific threat to the defense industrial base that operates
on a different timeline from the civilian economy. Defense systems have procurement
cycles of 10–20 years. A precision machinist, a shipyard welder, a specialty materials
engineer, or a ballistic systems quality controller cannot be trained in 6 months when
a defense program needs them. If AI transition erodes these specific workforce
concentrations — even if the workers move to growing service or care roles — the
defense production capacity cannot be rapidly rebuilt.

The DoD has documented this risk: the shipbuilding workforce (critical for naval
readiness) has been in decline for 30 years. Precision manufacturing occupations for
aircraft and missile systems are concentrated among workers above 50. These are ODRI
Tier 2 occupations — not facing immediate AI displacement, but vulnerable to general
workforce transition if the broader economic signal sends workers out of manufacturing.

### DED Program Design

**Joint DED-DoD Defense Industrial Workforce Guarantee (DIWG):**

Type A contracts with defense prime contractors and their tier-1 suppliers, requiring:

1. **Minimum workforce floors:** Companies holding DED-DoD DIWG contracts maintain
   minimum employment levels in designated precision manufacturing and systems integration
   occupations, regardless of AI efficiency gains. Floor is set at 90% of the 3-year
   average workforce in covered occupation categories.

2. **AI-augmented training investment:** DED co-funds on-the-job training for defense
   manufacturing workers in AI-augmented production systems. Workers gain skills that
   make them more productive in their existing roles, rather than being displaced by
   the AI systems. DIWG participation = DED covers 60% of training cost; employer covers 40%.

3. **Retention incentive:** $15,000/worker retention bonus for qualified workers in
   covered ODRI Tier 2 defense occupations who remain employed at their current facility
   for 5+ years. DED-funded; paid at years 3 and 5.

4. **Succession pipeline:** DIWG contractors commit to accepting 2 DED-retraining
   graduates per 100 DIWG-covered workers annually, with 24-month employment guarantees.
   This pipeline converts Phase 1 retraining graduates into defense manufacturing workers,
   maintaining the industrial base while creating defined employment for displaced workers.

**Fiscal cost:**
- DIWG contracts: ~$4–6B/year across 50–80 qualifying prime and tier-1 contractors
- Worker retention bonuses: ~$1–2B/year
- AI-augmented training co-funding: ~$500M–1B/year
- Retraining pipeline placement administration: ~$200M/year

**DoD contribution:** DoD provides the DIWG-enrolled facilities with enhanced
procurement preference (5% price preference over non-DIWG facilities on competitive
bids) — making DIWG participation financially attractive without requiring DED to
fund the entire cost.

## Program 5: Phase 2 ODRI Transition Monitoring

NEID expands the Phase 1 ODRI from displacement monitoring to **structural transition
tracking** — measuring not just where displacement is occurring but whether the Phase 2
investment is successfully channeling workers from disrupted to growing sectors.

| Metric | Source | Phase 2 Target |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Care sector job openings vs. placements | BLS JOLTS + DED placement data | Placement rate >50% of openings |
| Skilled trades apprenticeship starts | DOL registered apprenticeship database | 300,000/year by Year 3 |
| AWSI enrollment | DOL STC reporting | Peak 3M workers; declining by Year 4 |
| DSA AI Economy Fund enrollment | DED OCES | >1M workers by Year 3 |
| Profit-sharing program adoption rate | IRS ATC filings | >35% of eligible ATC payers |
| DIWG covered worker retention rate | DED contract reporting | >92% annual retention |
| ODRI Tier 1 occupation count | DED monitoring | Declining quarter-over-quarter by Year 2 |
| Net labor force participation rate | BLS household survey | Recovering toward pre-AI baseline |
| Median wage in care/trades sectors | BLS OES | +15% real over Phase 2 period |

**Phase 3 transition criteria:**
Phase 2 transitions to Phase 3 (long-run resilience and normalization) when:
- Net labor force participation rate has recovered to within 1 percentage point of
  pre-AI-displacement baseline
- ODRI Tier 1 occupation count is declining for 18+ consecutive months
- Care and skilled trades sectors show sustained wage growth above inflation for 3+ years
- AWSI enrollment is below 1M workers and declining
- DIWG workforce floors are being met by 95%+ of enrolled contractors without penalty

## Phase 2 ATC Revenue Rebalancing

Phase 1 ATC allocations are weighted toward emergency stabilization (extended UI, stimulus,
student loan suspension). As Phase 1 conditions stabilize, Phase 2 rebalances the statutory
allocation toward structural investment:

| Program | Phase 1 ATC Allocation | Phase 2 ATC Allocation |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Extended UI | 35% | 20% (displacement rate falling) |
| Displacement stimulus ($2,500) | 20% | 10% |
| Retraining tax forgiveness | 15% | 15% (sustained) |
| Student loan suspension | 10% | 5% |
| CRHP subsidy | 10% | 10% (sustained) |
| DSA match | 10% | 10% (sustained) |
| **CETC (care employer credit)** | — | 15% (new) |
| **TAP (trades accelerator)** | — | 10% (new) |
| **AWSI (work-sharing)** | — | 5% (new) |
| **Total** | **100%** | **100%** |

*Phase 2 ATC total projected: $80–130B/year at mature AI adoption scale. Rebalancing
does not require new legislation — the enabling statute grants DED Secretary authority
to adjust sub-allocations within statutory caps as Phase conditions change.*

## Phase 2 Cost Summary

| Program | Annual DED Cost | Funding Mechanism |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| CETC (care worker wage elevation) | ~$24.5B/year | ATC revenue |
| TAP (trades accelerator) | ~$4–7B/year | ATC + Type B contracts |
| Co-bot manufacturing | ~$2–4B/year | ATC + Type B contracts |
| AWSI (work-sharing) | ~$15–20B/year (peak) | ATC revenue (ATC payers self-fund partially) |
| ATC profit-sharing incentive | ~$0 net (rate reduction offset by program savings) | ATC rate structure |
| DSA AI Economy Fund | ~$0 admin (passive index) + Phase 1 DSA match | ATC revenue |
| DIWG (defense industrial) | ~$6–9B/year | DED + DoD joint |
| **Total annual Phase 2** | **~$52–65B/year (peak)** | Primarily ATC revenue |

**Net fiscal position:** ATC revenue at $80–130B/year more than covers the $52–65B/year
Phase 2 cost, with the surplus flowing into Phase 1 stabilization programs (declining but
still active), the ARBF bond guarantee fund, and general fiscal offset.

The political economy argument: every dollar of ATC Phase 2 spending is funded by the
productivity gains of AI-deploying companies — not by general taxpayers. The companies
that benefit from AI-driven labor cost reduction fund the programs that absorb the workers
they no longer employ and build the workforce they will need for the next decade.
