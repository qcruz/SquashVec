# AI Unemployment Scenario — Phase 1: Economic Triage and Stabilization

**Scenario:** AI-Driven Structural Unemployment
**Phase:** 1 of 3
**Timeline:** 0–24 months from DED trigger declaration
**Trigger:** BLS displacement rate in AI-vulnerable occupations exceeds 2× long-run average
  for 2 consecutive quarters; OR unemployment in any 3 of the 5 highest-risk occupation
  categories rises >5 percentage points within 12 months
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 1 Objective

AI-driven displacement differs from cyclical unemployment in a critical way: the jobs do
not return when the economic cycle recovers. The DED's Phase 1 objective is not to delay
displacement — that is not economically achievable — but to ensure that the transition period
does not permanently damage the economic position of displaced workers, does not destroy the
consumer demand base that the broader economy depends on, and creates the financial conditions
for retraining and sector mobility.

All DED interventions must address specific rising-cost or falling-income economic indicators.
Phase 1 focuses on: (1) income floor maintenance for displaced workers via financial instruments,
(2) strategic contracts that retrain toward DED-priority skill shortages, and (3) pre-positioned
financial incentives that make companies undergoing AI transition invest in workers rather than
only in technology.

### 2. Income Stabilization — Financial Instruments

*TODO: Full program design*

- **Extended TRAL activation:** In AI-displacement-declared zones, TRAL advance rate increases
  to 75% of expected refund. Workers in documented AI-displaced occupations receive 24-month
  income bridge at no interest. See `ded/tools/tax-advance-loans.md`
- **Displacement savings account (DSA):** Pre-tax savings vehicle — funded by both employer
  and worker contributions — that pays out at 100% on documented AI displacement. Employers
  who contribute to DSAs receive a DED-adjacent tax credit; creates financial incentive for
  companies to pre-fund worker transition rather than paying severance only
- **Portable benefits:** DED pre-designs the federal framework for portable health, retirement,
  and training benefits that follow the worker rather than the employer — removing the financial
  cliff that makes unemployment catastrophic for workers who do not have employer-sponsored plans

### 3. Strategic Contracts for Retraining Infrastructure

*TODO: Full contract design*

- Type B contracts with community colleges and technical training providers for capacity build-out
  in DED-priority skill areas: grid maintenance, water systems, advanced manufacturing,
  cybersecurity, agricultural technology
- These contracts serve dual purpose: they build the workforce the DED scenarios require,
  and they create employment for displaced workers in the training pathway itself (instructors,
  program administrators, lab technicians)
- CRHP training tracks as retraining pipelines: displaced workers who enroll in CRHP and
  complete relevant service tracks receive the healthcare benefit (TRICARE) and a DED-recognized
  credential that counts toward Type D contract eligibility

### 4. Transition Tax Credits — Making Companies Pay for Transitions

*TODO: Full design*

- Companies that deploy AI systems displacing more than 50 employees in a 12-month period
  owe a "transition contribution" — structured as a tax on the labor cost savings from AI
  deployment, paid into a federal retraining fund
- The contribution rate: 15% of documented annual labor cost reduction from AI deployment,
  for 5 years. A company that saves $100M/year in labor by deploying AI contributes $15M/year
  to the retraining fund.
- This is not a penalty on AI deployment — it is a correct pricing of the externality.
  The company captures the economic benefit; the fund compensates the workers who bear the cost.
