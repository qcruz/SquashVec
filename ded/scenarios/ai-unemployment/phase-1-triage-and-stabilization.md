# AI Unemployment Scenario — Phase 1: Economic Triage and Stabilization

**Scenario:** AI-Driven Structural Unemployment
**Phase:** 1 of 3
**Timeline:** 0–36 months from DED trigger declaration
**Trigger:** BLS displacement rate in AI-vulnerable occupations exceeds 2× long-run average
  for 2 consecutive quarters; OR unemployment in any 3 of the 5 highest-risk occupation
  categories rises >5 percentage points within 12 months; OR corporate AI capital expenditure
  displaces a documentable 500,000+ workers in a single fiscal year per WARN Act filings
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 1 Objective

AI-driven displacement is structurally different from every other DED scenario because
the disruption is not an external shock to be contained — it is a domestic productivity
transformation. The correct policy response is not to slow it. AI adoption that displaces
routine work while increasing output per worker is economically beneficial in aggregate.
The DED does not oppose that transition.

What the DED opposes is the unmanaged version: a transition where aggregate economic
gains are captured entirely by capital holders while individual workers absorb catastrophic
income losses with no bridge, no retraining infrastructure, and no social safety net
designed for structural (rather than cyclical) displacement.

**The economic concerns Phase 1 addresses:**
- Falling household income for displaced workers → falling consumer demand in retail,
  housing, and services → second-order employment losses in non-AI sectors
- Spiking UI, Medicaid, and disability claim costs that strain federal and state budgets,
  crowding out defense and infrastructure appropriations
- Tax revenue erosion as wage income compresses: each 1% reduction in employment in
  high-wage AI-exposed occupations costs ~$8–15B in annual federal income tax revenue
- Defense manufacturing and skilled industrial workforce erosion as workers leave
  precision-skill occupations for AI-resistant but lower-productivity alternatives
- Long-term human capital degradation if the transition cohort ages out of retraining
  viability — a workforce that was displaced at 45–55 and never retrained becomes a
  permanent dependency on Social Security Disability, not a recoverable economic asset

**What Phase 1 does not do:** The DED does not subsidize employers to delay AI adoption,
does not restrict AI deployment, and does not operate job placement or career counseling
services. Those functions belong to DOL, state workforce agencies, and the private sector.
The DED pre-positions the financial instruments and infrastructure contracts that make
the transition economically survivable — and redirects displaced labor toward sectors
the DED scenario library identifies as chronically undercapitalized.

## 1. Economic Intelligence — Occupation-Level Monitoring

The primary analytical failure in managing AI displacement is the lag between occupation-level
disruption and aggregate economic indicators. Aggregate unemployment can remain stable for
years while specific occupation categories are hollowed out — the displaced workers move
to lower-wage alternatives, cycle through UI, or exit the labor force. By the time aggregate
unemployment rises, the affected cohort has already been permanently economically damaged.

The DED monitors displacement at the **occupation and sector level**, not the aggregate level.

### AI Vulnerability Classification

The DED's National Economic Intelligence Division maintains a continuously updated
**Occupation Displacement Risk Index (ODRI)** based on:

- BLS Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) codes mapped to automation exposure
  probability (reference: Frey and Osborne 2013 Oxford study; McKinsey Global Institute
  automation potential by task type; updated annually with new AI capability evidence)
- Real-time corporate AI capex announcements filtered by sector and deployment type
- WARN Act 60-day advance layoff notifications — filtered for technology-driven mass layoffs
- BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW) by 4-digit NAICS and SOC
- Social Security Disability Insurance application rates by occupation — a leading indicator
  of workforce exit that precedes formal unemployment statistics by 12–18 months
- Early retirement rate spikes in high-exposure occupation categories

### Leading Indicator Dashboard

| Indicator | Watch Threshold | Phase 1 Trigger |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| ODRI displacement rate, top-5 occupations | +50% vs. 3-yr avg | +100% sustained 2 quarters |
| WARN Act filings — tech-driven layoffs | 200k/quarter | 400k/quarter |
| SSDI applications, high-exposure occupations | +15% annualized | +30% annualized |
| Early retirement rate, age 50–62 | +10% annualized | +20% annualized |
| Consumer spending, AI-exposed income quartile | -5% real | -12% real |
| Federal income tax receipts, wage income | -3% vs. forecast | -8% vs. forecast |
| Community college enrollment, displaced workers | Baseline tracked | — (monitoring only) |

The ODRI and tax revenue indicators are the DED's primary triggers because they are
the leading economic indicators of downstream budget and demand damage — not just
individual worker hardship.

## 2. AI Transition Contribution — Self-Funding the Response

Before designing income support programs, the DED establishes the funding mechanism
that makes them self-sustaining. The principle: the companies that capture the economic
benefit of AI-driven labor cost reduction contribute to the cost of transitioning the
workers who bear the loss.

### The Mechanism

Companies that document labor cost savings from AI deployment above a defined threshold
pay an **AI Transition Contribution (ATC)** — structured as a levy on those savings,
not a penalty on AI investment.

**Calculation:**
- Baseline: Any company that reduces employment in AI-exposed occupation categories
  by more than 100 workers in a 12-month period files an annual ATC disclosure with
  Treasury, documenting the total labor cost reduction from those reductions
- Rate: 18% of documented annual labor cost savings from AI-displacement events,
  assessed for 5 years from the first qualifying event
- Example: A financial services firm deploys AI that eliminates 500 analyst and data
  entry positions previously costing $80M/year in salary + benefits. ATC = $14.4M/year
  for 5 years = $72M total contribution from a single deployment event.

**What this is not:**
Not a tax on AI investment. Not a tax on automation generally. Not applied to productivity
improvements that do not reduce employment. Applied only to documentable labor cost
reductions from displacement — the externality is priced, not the technology.

**Revenue estimate:**
At AI capital expenditure trajectories projected through 2030 (Goldman Sachs: $200B+/year
in AI investment, displacing an estimated $400–600B in annual labor costs in aggregate),
an 18% ATC on documented displacement savings generates **$70–110B/year at full-scale
displacement** — fully funding the income stabilization programs below without requiring
new general appropriations.

**Legislative design note:**
ATC is structured as a per-worker excise tax equivalent, not a corporate income tax
adjustment, to simplify collection and reduce avoidance through accounting reclassification.
Administered by IRS; verified against WARN Act filings and BLS QCEW data.

## 3. Income Stabilization — Financial Instruments

### Displacement Savings Account (DSA)

**Design:** A pre-funded portable savings vehicle established for every worker in an
ODRI high-exposure occupation, jointly funded by employer and worker contributions:
- **Employer contribution:** 2% of salary annually for workers in ODRI Tier 1–2 occupations
  (high and very-high displacement risk). Partially funded by ATC tax credit offset for employers.
- **Worker contribution:** Voluntary, up to $3,000/year, pre-tax (matched by employer up to 1%)
- **Payout:** 100% lump sum on documented AI displacement; tax-free
- **Average balance at displacement:** $15,000–$40,000 for a worker with 5–10 years of
  contributions — enough to cover 6–12 months of expenses during retraining

**Why this is superior to severance:** Severance is paid by the employer at displacement,
creating incentive to minimize it and dispute eligibility. DSA funds are already the
worker's — vested, portable, and not contestable. The worker arrives at displacement
with a financial bridge already in place.

**DED role:** DED designs the DSA framework and the ATC tax credit that incentivizes
employer contribution. IRS administers. DOL certifies qualifying displacement events.

---

### TRAL Crisis Escalation — AI Displacement Track

The Tax Return Advance Loan program includes a pre-designed crisis escalation mode.
In counties or sectors where ODRI Tier 1 displacement has been declared:

- Advance rate escalates from steady-state 50% to **75% of expected tax refund**
- Extended repayment: 36-month 0% installment option (from standard 24-month)
- Workers with documented ODRI Tier 1 displacement in the prior 12 months are
  eligible for the AI-displacement track regardless of geography

This is not a new program — it is a pre-authorized mode within the existing TRAL
architecture. No new legislation required after Phase 1 declaration; activated by
DED Secretary certification of Phase 1 conditions.

Reference: `ded/tools/tax-advance-loans.md`

### CRHP as the Health Access Bridge

The single largest financial cliff at job loss is healthcare. A worker displaced from
a $65,000 salary job loses employer-sponsored health insurance; COBRA costs $700–1,400/month
for a family; Marketplace premiums for equivalent coverage in this income range are
$400–800/month after ACA subsidies. This is 10–20% of pre-displacement income spent on
maintaining health coverage during transition.

The Civilian Reserve Health Program resolves this directly. CRHP enrollment is open to
any civilian below the current upper age limit; for AI-displaced workers, enrollment
includes a DED-funded subsidy covering the annual fee for 24 months post-displacement
(funded from ATC revenue). In exchange, the enrolling worker completes the standard
CRHP annual training obligations — including enrollment in a DED-skill track that
begins redirecting their labor toward DED-priority infrastructure.

**Effect:** The healthcare cliff is removed. The worker maintains continuous coverage
at near-zero marginal cost. The DED gains a CRHP enrollee redirected toward the workforce
the scenario library requires.

Reference: `ded/proposal/civilian-reserve-health/overview.md`

## 4. Strategic Contracts — Retraining Infrastructure Build-Out

The DED does not operate retraining programs. It funds the infrastructure that makes
retraining economically viable for existing institutions — community colleges, technical
training providers, apprenticeship programs, and the national laboratory system.

### Type B Contracts: Training Provider Capacity Scale-Up

**Target domains for capacity build-out** (selected because they are simultaneously
DED-scenario-critical and AI-displacement-resistant — occupations requiring physical
presence, manual dexterity, and system-specific knowledge that AI cannot cheaply substitute):

| Training Domain | DED Scenario Connection | AI Displacement Resistance |
|-----------------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Grid maintenance and EHV transformer work | Grid Cyberattack, Oil Restriction | Very high — requires field work, physical access |
| Water systems operations | Drought, Pandemic | Very high — requires site-specific knowledge |
| Precision manufacturing (semiconductor fabs) | Semiconductor Collapse | High — complex physical systems |
| Cybersecurity (operational technology focus) | Grid Cyberattack, all scenarios | High — adversarial environment; judgment-dependent |
| Agricultural technology and precision ag | Drought, Oil Restriction | High — field conditions; biological complexity |
| Building electrification and HVAC | Oil Restriction, Grid | High — physical installation; residential access |
| Advanced medical technology operation | Pandemic | Medium-high — patient interaction required |
| Nuclear plant operations and maintenance | Oil Restriction, Grid | Very high — regulatory, safety-critical |

**Contract structure:** Type B developmental contracts with community college consortia
and apprenticeship programs to:
- Build curriculum for each domain above (DED specifications; aligned with industry needs)
- Expand physical training capacity (simulation labs, hands-on facilities)
- Recruit instructors — many sourced from ERP members and CRHP reserve with active credentials
- Guarantee minimum enrollment from DED-certified displaced worker track for 5 years

**The dual-purpose design:** These contracts simultaneously solve two DED problems:
(1) displaced workers have funded, accessible retraining paths; (2) the DED scenario
response plans have a pre-built workforce pipeline for the domains they most need.
The retraining investment is not a sunk cost — it directly capitalizes DED readiness.

### CRHP Service Tracks as Retraining Credential Pipeline

CRHP enrollment provides more than health access — it provides a federal credential.
Workers who complete a CRHP service track (agricultural, infrastructure, medical reserve,
cybersecurity, or industrial) receive:
- A DED-recognized skill certification recognized in federal contract solicitations
- Eligibility for Type D (individual expert reserve) contract consideration as credentials advance
- Priority consideration in CRHP deployment assignments that pay active-duty equivalent rates

This creates a natural progression: displaced worker → CRHP enrollment (healthcare access,
training income) → CRHP service track completion (federal credential, ongoing deployment income)
→ Type D ERP consideration at senior skill levels. Each step is self-funding from ATC revenue.

## 5. The ATC → Benefit Pipeline — The Policy Sell

The AI Transition Contribution is not only a funding mechanism. It is the central
political argument for the entire Phase 1 package, and it should be communicated as such.

**The direct line:**

> *The companies deploying AI that replaces your job pay into a fund. That fund pays you.
> Your transition benefits are funded by the productivity gains your replacement generated.*

This framing is politically durable across coalitions: it does not tax AI companies for
innovating — it prices the externality they create. Pro-business audiences recognize the
externality pricing logic. Pro-labor audiences recognize the accountability structure.
Fiscal conservatives see a self-funding mechanism that requires no general appropriations.

### ATC Revenue Allocation — The Fund Disbursements

Treasury maintains the **AI Transition Fund (ATF)** as a dedicated account. ATC
collections are allocated by statute to specific benefits in defined proportions.
The allocation is not discretionary — it is statutory, meaning the connection between
the levy and the benefit is direct and visible in the budget:

| Benefit | ATF Allocation | Mechanism | Per-Beneficiary Value |
|---------|---------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Extended UI (52 weeks, up from 26) | 35% of ATF | DOL administers; AI-displaced track auto-qualifies | $12,000–18,000 additional benefits per displaced worker |
| Displacement stimulus payment | 20% of ATF | IRS direct payment within 30 days of documented displacement | $2,500 per qualifying event |
| Retraining completion tax forgiveness | 15% of ATF | IRS credit; complete DED-certified program → $25,000 federal tax forgiveness | $25,000 per completed retraining |
| Student loan suspension | 10% of ATF | ED administers; automatic 24-month suspension on displacement, no interest accrual | $3,000–8,000 in avoided interest per worker |
| CRHP fee subsidy for displaced workers | 10% of ATF | DED administers; covers CRHP annual fee for 24 months | Healthcare coverage for $0 marginal cost |
| DSA employer contribution match | 10% of ATF | IRS administers; match employer DSA contributions for companies below ATC threshold | Increases DSA balance for workers at smaller employers |

**The compounding effect:** A displaced worker who accesses all available ATF benefits receives:
- Extended UI: $15,000 additional (average)
- Displacement stimulus: $2,500
- Retraining tax forgiveness: $25,000 (on completion)
- Student loan suspension savings: $5,000 (average)
- Healthcare: CRHP coverage for 24 months (~$15,000 value at commercial equivalent)

**Total: ~$62,500 in direct financial support** over the transition period — funded entirely
by the ATC levy on the companies that deployed the AI that caused the displacement.
This is the number to lead with in any policy presentation.

### Revenue Sufficiency at Scale

ATC rate sensitivity analysis:

| Scenario | Annual AI Labor Cost Reduction | ATC Revenue (18%) | ATF Benefits Funded |
|----------|-------------------------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Conservative (2026–2028) | $150B/year | $27B/year | ~1.8M displaced workers fully supported |
| Base case (2028–2032) | $400B/year | $72B/year | ~4.8M displaced workers fully supported |
| Accelerated (2030+) | $700B/year | $126B/year | ~8.4M displaced workers fully supported |

At the base case, the ATF self-funds the complete Phase 1 benefit package for the full
projected displacement cohort with no additional appropriation. The fund becomes net
positive by Year 3 as early ATC payers' contributions compound while the early displaced
cohort completes retraining and re-enters the workforce as taxpayers.

## 6. The AI Retraining Flywheel

The most overlooked dimension of AI-driven displacement is that the same AI capabilities
that eliminated certain jobs are also the most efficient tools for retraining displaced
workers into new ones. The DED deploys AI as the delivery infrastructure for the
retraining programs funded by the ATC — turning the displacement mechanism into the
recovery mechanism.

**The irony is the point.** Publicizing it is a policy asset, not a liability: the technology
that created the transition is being put to work solving it. This is a narrative the
private sector will fund alongside the government because it demonstrates AI's societal value.

### Personalized Learning Path Generation

Traditional retraining programs are cohort-based, curriculum-fixed, and slow — designed
for the speed of institutional decision-making, not for individual workers with specific
gaps to close on a financial deadline.

AI-powered learning path generation, deployed across DED-contracted training providers:
- **Skills gap assessment:** Worker inputs current skills and credentials; AI maps against
  target occupation requirements and identifies the specific gap, not a generic curriculum
- **Custom curriculum:** AI assembles a personalized learning sequence from existing
  course content, simulation modules, and competency exercises — targeting only the gaps,
  not re-teaching what the worker already knows
- **Adaptive pacing:** Progress monitoring adjusts difficulty and pacing in real time;
  a worker who advances fast moves faster; a worker who needs repetition gets it without
  falling behind a cohort schedule
- **Estimated time compression:** A traditional 2-year community college retraining program
  can be completed in 9–14 months for a worker who already has relevant foundational skills.
  AI-personalized paths have demonstrated 30–40% time reduction in military and corporate
  training programs (reference: DARPA Accelerated Learning program; Amazon Upskilling initiative).

### AI-Administered Certification

Credentialing is the bottleneck that turns fast learning into slow employment. Traditional
certification cycles are tied to testing windows, institutional schedules, and human evaluator
availability. An AI-administered competency certification system eliminates the bottleneck:

- **Continuous assessment:** Worker demonstrates competency on-demand — not on a semester
  calendar. A worker ready for certification on day 67 gets certified on day 67.
- **Multi-modal evaluation:** AI assesses written responses, simulation performance, code
  execution, and video-demonstrated physical procedures — covering both cognitive and
  applied skill in a single session
- **DED credential issuance:** DED-certified training providers issue DED-recognized credentials
  through the AI certification platform. These credentials are directly readable by federal
  contracting systems, CRHP enrollment systems, and ERP eligibility databases — no translation
  layer between certification and employment pathway
- **Fraud resistance:** AI-administered exams with biometric verification and behavioral
  anomaly detection; credential integrity is verifiable on-chain or through a DED-maintained
  digital registry

### Real-Time Job Matching

Credentials sitting in a registry are not employment. The DED closes the loop with
an AI-powered matching layer that connects certified workers to active openings in
DED-priority sectors:

- **Priority queue for federal contract positions:** Every DED Type A/B/C contract that
  creates employment requirements feeds openings to the matching system; DED-certified
  workers are pre-qualified, reducing employer screening cost to near zero
- **CRHP deployment matching:** Certified workers eligible for CRHP deployment receive
  automatically generated deployment offers matched to their skill profile and geographic
  availability
- **Private sector relay:** With worker consent, credentials and skills profiles are shared
  with DED-partner employers in priority sectors — creating a direct channel from DED
  certification to private employment in AI-resistant occupations

### The Flywheel Effect

```
ATC levy on AI deployment
        ↓
Funds AI-powered retraining infrastructure
        ↓
Displaced workers retrain 40% faster via AI-personalized paths
        ↓
Certify faster via AI-administered continuous assessment
        ↓
Match faster to DED-priority sector openings
        ↓
Re-enter workforce as taxpayers (reduces net ATF cost)
        ↓
DED-priority sectors (grid, water, precision mfg) build out faster
        ↓
Increased infrastructure capacity reduces economic vulnerability costs
        ↓
Lower long-run scenario response cost → more ATF surplus available
        ↓
Reinvest surplus in next retraining cohort
```

The flywheel generates three compounding returns simultaneously:
1. Workers transition faster → ATF costs per worker fall with each cohort
2. DED-priority sectors build capacity faster → scenario response readiness increases
3. Tax base recovers faster → federal revenue loss from displacement is shorter-duration

The AI displacement scenario is also the DED's primary opportunity to advance the national
AI safety and alignment research that protects critical infrastructure from AI-related failure.

The same AI transition that is displacing workers in financial services and customer support
is also being integrated into power grid management, pharmaceutical manufacturing, defense
logistics, and healthcare systems — creating exactly the national security risks Accelerator
Alpha is designed to address (see `ded/tools/expert-reserve-program.md`).

**Phase 1 connection:** The AI companies undergoing the most aggressive deployment — and
therefore generating the largest ATC contributions — are the same companies whose researchers
are the primary recruitment pool for Accelerator Alpha. The DED uses the ATC funding
relationship to establish the ERP enrollment conversations:

- Companies paying ATC contributions receive preferred Type D ERP enrollment pathways
  for their AI researchers — their best people can serve in the reserve, build the
  national AI security relationship, and receive the ERP benefit package
- Companies whose researchers participate in Accelerator Alpha receive ATC rate reduction
  (not elimination): 12% instead of 18% on documented displacement savings
- This creates the aligned incentive: deploy AI (economic benefit), pay the transition
  cost (ATC), AND contribute your researchers to the safety program that protects the
  national infrastructure your AI is being integrated into

The result is that the companies moving fastest on AI deployment are simultaneously funding
the retraining of displaced workers and contributing to the safety research that prevents
their own AI systems from becoming a national security liability.

## 8. Military Cost Connection

The AI displacement scenario directly affects defense readiness through three channels:

**Channel 1 — Tax revenue and defense appropriations:**
Federal income tax receipts on wage income are the primary funding source for discretionary
spending, including defense. A structural compression of wages in high-exposure occupations —
even without aggregate unemployment increases — reduces federal tax revenue. Every 1% reduction
in annual wages among the top 40% of earners (the AI-most-exposed income range) costs
approximately $12–18B in federal income tax receipts annually. At 10% wage compression
across this cohort over 5 years, the cumulative revenue loss is $60–90B — equivalent to
3–5% of the annual defense budget.

**Channel 2 — Defense manufacturing workforce:**
The U.S. defense industrial base depends on a skilled manufacturing workforce: machinists,
welders, electronics assemblers, precision fabricators, quality control technicians. These
occupations are ODRI Tier 2–3 — moderate AI exposure, but the workers who leave them
are not easily replaced. A displaced defense-sector machinist who retrains as a delivery
driver is permanently lost from the defense industrial talent pool.

The DED Type B retraining contracts explicitly preserve this pipeline: defense-adjacent
manufacturing skills (precision machining, electronics assembly, materials testing) are
designated retraining target domains under DoD co-funding arrangements, ensuring that
the displaced manufacturing workforce is retrained into equivalent DED/DoD-relevant roles
rather than scattered into non-recoverable alternatives.

**Channel 3 — DoD AI adoption pace:**
The same AI transition that is displacing civilian workers is also available to accelerate
DoD logistics, maintenance planning, intelligence analysis, and procurement management.
But DoD AI adoption lags commercial deployment because the safety and integration frameworks
don't exist. The ATC-funded Accelerator Alpha program produces exactly those frameworks —
accelerating DoD's ability to capture AI productivity benefits while the DED manages the
workforce transition that adoption requires. The two programs are designed as a package.

## 9. Phase 1 → Phase 2 Transition

Phase 1 stabilizes the economic floor. Phase 2 focuses on the structural labor market
transformation: human-complementary sector investment, work-sharing frameworks, and
AI productivity dividend mechanisms that broaden the benefit of AI adoption beyond
capital holders.

Phase 2 triggers:
- AI displacement rate stabilizes but at permanently higher level (displacement is structural,
  not cyclical — the jobs are not returning)
- Retraining pipeline from Phase 1 Type B contracts is producing graduates at scale
- ATC revenue is stable and sufficient to fund Phase 2 expansion programs
- CRHP enrollment from displaced workers has created a sufficient reserve for infrastructure deployment

See `ded/scenarios/ai-unemployment/phase-2-transition.md` [STUB].
