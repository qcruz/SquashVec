# Rare Earth Denial Scenario — Phase 2: Supply Diversification

**Scenario:** Rare Earth and Critical Mineral Supply Denial
**Phase:** 2 of 3
**Timeline:** 18–54 months from supply restriction detection
**Entry Conditions:** Phase 1 transition criteria met (see phase-1 close)
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 2 Objective

Phase 1 bought time. Phase 2 uses it.

The Phase 1 stockpile provides 12–24 months of Tier 1 (defense and clean energy) coverage.
The Phase 1 allied sourcing contracts are producing deliveries. The CMISS inventory dashboard
is running. Phase 1 recycling investments are operational. The panic window has passed.

Phase 2 is the structural response: the period in which new non-Chinese supply chains are
built from mining through processing through finished material — in the U.S., in allied
nations, and through a redesigned international coordination framework that prevents any
single actor from replicating China's position.

**Phase 2 does not assume the Chinese restriction will be lifted.** Planning on a return to
pre-crisis supply relationships is the error every previous critical mineral strategy has
made. Phase 2 assumes the restriction is permanent and builds accordingly.

**Phase 2 success criteria:** By month 54, non-Chinese supply covers ≥50% of U.S. demand
for neodymium-iron-boron magnets, gallium, germanium, and cobalt. Domestic processing
capacity exists at commercial scale for at least two of these four categories. The U.S.
is no longer in a crisis posture — it is in a managed transition to structural independence.

## Program 1: Domestic REE Processing Build-Out

### The Processing Gap

Mountain Pass, California is the largest rare earth deposit outside China by resource grade.
MP Materials has been mining there since 2020. As of 2025, the vast majority of Mountain
Pass concentrate still goes to China for separation and refining — because the U.S. has
essentially no commercial-scale REE separation capability.

This is the central strategic fact of the rare earth supply chain: the U.S. has the ore,
but China has the chemistry. Rare earth separation is chemically complex (the 17 elements
are nearly identical in atomic radius, making separation difficult), capital-intensive, and
historically concentrated in China because China invested in it when no one else would.

Phase 2's primary domestic task is building the separation and refining capacity that
converts U.S. mined concentrate into usable materials.

### DED Program: Mountain Pass Full Integration (Type B + Type A)

**Stage 1 — Separation facility expansion:**
MP Materials completed a Phase 1 separation facility at Mountain Pass capable of processing
a portion of its concentrate domestically. DED Phase 2 extends this with:

- Type B co-investment: $1–1.5B in additional capital for a full-scale separation plant
  capable of processing Mountain Pass's entire annual output (~45,000 metric tons of REE oxide
  concentrate equivalent) into separated individual oxide streams
- Anchor buyer commitment: DED commits to purchase 50% of separated oxide output for 15 years
  at floor prices (90% of 3-year trailing spot); MP Materials sells the remainder commercially
- IP development: Mountain Pass separation facility operates under a technology-sharing
  agreement with Oak Ridge National Laboratory and Ames National Laboratory; process
  improvements developed at the facility enter the DED IP Asset Register and are
  available for licensing to allied processors

**Stage 2 — Alloy and magnet manufacturing:**
Separation produces oxide. Defense and clean energy applications need finished magnets.
The full integration chain is: oxide → metal → alloy → sintered magnet. Currently each
step involves either China or a very small number of non-Chinese producers.

- DED Type B contracts with Urban Mining Company and emerging domestic magnet manufacturers
  (Vacuumschmelze US, Arnold Magnetic Technologies) for:
  - NdFeB alloy production from domestic oxide ($300–500M capital investment)
  - Sintered magnet production line — the highest-value step in the chain ($500M–1B)
- Defense procurement preference: DoD procurement standards require that NdFeB magnets in
  new defense contracts must source from DED-certified supply chains (domestic or allied
  processing) within 36 months of Phase 2 activation; creates the demand pull that makes
  the magnet line investment bankable without full government offtake

**New domestic REE site development:**
Mountain Pass is the largest U.S. deposit but not the only one. DED:

- Fast-track permitting support for Round Top (West Texas, heavy REE deposits including
  terbium and dysprosium — the high-value elements for high-temperature magnets), Bear
  Lodge (Wyoming), and Bokan Mountain (Alaska)
- Permitting support mechanism: DED assigns Expert Reserve Program experts (environmental,
  geological, regulatory) to accompany producers through the permitting process; reduces
  typical 7–10 year permitting timeline by 2–4 years
- Advance purchase commitments for output from new sites on the same floor-price structure
  as Mountain Pass; provides financing certainty before permitting is complete

**Cost:** $3–5B over Phase 2 (36 months); generates domestic REE oxide production at 30–50%
of current U.S. demand by Phase 2 close.

## Program 2: Allied Processing Network

### Why Allied Processing Is the Priority

Even with domestic expansion, the U.S. cannot mine and process all critical minerals it
needs within 5 years. The geology does not support it for all materials (cobalt deposits
in the continental U.S. are limited; graphite is minimal; gallium requires aluminum
smelting operations not easily replicated). And rebuilding an entire domestic supply chain
for fifteen mineral categories simultaneously would require capital and permitting timelines
that exceed the Phase 2 window.

The realistic Phase 2 architecture is: domestic production for the highest-security materials
(NdFeB magnets, gallium for defense semiconductors) + allied production for the rest
(Australian REE separation, Canadian cobalt refining, UK/EU gallium/germanium recovery).

This is not dependence. This is the AUKUS / Five Eyes logic applied to industrial supply:
trusted allies holding complementary positions in a coordinated supply network that no
single actor can deny collectively.

### DED Program: Critical Minerals Allied Processing Initiative

**Australia — REE separation at scale:**

Lynas Rare Earths is building a separation facility in Odessa, Texas (DoD-backed). Its
Mt. Weld mine in Australia is one of the world's richest REE deposits by grade. Phase 1
started the Lynas TX investment; Phase 2 completes it and extends to Australia:

- Type B co-investment: DED provides an additional $800M–1.2B for Lynas to build
  a second-stage Mt. Weld separation facility in Australia capable of producing
  separated heavy REE oxides (terbium, dysprosium, europium) — elements where
  China's position is most concentrated
- Bilateral framework: formalized U.S.-Australia Critical Minerals Partnership with
  10-year take-or-pay commitments; Australian facility output earmarked 40% for U.S.
  defense and clean energy procurement at agreed floor prices
- IP exchange: DED licenses Oak Ridge REE separation IP to Lynas Australia in exchange
  for pricing stability clauses and U.S.-priority allocation during Tier 2/3 scenarios

**Canada — cobalt and nickel refining:**

DRC cobalt is the dominant supply, but most DRC cobalt refining capacity is Chinese-owned
(CMOC, Huayou). The goal is not to replace DRC mining (geologically irreplaceable) but to
build non-Chinese refining so DRC ore can be processed outside China's control.

- Type B investment: DED co-invests with Glencore (Canadian HQ), Vale, and new entrants
  in hydrometallurgical cobalt refining capacity in Quebec and Ontario ($600M–1B)
- Ring of Fire offtake: Phase 1 established Canadian cobalt offtake contracts; Phase 2
  funds the refining capacity that converts those contracts from ore to finished cathode material
- DRC engagement: DED coordinates with State Department on U.S. development assistance
  packages for DRC that are explicitly conditioned on non-Chinese refining agreements for
  U.S.-destined cobalt; replaces Chinese refining with allied refining without disrupting
  DRC mining (which employs hundreds of thousands)

**United Kingdom / European Union — gallium and germanium:**

Gallium is a byproduct of aluminum smelting. Germanium is a byproduct of zinc smelting
and coal combustion. The U.S. and EU have both, but recovery rates are low because
China's historically low prices made recovery uneconomical.

- Type B contracts with Nyrstar (zinc-germanium), Aluminium Dunkerque (France, gallium
  recovery from aluminum smelting), and UK Critical Minerals Institute participants to
  fund byproduct recovery equipment ($400–600M total across allied facilities)
- Guaranteed purchase at 90% of pre-crisis average prices makes recovery projects
  bankable at current prices, not speculative on future shortage
- EUREKA/Horizon coordination: DED coordinates with EU Critical Raw Materials Act
  implementation to ensure U.S. and EU allied processing investments are complementary
  rather than duplicative

**Brazil — REE and niobium:**

Brazil holds large REE deposits (CBMM is already the world's dominant niobium producer —
a DED model for what allied commodity dominance looks like). Brazilian REE mining is
underdeveloped relative to geology.

- Type C contracts for Brazilian REE oxide output; DED floor-price purchase commitments
  for 10 years provide the demand certainty Brazilian producers need to invest
- DED Type B co-investment in Brazilian REE separation capacity — Brazil currently lacks
  domestic separation; investment creates in-country value and reduces dependence on shipping
  unprocessed ore to China
- Bilateral trade framework: Brazil REE processing investment bundled with broader U.S.-Brazil
  economic engagement; positions Brazil as the southern-hemisphere anchor of the allied
  processing network alongside Australia in the eastern hemisphere

**Cost:** $3–5B in Type B co-investment across allied processing facilities;
$5–8B in Type C purchase commitments over Phase 2 (36 months).

## Program 3: Battery Chemistry Diversification

### The Battery Transition Within the Transition

The energy transition requires EV batteries. Current EV battery chemistry (lithium nickel
manganese cobalt oxide — NMC) uses cobalt and nickel, two minerals with significant
Chinese processing dominance. A rare earth denial coincident with an EV transition
push is doubly damaging: the substitute for oil runs on the same supply chains as the
denied material.

Phase 2 accelerates the shift to battery chemistries that reduce or eliminate
Chinese-controlled minerals — not as an experimental technology, but as a procurement standard.

### DED Program: Next-Generation Battery Chemistry Accelerator (Type B + Accelerator)

**Lithium-iron-phosphate (LFP) domestic manufacturing:**

LFP batteries use no cobalt and no nickel. They are lower energy density than NMC but
adequate for most passenger vehicle applications, grid storage, and fleet vehicles. China's
domestic EV market is already majority LFP. The U.S. market is majority NMC because U.S.
manufacturers (GM, Ford, Stellantis) built NMC supply chains before the supply disruption.

- DED Type B co-investment with:
  - Ford BlueOval City LFP line expansion ($600M–1B additional capacity above planned)
  - Redwood Materials LFP cathode active material plant ($400–600M; reduces dependence on
    Chinese cathode material even where LFP chemistry is used)
  - New domestic entrants (American Battery Technology Company, Lithium Americas) for
    integrated LFP production from domestic lithium deposits
- Federal procurement mandate: GSA, USPS, DoD non-combat fleet shifts to LFP-only
  procurement for all vehicles ordered from Phase 2 month 6 onward; creates 500k+
  unit/year anchor demand that funds the scale-up
- LFP grants: DED offers $500/kWh production tax credit (above existing IRA credit)
  for domestically produced LFP cathode material meeting DED certification standards;
  accelerates the economics of domestic LFP vs. imported Chinese LFP

**Sodium-ion battery acceleration:**

Sodium-ion batteries use no lithium, no cobalt, and no nickel. They use sodium (effectively
unlimited domestic supply), iron, and manganese. Energy density is lower than LFP but
adequate for stationary grid storage. This makes them the ideal battery chemistry for
long-duration grid storage — replacing pumped hydro in applications where geography prohibits it.

- DED Accelerator program: $300–500M over 36 months for sodium-ion scale-up
  (Natron Energy, HiNa Battery Technology US subsidiary, CATL's SIB technology licensed
  to U.S. manufacturers as a condition of U.S. market access)
- Grid storage anchor buy: DED commits to purchase 5 GWh of sodium-ion grid storage
  capacity for the Strategic Reserve network; provides the scale-up demand that moves
  sodium-ion from pilot to commercial scale
- Technology transfer: sodium-ion process IP developed under DED Accelerator program
  enters IP Asset Register; available for licensing to domestic manufacturers at below-cost
  rates to accelerate adoption

**Solid-state battery development:**

Solid-state batteries potentially use significantly less lithium per kWh of storage than
liquid-electrolyte designs. DED Accelerator program (Type D, classified) for solid-state
battery development coordinated with DoD (for applications where weight savings matter
most); IP retained by government; licensed to U.S. manufacturers for commercial applications.

**Cost:** $2.5–4B over Phase 2; generates domestic LFP manufacturing at 30–40% of
new EV production by Phase 2 close; sodium-ion at commercial scale for grid storage.

## Program 4: Rare-Earth-Free Magnet Commercial Scale-Up

### From R&D to Production

Phase 1 initiated substitution research — ferrite-based and manganese-bismuth magnet
formulations at 60–80% of NdFeB performance, suitable for 30–40% of applications.
Phase 2 moves the best-performing formulations to commercial production.

### DED Program: Alternative Magnet Production Initiative (Type B)

**Ferrite magnet production expansion:**

Ferrite (iron oxide) magnets are the existing non-rare-earth standard — widely used in
motors, sensors, and speakers, but insufficient for high-performance applications (EV drive
motors, wind turbine generators, guided missile systems). Phase 2 focuses ferrite expansion
on the applications where it is already sufficient but where manufacturers switched to NdFeB
for performance-above-requirement reasons.

- DED procurement standards revision: identify federal procurement categories where
  NdFeB performance exceeds requirement by >20%; mandate transition to ferrite for
  those categories within 18 months
- Type B contracts with Hitachi Metals US, Arnold Magnetic Technologies, and TDK Magnetics
  US for ferrite production expansion: $200–400M total; increases domestic ferrite
  capacity by 40–60% within 24 months

**Manganese-bismuth (MnBi) magnet development:**

MnBi magnets retain magnetic performance at high temperatures — historically the key
advantage of dysprosium-enhanced NdFeB. Phase 1 research at Ames Lab and ORNL has
demonstrated competitive performance at operating temperatures below 150°C. Phase 2:

- DED Accelerator (Type B): $400–600M for MnBi scale-up from laboratory to pilot production
- DoD qualification: DED/DoD joint qualification program for MnBi magnets in defense
  applications (radar cooling systems, actuators, submarine sonar); qualification makes
  MnBi a DoD-approved substitution that downstream contractors can use without case-by-case
  approval
- Commercial licensing: MnBi process IP licensed to U.S. and allied manufacturers;
  DED retains royalty rights that fund continued Accelerator program

**Amorphous metal alloy motors:**

Amorphous metal stators (used in Hitachi's high-efficiency motor designs) can reduce
rare earth content per kilowatt of motor output by 30–50% even when rare earth magnets
are retained. This is a demand-reduction approach within the rare earth magnet category
rather than a substitute for it.

- DED Type B: $150–250M co-investment with amorphous metal motor manufacturers
  (Metglas / Hitachi Metals US) for domestic production scale-up
- Federal efficiency standard: DED and DOE update federal motor efficiency standards
  (EISA 2007 successor) to require amorphous metal stators in new federal procurement
  of motors above 5 HP; reduces federal REE demand by 25–35% within 3 years

**Cost:** $800M–1.3B over Phase 2; reduces NdFeB demand by 15–25% by Phase 2 close
through substitution in applications where alternatives are performance-adequate.

## Program 5: International Mineral Coordination Framework

### The Missing Institution

The G7 has a supply chain resilience commitment (the Minerals Security Partnership, MSP,
launched 2022). The MSP is a coordination forum — it lacks purchase commitment authority,
stockpile-sharing agreements, and a price stabilization mechanism. When China tightens
export controls, every MSP member bids for the same available non-Chinese supply, driving
prices up and defeating the coordination rationale.

Phase 2 builds the institutional infrastructure that converts the MSP from a discussion
forum into an effective collective response mechanism.

### DED Program: Critical Minerals Security Architecture

**Joint G7 Critical Mineral Reserve:**

Modeled on the IEA coordinated oil release mechanism (which successfully defended against
OPEC supply shocks in 1974, 1991, and 2022):

- DED proposes a G7 Critical Minerals Coordinating Committee (CMCC), operationally
  modeled on the IEA's emergency sharing mechanism
- Each G7 member commits to maintaining minimum stockpile levels (30, 60, or 90 days
  depending on mineral and member-state consumption rate) in an agreed set of minerals
- In a declared supply emergency (analogous to IEA's "disruption trigger"), members
  agree to release proportional quantities to a shared allocation pool
- Pricing mechanism: CMCC releases priced at agreed formula (prevents member-state
  stockpile release at extortionate prices to other members)
- The U.S. DED is the institutional anchor; the CMCC is modeled on DED's domestic
  framework scaled to allied coordination

**Allied market access conditionality:**

DED establishes mineral market access as a condition of certain trade and technology
agreements:

- U.S. semiconductor export licenses for advanced manufacturing equipment to allied
  nations explicitly reference mineral supply cooperation in the bilateral framework
- Technology cooperation under AUKUS Pillar 2 (advanced capabilities) includes a mineral
  supply annex: Australia commits to U.S.-priority REE allocation in a declared shortage
  in exchange for U.S. advanced technology transfer
- EU Critical Raw Materials Act alignment: DED/USTR coordinate to ensure U.S. and EU
  strategic mineral stockpile purchases do not compete against each other; joint
  procurement for some categories reduces per-unit cost for both

**Anti-concentration provisions:**

Phase 2's most important long-run design goal is preventing any other single actor from
replicating China's position in the post-Chinese-dominance mineral supply landscape.
Specifically:

- DED allied processing contracts include anti-acquisition provisions: processing facilities
  built with DED co-investment cannot be majority-acquired by an entity domiciled in a
  country on the CMCC restricted list without CFIUS-equivalent review
- IP licensing from DED programs is restricted to CMCC-member nations or certified
  neutral processors; prevents DED-developed REE separation chemistry from flowing
  to a new monopoly actor
- Supply geography targets: DED actively avoids creating a new geographic concentration;
  explicitly targets a five-country minimum for any critical mineral category (no single
  country should exceed 35% of U.S. supply for any mineral in the target set by Phase 3)

**Cost:** $200–400M for U.S. administration and coordination of CMCC; the substantive
cost is the co-investment programs above, not the coordination mechanism itself.

## Program 6: DRC Cobalt Engagement

### The Cobalt Governance Problem

The Democratic Republic of Congo accounts for approximately 70% of global cobalt mining.
This share is essentially irreplaceable on a 5-year horizon — no other mineral region has
comparable deposits at comparable grade. Chinese companies (CMOC, Huayou Cobalt) control
approximately 80% of DRC cobalt refining capacity, either directly or through DRC joint ventures.

The U.S. cannot simply substitute away from DRC cobalt. The geology is what it is. The
strategic goal is to ensure that DRC cobalt reaches the U.S. through non-Chinese refining
channels — so that even if China controls 80% of DRC refining, the U.S. has a guaranteed
non-Chinese refining pathway for the supply it needs.

### DED Program: Congo Basin Minerals Initiative

**U.S.-DRC bilateral minerals agreement:**

DED, in coordination with State Department, proposes a U.S.-DRC strategic minerals
partnership:

- U.S. investment package: $1–2B in DRC infrastructure investment (power, transport,
  processing) through DFC (Development Finance Corporation) funding, conditioned on
  DRC government commitment to:
  - Priority refining access for U.S.-destined cobalt through non-Chinese facilities
  - Environmental and labor standard improvements in artisanal mining (ASM) sector
    (significant child labor and environmental damage from ASM; improving standards
    is both ethically required and reduces Western regulatory resistance to DRC cobalt)
  - Preferred economic partner status for U.S. and allied investors in new refining
    capacity

**Artisanal mining sector formalization:**

Approximately 20–30% of DRC cobalt comes from artisanal mining — informal, largely
unregulated, associated with child labor and safety violations. This supply is currently
laundered into supply chains and is the primary reason Western companies face ESG pressure
on DRC cobalt.

- DED co-funds (with State Department and DFC) a DRC ASM formalization program:
  cooperative mining associations, safety standards, traceability technology (blockchain
  tagging from mine to refinery), labor standards enforcement
- Certification mechanism: DED-certified "traceable cobalt" receives pricing premium
  in U.S. market; creates economic incentive for formalization
- This is not altruism: it is the pre-condition for Western supply chains being able to
  use DRC cobalt without ESG liability, which is the pre-condition for getting DRC cobalt
  through non-Chinese refining channels

**Non-Chinese refining investment:**

- DED/DFC co-investment with Glencore and new entrants in DRC-based cobalt refining
  capacity using non-Chinese technology and management
- Goal: 30% of DRC cobalt refining capacity in non-Chinese hands by Phase 2 close;
  DED-certified traceable supply of at least 60% of U.S. cobalt requirements

**Cost:** $1.5–2.5B in DFC and DED co-investment; generates the DRC supply pathway
that is geologically irreplaceable in any realistic Phase 2 timeframe.

**Comparison:** A Tier 2 rare earth denial with no Phase 2 response — stockpile depletion
followed by uncontrolled shortage — carries an estimated $150–400B in cumulative economic
damage over 5 years. The $17–27B Phase 2 program prevents the majority of that damage
by building physical supply chains before the stockpile is exhausted.

**Phase 2 cumulative with Phase 1:** ~$31–49B over 5 years total. Against the Tier 2
damage estimate of $150–400B, the intervention ratio is 1:5 to 1:13.

## Phase 2 → Phase 3 Transition Criteria

Phase 3 (Structural Independence) begins when:

- Non-Chinese supply covers ≥50% of U.S. demand for NdFeB magnets, gallium, germanium,
  and cobalt (the four highest-security categories)
- Domestic processing capacity exists at commercial scale for at least two of the four
  categories (target: NdFeB and gallium for defense)
- CMCC is operational with all G7 members meeting minimum stockpile commitments
- LFP is at 30%+ of new U.S. EV production; sodium-ion at commercial scale for grid storage
- DRC traceable cobalt supply certified for ≥60% of U.S. cobalt requirement
- No single non-U.S. country supplies >35% of U.S. demand for any mineral in the target set
