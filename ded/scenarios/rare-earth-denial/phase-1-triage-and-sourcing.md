# Rare Earth Denial Scenario — Phase 1: Triage and Sourcing

**Scenario:** Rare Earth and Critical Mineral Supply Denial
**Phase:** 1 of 3
**Timeline:** 0–24 months from supply restriction detection
**Trigger:** Chinese export controls on one or more critical mineral categories; confirmed
  supply disruption from DRC cobalt; or NEID early warning of inventory depletion in
  Tier 1 defense or clean energy production supply chains
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 1 Objective

Rare earth denial does not arrive as a single event. It escalates — export licensing
restrictions, then tighter controls, then selective enforcement, then full embargo. The
2010 Japan rare earth embargo (after a fishing trawler incident) lasted two months; the
2023 gallium/germanium controls have been in effect and tightening for three years.

The Phase 1 window is the period **between the first supply restriction signal and the
point at which production lines in defense, clean energy, and semiconductors begin to
halt**. Depending on existing inventory levels, this window is typically 6–18 months.
DED Phase 1 objective: use that window to:

1. **Assess actual exposure** — distinguish real shortage from precautionary hoarding
2. **Activate stockpiles** — DoD and DED strategic reserves buy time for alternatives
3. **Fast-track allied sourcing** — existing allied deposits contracted before spot
   market price panic absorbs available supply
4. **Begin substitution and recycling** — reduce demand while sourcing alternatives

Every day of Phase 1 response buys time for the Phase 2 supply diversification
investments that take 2–5 years to produce material output.

## Program 1: Critical Mineral Inventory and Surveillance System (CMISS)

### The Information Problem

The U.S. government currently has no real-time view of critical mineral inventory across
the supply chain. When Chinese export controls tighten, the immediate question is:
*How long before production halts?* The answer requires knowing:
- How many months of REE, gallium, germanium, and cobalt do domestic manufacturers hold?
- Where are the inventory concentrations (a few large holders vs. broadly distributed)?
- Which end-uses (defense vs. consumer electronics) have the least inventory buffer?
- Are spot prices rising due to real shortage or speculative hoarding?

Without this information, DED cannot prioritize allocation, cannot size the stockpile
release needed, and cannot calibrate the Phase 1 response duration.

### DED Program: CMISS Implementation

**Voluntary + incentivized quarterly inventory reporting:**
Modeled on NSIVS (semiconductor inventory system from Semiconductor Phase 1), CMISS
establishes quarterly inventory reporting for:
- REEs (neodymium, dysprosium, praseodymium, terbium, europium) by processing stage
  (oxide, metal, alloy, finished magnet)
- Gallium and germanium by form (primary metal, compound, wafer)
- Cobalt by form (cathode, sulfate, metal) and end-use (battery, aerospace, defense)
- Lithium carbonate/hydroxide
- Graphite (natural and synthetic anode material)

**Reporting incentive:** Participants receive priority access to DED stockpile releases
(valued at the spread between spot and contract price during a shortage) in exchange for
quarterly inventory disclosure. Participation is voluntary but the incentive is substantial.

**CMISS → NEID integration:**
Aggregated (anonymized) inventory data feeds NEID's critical mineral dashboard in real
time:
- Days of supply remaining at current consumption rates for each mineral/end-use category
- Alert levels: Green (>12 months), Yellow (6–12 months), Orange (3–6 months), Red (<3 months)
- Triggers: Orange alert → Phase 1 activation recommendation; Red → automatic Phase 1
  declaration

**NEID early warning capability:**
NEID continuously monitors Chinese export license approval rates, processing facility
output reports, and shipping data. A declining approval rate is a leading indicator of
tighter controls — NEID can identify the escalation pattern 6–12 months before it produces
an inventory crisis, enabling Phase 1 pre-positioning before public announcement.

**Cost:** $150–250M setup; $80–120M/year operating. Eliminates the information vacuum
that currently means the government learns of a shortage from industry press releases
rather than from continuous monitoring.

## Program 2: Strategic Stockpile Activation and Expansion

### What Exists

The DoD National Defense Stockpile (NDS) holds some REEs and critical minerals, but:
- Inventory levels are classified; public estimates suggest significant gaps between current
  holdings and recommended levels
- The stockpile is oriented toward DoD end-use, not broader industrial or clean energy demand
- Historical underfunding has left many categories below even DoD's own minimum targets
- Germanium, gallium, and cobalt holdings are generally assessed as inadequate for a Tier 2
  denial event lasting more than 6–12 months

### DED Program: Integrated Stockpile Build (Type A Contracts)

**DED stockpile distinct from NDS:**
DED maintains a separate civilian strategic reserve covering industrial, clean energy,
and semiconductor end-uses that the DoD NDS does not cover. The two reserves are
coordinated through the JCED interagency framework but serve different user communities.

**Priority materials and target levels:**

| Material | Target Coverage | Form | Priority Use |
|----------|----------------|------|-------------|
| Neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB) magnets | 24 months | Finished magnets (DoD spec) | Missiles, motors, radar |
| Neodymium oxide | 18 months | Oxide ready for alloy | Wind turbines, EVs |
| Dysprosium | 24 months | Metal | High-temp magnet coercivity |
| Terbium | 18 months | Metal | Magnet performance additive |
| Gallium | 18 months | Primary metal | Semiconductor LEDs, compound semiconductors |
| Germanium | 18 months | Metal | Fiber optics, IR optics, semiconductors |
| Cobalt | 12 months | Cathode material + metal | EV batteries, aerospace superalloys |
| Graphite (synthetic) | 12 months | Anode material | EV battery production |

**Acquisition strategy:**
DED builds the civilian stockpile through Type A contracts with:
1. Domestic and allied producers (MP Materials, Lynas Australia, SRK Consulting/Canada)
   at pre-agreed floor prices — same advance purchase commitment logic as the SPR
2. Spot purchases when prices fall below the 3-year average — DED maintains a
   $3–5B rotating purchase authorization for opportunistic inventory building
3. Commodity-backed financing: DED issues stockpile bonds backed by the physical
   commodity held in reserve; allows private investors to hold exposure to critical
   mineral prices while funding the government stockpile

**Stockpile release mechanism:**
- CMISS Orange/Red alert triggers automatic release authorization to CMISS-enrolled
  manufacturers at contract prices (below spot during shortage)
- Release priority: Tier 1 (defense manufacturing) → Tier 2 (grid and clean energy
  infrastructure) → Tier 3 (industrial) → Tier 4 (consumer)
- Release rate: capped at 15%/month of total holdings to prevent rapid depletion;
  forces demand rationalization alongside physical release

**Build cost:** $8–12B over 5 years to reach target inventory levels from current gaps;
ongoing $1–2B/year for rotation and replenishment.

## Program 3: Allied Sourcing Fast-Track (Type C Contracts)

### The Allied Supply Geography

The U.S. does not lack REE deposits — Mountain Pass in California is the largest outside
China. What the U.S. lacks is **processing capacity**. China processes 85%+ of global
rare earth output even for material mined elsewhere. A supply denial strategy that focuses
only on mining misses the point: material mined in Australia, Canada, or Greenland still
goes to China for separation and processing.

| Country | What They Have | What's Missing |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Australia (Lynas) | World's largest non-Chinese REE mine; Mt. Weld | Processing (Lynas built a facility in Malaysia, now building in TX) |
| Canada | Large cobalt, nickel, REE deposits | Processing and refining outside China |
| Greenland | Kvanefjeld (REE + uranium); politically complex | Mining permits contested; processing nonexistent |
| Brazil | Significant REE deposits (CBMM niobium already commercial) | REE processing; investment-ready |
| South Africa | PGMs; some REEs | Processing exists for PGMs; REE processing limited |
| DRC | 70%+ of global cobalt mining | Refining dominated by Chinese-owned facilities |

**The critical gap is processing, not mining.**

### DED Program: Allied Processing Capacity Contracts

**Priority 1 — Lynas Texas Facility acceleration:**
Lynas Rare Earths is building a REE separation and processing facility in Odessa, TX
(Defense Production Act co-investment from DoD). DED extends this with:
- Type B co-investment: DED provides $500M–1B in additional facility financing to expand
  Texas capacity to full throughput (currently funded at partial scale)
- Anchor buyer commitment: DED commits to purchase 40% of Texas facility output at a
  floor price equal to 90% of 3-year trailing spot price for 15 years
- This APC converts the Lynas TX investment from uncertain-commercial to bankable;
  enables Lynas to raise private capital at investment grade rather than junk bond rates

**Priority 2 — Australian processing expansion:**
Australia has geologic supply and growing political will (Australia passed Critical Minerals
Strategy 2023; AUKUS framework includes mineral supply commitments). DED:
- Type C contracts with Australian producers: guaranteed purchase of processed REE
  oxide output at floor prices; multi-year framework agreements providing Australian
  industry with the demand certainty needed to justify processing investment
- IP licensing: DED offers access to U.S. REE separation technology developed under
  ORNL and Ames Lab programs in exchange for processing priority commitments and
  pricing stability clauses

**Priority 3 — Canadian cobalt and nickel:**
Canada's Ring of Fire (Ontario) contains enormous cobalt and nickel deposits. Permitting
has been the primary bottleneck (complex Ontario First Nations consultation requirements).
DED:
- Supports Canadian federal government investment in First Nations consultation and
  infrastructure development that unlocks Ring of Fire permitting — not U.S. extraction
  rights, but U.S.-backed investment in the Canadian partner's ability to develop
- Type C contracts for guaranteed purchase of Ring of Fire cobalt at floor prices;
  15-year offtake commitments
- Refining: DED Type B investment in Canadian cobalt refining capacity (currently most
  Ring of Fire cobalt goes to China for refining)

**Phase 1 procurement surge:**
On Phase 1 activation, DED immediately exercises its pre-negotiated options under
Type C allied sourcing contracts for maximum available delivery in the 6–18 month window.
This is not emergency procurement — these are pre-signed contracts with delivery schedules;
Phase 1 simply activates the maximum purchase option.

**Cost:** $3–5B/year in Type C purchases during active Phase 1 (12–24 months); $1–2B/year
in Type B co-investment in processing capacity (multi-year).

## Program 4: Urban Mining and Recycling Activation

### The Domestic Circular Economy Opportunity

The U.S. discards enormous quantities of critical minerals embedded in end-of-life electronics,
EV batteries, and industrial equipment:
- ~200 million consumer electronics discarded annually; contain REEs, cobalt, gallium
- EV battery recycling: growing rapidly as first-generation EVs reach end of life;
  lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese recoverable
- NdFeB magnet recycling: motors, hard drives, wind turbines; REE recovery currently near zero
- Industrial catalysts (petroleum refining, chemical processing): significant PGM content;
  recovery rates improving but well below potential

Current U.S. critical mineral recovery rates from end-of-life products: generally <1–5%
for most REEs; 50–60% for cobalt in some streams; near-zero for gallium and germanium.

### DED Program: Urban Mining Accelerator (Type B)

**Electronics recycling co-investment:**
- Type B contracts with domestic electronics recyclers (Li-Cycle, Redwood Materials,
  American Manganese, Retriev Technologies) for:
  - Capital investment in REE-capable separation lines (currently rare; most recyclers
    do not have REE recovery capability because the economics are marginal without scale)
  - Technology licensing: ORNL and Ames Lab have developed REE separation chemistry
    applicable to recycled feedstock; DED licenses this IP to recyclers at below-cost
    rates (recovering cost via royalty share)
  - Guaranteed minimum purchase price for recovered REEs — same floor price logic as
    Type C allied sourcing; makes the investment bankable

**EV battery recycling fast-track:**
- DED co-investment with Redwood Materials, Li-Cycle, and new entrants to build U.S.
  hydromet processing capacity for EV battery black mass
- Target: 80% domestic recycling rate for EV batteries by 2030 (currently <20%)
- Recovery targets: 95%+ lithium, 98%+ cobalt, 99%+ nickel from hydromet processing
- CMISS enrollment: all major recyclers report recovered material to CMISS; feeds
  the domestic supply picture

**NdFeB magnet recycling:**
- Currently near-zero recovery; significant potential in wind turbine nacelles (2–5 kg
  REE per turbine), hard drives (5–10g each but hundreds of millions units/year), and
  EV motors
- DED Type B with emerging companies (Urban Mining Company, Cyclic Materials UK-US joint):
  $200–400M to build first commercial-scale NdFeB magnet recycling line in the U.S.
- IP development: hydrogen decrepitation and other magnet-to-magnet recycling processes
  under DED Accelerator; IP into Asset Register

**Realistic Phase 1 contribution:**
Urban mining cannot replace primary supply on a 12–24 month Phase 1 timeline — the
infrastructure doesn't exist yet. Phase 1 activation primarily:
- Provides the investment signal that moves recyclers to maximum throughput on existing capacity
- Initiates the Type B investments whose output begins in Phase 2/3
- Reduces marginal demand by recovering material currently wasted, extending stockpile life

**Estimated Phase 1 contribution:** 5–10% of critical mineral demand from recycling
(primarily cobalt and lithium from existing EV battery streams); grows to 15–25% by Phase 2/3.

**Cost:** $1–2B/year in Type B co-investment; $500M–1B in guaranteed purchase prices.

## Program 5: Substitution and Demand Reduction

### The Demand Side of the Equation

Phase 1 focus is supply — but reducing demand for the denied materials buys the same time
as acquiring more supply. Key demand reduction levers activatable within Phase 1:

**Rare-earth-free magnet deployment (Type B accelerator):**
- DARPA GIDDEON and university programs have demonstrated ferrite-based and manganese-bismuth
  magnets with 60–80% of NdFeB performance at zero rare earth content
- DED Type B contracts to bring the best-performing rare-earth-free formulations to
  production scale within 18–30 months
- DoD procurement preference: DED/DoD joint procurement standards begin crediting rare-earth-free
  alternatives in defense procurement evaluations; creates demand pull that funds the scale-up
- Not a complete substitute — high-performance NdFeB remains superior; but 30–40% of
  applications (lower-performance motors, some sensors) can transition without performance loss

**Lithium-iron-phosphate (LFP) battery substitution:**
- LFP batteries use no cobalt and no nickel; already dominant in China's domestic EV market;
  CATL and BYD have demonstrated LFP at competitive cost with NMC
- U.S. domestic LFP manufacturing: nearly nonexistent; DED Type B contracts with
  Ford/SK Innovation (BlueOval City LFP line) and emerging domestic producers to
  accelerate LFP capacity
- Phase 1 mandate: federal fleet procurement (GSA, USPS, DoD non-combat vehicles) shifts
  to LFP-only for orders placed during Phase 1; reduces cobalt demand in federal procurement

**Gallium/germanium efficiency programs:**
- Gallium nitride (GaN) devices use 2–5x less gallium per unit of output vs. older designs;
  accelerated transition to GaN at defense and industrial scale reduces demand
- DED procurement standards require GaN (not older GaAs) for all new defense electronics
  contracts; creates market pull for efficiency improvement

**Demand reduction estimates (Phase 1, 12–24 months):**
- LFP substitution in federal fleet: ~8–12% reduction in government cobalt demand
- GaN efficiency program: ~10–15% reduction in gallium consumption per unit of output
- Rare-earth-free motor pilots: minimal Phase 1 contribution (scale-up takes longer)
- Combined demand reduction: 10–15% of affected materials — meaningful extension of
  stockpile duration without requiring Phase 2 supply investments to materialize

## Phase 1 Resource Allocation Summary

The Phase 1 financial envelope is sized to cover 18–24 months of sustained response:

| Program | Phase 1 Cost (18–24 months) |
|---------|---------------------------|
| CMISS build-out and operation | $400–600M |
| Stockpile acquisition (existing gaps) | $4–6B |
| Allied sourcing Type C purchases (surge) | $6–10B |
| Urban mining Type B investment | $2–3B |
| Substitution program Type B | $1–2B |
| CRHP ERP deployment (materials scientists) | $200–400M |
| **Total Phase 1 (18–24 months)** | **~$14–22B** |

**Comparison:** A Tier 2 rare earth denial event (controls on REEs + battery minerals,
consistent with escalated Taiwan tensions) would cost an estimated $150–400B in supply
chain disruption, defense readiness degradation, and clean energy transition setback over
a 3–5 year period. A $14–22B Phase 1 response that extends the response window, reduces
panic buying, and initiates the allied supply chain that prevents Tier 3 escalation is
among the highest-ROI interventions in the DED portfolio.

---

## Phase 1 → Phase 2 Transition Criteria

Phase 2 (Supply Diversification) begins when:
- CMISS fully operational; real-time inventory picture available
- Stockpile levels at 12+ months coverage for Tier 1 (defense) categories
- Lynas TX and at least one additional allied processing facility online
- Urban mining operational capacity at 10%+ of demand for cobalt and lithium
- Phase 1 sourcing contracts producing material deliveries (not just commitments)
