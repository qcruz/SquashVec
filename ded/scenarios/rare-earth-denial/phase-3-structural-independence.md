# Rare Earth Denial Scenario — Phase 3: Structural Independence

**Scenario:** Rare Earth and Critical Mineral Supply Denial
**Phase:** 3 of 3
**Timeline:** 48–120 months (4–10 years) from supply restriction detection
**Entry Conditions:** Phase 2 transition criteria met
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 3 Objective

Phase 3 is not a crisis response. It is the design of a permanent supply architecture that
makes a critical mineral denial scenario no longer capable of producing an acute crisis.

The distinction matters. Phases 1 and 2 operated in the shadow of the original disruption —
managing inventory, building alternative supply chains, preventing production halts. Phase 3
assumes those emergency measures have succeeded and focuses on the structural conditions that
prevent the next scenario from requiring emergency measures at all.

**Phase 3 success criteria:** By year 10, the U.S. and its allies can absorb a complete
cutoff of Chinese critical mineral exports — including rare earths, battery minerals, gallium,
germanium, graphite, tungsten, and magnesium — without a material production disruption in
defense manufacturing, clean energy infrastructure, or domestic electronics supply. This does
not require domestic self-sufficiency in every category. It requires that no single actor's
supply decision can produce an unmanageable disruption.

## Program 1: End-to-End Domestic REE Supply Chain

### The Goal

Phase 2 built domestic mining and separation. Phase 3 completes the integration: mining →
separation → alloy → magnet → end-product, all within a DED-certified domestic supply chain
capable of serving Tier 1 (defense) demand without import dependency.

This does not mean all NdFeB magnets used in the U.S. economy are domestically produced.
It means the magnets in guided missiles, radar systems, submarine motors, and next-generation
defense platforms are. The defense supply chain achieves full independence; the commercial
supply chain achieves sufficient allied coverage that no adversarial supply action can produce
a defense readiness impact.

### DED Program: Defense Critical Materials Independence (Type A + Accelerator)

**NdFeB magnet domestic supply certification:**

- DED establishes the DED Critical Materials Independence Certification (CMIC) — a supply
  chain certification for defense-grade rare earth magnets covering every processing step
  from oxide to finished magnet
- DoD procurement policy (Phase 3 mandate, building on Phase 2 preference): all new defense
  contracts for systems containing NdFeB magnets must source from CMIC-certified supply
  chains within 48 months of Phase 3 activation
- DED maintains a CMIC supplier registry; any domestic or allied processor meeting the
  certification standards is eligible; this is not a single-supplier system

**Rare earth separation capacity targets:**

By Phase 3 close, the U.S. and allied network should have the following non-Chinese
separation capacity online:

| Category | Phase 3 Target | Primary Sources |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| Light REEs (Nd, Pr, La, Ce) | 100% of U.S. defense demand; 60% of total demand | Mountain Pass, Brazilian producers, Lynas TX |
| Heavy REEs (Dy, Tb, Eu, Gd) | 100% of defense demand; 40% of total demand | Round Top TX, Lynas Australia, Bear Lodge WY |
| Gallium | 100% of defense semiconductor demand | Domestic byproduct + EU allied recovery |
| Germanium | 80% of defense demand | Domestic byproduct recovery + allied |

**Material efficiency mandate:**

DED and DoD jointly establish material efficiency standards for all defense procurement:
- Maximum rare earth content per unit of magnetic flux (Wb/kg); requires manufacturers to
  demonstrate they are using optimized alloy compositions rather than excess material
- Regenerative magnet design standards: defense systems designed for magnet recovery and
  re-sintering at end of service life; reduces lifecycle REE demand per platform
- These standards do not reduce defense capability — they ensure REE content in defense
  systems is minimized to the technically required level, extending stockpile life and
  reducing supply chain exposure

**Cost:** $3–5B over Phase 3 (60 months); achieves defense REE supply independence.

## Program 2: Commercial Supply Chain Resilience Standards

### Beyond Defense

Defense critical materials independence is achievable on a 10-year horizon and is the
non-negotiable core of Phase 3. The commercial economy — EVs, wind turbines, consumer
electronics, industrial equipment — is a different problem. Total U.S. commercial demand for
rare earth magnets is 5–10× defense demand; domestic supply chain independence for all of it
is neither feasible nor necessary.

The commercial resilience target is not independence but diversification: no single actor
should be able to produce an acute shortage in commercial REE supply through a supply
decision. That requires reaching the anti-concentration target (no country >35% of U.S.
supply) and maintaining the allied processing network at scale.

### DED Program: Critical Mineral Supply Chain Transparency and Standards

**Mandatory supply chain disclosure (commercial threshold):**

Building on the Phase 0/Phase 1 CMISS voluntary reporting system:

- Phase 3 legislative action: CMISS reporting becomes mandatory for manufacturers above
  a defined production threshold (recommend: manufacturers with >$500M in annual U.S. sales
  of products containing critical minerals)
- Disclosure format: annual supply chain transparency report filed with DED (similar to
  conflict minerals disclosure under Section 1502 of Dodd-Frank, but covering a broader
  mineral set with a different focus — concentration, not sourcing ethics)
- CMISS mandatory data feeds the NEID commercial mineral dashboard; DED publishes aggregate
  (non-proprietary) concentration risk indicators quarterly so markets can see the systemic
  exposure

**Commercial concentration limits:**

DED establishes Concentration Risk Score thresholds above which regulatory action is triggered:

- CRS > 0.65 for any mineral category: DED issues public exposure notice; NEID dashboard
  shows red alert; DED activates allied sourcing expansion contracts
- CRS > 0.75: DED recommends mandatory supply diversification requirements for new
  federal procurement in affected categories; triggers Strategic Reserve expansion
- The thresholds are pre-announced; the rules activate automatically; this is the
  "automated replenishment rule" applied to commercial supply chain concentration

**Recycling rate mandates:**

Phase 2 built the recycling infrastructure. Phase 3 mandates the recovery rates:

- By Year 8 of Phase 3: 50% minimum recovery rate for REEs from NdFeB magnets in
  end-of-life applications in federally regulated product categories (EVs, wind turbines,
  defense systems)
- By Year 10: 50% minimum recovery rate for cobalt from EV batteries (from currently <20%)
- By Year 10: 25% minimum recovery rate for gallium from electronics end-of-life
- Compliance mechanism: Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) structure — manufacturers
  that sell products containing critical minerals in the U.S. are responsible for ensuring
  the recovery rate is met, either through their own recycling programs or through certified
  third-party recyclers
- DED issues recovery rate credits; manufacturers that exceed the target can sell credits
  to those who fall short; creates a market mechanism for cost-effective compliance

**Material efficiency standards (commercial):**

- DED and DOE jointly establish efficiency standards for commercially sold products in
  high-REE-demand categories (EV motors, wind turbine generators, industrial motors)
- Standards set maximum REE content per unit of rated output; manufacturers must demonstrate
  compliance or seek an efficiency waiver
- Timeline: draft standards Year 3 of Phase 3; final standards Year 5; compliance required
  Year 7; creates 5-year transition window for commercial product redesign

**Cost:** $300–600M for CMISS mandatory system upgrade and DED enforcement capacity;
minimal ongoing cost (industry bears compliance cost through EPR and efficiency standards).

## Program 3: International Minerals Governance Reform

### The Rules-Based Order for Minerals

The current international minerals governance framework is inadequate. The World Trade
Organization's rules on export restrictions apply to goods but have been systematically
circumvented through the use of licensing systems, quotas framed as environmental regulation,
and state-owned enterprise procurement preferences. China's 2010 REE quota and 2023
gallium/germanium controls both exploited these gaps.

Phase 3 requires building an international governance framework that makes weaponized
supply restrictions genuinely costly and reduces their effectiveness.

### DED Program: Critical Minerals Trade Architecture

**WTO Mineral Security Protocol:**

DED, working through USTR and State, advances a WTO-level multilateral agreement on
critical mineral trade:

- Export restriction disciplines: clear rules distinguishing legitimate conservation
  measures (environmental regulation of domestic mining) from prohibited supply weaponization
  (export controls designed to disadvantage specific importing countries)
- Dispute resolution: fast-track WTO panel procedure for critical mineral trade disputes;
  current WTO timelines (3–5 years to resolution) are incompatible with crisis response
- Countermeasure authorization: WTO members affected by a supply weaponization finding
  are authorized to impose proportional trade measures within 90 days of a panel finding;
  reduces the political cost of response

This is a long-run diplomatic project. DED provides the technical analysis — the
CRS methodology, the EAL data, the concentration maps — that supports the U.S. negotiating
position. The CMCC (established in Phase 2) provides the allied coalition for negotiations.

**Free Trade Agreement mineral chapters:**

Every U.S. bilateral and regional FTA negotiated after Phase 3 activation includes a
Critical Minerals Chapter:

- Mutual obligation to maintain minimum strategic stockpiles (30 days equivalent)
- Anti-weaponization commitments: parties agree not to use export restrictions as
  geopolitical leverage against other parties
- Supply priority access: in a declared mineral security emergency, parties with
  available supply agree to offer priority access to other parties at pre-agreed price
  formulas before selling to non-party nations
- These are not hypothetical commitments — the CMCC framework from Phase 2 provides
  the institutional infrastructure to operationalize them

**Chinese market engagement:**

Phase 3 does not assume permanent confrontation with China on minerals. The goal is
leverage, not decoupling. Once the U.S. and allied network covers 50%+ of critical mineral
demand from non-Chinese sources:

- The U.S. has genuine alternatives; it is no longer hostage
- China faces a sustained loss of export market share and the economic damage of
  reduced demand for its processed minerals
- DED establishes a critical minerals dialogue channel with China through USTR and State:
  willingness to restore some market share in exchange for binding supply reliability
  commitments and pricing disciplines
- This is the SRID (Supply Restriction with IP reversal) mechanism applied at scale:
  the U.S. holds IP licensing and technology access as leverage for supply behavior
  commitments; China's access to U.S. chip equipment, agricultural technology, and
  financial services is the negotiating leverage that DED's economic security mandate makes available

**Cost:** State/USTR diplomatic cost; DED provides analytical support; the financial
cost is in the programs above, not the governance architecture itself.

## Program 4: Long-Run Technology Independence

### Reducing Demand, Not Just Supply

The deepest form of strategic independence is not having alternative suppliers — it is
reducing the economy's physical dependence on the at-risk materials. Phase 3's technology
program focuses on the long-run research investments that alter the underlying material
requirements of the economy.

### DED Program: Critical Materials Technology Independence Initiative (Accelerator)

**Rare-earth-free permanent magnet commercialization:**

Phase 1 initiated research; Phase 2 scaled MnBi and ferrite to pilot. Phase 3 targets
full commercial production of rare-earth-free magnets capable of matching NdFeB in a
majority of applications:

- Iron-nitride (Fe₁₆N₂): theoretical magnetic properties comparable to NdFeB with zero
  rare earth content; currently at laboratory scale; DED Accelerator program ($300–500M
  over 5 years) for commercial scale-up at ORNL/Ames Lab in partnership with 2 domestic
  manufacturers
- Cobalt-free NdFeB variants: reduced dysprosium content (15–30% reduction) through
  grain boundary engineering; commercially closer than Fe₁₆N₂; DED Accelerator for
  rapid manufacturing adoption ($150–250M)
- Material genomics: DED funds Materials Genome Initiative expansion specifically for
  permanent magnet alternatives; machine-learning-guided discovery of high-performance
  magnetic materials with lower critical mineral content

**Next-generation battery materials:**

- Solid-state batteries at commercial scale (Phase 2 initiated): Phase 3 transitions
  solid-state from defense applications to commercial EV market; dramatically reduces
  lithium per kWh vs. liquid electrolyte
- Direct lithium extraction (DLE): DLE technologies extract lithium from brines in
  hours rather than months (traditional evaporation pond method); opens domestic
  lithium resources (Salton Sea, Arkansas brines) that are uneconomical with traditional
  extraction; DED Accelerator ($400–600M) for DLE commercial scale-up
- Lithium recycling at 95%+ rate: current lithium recovery from battery recycling is
  70–80%; DED Accelerator for closed-loop lithium recovery process achieving 95%+ from
  all battery chemistries by Year 8

**Semiconductor material alternatives:**

- Gallium oxide (Ga₂O₃) power semiconductors: gallium oxide uses ~30% less gallium
  per device than gallium nitride while offering superior breakdown voltage for power
  electronics; DED Accelerator for Ga₂O₃ manufacturing scale-up
- Germanium-free fiber optics: ZBLAN and silica-core alternatives to germanium-doped
  fiber; already commercially available in specialty applications; DED procurement
  standards push adoption in federal communications infrastructure
- Silicon carbide (SiC) expansion: SiC power semiconductors use no gallium or germanium;
  Wolfspeed domestic production expansion (DED Type B co-investment, $300–400M) for
  defense power electronics substitution

**Cost:** $2–3B over Phase 3 in Accelerator programs; these are 5–10 year technology
investments whose payoff extends well beyond Phase 3.

## Program 5: American Sovereign Fund Space Mining Integration

### The 30-Year Horizon

On a 30+ year horizon, the most durable solution to critical mineral concentration is
access to asteroid and lunar resources, where the relevant materials exist in quantities
that dwarf terrestrial deposits. A single M-type asteroid contains more nickel, cobalt,
platinum, and iron than all of Earth's estimated reserves.

The Space Mining Incentives program (detailed in `proposal/space-mining-incentives.md`)
is primarily a Phase 3 and Phase 4 investment from the rare earth denial perspective —
the technologies are not ready for Phase 1 or 2 timelines. But Phase 3 is when the
investments are made that enable Phase 4 access.

**DED Phase 3 space mining actions:**

- DED Accelerator (Type D, classified) for in-situ resource utilization technologies
  specifically targeting cobalt, platinum group metals, and nickel recovery from near-Earth
  asteroid material — the minerals where Earth-based supply diversification is hardest
- Space Mining International Framework: DED coordinates through State the negotiation of
  a bilateral or multilateral framework establishing liability, property rights, and trade
  rules for space-derived minerals; prevents the space mining sector from developing
  under a legal vacuum that a single actor (Chinese or private) could exploit
- Strategic Space Resource Reserve: DED establishes the legal framework for asteroid-derived
  minerals to be deposited in the national strategic reserve system; the reserve mechanism
  is ready before the supply exists

**Cost:** $1.5–2.5B in Accelerator programs; primarily a technology investment whose
critical mineral return is post-Phase 3.

permanent cost of maintaining the insurance against Tier 2 and Tier 3 scenarios — the
annual premium on a policy that prevents $150–400B events.

## End State: What Structural Independence Looks Like

At Phase 3 close (Year 10), the rare earth denial scenario no longer produces an acute
crisis for the following reasons:

**Supply:** Non-Chinese sources cover >50% of U.S. critical mineral demand across the
target mineral set. No single country supplies >35% of any category. Domestic processing
exists at commercial scale for defense Tier 1 materials.

**Demand:** Commercial demand for the most concentrated minerals (REEs, cobalt, gallium)
has declined 20–35% from the Phase 1 baseline through substitution (LFP, ferrite/MnBi,
Ga₂O₃), material efficiency standards, and recycling mandates.

**Recycling:** Domestic circular economy provides 20–30% of critical mineral demand from
recovered material, reducing dependence on primary supply for both domestic and allied
sources.

**Governance:** CMCC is operational; G7 minimum stockpile commitments are met; WTO
disciplines on mineral supply weaponization are in negotiation or in force; every new
U.S. bilateral FTA includes a mineral security chapter.

**Technology:** Rare-earth-free magnet alternatives (MnBi, Fe₁₆N₂) are in commercial
production at 30–40% cost parity with NdFeB; solid-state batteries are in commercial EV
production; direct lithium extraction is commercial scale.

**Result:** A Chinese decision to restrict all mineral exports — the Tier 3 scenario —
produces a managed adjustment, not a crisis. Stockpiles cover the 12–18 month window
needed for allied sources to fill the gap. Substitution reduces demand. Domestic processing
covers defense. The scenario is expensive but not acute.

This is not independence. It is resilience. The goal was never to decouple from the global
mineral economy — it was to ensure that no single actor's decisions about that economy can
compromise U.S. defense readiness, clean energy transition, or economic stability.
