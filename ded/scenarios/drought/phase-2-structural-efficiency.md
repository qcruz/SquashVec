# Drought Scenario — Phase 2: Structural Water Efficiency

**Scenario:** Regional or Continental Drought
**Phase:** 2 of 3
**Timeline:** 18 months – 7 years from drought declaration
**Trigger:** Phase 1 transition criteria met (see phase-1-demand-management.md)
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 2 Objective

Where Phase 1 manages the immediate allocation of constrained water, Phase 2 restructures
the underlying systems that determine how much water the economy requires. The goal is
a permanent reduction in water intensity across agriculture, industry, and municipalities
— so that a drought of equivalent severity in the future causes less economic damage
than the one that triggered the program.

This phase is where the DED's investment logic shifts from emergency response to structural
value creation: the water efficiency infrastructure built under drought conditions has
lasting economic benefits when the drought ends.

## Key Programs (to be built out)

### 1. National Drip Irrigation Conversion Program

- Subsidized transition from flood and sprinkler irrigation to drip and micro-irrigation
- 40–60% water savings per acre; also improves yields and reduces fertilizer runoff
- Estimated capital cost: $600–$1,200/acre; DED subsidy covers 50–70%
- Target acreage: 10–20 million acres over 5 years (primary targets: cotton, alfalfa, almonds)
- Financing structure: Type B strategic contracts with irrigation equipment manufacturers
  for domestic production scale-up

### 3. Desalination Program Acceleration

*TODO: Coastal state analysis, permitting framework, cost projections*

- California, Texas, and Florida have highest near-term desalination potential
- Current barrier: 5–10 year environmental permitting timelines
- DED fast-track permitting framework: reduce to 18 months for facilities meeting
  pre-approved design standards
- Energy cost is the key variable: desalination + solar/offshore wind integration
- Reference: Israel gets 80% of municipal water from desalination at $0.50–0.60/m³

### 4. Recycled Water Expansion (Non-Potable and Potable Reuse)

*TODO: State-by-state analysis, regulatory model, investment case*

- Indirect potable reuse (IPR): treated wastewater recharged to aquifer then extracted
  as drinking water — already operational in Orange County, CA (provides 100M gallons/day)
- Direct potable reuse (DPR): treated wastewater delivered directly to drinking water system
- Agricultural recycled water: non-potable use for irrigation, currently restricted in most states
- DED role: model state regulations, funding for treatment infrastructure, public communication

### 5. Federal Crop Insurance Restructuring

- Current federal crop insurance incentivizes planting the same high-water crops annually
- Phase 2 restructuring: premium discounts for drought-tolerant variety adoption;
  coverage adjustments that remove artificial subsidy for water-intensive crops in drought zones
- Interaction with ECTP from Phase 1 — make temporary crop shifts permanent via insurance incentive
