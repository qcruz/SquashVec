# Drought Scenario — Phase 2: Structural Water Efficiency

**Scenario:** Regional or Continental Drought
**Phase:** 2 of 3
**Timeline:** 18 months – 7 years from drought declaration
**Trigger:** Phase 1 transition criteria met (see phase-1-demand-management.md)
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 2 Objective

Phase 1 manages the immediate economic stress of constrained water — using financial
incentives to redirect demand, activate spare capacity, and prevent cascading price increases.
Phase 2 restructures the underlying infrastructure that determines how much water the economy
requires in the first place.

The objective is a permanent reduction in water intensity across agriculture, industry, and
municipalities — so that a drought of equivalent severity in the future causes materially
less economic damage than the one that triggered the program. Where Phase 1 interventions
expire when water conditions normalize, Phase 2 investments persist and compound.

**This is the DED's investment thesis for drought:** A crisis is not only a cost.
It is the political window to restructure systems that would not otherwise be touched.
The infrastructure built during drought conditions has lasting value when the drought ends.

### Phase 2 Trigger Criteria (from Phase 1)

Phase 2 activation begins when any of the following are met:
- Phase 1 has been active for 18+ months
- PDSI drought index has remained below -2.0 in 3+ major agricultural basins for 12 consecutive months
- Ogallala water table has declined more than 3 feet in the trailing 24-month period in
  2+ states
- Phase 1 demand management tools have reached full deployment with no improvement in
  reservoir levels or aquifer recharge rates

Phase 1 tools remain active during Phase 2 unless individually de-escalated by DED
economic intelligence monitoring showing conditions met for specific withdrawal.

## 1. National Drip Irrigation Conversion Program

### Problem

The United States irrigates approximately 55 million acres. The dominant irrigation methods —
flood irrigation (most water-intensive) and center-pivot sprinkler systems — lose 30–50%
of applied water to evaporation, runoff, and deep percolation before it reaches plant roots.

In the Great Plains and Southwest, where groundwater is both the primary irrigation source
and a declining nonrenewable resource, this inefficiency is a direct driver of aquifer
depletion. Only approximately 300,000 acres of the 55 million irrigated acres in the U.S.
use drip or micro-irrigation — less than 1%.

Drip irrigation (surface and subsurface) delivers water directly to root zones at
15–30% of the evaporative loss of overhead or flood methods:

| Method | Efficiency | Water Loss | Typical Yield Impact |
|--------|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| Flood irrigation | 50–65% | 35–50% | Baseline |
| Center-pivot sprinkler | 70–85% | 15–30% | +5–10% vs. flood |
| Drip / micro-irrigation | 88–95% | 5–12% | +10–90% vs. flood |

*Water savings of 30–60% per converted acre; yield improvements of 10–30% for most
field crops, up to 90% for high-value fruits and vegetables.*

### Program Design

**Eligible conversions:**
- Surface drip: $800–$1,600/acre installed
- Subsurface drip (SDI): $1,200–$3,500/acre installed (higher durability; 15–20 year lifespan)
- Micro-irrigation for orchards/vineyards: $1,500–$3,000/acre

**DED subsidy structure:**

| Farm Size | DED Subsidy Rate | Notes |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| <500 acres | 70% of installed cost | Maximum accessibility for small operators |
| 500–2,000 acres | 55% of installed cost | Mid-tier; captures most family farms |
| 2,000–10,000 acres | 40% of installed cost | Large commercial; still improves ROI below 3 years |
| >10,000 acres | 25% of installed cost | Corporate operations; partial contribution only |

**Financing:** Remaining cost covered by USDA Farm Service Agency loan at 2.5% interest,
20-year term — making out-of-pocket cash requirement near zero for qualified operators.
Unsubsidized ROI on drip conversion is already 3–5 years through water and energy savings
alone; subsidy accelerates adoption to the Phase 2 timeline.

**Priority targeting:**
1. High-depletion Ogallala counties (Kansas, Texas, Nebraska, Oklahoma panhandle)
2. Colorado River Basin irrigators (Arizona, California Imperial Valley, Colorado, Utah)
3. Central Valley California (groundwater-dependent operations in SGMA-restricted basins)
4. High-value crop operations in drought-declared counties (fastest economic payback)

**Target scale:** 15 million acres converted over 5 years (Phase 2 duration). This represents
~27% of all U.S. irrigated acreage — sufficient to reduce national agricultural water demand
by approximately 18–25% and to essentially halt accelerated Ogallala depletion in the
highest-stress zones.

**DED instrument:** Type B strategic contracts with the domestic irrigation equipment
manufacturing sector to scale domestic production of drip tape, emitters, and controllers.
The conversion program creates a large, predictable demand signal; Type B contracts
ensure U.S. manufacturers can meet it without import dependence. IP developed under
Type B contracts for precision irrigation controls (IoT soil moisture sensors, AI-managed
irrigation scheduling) is retained in the DED IP Asset Register.

**Estimated program cost:**

| Component | Estimate |
|-----------|---------|
| DED subsidy (15M acres × avg $600 subsidy/acre weighted) | ~$9B over 5 years |
| USDA FSA loan facilitation (non-cash; credit risk) | ~$4–6B in outstanding loans |
| Type B manufacturing contracts | ~$2–3B over 5 years |
| Administration and technical assistance | ~$500M |
| **Total DED commitment** | **~$12–15B over 5 years** |

**Expected return:**
- Water savings: 15M acres × 30% water reduction × avg $0.08/m³ water cost = ~$800M–$1.2B/year
  in reduced water costs to operators (private benefit)
- Aquifer preservation: Ogallala depletion avoided by converting 3M high-depletion acres = decades
  of aquifer life extended; agricultural value of Ogallala-dependent production is $20B+/year
- Yield improvement: 10–20% yield increase on converted acreage at $200–400/acre net margin
  improvement = $3–6B/year in additional farm income on converted acres

## 2. Ogallala Aquifer Management Compact

### The Problem: Structural Policy Failure

The Ogallala Aquifer underlies approximately 174,000 square miles across 8 states (South
Dakota, Nebraska, Wyoming, Colorado, Kansas, Oklahoma, New Mexico, Texas) and supplies
irrigation water for approximately 27% of all U.S. irrigated acreage and 30% of groundwater
used for irrigation nationally. An estimated 94% of extracted Ogallala water goes to
agriculture; Ogallala-dependent production accounts for more than $20 billion in annual
food and fiber output.

The depletion is severe and accelerating:
- Average water table decline: **16.8 feet** since large-scale irrigation began (mid-20th century)
- Kansas average: **28.2 feet** of decline; some areas losing 4–6 feet per year
- Natural recharge rate: approximately **0.5 inches per year** against 4–6 feet of extraction
- Approximately 30% of the Kansas portion of the aquifer has already reached "Day Zero"
- At current rates, 70% depletion across the aquifer is projected within 50 years

**The governance failure is not agricultural — it is legal.** Most states governing the
Ogallala use prior appropriation water law ("first in time, first in right"), which gives
earlier water rights holders a legal entitlement to pump without limit up to their permitted
amount regardless of aquifer health. No federal law governs groundwater extraction rates.
No interstate compact requires sustainable yield management. Federal farm policy actively
subsidizes depletion through equipment depreciation deductions and crop insurance structures
that reward planting water-intensive crops.

### DED Program Design

**Phase 1 precondition:** The Phase 1 metering requirement (smart meter installation as a
condition of Type C irrigation contracts and tax credits) provides the real-time extraction
data that makes any management compact enforceable. Phase 2 cannot proceed to binding
extraction limits without Phase 1 metering in place.

**Program: Ogallala Sustainable Yield Compact**

A federal framework compact modeled on the Colorado River Compact, offered to the 8
Ogallala states with financial incentives for participation:

*Compact structure:*
1. **Sustainable yield calculation:** USGS-maintained aquifer recharge monitoring produces
   annual sustainable yield figures by county and sub-aquifer zone — the maximum extraction
   rate consistent with no net long-term depletion. Published annually. This is the reference
   number for all allocation decisions.

2. **Baseline allocation:** Each state receives a baseline annual extraction allocation
   equal to its 3-year average extraction multiplied by a convergence factor:
   - Year 1–3: 95% of baseline (initial reduction; minimal disruption)
   - Year 4–6: 85% of baseline
   - Year 7–10: Convergence to sustainable yield as determined by USGS monitoring

3. **Trading market:** States and individual permit holders can buy and sell extraction
   rights within state allocations. Federal framework establishes the registry and settlement
   infrastructure; states administer within their allocations. Trading markets allow
   economic efficiency — high-value users acquire rights from low-value users rather than
   every user cutting proportionally.

4. **Fallowing payments:** DED provides direct payments to farmers who voluntarily retire
   irrigation rights permanently. Modeled on USDA Conservation Reserve Program but for
   groundwater rights. Payment = 10-year net income value of the land at current crop
   prices — making voluntary retirement economically rational. Estimated cost: $500–$800M/year
   to retire 500,000–700,000 acres of high-depletion cropland annually.

**Federal incentives for compact participation:**
- States that join receive priority access to drip conversion subsidies (Program 1 above),
  USDA drought resilience contracts, and DED Type C water infrastructure contracts
- States that do not join face a progressive reduction in federal crop insurance premium
  subsidy for Ogallala-dependent operations — making non-participation increasingly costly
  without being formally mandated

**Interaction with federal crop insurance restructuring (Program 5):** Phase 2 restructures
the crop insurance actuarial tables for Ogallala-dependent operations to reflect actual
water depletion risk — currently, federal premium subsidies mask this risk and create
artificial economic justification for water-intensive crop production in declining aquifer zones.

## 3. Desalination Acceleration Program

### Context

Israel provides the global benchmark: 5 large-scale seawater reverse osmosis (SWRO) plants
produce approximately 80% of the nation's municipal water supply at a cost of $0.41–$0.65/m³
(Sorek B facility: $0.41/m³ — the current world cost benchmark). Israel has functionally
eliminated its municipal water supply vulnerability through a 20-year desalination investment
program.

The United States has approximately 1,400 desalination facilities, but the vast majority
are small brackish water operations in inland areas. Total SWRO capacity serving municipal
water is negligible relative to national need. The two primary barriers are not technological
or economic — they are permitting and political:

- **Permitting timeline:** A new coastal SWRO facility in California requires 5–10 years
  of environmental review under CEQA, the Clean Water Act (316(b) cooling water intake),
  and the Coastal Zone Management Act. The Poseidon Carlsbad plant (opened 2015, 50 MGD)
  took 17 years from proposal to operation.
- **Energy cost:** SWRO requires approximately 3–4 kWh/m³. At $0.08/kWh (industrial rate),
  energy adds ~$0.24–0.32/m³ to production cost — the primary variable above Israeli
  benchmark levels.

### DED Program Design

**Fast-track permitting framework:**

DED proposes a federal Pre-Approved Design Standard (PADS) for SWRO facilities that
meet defined environmental performance criteria:
- Subsurface intake (slant wells or infiltration galleries) eliminating marine organism
  impingement — the primary 316(b) concern
- Brine dilution to 1.5× ambient salinity at the discharge point
- Facility sited ≥500m from marine protected areas

Facilities meeting all PADS criteria receive:
- Federal environmental review pre-certification (eliminating NEPA de novo review —
  facility reviews the standard, not the project)
- Expedited Coastal Zone consistency determination: 6 months maximum
- Federal permitting pre-emption of state review delays beyond 18 months for facilities
  above 25 MGD serving public water systems in drought-declared areas

**Estimated permitting acceleration:** 17-year → 3-year timeline for compliant facilities
in drought-declared coastal counties.

**Energy cost reduction:**
- Type B contracts with offshore wind and coastal solar developers that include co-location
  of desalination load — SWRO runs on off-peak renewable generation at $0.03–0.04/kWh
  rather than grid rates, reducing energy cost to $0.09–0.12/m³ and overall production
  cost toward the $0.45–0.55/m³ range
- Type B R&D contracts targeting next-generation low-energy membranes (DOE target:
  $0.50/m³ total cost)

**DED investment case:**

| Program Component | Estimate |
|-------------------|---------|
| Federal pre-certification program development | ~$200M one-time |
| Type B contracts for co-location R&D and demonstration facilities | ~$3–4B over 5 years |
| Federal loan guarantees for qualifying municipal SWRO projects (30 MGD+) | ~$8–12B contingent exposure |
| Administration and permitting coordination | ~$100M/year |

**Projected capacity addition:** 10–15 major SWRO facilities (25–100 MGD each) commissioned
within Phase 2 timeline = 250–750 MGD of new drought-proof freshwater supply for coastal
municipalities. For context, San Diego's Carlsbad plant provides ~7% of the region's water —
a 10-plant national program provides meaningful coverage for California, Texas, and Florida
coastal metro areas.

## 4. Recycled Water Expansion

### Current State

Treated municipal wastewater is the most underutilized water source in the United States.
Approximately 32 billion gallons per day (BGD) of municipal wastewater is treated and
discharged — most of it into rivers and oceans. Less than 10% is recycled for any purpose.

Three reuse tiers exist with increasing value and investment requirement:

**Non-potable reuse (NPR):** Tertiary-treated water delivered via a separate "purple pipe"
distribution system for irrigation (parks, golf courses, agriculture), industrial cooling,
and toilet flushing. Costs: $0.30–$0.60/m³. Orange County Water District, CA (GWRS):
produces 130 million gallons/day (MGD) — the largest water recycling facility in the world.

**Indirect Potable Reuse (IPR):** Highly treated recycled water is recharged into
groundwater aquifers or surface reservoirs, where it blends with natural sources before
being extracted and treated again as drinking water. Regulatory framework: well established
in California, Texas, Arizona. Cost: $0.40–0.70/m³.

**Direct Potable Reuse (DPR):** Treated recycled water enters the drinking water distribution
system directly without an environmental buffer step. The treatment technology (advanced
oxidation, reverse osmosis, UV) produces water cleaner than most natural sources. Cost:
$0.30–0.50/m³. Regulatory status: California adopted DPR regulations in 2023; Texas in
progress; most states have no framework.

### DED Program Design

**Model State DPR Regulatory Framework:**

DED funds and publishes a Model State Direct Potable Reuse Regulation that states can
adopt directly. The model regulation covers treatment standards, monitoring requirements,
operator certification, and public notification. States adopting the model regulation receive
priority access to DED water infrastructure Type B contracts and federal loan guarantees.

Goal: all 50 states with populations above 1M in drought-risk zones have an adopted
DPR framework within Phase 2 timeline.

**Federal Investment Package:**

| Investment | Instrument | Amount |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| IPR facility construction grants (>10 MGD) in drought-declared basins | Type C contracts | $4–6B over 5 years |
| DPR pilot facilities (one per state adopting model regulation) | Type B contracts | $2–3B over 5 years |
| Agricultural recycled water distribution infrastructure | Type C contracts with water utilities | $2–4B over 5 years |
| Model regulation development and state technical assistance | Grant program | $300M |

**Expected capacity addition:** 15–25 new IPR/DPR facilities nationally at 10–50 MGD each
= 150–500 MGD of new drought-proof municipal water supply over Phase 2 timeline.

**Agricultural recycled water:** DED negotiates framework agreements between municipal
water utilities and agricultural users in shared basins — utilities sell treated non-potable
water to farmers at $0.20–0.40/m³ (below groundwater pumping cost in depleted aquifer
areas), reducing agricultural aquifer demand while generating utility revenue to offset
treatment costs.

## 5. Federal Crop Insurance Restructuring

### The Market Signal Problem

Federal crop insurance currently subsidizes approximately 63% of total premiums, creating
an actuarial distortion: the government prices risk at well below its actual level,
which artificially supports water-intensive crop production in drought-prone or
aquifer-depleted zones. A cotton or almond farmer in a declining aquifer zone pays
below-actuarial-cost insurance premiums — which means the market is sending a signal that
water-intensive production in that zone is economically rational. It is not.

This is not a criticism of crop insurance as a program. It is a design flaw: premium
subsidies are uniform, when they should be risk-differentiated to reflect actual
long-term water availability. In drought zones with declining aquifer levels, actual
long-term water risk is much higher than the subsidized premium reflects.

### DED Program Design

**Risk-differentiated premium pricing:**
DED, working through USDA/RMA, establishes a **Drought Risk Premium Adjustment (DRPA)**
for Ogallala-dependent and drought-stressed basin operations:

- Operations in counties where the NEID Drought Risk Score (DRS) exceeds 7/10:
  premium subsidies reduced from 63% to 45% — reflecting actual elevated risk
- Operations with documented aquifer depth below economically viable pumping threshold
  (county-specific): premium subsidies reduced to 30%
- Operations that have completed DED drip irrigation conversion (Program 1):
  premium subsidies increased to 72% — rewarding demonstrated water efficiency

This restructures the price signal without removing the safety net: insurance remains
available, but it reflects actual risk levels rather than masking them.

**Crop transition support:**
Complementing Phase 1's Emergency Crop Transition Payments (ECTP), Phase 2 provides
permanent premium structures that make drought-tolerant varieties economically preferable:

- USDA Multi-Year Crop Transition Guarantee: 5-year premium reduction of 15% for
  operations that permanently shift from water-intensive crops (cotton, alfalfa, almonds)
  to drought-tolerant alternatives (sorghum, winter wheat, native grasses, hemp)
  in high-DRS counties
- This makes the Phase 1 temporary transition economically rational to maintain
  permanently — converting emergency-driven behavior change into structural market signal

**Estimated fiscal impact:**
- DRPA reduces federal premium subsidy expenditure in high-DRS counties by approximately
  $1.5–2.5B/year (current federal crop insurance cost ~$10B/year; Ogallala/Colorado
  River basin share is significant)
- Savings partially offset DED drip conversion and aquifer management costs
- Net: crop insurance restructuring pays for itself within 3–4 years in reduced premium
  subsidy expenditure while permanently improving water use incentives

| Crop insurance restructuring uptake | USDA RMA policy database | DRPA active in all Tier 1 drought counties |
| Aquifer compact state participation | Compact secretariat | 6 of 8 Ogallala states by Year 3 |

**Phase 3 transition criteria:**
Phase 2 transitions to Phase 3 (resilience and normalization) when:
- Drip conversion target is ≥75% achieved
- Ogallala compact is active in 6+ states with sustainable yield monitoring
- At least one major SWRO facility is operational in each of CA, TX, FL
- DRPA is active and NEID DRS is declining in 80% of previously-stressed counties
- PDSI returns to neutral (≥-1.0) in 2 of 3 major agricultural basins for 12+ consecutive months

**For context:** A Tier 2 drought event without Phase 2 investment is estimated to cost
$30–60B in direct agricultural losses over a 5-year period (based on historical drought
loss data from 2012 drought: $35B in agricultural losses in a single year). Phase 2's
$34–48B investment is less than the cost of a single Tier 2 drought event, and it
permanently reduces the damage from all future drought events.
