# Drought Scenario — Phase 1: Demand Management and Allocation

**Scenario:** Regional or Continental Drought
**Phase:** 1 of 3
**Timeline:** 0–24 months from drought declaration
**Trigger:** USDA drought monitor shows D3–D4 (Extreme–Exceptional) across ≥15% of a major agricultural region, or Ogallala Aquifer depletion rate exceeds 2× long-run average in any 12-month period
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 1 Objective

Stabilize water allocation, prevent permanent agricultural capacity loss, and protect
household food security while structural adaptation programs are designed and funded.

A drought in its first two years is a management problem — distribution of a constrained
resource across competing demands. The DED's role in Phase 1 is to resolve the allocation
disputes before they become legal or political crises, deploy income support before food
prices cause household hardship, and front-load the programs that prevent irreversible
outcomes (aquifer overdraft, farm bankruptcy, crop portfolio lock-in on water-intensive varieties).

---

## 1. Water Priority Allocation

### The Core Problem

Water rights law in the United States is a patchwork: eastern states use riparian rights
(adjacent landowners share water), western states use prior appropriation ("first in time,
first in right"). In a drought, these legal frameworks produce allocation outcomes that
do not reflect national economic security priorities. A senior water rights holder may
be a golf course; a junior holder may be a critical food processing facility.

The DED does not override water law — that requires state and interstate action. It
pre-negotiates the allocation framework before the crisis so that states move quickly
when drought conditions arrive.

### Pre-Built Allocation Compact (PBAC) Program

**Mechanism:** DED's Office of Strategic Assets brokers and maintains bilateral and
multilateral water allocation compacts between states in shared river basins. These
compacts define priority order during declared drought emergencies.

**Priority order (emergency declaration):**

| Priority | Use Category | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Municipal drinking water | No substitute; life-critical |
| 2 | Food processing and cold storage | Prevents spoilage of existing supply |
| 3 | Livestock watering | Loss of livestock is multi-year income loss; irreversible in breeding stock |
| 4 | High-value perennial crops (orchards, vineyards) | Multi-year investment; killing a mature orchard eliminates 7–12 years of replanting time |
| 5 | Annual grain crops | Painful but recoverable; can substitute dryland varieties |
| 6 | Industrial cooling (thermoelectric) | Coordinate with grid operator on generation dispatch alternatives |
| 7 | Recreational and non-essential commercial | Curtailed under emergency |

**Key basins with pre-negotiated compacts required:**
- Colorado River Basin (7 states + Mexico; already under stress via 2007 Interim Guidelines)
- Ogallala Aquifer states (8 states: SD, NE, WY, CO, KS, OK, NM, TX — no existing binding compact)
- Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta (California internal; triggers federal nexus under Reclamation law)
- Mississippi River system (low-flow years reduce barge capacity and cooling water access)

**DED role:** Mediator and compact drafter; does not adjudicate water rights. Provides
economic analysis to support state decisions. Activates compact terms upon governor
declaration + DED drought emergency certification.

## 2. Emergency Crop Shift Program

### Rationale

The most water-intensive crops — rice, cotton, almonds, pistachios, alfalfa — use
2–10× the water per acre of staple grains. In a drought, continuing to grow water-intensive
crops exhausts aquifer and surface water reserves that could sustain food staple production.

An orderly crop shift prevents chaotic abandonment and preserves farm income during transition.

### Crop Water Intensity Reference

| Crop | Water Use (acre-feet/acre/year) | Economic Value ($/acre) |
|------|---------------------------------|------------------------|
| Almonds | 3.5–4.5 | $5,000–$8,000 |
| Pistachios | 3.0–4.0 | $6,000–$10,000 |
| Cotton | 2.0–3.0 | $600–$900 |
| Rice (flooded) | 4.0–6.0 | $400–$700 |
| Alfalfa | 3.5–5.0 | $700–$1,100 |
| Corn | 1.0–1.5 | $500–$800 |
| Wheat (winter, dryland) | 0.3–0.8 | $300–$500 |
| Sorghum | 0.5–1.0 | $300–$450 |

### DED Emergency Crop Transition Program (ECTP)

**Trigger:** Phase 1 drought declaration.

**Structure:** Federal crop insurance buyout + replanting subsidy for voluntary crop shifts
in designated high-stress water basins.

**Option A — Temporary fallowing:** Farmer receives fallowing payment ($300–$600/acre,
calibrated to regional crop value) in exchange for leaving designated acreage unirrigated
for the drought year. Fields remain in soil conservation programs. No permanent change.

**Option B — Annual crop substitution:** Farmer receives transition payment + drought-tolerant
seed at no cost to shift from water-intensive annual (cotton, rice) to drought-tolerant
grain (sorghum, dryland wheat, chickpeas). Payment: 60% of prior-year net revenue from
the acreage, for 2 years.

**Option C — Perennial crop deferral:** For orchards and vineyards, subsidize reduced
irrigation ("deficit irrigation") below economic optimum but above tree death threshold,
using research-validated minimum water requirements. Payment: 40% of expected revenue
loss from yield reduction. This preserves the long-term capital asset at reduced water cost.

**Seed reserve:** DED maintains a 90-day emergency seed reserve for drought-tolerant annual
varieties at regional distribution points. See `ded/assets/industrial-capacity.md` (Grain
Storage entry). Drought-tolerant varieties pre-positioned: sorghum (grain and forage),
hard red winter wheat, dryland barley, chickpeas, dry edible beans, field peas.

**Scale:** Program targets 2–5 million acres in Phase 1 (roughly the acreage under
D4-extreme drought in a Tier 2 event). Estimated cost: $1.5–$4.0B/year.

## 3. Agricultural Water Metering and Monitoring

### Problem

No federal baseline exists for agricultural water use. The USGS Farm and Ranch Irrigation
Survey runs every 5 years. Real-time groundwater levels are measured at approximately
22,000 wells nationally — inadequate for dynamic allocation management.

Without metering, allocation targets are unenforceable and water use cannot be accurately
tracked during a drought emergency.

### DED Water Intelligence Network (WIN)

**Immediate action (0–6 months):** Deploy 5,000 additional automated groundwater monitoring
wells in the Ogallala region and Central Valley, complementing USGS network. Cost: ~$250M
capital; $15M/year operating.

**12-month target:** Real-time water use reporting mandatory for all irrigation operations
above 100 acre-feet/year in declared drought zones. Data transmitted to state water agencies
and DED status tracking system.

**DED status file:** `ded/status/water/ogallala-levels.md` (to be created)

**Benefit beyond Phase 1:** This metering infrastructure is the prerequisite for aquifer
management law in Phase 2. Cannot establish water trading markets or cap-and-trade systems
without a metering baseline.

## 4. Hydroelectric Power Backup Dispatch

### Problem

Six percent of U.S. electricity comes from hydroelectric generation. In a drought year,
major facilities — Hoover Dam, Glen Canyon, Grand Coulee — reduce output by 20–70%.
The Pacific Northwest is 40–60% hydro-dependent in normal years. Western grid operators
face both reduced supply and increased demand (more air conditioning in a hot drought year).

### Coordinated Dispatch Protocol

**DED role:** DED's Office of Scenario Analysis maintains a pre-built hydroelectric
shortage dispatch protocol coordinated with NERC and the Western Electricity Coordinating
Council (WECC).

**Protocol elements:**

*Tier 1 (5–15% hydro reduction):*
- Increase natural gas combined-cycle dispatch to fill gap
- Pre-negotiate capacity factor increases with neighboring coal and nuclear plants
- Request demand response activation from industrial customers (voluntary)

*Tier 2 (15–35% hydro reduction):*
- Emergency import requests from neighboring interconnections (Canadian hydro; Southwest gas)
- Demand response mandatory for large industrial customers in affected regions
- Emergency permitting for temporary diesel generation at critical facilities

*Tier 3 (>35% hydro reduction):*
- Rolling conservation voltage reduction (CVR) — reduces voltage by 3–5%, cutting demand 1.5–3%
- Industrial production curtailment schedule (prioritizes food processing, water treatment,
  medical facilities)
- Mutual aid requests to Eastern Interconnection

**Pre-built requirements:** DED maintains signed mutual aid agreements with Canadian
provincial utilities (BC Hydro, Manitoba Hydro) and Mexican CFE for emergency power import.

## 5. Food Price Stabilization

### Price Pressure Mechanism

Drought reduces domestic agricultural supply. If global breadbasket droughts occur
simultaneously (which climate models suggest are more likely under warming), import
substitution is not available. Domestic grain and vegetable prices can spike 20–60%
within 6 months of a major drought (reference: 2012 Midwest drought; corn up 45%,
soy up 22% within 90 days of USDA crop failure declaration).

Low-income households spend 30–40% of income on food. A 40% price spike is a household
financial crisis, not an inconvenience.

### Strategic Grain Reserve Release

**Mechanism:** The DED maintains Strategic Reserve Contracts (Type C) with grain elevator
operators in non-drought regions holding emergency grain inventory. See `ded/tools/strategic-contracts.md`.

**Activation threshold:** Wholesale corn or wheat price rises >20% above 6-month moving
average, or retail food CPI rises >15% annualized.

**Release protocol:** DED activates first-call rights under Type C reserve contracts;
grain enters domestic market at pre-agreed pricing. Release quantity: calibrated to
offset no more than 50% of production gap (to avoid destroying future market incentives
for farmers to hold reserves voluntarily).

**Duration:** Reserve releases are a bridge, not a solution. Phase 1 releases are designed
to last 90–180 days while crop shift programs take effect.

### Emergency Food Assistance Expansion

**SNAP purchasing power adjustment:** In a declared DED drought emergency, SNAP benefits
are automatically indexed to the USDA Thrifty Food Plan updated monthly rather than the
standard annual update. This prevents a lag between food price spikes and benefit adjustment.
Estimated cost: $3–8B/year depending on price spike magnitude.

**School nutrition program continuity:** DED pre-coordinates with USDA to ensure school
lunch and breakfast program supply contracts include drought-contingency sourcing from
non-affected regions. Continuity of child nutrition is a Phase 1 non-negotiable.

## 6. Municipal Water Conservation Mandates

**Trigger:** Municipal water system declares Drought Stage 2 or higher under state criteria.

**DED model municipal drought ordinance (pre-built for state adoption):**

| Stage | Trigger | Mandatory Restrictions |
|-------|---------|----------------------|
| Stage 1 — Watch | 10% below normal supply | Voluntary reduction target; public education |
| Stage 2 — Warning | 20% below normal | Outdoor watering 2 days/week; no car washing; restaurant water on request only |
| Stage 3 — Emergency | 35% below normal | Outdoor watering prohibited; industrial use reduction 15%; tiered pricing activated |
| Stage 4 — Critical | 50% below normal | Essential use only; industrial curtailment 30%; emergency distribution if needed |

**Tiered pricing:** In Stage 3+, water price per unit increases sharply above a baseline
household allocation. Low-income households receive a subsidy credit to cover baseline
amount at Stage 1 pricing. The price signal falls on discretionary use only.

**Recycled water fast-track:** DED pre-built permitting framework allows municipal
wastewater treatment facilities to begin agricultural and industrial recycled water
supply within 90 days of Stage 3 declaration, bypassing standard state permitting timelines
for non-potable applications.

## 7. Income Support Coordination

Drought creates a dual income crisis: agricultural workers lose work when crops are fallowed;
farm owners lose revenue from reduced yields and transition costs.

**Agricultural worker income bridge:**
- Immediate SNAP eligibility expansion to farmworkers in declared drought counties
  (remove categorical exclusions for certain worker visa categories in emergency)
- Unemployment insurance for agricultural workers in states that exclude them under
  standard UI (federal emergency UI extension, funded by DED appropriation)
- Emergency cash assistance: $500/month for 6 months for farmworkers with documented
  agricultural employment in the prior season — paid via existing IRS direct payment
  infrastructure (see `ded/tools/stimulus-checks.md`)

**Farm income bridge:**
- ECTP transition payments (see Section 2 above) provide the primary farm income bridge
- Emergency loan guarantees through USDA Farm Service Agency, with DED backing for loans
  to farms in drought counties above normal FSA cap
- Tax Return Advance Loan (TRAL) early activation in drought-designated counties: advance
  rate increased to 75% in Phase 1 (from steady-state 50%). See `ded/tools/tax-advance-loans.md`

## Phase 1 → Phase 2 Transition Criteria

Phase 2 begins when any of the following are met:
- Drought persists beyond 18 months with no significant precipitation relief forecast
- Ogallala depletion rate exceeds replacement rate by 3× for 2 consecutive years
- Agricultural water use reductions from Phase 1 programs are insufficient to prevent
  continued aquifer decline
- Food price index remains >20% above baseline 12 months after reserve release

Phase 2 focuses on structural water efficiency — drip irrigation conversion, aquifer
management law, desalination acceleration. See `phase-2-structural-efficiency.md` [STUB].
