# Drought Scenario — Phase 1: Economic Stabilization

**Scenario:** Regional or Continental Drought
**Phase:** 1 of 3
**Timeline:** 0–24 months from DED drought declaration
**Trigger:** Agricultural water costs rise >25% year-over-year in a major basin, OR
  federal crop insurance claims exceed 2× baseline in any USDA Farm Production Region,
  OR Ogallala Aquifer depletion rate exceeds 150% of long-run average for 2 consecutive
  reporting quarters
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 1 Objective

Stabilize the economic cost of water and food inputs before household prices and
farm viability are damaged beyond short-term recovery. Phase 1 is triggered by
economic indicators, not emergency declarations. By the time drought is a
headline crisis, the window for low-cost intervention has already passed.

**Economic concerns this phase addresses:**
- Rising food commodity prices (directly increases household cost of living; also
  increases cost of military food service and field rations ~$5B/year)
- Rising water utility rates in drought-stressed municipal systems
- Farm income instability from crop yield losses (reduces the tax base; reduces
  demand for agricultural equipment and inputs across the farm economy)
- Rising natural gas prices from hydroelectric shortfall (power plants compensate
  by burning more gas; utilities pass cost to consumers and industrial users)

**What Phase 1 does not do:** DED does not ration water, mandate crop changes,
or operate distribution systems. Phase 1 activates pre-built financial incentive
packages that make the economically rational choice for private actors align
with national economic security.

## 1. Strategic Contracts — Activated in Phase 1

### Type B: Drought-Tolerant Crop Genetics Development

Activate the OPP-01 development contract (see `ded/economics/value-discovery.md`):
pre-built Type B agreements with Bayer/Corteva/Syngenta and USDA ARS to accelerate
development of drought-tolerant staple crop varieties under classification.

This is simultaneously an Accelerator Charlie deployment (see `ded/tools/expert-reserve-program.md`):
recruited seed company scientists, USDA researchers, and land-grant university
plant geneticists convene under security classification, removing competitive IP
barriers so that competing companies share breeding advances freely. The government
retains the resulting IP under pre-agreed terms.

**Why Phase 1 (not Phase 2):** Crop variety development takes 3–7 years from breeding
to commercial deployment. Starting at Phase 1 declaration means varieties are available
by Phase 2 or Phase 3. Starting at Phase 2 means they arrive too late for this drought
cycle.

**Economic output:** Domestic drought-tolerant seed supply reduces import dependence,
lowers per-acre input costs for farmers, and generates licensing revenue via OPP-01
trade agreement structure when distributed to allied agricultural nations.

---

### Type B: Drip Irrigation Equipment Manufacturing Scale-Up

Drip and micro-irrigation reduces agricultural water use 40–60% per acre versus
flood irrigation. The barrier to farmer adoption is upfront capital cost (~$800–$1,200/acre
installed). The barrier to rapid national deployment is domestic manufacturing capacity.

**Contract structure:** Type B strategic contracts with domestic drip irrigation
equipment manufacturers (Jain Irrigation, Lindsay Corporation, Valmont Industries)
to scale U.S. production capacity by 3× within 18 months, with DED as guaranteed
off-taker for the capacity increase at negotiated unit prices.

DED then re-deploys this capacity via the financial incentive package in Section 2.

**CRHP labor component:** Certified CRHP civilian reserve members in agricultural
service tracks are pre-trained in drip irrigation installation. In a Phase 1 drought
declaration, CRHP deployment orders activate these teams for direct on-farm installation
work. This reduces farmer labor cost and installation timeline, lowering the effective
per-acre conversion cost.

---

### Type C: Strategic Grain Reserve Contracts

Pre-position first-call rights on 90–120 days of domestic grain supply through Type C
reserve contracts with grain elevator operators in non-drought regions. These are
existing private storage facilities; DED pays an annual retainer for guaranteed access
at pre-agreed pricing during a declared drought period.

**Economic purpose:** When domestic grain supply falls, commodity markets reprice
immediately — futures move within weeks of crop loss projections. Reserve access lets
DED stabilize domestic wholesale prices without the overhead of government-owned
stockpiles. The Type C structure means private operators manage storage, rotation, and
insurance efficiently. DED's cost is the retainer (~$200–400M/year for 90-day reserve),
not full inventory ownership.

---

### Type A: Hydroelectric Substitute Generation — Standby Contracts

Hydroelectric output falls 20–70% in severe drought. Western grid operators face
simultaneous supply reduction and demand increase (cooling load in hot drought years).
Rising wholesale power prices raise electricity costs for all users — including
military installations, water treatment facilities, and food processing.

**Contract structure:** Type A readiness maintenance contracts with natural gas
combined-cycle (NGCC) operators in WECC to maintain certified standby capacity
at defined cost per megawatt-hour, activated automatically when WECC declares
hydro shortage conditions. Contracts specify ramp timeline (72-hour notice to full
output) and pricing caps.

**Economic effect:** Prevents wholesale power price spikes that would otherwise
cascade into water treatment costs, food processing costs, and residential utility rates.

## 2. Financial Incentive Package — Water Efficiency

The DED does not mandate irrigation conversion. It pre-builds the financial package
that makes conversion the economically dominant choice for farmers.

### Federal Irrigation Efficiency Tax Credit

**Structure:** 50% investment tax credit for conversion from flood/sprinkler to
drip or micro-irrigation systems, claimable over 3 years.

**Activation:** Pre-authorized at Phase 1 declaration for farms in designated
drought-stressed basins. IRS executes; DED pre-funds via appropriation.

**Interaction with Type B manufacturer contracts:** DED's scaled domestic production
(Section 1) ensures credit activation does not produce a supply shortage that
defeats its purpose. The manufacturer contract and the tax credit are designed as
a paired deployment.

**Cost:** ~$1.2–2.4B/year in tax expenditure for 2–4M acres converted;
offset by farm income stabilization (reduced insurance payouts, sustained yields).

### Crop Transition Payment (Voluntary)

For farms in the highest-stress water zones, a voluntary transition payment makes
temporary fallowing or shift to dryland crops financially equivalent to irrigated
production:

- **Fallowing:** $300–500/acre/year for documented unirrigated acres in drought zones
  (calibrated to regional net revenue per acre; funded via USDA FSA emergency reserve)
- **Dryland substitution:** Seed cost + $200/acre transition payment for shift to
  drought-tolerant annuals. DED provides the seed from OPP-01 reserve stock.

This is not a mandate — it is a price. Farmers who find their current crop more
economical continue. The payment ensures that economic incentives, not only agronomic
preference, favor adaptation.

### Crop Insurance Restructuring

DED's Office of Citizen Economic Security works with USDA Risk Management Agency to
restructure crop insurance premium schedules in drought-designated zones:

- **Premium discount:** 20% reduction for adoption of drought-tolerant varieties
  on ≥30% of insured acreage
- **Coverage adjustment:** Remove federal premium subsidy for water-intensive crops
  (rice, cotton) grown in USDA-designated Extreme Drought counties. Subsidy continues
  for drought-adapted alternatives. The market signal falls on the crop choice, not
  the farmer.

**Economic effect:** Restructuring shifts crop mix via insurance economics — no mandate
required. The same mechanism used to incentivize cover crops via conservation compliance
provisions is applied here to drought adaptation.

## 3. CRHP Civilian Reserve Deployment

The Civilian Reserve Health Program (CRHP) maintains several agricultural and
infrastructure service tracks (see `ded/proposal/civilian-reserve-health/`).
Phase 1 drought declaration triggers deployment authorization for:

**Agricultural Water Infrastructure Track:**
- Drip irrigation installation and maintenance (certified teams)
- On-farm water monitoring equipment installation (sensors, metering)
- Soil moisture management and precision agriculture setup

**Civil Infrastructure Track:**
- Water distribution system inspection and minor repair
- Municipal water recycling system installation support
- Irrigation canal lining and sealing (reduces conveyance loss 15–30%)

**Deployment model:** CRHP members mobilized on 14-day notice; compensated at
active-duty equivalent rate during deployment (self-funded by DED appropriation).
Farmer or municipality pays materials only. The labor cost — historically the
bottleneck in adoption of water-efficient infrastructure — is removed from the
farmer's decision calculus.

**Economic effect:** The civilian reserve provides a surge labor capacity that
cannot be supplied by private contractors at the scale and speed needed. This is
the direct analogue to the WPA model — but funded as military training, not as
welfare, and drawing on a pre-trained reserve rather than unemployed workers.

## 4. Economic Intelligence — Phase 1 Monitoring

The DED's National Economic Intelligence Division monitors the following leading
indicators on a weekly basis during a Phase 1 drought declaration:

| Indicator | Early Warning Threshold | Phase 2 Trigger |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------|
| CBOT corn futures (front month) | +15% vs. 6-mo avg | +35% sustained 60 days |
| CBOT wheat futures | +15% vs. 6-mo avg | +35% sustained 60 days |
| USDA Crop Progress Report (Good/Excellent %) | <50% national | <35% or regional collapse |
| Ogallala depth-to-water (USGS) | Decline >1 ft/year | Decline >2 ft/year |
| Western Interconnection wholesale power price | +20% vs. prior year | +40% |
| Municipal water utility rate index | +10% annualized | +25% annualized |
| Federal crop insurance indemnity claims | 1.5× baseline | 2.5× baseline |
| Military base water procurement cost | +10% annualized | Reported to SecDef |

When Phase 2 triggers are met on any 3 of these indicators simultaneously, the
DED Secretary presents Phase 2 authorization to the President.

## 5. Military Cost Linkage

Drought is not only a civilian economic concern. The Department of Defense spends
approximately:

- **$4–6B/year** on food service, rations, and contracted field feeding
- **$500M–$1B/year** on water procurement and treatment at domestic installations
- An additional amount embedded in weapons system and vehicle procurement that
  reflects steel, aluminum, and specialty material prices — all influenced by
  energy and water costs in industrial production

A sustained drought that raises corn, wheat, and soy prices by 30% raises military
food service costs proportionally. A drought that raises Midwest industrial water
costs 20% raises the production cost of every manufactured military component
that requires water in its process.

The DED presents this cost analysis to the Secretary of Defense and the Office of
Management and Budget as part of the Phase 1 economic impact assessment. This
frames DED's intervention costs against the defense budget baseline — the $2–4B
Phase 1 investment package is directly compared to the $1–3B in projected DoD
cost increases avoided.

## Phase 1 → Phase 2 Transition

Phase 2 activates when Phase 2 triggers are met (see Section 4) or at 18 months
if drought has not abated. Phase 2 focuses on the structural water efficiency
investments that take 3–10 years to complete: aquifer management frameworks,
large-scale desalination, and recycled water systems.

See `ded/scenarios/drought/phase-2-structural-efficiency.md`.
