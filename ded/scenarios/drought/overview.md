# Scenario: Regional or Continental Drought

## Scenario Definition

A sustained, large-scale drought — caused by climate-driven precipitation shifts,
persistent high-pressure systems, or aquifer depletion — that reduces agricultural
output, freshwater availability, and hydroelectric generation across a significant
portion of the U.S. or globally over a multi-year period.

Unlike acute disruptions, drought develops slowly but compounds over time.
Aquifer depletion is irreversible on human timescales. A 3-year drought is
recoverable; a 10-year drought that depletes the Ogallala Aquifer is not.

---

## Why Drought Is a Tier-1 DED Concern

- The U.S. agricultural system is heavily irrigated — **~80% of freshwater withdrawals** are for agriculture
- The Ogallala Aquifer underlies 30% of U.S. groundwater-irrigated land and is being depleted faster than it recharges
- California produces ~13% of U.S. agricultural value and is chronically water-stressed
- Hydroelectric generation (~6% of U.S. electricity) drops sharply in drought years
- Global grain markets are highly correlated — a drought in multiple breadbaskets simultaneously (U.S., Ukraine, Australia, India) causes global food price spikes

| Tier | Definition | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | Single-region, 1–3 year drought | 2012 Midwest drought; California 2012–2017 |
| Tier 2 | Multi-region, 3–7 year drought | 1930s Dust Bowl (regional); Australia Millennium Drought |
| Tier 3 | Continental, >7 year or permanent aquifer loss | Hypothetical megadrought; Ogallala collapse |

---

## Critical Systems Affected

1. **Grain and livestock production** — irrigation water is the direct input
2. **Hydroelectric power** — reservoir levels drop, generation falls
3. **Thermoelectric cooling** — power plants require water for cooling; may have to reduce output
4. **Municipal water supply** — cities compete with agriculture for the same aquifer
5. **Inland waterway freight** — low river levels reduce barge capacity (Mississippi system)
6. **Food prices globally** — U.S. is a major exporter; domestic drought raises world prices

---

## Phase Responses

### Phase 1 (0–2 years): Demand Management and Allocation

- **Water priority allocation:** Agriculture vs. municipal vs. industrial, by law before crisis
- **Crop shift:** Reduce water-intensive crops (rice, cotton, almonds) in drought regions; expand dryland farming
- **Drought-tolerant crop substitution:** Emergency seed program, fast-track variety adoption
- **Municipal conservation mandates:** Tiered pricing, use restrictions, recycled water expansion
- **Hydroelectric backup:** Pre-planned natural gas/coal/nuclear dispatch ramp to compensate

### Phase 2 (2–7 years): Structural Water Efficiency

- **Drip irrigation conversion:** Subsidized transition from flood to drip/micro-irrigation (40–60% water savings)
- **Crop portfolio shift:** Federal crop insurance restructuring to incentivize drought-resistant varieties
- **Aquifer management law:** Groundwater use caps, metering, trading markets
- **Desalination programs:** Coastal states accelerate desalination capacity build
- **Recycled water expansion:** Industrial and agricultural use of treated wastewater

### Phase 3 (Long-term): Structural Resilience

- **Managed aquifer recharge:** Capture flood water for aquifer replenishment
- **Agricultural relocation:** Gradually shift water-intensive crops to wetter regions
- **Urban heat island reduction:** Reduce cooling demand that strains water-cooled power plants
- **National water atlas:** Map all freshwater dependencies with depletion timelines
