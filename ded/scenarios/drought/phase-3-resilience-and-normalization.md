# Drought Scenario — Phase 3: Resilience and Normalization

**Scenario:** Regional or Continental Drought
**Phase:** 3 of 3
**Timeline:** 5–20 years from Phase 2 activation; permanent structural condition
**Trigger:** Phase 2 transition criteria met (see phase-2-structural-efficiency.md)
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

---

## Phase 3 Objective

Phase 1 managed immediate economic stress. Phase 2 restructured the physical infrastructure
of water use. Phase 3 builds the permanent institutional and market architecture that makes
the U.S. **drought-resilient by design** — not just after a crisis has forced investment,
but as a standing feature of the agricultural and water economy.

Phase 3 is less about response and more about **embedding drought resilience into the baseline
conditions** so that future droughts trigger Phase 1 economic management rather than Phase
2–3 emergency restructuring.

**The Phase 3 test:** A Tier 2 drought that would have caused $60–120B in economic damage
before DED investment should cause $15–30B of damage after Phase 3 programs are mature —
a 75%+ reduction in scenario severity.

### Phase 3 Trigger Criteria (from Phase 2)

Phase 3 is entered when the following structural indicators are met:
- Drip irrigation conversion has reached 40%+ of irrigated acreage in priority basins
- Ogallala Compact or equivalent multi-state framework is in operation with binding extraction limits
- At least one major desalination facility is operational in the Southwest or Texas Gulf Coast
- Crop insurance premium restructuring (DRPA) is in effect in at least 3 USGS drought-risk zones
- Phase 1 acute crisis indicators have been below Tier 1 thresholds for 24+ months

## Program 1: National Water Market and Pricing Infrastructure

### The Problem

Water in the U.S. is the most misallocated major resource in the economy. The combination of:
- Prior appropriation doctrine (first-in-time senior rights immune to market reallocation)
- Below-cost federal irrigation water pricing (Bureau of Reclamation subsidized rates,
  often $15–50/acre-foot for water that costs $500–2,000/acre-foot to deliver in market terms)
- Absence of interstate water trading markets
- Political entrenchment of agricultural water allocations regardless of economic use value

...produces a system where water goes to low-value uses (flood-irrigated alfalfa and cotton)
while cities, industry, and high-value agriculture compete at the margin for scarce supply.

This is not a water shortage problem in the aggregate — it is a market failure problem.
California grows 30% of the nation's vegetables using water priced below long-run marginal cost.
Arizona grows cotton at a federal water subsidy rate that makes no economic sense at market
prices. The drought of record is in many cases a drought of misallocation.

### DED Program: Water Market Development Contracts (Type B)

**Water trading platform co-investment:**
DED co-invests (Type B) with states and water districts to develop standardized water rights
trading infrastructure:
- Legal framework: model state legislation for temporary water transfers across senior/junior
  right holders; does not require prior appropriation reform — works within existing doctrine
- Trading platform: standardized contract terms, pricing, delivery logistics, and verification
  that water transferred is actually applied to contracted use
- Priority basins: Colorado River, Rio Grande, Platte, Arkansas, Republican — the 5 most
  over-allocated basins where trading would generate the largest efficiency gains
- Federal incentive: DED provides $500M in matching grants to states that establish
  qualifying trading frameworks; federal water delivery infrastructure (Bureau of Reclamation)
  prioritizes states with active trading programs

**Below-cost water pricing reform:**
Bureau of Reclamation pricing for federal project water is set by congressional authorization,
not by DED authority. DED's role is indirect:
- DED cost-benefit analysis demonstrating the national economic cost of below-cost pricing
  provides the analytical foundation for congressional reform
- DED Type A contracts with states: federal incentive payments to states that voluntarily
  move federal project water to cost-recovery pricing over a 10-year transition
- Transition payments: farmers in affected districts receive a 10-year income support payment
  (Type A) during the transition to market pricing — making the political economy manageable
  by separating the reform from the income loss

**Interstate water compact modernization:**
Existing interstate water compacts (Colorado River Compact 1922, Rio Grande Compact 1938)
were designed for precipitation and population levels that no longer exist. DED:
- Funds the hydrological and economic analysis that states need to renegotiate compacts
  (a bottleneck: states won't enter renegotiation without credible shared data)
- Provides a federal mediation structure (neutral referee) to separate the technical from
  the political in compact renegotiation
- Establishes emergency compact provisions — pre-agreed shortage allocation rules that
  activate automatically on drought declaration rather than requiring 2-year renegotiation
  during an active crisis

**5-year cost:** $1.5–2.5B in matching grants and transition payments; high leverage ratio —
shifting 10% of agricultural water to higher-value uses in the Colorado Basin alone is worth
an estimated $8–15B in annual economic output.

## Program 2: Climate-Adapted Crop Geography

### The Problem

The current distribution of crops across U.S. agricultural land reflects 19th- and
early 20th-century hydrology, not 21st-century water availability. Cotton is grown in
Arizona and Texas on aquifer water that will be depleted within 20–40 years. Alfalfa
exports to Saudi Arabia and China consume more Colorado River water than Las Vegas.
Corn is grown in Kansas counties where the Ogallala has already been depleted by 60%+.

Phase 2 built the water efficiency infrastructure. Phase 3 addresses the underlying
crop geography question: **what should be grown where**, given projected water availability
over the next 50 years.

### DED Program: National Crop Transition Planning Framework

**Voluntary Regional Crop Transition Plans:**
DED works with USDA and state agricultural agencies to produce **50-year crop geography
scenarios** for each major agricultural region based on USGS groundwater projections and
NOAA climate models:
- Published projections for every USGS watershed: projected water availability at current
  extraction rates (10, 25, 50 year); projected viable crop options at each water availability level
- Voluntary transition planning: counties and water districts use the projections to develop
  25-year voluntary crop transition plans; DED provides planning grants ($50M/year)
- Market signal reinforcement: DRPA (crop insurance restructuring from Phase 2) already
  prices drought risk into insurance premiums; Phase 3 extends DRPA to price **long-run
  aquifer depletion risk** into premiums for crops in depleted zones

**Crop transition incentive payments:**
For farmers in the highest-risk zones (Ogallala depletion front, Central Valley water
curtailment areas, Rio Grande overdraft regions), DED offers:
- 15-year Type A transition contracts: guaranteed annual payment to shift from
  water-intensive annual crops (corn, cotton, alfalfa) to perennial crops with lower
  water demand (orchards, vineyards, drought-tolerant grasses) or to fallowing with
  soil carbon payments
- Payment structure: 80% of the 10-year average net income from the displaced crop,
  for 15 years — sufficient to cover the full transition period while the new crop/use
  matures or the soil carbon value accrues
- Targeted at the 5M acres most at risk from aquifer depletion in the next 25 years

**Drought-Tolerant Variety Accelerator:**
DED Accelerator program for drought-tolerant seed development:
- Type D contracts with USDA ARS, national labs (Argonne, PNNL), and commercial seed
  companies for accelerated development of drought-tolerant corn, soy, wheat, and
  sorghum varieties
- Target: varieties that produce 80%+ of baseline yield at 40% of current water input
- Timeline: 8–12 years to commercial deployment (consistent with existing R&D timelines;
  DED investment compresses this by 3–5 years)
- IP framework: Type D contract; commercial rights to seed companies; DED royalty-free
  license for use in federal procurement and allied sharing

**5-year cost:** $3–5B in transition payments + $500M–1B Accelerator; long-run value is
permanent extension of Ogallala viability and reduction in crop insurance losses.

## Program 3: National Water Intelligence System (NWIS)

### The Problem

The U.S. has no integrated, real-time water supply intelligence system. USGS monitors
groundwater in some aquifers. NOAA monitors precipitation and snowpack. State agencies
track surface water allocations. But these systems are siloed, delayed (some groundwater
reports are quarterly or annual), and not integrated into a decision-support framework
for agricultural, municipal, and industrial water managers.

The 2012 drought was not fully recognized as a Tier 2 drought until it had already caused
most of its damage. The 2022 Colorado River crisis required emergency negotiations under
crisis conditions that could have been anticipated 5+ years earlier if integrated
monitoring had existed.

### DED Program: NWIS Build-Out

**Real-time groundwater monitoring expansion:**
USGS currently monitors approximately 30,000 wells nationally; an estimated 600,000+
additional observation wells are needed for comprehensive Ogallala and Central Valley coverage.
DED co-invests with USGS and state water agencies:
- $800M–1.2B over 5 years for sensor deployment and telemetry
- Real-time data (15-minute reporting) vs. current monthly/quarterly delays
- Integrated with NEID (DED economic intelligence dashboard) for automatic drought
  indicator calculation

**Agricultural water use metering:**
Approximately 60% of agricultural irrigation withdrawals are unmetered. Without metering:
- Groundwater extraction trading markets cannot verify delivery
- Ogallala Compact extraction limits cannot be enforced
- Conservation program payments cannot be verified

DED cost-shares meter installation with states and water districts:
- $400–600M over 5 years for the highest-priority unmetered agricultural wells
- Data feeds into NWIS in real time; anonymized aggregate data public via NEID

**NWIS → NEID Integration:**
The National Economic Intelligence Dashboard (NEID) already tracks drought indicators
(Palmer Drought Severity Index, crop yield forecasts). NWIS adds:
- Real-time aquifer depletion rate (current month vs. historical average)
- Surface water storage (reservoir levels, snowpack water equivalent)
- Agricultural demand estimate (planted acreage × crop water requirement × weather)
- Demand-supply balance: projected water deficit/surplus for each major basin at 30, 90,
  365-day horizon
- Phase protocol trigger: NWIS automatically calculates whether Phase 1 trigger conditions
  are approaching, giving 6–18 months advance warning vs. current reactive declaration process

**5-year cost:** $1.5–2B; enables $5–10B in annual efficiency gains from better-targeted
irrigation and earlier phase declaration.

## Program 4: Long-Run Desalination and Alternative Source Development

Phase 2 built the first tranche of desalination capacity under emergency conditions. Phase 3
expands desalination into a major structural water source for the Southwest and Texas Gulf Coast
using the Pre-Approved Design Standards (PADS) and renewable co-location requirements
established in Phase 2.

### Expansion Targets (Phase 3)

| Region | Phase 3 Capacity Target | Primary Source | Key Facility |
|--------|------------------------|----------------|-------------|
| Southern California | 200–400 MGD | Pacific Ocean | Huntington Beach-class facilities x3 |
| Texas Gulf Coast | 300–500 MGD | Gulf of Mexico | Houston-Galveston metro supply |
| Arizona | 100–200 MGD | Sea of Cortez (piped) | CAP supplemental supply |
| Nevada / Las Vegas | 50–100 MGD | Colorado River augmentation via desal | Reduce Colorado draw by equivalent |
| Florida | 200–300 MGD | Atlantic / Gulf | Tampa Bay scale-up; regional expansion |

**Total Phase 3 target:** 850–1,500 MGD of additional desalination capacity; equivalent to
supplying clean water to 8–14 million people or reducing agricultural Colorado River draws
by 500,000–750,000 acre-feet/year.

**Cost:** $15–25B in total facility capital over 10 years; $8–12B DED co-investment (50%);
remaining private and municipal capital via DED loan guarantees.

**Long-run economics:** At $0.50–$0.70/m³ (Phase 3 target using PADS + renewable power),
desalinated water competes with the economic value of water in drought-stressed regions where
spot water prices routinely reach $1,000–3,000/acre-foot ($0.81–2.43/m³). The subsidy
requirement falls to near zero once the infrastructure is built and financed.

## Program 5: Drought Resilience Bonds

As Phase 3 programs mature and the NWIS provides reliable forward-looking drought indicators,
DED introduces a **Drought Resilience Bond (DRB)** structure — an extension of the
outcome-linked bond framework to long-horizon water investment:

**Structure:**
- 20–30 year infrastructure bonds issued by water utilities, municipalities, or state water agencies
- DED-guaranteed floor coupon: SOFR + 1.5% guaranteed regardless of water conditions
- Above-baseline payout: bonds pay SOFR + 4–5% during years where NWIS regional water
  deficit exceeds 25% of normal (drought years) — the elevated coupon compensates bondholders
  for the stress period and signals the value of the infrastructure they funded
- Proceeds restricted to: desalination, drip irrigation conversion, water recycling,
  or aquifer recharge infrastructure

**Purpose:**
- Mobilizes private capital for Phase 3 infrastructure without requiring full federal expenditure
- Creates a private market incentive aligned with long-run water security (bond investors
  benefit from the infrastructure that reduces their drought-year coupon exposure)
- Municipalities and water utilities can finance 20-year infrastructure projects with federal
  credit support rather than large upfront appropriations

**Estimated mobilization:** $30–50B in private infrastructure capital over 20 years against
$3–5B DED guarantee exposure.

## Phase 3 — Scenario Impact Assessment

If Phase 3 programs reach maturity (10–15 years post-initiation):

| Metric | Baseline (no DED) | Post-Phase 3 |
|--------|------------------|--------------|
| Ogallala depletion rate | 4–6 feet/year average | 1–2 feet/year (sustainable yield Compact + crop transition) |
| Tier 2 drought economic cost | $60–120B | $15–30B (75%+ reduction) |
| Colorado River overallocation | 1.5M+ acre-feet/year deficit | Near balance (desal augmentation + trading + crop transition) |
| Agricultural water use | ~130M acre-feet/year | ~85–95M acre-feet/year (25–35% reduction) |
| Desalination contribution | <1% of western water supply | 8–15% of southwestern municipal supply |
| Water market coverage | <5% of western water tradeable | 40–60% tradeable under standardized frameworks |
| Early drought warning | 0–6 months advance | 12–24 months advance (NWIS) |
