# Scenario: Pandemic — Labor and Supply Chain Collapse

## Scenario Definition

A fast-spreading pathogen causes simultaneous labor shortages across critical
sectors, overwhelms healthcare capacity, and triggers government-mandated or
voluntary reductions in economic activity. Unlike a physical supply disruption,
a pandemic attacks the **human layer** of all supply chains simultaneously.

The 2020 COVID-19 pandemic is the reference event. A more severe or differently
structured pathogen (higher mortality, longer incubation, antimicrobial resistance)
would produce more extreme versions of the same cascades.

---

## Why This Scenario Differs From Others

Most disruption scenarios affect a specific input (oil, water, a mineral).
A pandemic disrupts **labor and coordination capacity across all sectors at once**.

Key differences:
- No physical input is missing — workers are missing
- Supply chains fail not from resource scarcity but from operational collapse
- Healthcare itself is a critical input that gets depleted first
- Public behavior (fear, hoarding, compliance) becomes as important as policy
- International coordination breaks down as every nation faces the same crisis

## Severity Tiers

| Tier | Characteristics | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | 5–15% workforce reduction, manageable healthcare system | COVID-19 (2020) |
| Tier 2 | 15–35% workforce reduction, healthcare overwhelmed | 1918 flu second wave |
| Tier 3 | 35%+ workforce reduction, cascading institutional failures | Hypothetical severe pandemic |

---

## Critical Systems Affected (in order of vulnerability speed)

1. **Healthcare** — staff sick, PPE shortage, ICU overflow — collapses first
2. **Food processing** — large shared facilities, cannot remote work
3. **Freight and logistics** — driver shortages, cross-border closure
4. **Energy** — power plant and refinery staffing minimum thresholds
5. **Water treatment** — skeletal crew operations required
6. **Agriculture** — seasonal labor concentrated, impossible to distance
7. **Manufacturing** — assembly lines halt; cannot substitute easily
8. **Financial services** — operational risk, liquidity management

---

## Cascade Timeline (Tier 2 Baseline)

| Week | Effects |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | Healthcare surge begins; PPE, ventilator shortages apparent |
| 2–4 | School closures → parent workforce absence; panic buying |
| 1–2 mo | Food processing plants close or reduce capacity; freight slowdown |
| 2–3 mo | Energy sector minimum staffing becomes critical |
| 3–6 mo | Food supply chain gaps visible; inflation spike |
| 6+ mo | Structural economic damage; labor force participation changes |

## Phase Responses

### Phase 1 (0–6 months): Maintain Critical Operations

- **Healthcare surge capacity:** Pre-positioned ventilators, PPE stockpiles, field hospital deployment plans
- **Critical worker designation:** Pre-written essential worker classifications across all sectors
- **Supply chain priority list:** Which factories, distribution centers, and logistics operations must stay open
- **International coordination:** Pre-negotiated agreements to keep critical goods flowing across borders
- **Food system stabilization:** Anti-hoarding authority, retail purchase limits, regional stockpile releases

### Phase 2 (6 months–3 years): Structural Adaptation

- **Remote work infrastructure:** Broadband expansion, device access programs, employer compliance frameworks
- **Automation acceleration:** Fast-track investment in food processing, logistics, and manufacturing automation
- **Domestic PPE/pharmaceutical manufacturing:** Defense Production Act build-out of domestic supply
- **Workforce resilience:** Cross-training programs, redundant staffing models, gig workforce integration

### Phase 3 (Long-term): Systemic Resilience

- **Strategic medical stockpile standards:** Maintained nationally, not depleted between pandemics
- **Distributed food processing:** Reduce single-facility concentration
- **Permanent remote work infrastructure:** Treat broadband as critical infrastructure
- **Pathogen early warning system:** Pre-positioned surveillance, rapid genomic sequencing networks

## DED Programs Summary

Programs proposed across all phases of this scenario and how they reduce impact:

**Healthcare Surge Capacity**
- **CRHP (Civilian Reserve Health Program)** — pre-enrolled civilian healthcare workers mobilized as surge labor for hospitals and field facilities; removes the ad-hoc staffing crisis of 2020
- **Strategic medical stockpile contracts (Type A)** — pre-positioned PPE, ventilators, and pharmaceutical reserves maintained at 90-day national supply; rotated continuously so they are not depleted between pandemics
- **DPA (Defense Production Act) coordination** — DED as lead coordinator for Title III domestic manufacturing surge of PPE, vaccines, and critical medical equipment

**Income and Household Protection**
- **Stimulus checks** — direct transfers to displaced workers; DED pre-designs the delivery infrastructure so checks deploy within 14 days of declaration, not 6+ weeks
- **TRAL (Tax Return Advance Loans) — pandemic track** — 90% advance against future refund for workers in declared pandemic sectors; covers gap between job loss and UI processing
- **Extended UI expansion** — pre-written statutory framework for UI duration and coverage expansion, activated on Tier 2 declaration rather than passed mid-crisis

**Supply Chain Continuity**
- **Type B contracts for domestic pharmaceutical manufacturing** — co-investment with domestic API and drug manufacturers; DED retains IP; reduces 80%+ import dependency on critical medicines
- **Type C supply chain contracts** — targeted investment to build domestic production of the goods most disrupted in 2020: N95 masks, gloves, testing materials, generic pharmaceuticals
- **Allied supply access agreements** — pre-negotiated priority import access for critical medical goods with allied nations; avoids the competitive bidding chaos of 2020

**Workforce and Economic Continuity**
- **Remote work infrastructure investment (Type B)** — broadband expansion, device access, and employer framework pre-built so remote-capable workers transition within days, not months
- **Internship floor (sector-adjusted)** — contact-intensive sector suspensions; remote-capable sectors maintain or accelerate the pipeline; preserves workforce development through the crisis
- **CRHP deployment to food processing and logistics** — civilian reserve augments workforce in critical-but-dangerous processing facilities to maintain food supply chain
- **Mandatory Paid Internship Floor** — remote-capable employers maintain entry-level development pipeline; provides income for young workers who lose jobs in hospitality and retail
