# Scenario: Global Shipping Blockade or Disruption

## Scenario Definition

A sustained disruption to international maritime shipping — through naval conflict,
piracy escalation, chokepoint denial, or port infrastructure attack — that
reduces global goods trade by 20–60% for a period of months to years.

---

## The Maritime Dependency

~90% of global trade by volume moves by ship. The U.S. imports approximately:
- ~$3 trillion in goods annually, majority by sea
- Critical inputs: electronics, pharmaceuticals, clothing, consumer goods,
  industrial components, and increasingly food

**Key chokepoints:**
| Chokepoint | Global Trade Share | Alternate Route Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Strait of Hormuz | ~20% of oil, ~17% of LNG | Very long, expensive |
| Strait of Malacca | ~25% of traded goods | Longer routes |
| Suez Canal | ~12% of global trade | Cape of Good Hope (+14 days) |
| Panama Canal | ~5% of global trade | Cape Horn or rail |
| Bab-el-Mandeb (Red Sea) | ~10% of global trade | Around Africa |

The 2024 Houthi Red Sea attacks demonstrated how a relatively minor actor
can disrupt a major global trade route with significant economic effect.

---

## Severity Tiers

| Tier | Effect | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | Single chokepoint, rerouting possible | Months; 20–30% shipping cost increase |
| Tier 2 | Multiple chokepoints, naval conflict | 6–18 months; major supply gaps |
| Tier 3 | Pacific or Atlantic blockade | Years; fundamental supply chain restructuring |

---

## Phase Responses

### Phase 1: Rerouting and Inventory

- **Freight rerouting coordination:** DED + DOT + Coast Guard coordinate alternative routes
- **Port surge capacity:** Pre-designated overflow ports and inland logistics hubs
- **Inventory draw-down authorization:** Industry allowed to deplete strategic inventory without penalty
- **Air freight prioritization:** Critical goods (pharma, defense components) shifted to air

### Phase 2: Domestic Production Substitution

- **Import substitution program:** Defense Production Act contracts for domestic production of blocked goods
- **Jones Act waiver coordination:** Expand domestic coastal shipping
- **Allied trade rerouting:** Coordinate with Canada, Mexico for land-border alternatives

### Phase 3: Structural Import Independence

- **Strategic import reduction:** Identify top 50 goods by import volume and domestic substitution potential
- **Nearshoring incentives:** Tax and procurement policy to move manufacturing to Americas
- **Domestic pharmaceutical manufacturing:** Top priority — currently 80%+ of APIs imported
