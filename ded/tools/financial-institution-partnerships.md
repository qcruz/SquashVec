# DED Tool Analysis: Financial Institution Partnerships

**Category:** Financial Instruments / Infrastructure Contracts
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Relevant Scenarios:** All scenarios — cross-cutting distribution and infrastructure tool
**DED Tool Rating:** Tier A — Structural; the universal delivery layer for DED's citizen-
  facing programs; enables all account-based program distribution without building a
  parallel government banking infrastructure
**Related Tools:** [strategic-contracts.md](strategic-contracts.md) · [ebank-distributed-ledger](../proposal/ebank-distributed-ledger.md) · [outcome-linked-bonds.md](outcome-linked-bonds.md)
**Related Programs:** NIT · CRHP · ASF · eBank Network · Cashless Migration

## 1. Mechanism

A Financial Institution Partnership is a competitive government contract awarded to
banks, credit unions, and community development financial institutions to deliver
DED program services to the public. The contracting institution earns per-account
revenue in exchange for meeting defined coverage obligations, service standards,
account terms, and infrastructure requirements.

The tool addresses a structural problem: DED programs that reach every American
require distribution infrastructure that touches every American. Building a
government-owned bank to deliver that infrastructure would be expensive, politically
fraught, and slow. Contracting with existing financial institutions — which already
have branches, digital platforms, regulatory compliance frameworks, and customer
relationships — uses infrastructure that already exists, at a fraction of the cost
of building a parallel system.

Financial Institution Partnerships are **not** grants or subsidies. The institution
earns revenue by performing a service to defined standards. If standards are not met,
payment is reduced or withheld. If the institution exits the contract without a
designated replacement, it is ineligible for future DED contracts. The relationship
is a service contract with performance accountability — the same model that governs
military base commissary operation, Medicaid managed care contracting, and SNAP EBT
card services.

**What the contracting institution provides:**
- Universal no-fee basic accounts for all DED program recipients in its coverage zone
- Direct credit receipt and passthrough for NIT, CRHP, ASF dividend, and Tax Return
  Advance Loan payments
- Physical account access points (branch, ATM, USPS partnership node, or CRHP mobile
  unit) within defined distance standards for all covered accounts
- eBank distributed ledger node operation for its designated geographic coverage zone
- EEO-1 supplement compliance reporting for Job Posting Accountability program
- Anti-predatory account terms: no minimum balance requirements, no overdraft fees
  on basic accounts, no ChexSystems screening for basic account eligibility, no
  closure for non-activity periods below 12 months

**What DED provides in return:**
- Per-account contract revenue: proposed $18–$28/year per active basic account
  (calibrated to cover incremental servicing cost plus modest margin; below current
  market cost for unbanked population acquisition and service)
- Type B co-investment for eBank node facility upgrades (branch-to-data-center
  conversion capital; 40–60% DED share)
- Infrastructure operator designation and regulatory framework (utility-style
  rate protection for eBank node operations; reduced competitive pressure on the
  infrastructure function)
- First-mover positioning in the eBank distributed ledger network — FIP institutions
  are the preferred operators for node assignments in their geographic coverage zone
- Access to the distributed ledger's 0.25% regulated processing rate on all
  ledger-routed transactions within their zone

**The anti-cherry-picking structure:**

The most important design feature: contracts are geographic, not program-specific.
An institution cannot bid for the profitable urban NIT accounts while declining
the rural unbanked accounts in the same region. The contract covers a defined
geographic zone — typically a Federal Reserve sub-district or a state — and requires
the institution to serve all DED program recipients in that zone, regardless of
profitability at the individual account level.

Within a zone, institutions earn the same per-account rate for every account.
The urban professional receiving an ASF dividend earns the institution the same
$18–28/year as the rural unbanked recipient receiving NIT. This cross-subsidization
is the mechanism that makes universal coverage commercially viable — the high-volume,
low-touch accounts in dense areas subsidize the higher-cost rural and unbanked accounts.

## 2. Deployment Levels

### Local / Pilot

A single metropolitan area or rural county cluster. DED awards a pilot contract
to 2–3 competing institutions in the pilot zone. Pilot covers NIT distribution
only (the first program to activate). Measures: account opening rate, account
usage rate, physical access time for covered population, cost per account, incident
rate for prohibited account terms (overdraft fees, minimums applied to basic accounts).

Pilot data informs the national contract structure before full deployment.

### Regional

A Federal Reserve district or multi-state region. Contracts cover NIT + CRHP +
eBank node operation. Multiple competing institutions can hold adjacent sub-zone
contracts within the region. Regional deployment tests the geographic anti-cherry-picking
structure, the eBank node co-investment terms, and the cross-program account consolidation.

### National (Full Deployment)

All DED programs in all zones covered by Financial Institution Partnership contracts.
Full program coverage: NIT, CRHP, ASF dividends, Tax Return Advance Loans, eBank
node operation, cashless migration universal account guarantee. Anti-concentration
cap: no single institution holds contracts covering more than 20% of the national
account base.

## 3. Scale Options

### Pilot Scale (Year 1)

- Coverage: 3 pilot zones (proposed: one dense urban, one mixed, one rural)
- Programs: NIT account hosting only
- Institutions: 8–12 contracts nationally (mix of large national banks, regional
  banks, and credit union networks)
- Account volume: ~2–3M accounts (NIT pilot population)
- DED cost: ~$50–80M/year (per-account revenue at pilot scale)
- eBank: 10-city eBank pilot as an optional extension to pilot FIP contracts

### Limited Scale (Years 2–4)

- Coverage: all metro areas + county seats; approximately 70% of U.S. population
- Programs: NIT + CRHP + eBank node operation
- Account volume: ~40–60M accounts
- DED cost: ~$720M–$1.7B/year in per-account revenue + Type B co-investment
- FIP contracts now cover the Unified Citizen Account (NIT + CRHP on same account)

### Full Deployment (Year 5+)

- Coverage: national; 95%+ of U.S. adult population within defined access standards
- Programs: all DED distribution programs; full eBank node operation; cashless
  migration universal access guarantee
- Account volume: ~200–220M accounts
- DED cost: ~$3.6–6.2B/year in per-account revenue
- eBank co-investment: $8–15B total over buildout period (Type B)
- USPS node agreements cover rural areas where no FIP institution has a physical presence

## 4. Historical Precedents

### SNAP EBT Card Services (1996–present)

The most direct analog. Electronic Benefits Transfer for SNAP (food stamps) is
delivered through contracts with private banks and payment processors. State
governments competitively contract with financial institutions to issue and service
EBT cards for SNAP recipients. The contracting institution earns transaction fees;
the state earns administrative efficiencies over paper vouchers; recipients gain
wider merchant acceptance.

Key data: EBT card contracts cover 42M+ recipients. Administrative cost per
recipient-year is approximately $40–70 through contracted EBT services vs.
$90–120 for equivalent paper-based systems. The FIP per-account rate of $18–28
is well within the EBT precedent range.

**What FIP does differently:** SNAP EBT contracts are program-specific (SNAP only)
and state-administered. FIP contracts are multi-program, federally administered,
and include infrastructure obligations (eBank nodes) beyond simple card services.
The geographic anti-cherry-picking structure is also more explicit in FIP design
than in most EBT contracts.

### Medicaid Managed Care (1980s–present)

State governments contract with private insurance companies to deliver Medicaid
benefits to enrollees. The insurer receives a per-member per-month capitation payment
and is responsible for managing the health needs of the enrolled population within
that payment. Medicaid managed care now covers approximately 70% of all Medicaid
enrollees (~60M people).

Key data: Managed care capitation rates average $450–$700/member/month depending
on population health status. Quality withhold provisions — where 1–5% of the
capitation payment is contingent on performance metrics — have been shown to improve
quality of care in participating plans.

**What FIP borrows:** The per-account (per-member) payment structure; the geographic
coverage obligation; the quality withhold concept (FIP performance metrics tied
to a portion of per-account revenue); and the competitive renewal structure
(contracts are re-bid at defined intervals to maintain market discipline).

### Military Banking — On-Base Financial Institutions (DoD)

The Department of Defense contracts with banks and credit unions to operate
financial institutions on military installations. The contracting institution
has exclusive or preferred access to the installation's customer base in exchange
for providing services at terms DoD specifies — including limits on fees, minimum
product offerings, and financial counseling services. Approximately 250 on-base
banks and credit unions serve the military community.

Key data: DoD negotiates rates and fee structures through the Banking Service
Memorandum of Understanding. Credit unions dominate on-base financial services
because their member-owned structure produces lower fees. The military banking
model demonstrates that government can specify account terms (no hidden fees,
counseling requirements) as contract conditions without operating the bank itself.

**What FIP borrows:** The service-term specification model — DED specifies the
account terms that FIP institutions must offer (no minimum balance, no overdraft
on basic accounts, no ChexSystems screening), just as DoD specifies the financial
product terms for on-base banking. The institution delivers the service; the
government specifies the terms.

### VA Home Loan Guarantee Program (1944–present)

The Department of Veterans Affairs does not make home loans. It guarantees loans
made by private lenders to qualified veterans. The guarantee converts high-risk
borrowers (veterans with limited down payment and credit history) into effectively
low-risk borrowers from the lender's perspective, because the government absorbs
the default risk up to the guarantee limit. The program has enabled 28M+ home
purchases since 1944 with a remarkably low default rate.

Key data: VA-guaranteed loans have a lifetime default rate of approximately 3%,
lower than conventional loans, partly because the guarantee structure incentivizes
lenders to offer good terms and counseling. The guarantee costs Treasury approximately
$1.5–2.5B/year against $300B+ in annual loan volume — a leverage ratio of 150:1.

**What FIP borrows:** The government-as-risk-absorber model. DED's per-account
revenue functions similarly to the VA guarantee: it converts unprofitable account
relationships (unbanked, rural, low-balance) into profitable ones by supplementing
the institution's economics. The government absorbs the cost of universal coverage;
the institution delivers the service.

### Community Reinvestment Act (1977–present)

The Community Reinvestment Act requires federally insured banks to serve the
credit needs of all communities in their geographic footprint, including low-
and moderate-income neighborhoods. Banks are rated on CRA compliance; poor ratings
affect their ability to get regulatory approval for mergers, acquisitions, and
new branches.

Key data: CRA has directed an estimated $6T+ in loans and investments to
underserved communities since 1977, though effectiveness research shows highly
variable outcomes depending on enforcement rigor.

**What FIP borrows and improves:** CRA's geographic obligation concept — banks
cannot serve only profitable areas. FIP operationalizes this more directly: the
per-account revenue makes serving underserved areas financially rational rather
than a regulatory compliance burden. CRA requires service; FIP pays for service.
The shift from mandate-without-compensation to obligation-with-payment is the
key improvement.

## 5. DED Applications

### Primary Application: Unified Citizen Account Distribution

Every DED program that makes payments to individuals delivers through FIP accounts:

| Program | Payment type | FIP delivery mechanism |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| Negative Income Tax | Monthly/bi-monthly direct credit | eBank distributed ledger credit to FIP account |
| CRHP training compensation | Per-training-day payment | Same account; automated on attendance verification |
| ASF dividend | Annual distribution | Same account; national ledger credit at distribution date |
| Tax Return Advance Loan | Advance against tax entitlement | Same account; loan advanced by FIP institution; repaid from IRS settlement |
| Stimulus checks (crisis activation) | One-time direct credit | Same account; emergency activation; no additional enrollment |

Single enrollment covers all five delivery mechanisms. The account is opened once;
payments from multiple programs credit to it automatically.

### Infrastructure Application: eBank Node Operation

FIP institutions are the primary operators of the eBank distributed partitioned
ledger network. The geographic zone of a FIP contract aligns with the node
coverage zone — the institution that holds the FIP contract for a region also
operates the eBank nodes for that region.

DED co-investment (Type B) funds 40–60% of branch-to-node conversion costs.
The institution funds the remainder and earns the regulated eBank node processing
revenue (fees on all ledger-routed transactions in the zone, capped at 0.25%).

### Crisis Application: Emergency Distribution Backbone

In a DED-declared emergency (Tier 1, 2, or 3), stimulus checks, emergency loans,
and supply-constrained benefit payments all route through FIP accounts. The FIP
network is pre-built and tested before the crisis — not assembled during it.

The emergency distribution speed advantage: in a crisis, Treasury does not need to
ask who has a bank account, build a payment list, or wait for direct deposit
enrollment. Every person enrolled in any DED program already has a FIP account.
Payments reach those accounts within hours of authorization.

For the unbanked who are not yet enrolled, CRHP mobile units and USPS nodes are
pre-positioned to open accounts and deliver payments within 24–48 hours of crisis
activation — because the account-opening infrastructure is also operated through FIP.

### Cashless Migration Application: Universal Access Guarantee

Currency Modernization Act Title II requires that every U.S. resident have access
to a no-fee basic distributed ledger account. FIP contracts are the mechanism that
delivers this guarantee. The geographic coverage obligation means that every area
of the country — including the rural counties and unbanked-concentrated neighborhoods
where commercial banking has historically underinvested — is served by a FIP institution
with an obligation to open and maintain basic accounts for any resident.

The cashless transition's binding access precondition (95% unbanked account penetration
before legal tender sunset) is verified through FIP contract compliance reports,
which are filed quarterly with DED and publicly accessible through NEID.

## 6. Targeting Variants

### Geographic Zone Variants

**Urban dense zones:** FIP contracts in dense urban areas are highly competitive.
Multiple institutions bid; contract goes to the institution offering the lowest
total cost (per-account rate required from DED) while meeting coverage and term
standards. The urban cross-subsidy effect is strongest here — large account volumes
produce low per-account cost, generating margin that subsidizes rural obligations.

**Rural/frontier zones:** DED may need to act as anchor — offering a higher per-account
rate ($28–40/year) in rural frontier zones where account volumes are low and physical
access costs are high. Rate differential is funded from the transaction settlement
levy. USPS node agreements supplement FIP coverage where no institution can achieve
physical access standards without USPS partnership.

**Tribal nation zones:** Tribal lands present a distinct legal and infrastructure
context. DED establishes a separate Tribal Financial Institution Partnership track,
in coordination with the Bureau of Indian Affairs and tribal governments, using tribal
credit unions and community development financial institutions as preferred contractors.
Tribal FIP contracts include sovereignty-compatible account terms and governance.

### Institution Type Variants

**Large national banks:** High-volume accounts; strong digital infrastructure; risk
of concentration (mitigated by 20% anti-concentration cap). Well-suited for urban
dense zones and serving the fully banked population.

**Regional and community banks:** Better local knowledge; stronger rural relationships;
less capital for eBank node conversion. DED co-investment terms should be more
generous for community banks (60% DED share vs. 40% for large banks) to offset
smaller balance sheet capacity.

**Credit unions:** Member-owned; structurally aligned with FIP's no-fee, no-predation
account terms (their business model already prohibits most of the prohibited terms).
Strong historical presence in military and underserved communities. Credit unions
are the preferred institution type for Tribal and military community zones and
are given preference in rural zone bid evaluation.

**Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs):** Mission-aligned institutions
with deep relationships in underserved populations. CDFIs are eligible for FIP
contracts in zones where no credit union or community bank has sufficient scale.
DED provides enhanced co-investment (up to 75% DED share for eBank node conversion)
and technical assistance for CDFIs entering the FIP network.

### Performance-Linked Payment Variants

**Standard structure:** Per-account revenue paid monthly, based on active account
count in the prior month.

**Quality withhold variant:** 10% of per-account revenue is held in escrow and
released quarterly based on performance metrics:
- Account opening rate for unbanked/underbanked residents in the zone (target: 90%+)
- Physical access standard compliance (target: 95%+ of zone residents within 15
  miles of an access point)
- Prohibited term incident rate (target: zero — any verified overdraft fee or
  minimum balance violation on a basic account triggers withhold)
- Partition mode reliability (target: 99.5%+ uptime during normal operation;
  successful partition mode activation within 90 seconds of connectivity loss)

**Bonus structure:** Institutions that achieve measurable improvements in unbanked
conversion rates above the target receive a supplemental per-account bonus ($5–10)
for net-new previously-unbanked accounts opened. Creates active commercial incentive
to reach the hardest-to-serve populations.

## 7. Interaction Effects

### With the Negative Income Tax

The NIT's political durability is partly a function of how it is experienced. A
government check in the mail is experienced as a government program. A credit to
an account at a local bank — the same bank where the recipient deposits their
paycheck, pays their bills, and accesses their savings — is experienced as part
of normal financial life. FIP normalizes NIT distribution in a way that prevents
the program from feeling like a stigmatized welfare transfer.

The friction reduction also matters for participation: every hour of enrollment
paperwork, every wait at a government office, every confusing form is a barrier
that suppresses participation among the populations who need the program most.
FIP account opening at a familiar local institution, with staff trained to assist,
reduces the effective participation barrier substantially.

### With CRHP

CRHP enrollment is tied to the FIP account. A CRHP reservist who completes a
training weekend receives automatic credit to their FIP account within 48 hours
of attendance verification. No separate payroll process; no paper check; no
banking delay. The immediacy of payment reduces the administrative friction of
CRHP participation and reinforces the employment-like experience of reserve service.

The CRHP also provides labor to FIP infrastructure: CRHP-enrolled technicians
with network operations training are the maintenance workforce for the eBank nodes
that FIP institutions operate. FIP institutions that meet their node operator
obligations are, in effect, employing CRHP reservists as the technical staff
for that obligation.

### With the ASF Dividend

At Year 15, the first ASF dividend is distributed to all eligible citizens.
The dividend flows through FIP accounts — the same account that has been receiving
NIT and CRHP payments since Year 1 or 2. For the recipient, it is a credit to
their account with a notification that it is their dividend from the national fund.
No new enrollment; no new account; no new institution relationship.

The political effect: the dividend's visibility depends on how it is experienced.
A credit to a familiar account at a familiar institution — with a statement line
reading "American Sovereign Fund Annual Dividend" — is experienced as income, not
as a government program. This is exactly how the Alaska Permanent Fund dividend
is experienced by Alaskans, and it is why they defend it across all political lines.

### With the eBank Distributed Ledger

FIP contracts and eBank node contracts are co-extensive — the same institution,
the same geographic zone, the same facility. This means:

- The institution has a direct financial interest in eBank node uptime (processing
  revenue depends on the node being operational)
- eBank node technical failures are self-incentivized to resolve (the institution
  loses processing revenue during downtime)
- DED does not need to build a separate monitoring or enforcement apparatus for
  node uptime — the institution's own economics create the incentive

The co-extension also means that in a partition event, the FIP institution's
staff are the same people managing both the customer relationship and the node
operation. There is no coordination gap between "the bank" and "the infrastructure
operator" because they are the same entity with a single contract.

### With the Cashless Migration

FIP coverage milestones are the cashless migration's binding preconditions.
The cashless migration plan specifies that legal tender sunset cannot occur until
unbanked account penetration reaches 95%, eBank node coverage reaches 95% of
counties, and all population segments have had 3+ years of reliable access.
These milestones are measured through FIP compliance reporting.

This means the cashless transition is structurally gated on FIP performance.
If FIP institutions fail to achieve coverage milestones, the transition is delayed —
not waived, but delayed until the access infrastructure exists. The population
is never pushed off cash before the alternative is ready.

### With Outcome-Linked Bonds

FIP institutions are natural buyers for DED Outcome-Linked Bonds. The bonds offer
exposure to DED program outcomes (crop yields, energy prices, employment levels)
that are correlated with the credit risk in FIP institutions' loan portfolios.
A bank with heavy agricultural lending in the Corn Belt is exposed to drought
risk in its loan book; an inverse crop yield bond provides a natural hedge.

DED can structure the bond offering to prefer FIP institutions as buyers, creating
a direct financial link between program performance and FIP institution stability.
An FIP institution that holds outcome bonds is more financially resilient in the
scenarios that stress its loan portfolio — and is therefore a more reliable
infrastructure operator in exactly the scenarios when reliability matters most.

## 8. Measurement Framework

### Leading Indicators (Signal the tool is working before full effect)

- Account opening velocity in the first 90 days of contract activation
- Unbanked population enrollment rate (new accounts opened for previously unbanked)
- eBank node uptime from first day of operation
- Per-account revenue paid vs. per-account revenue withheld (quality withhold rate)
- Time to first NIT credit from contract activation date

### Primary Metrics (Direct outcome measurement)

- Account penetration rate: % of DED program-eligible adults with an active FIP account
  in the coverage zone
- Unbanked conversion rate: % of previously unbanked adults now holding FIP accounts
- Physical access compliance: % of zone residents within 15 miles of an access point
- Prohibited term incident rate: number of verified violations per 100,000 accounts per year
- Emergency distribution speed: median hours from authorization to account credit
  during a declared DED emergency
- eBank node reliability: % uptime; partition mode activation success rate

### Lagging Indicators (Second-order effects; watch for unintended consequences)

- NIT participation rate vs. eligible population (FIP should increase participation;
  a stagnant or declining rate suggests enrollment friction remains)
- CRHP enrollment rate vs. eligible population (same logic)
- ASF dividend claiming rate at Year 15 (should approach 100%; shortfall indicates
  account or notification failures)
- Consumer complaint rate to CFPB for FIP institution accounts (monitors for
  unauthorized fee practices not captured in compliance reporting)
- Geographic banking concentration: monitor whether FIP contracts accelerate or
  decelerate bank branch consolidation in rural areas

## 9. Risks and Failure Modes

### Bank Failure / Contract Abandonment

**Risk:** A FIP institution fails (bankruptcy) or exits the contract zone, leaving
accounts without a service provider.

**Mitigation:** FIP contracts require a 180-day transition notice and DED designation
of a replacement operator before exit. DED maintains a backup operator list for
each zone — the second-ranked bidder in the original competition is pre-designated
as the contingency operator. FDIC coordination ensures account balances are protected
through existing deposit insurance during any transition. eBank node operation
transfers to the contingency operator on a defined technical protocol.

The prohibition on abandonment without designated replacement is the structural
protection. An institution that exits without compliance loses eligibility for future
DED contracts and faces regulatory consequences in its FDIC and OCC relationships.

### Geographic Cherry-Picking Within a Zone

**Risk:** FIP institutions serve the urban core of their zone but provide
inadequate access to rural periphery within the same contract zone.

**Mitigation:** Physical access standards are verified quarterly through DED audits,
not solely through self-reported compliance. The quality withhold is specifically
calibrated to the rural access metric — 10% of revenue is at risk if rural access
targets are not met. USPS node agreements serve as the backstop, but USPS node
activation in a zone also triggers a compliance review for the FIP institution
responsible for that area.

### Predatory Terms on Basic Accounts

**Risk:** FIP institution applies fees, minimums, or overdraft charges to accounts
that are designated basic accounts and should be fee-free.

**Mitigation:** CFPB has concurrent oversight authority over all FIP accounts.
Any verified prohibited term application triggers immediate payment withhold,
a mandatory remediation plan within 30 days, and potential contract termination
for repeated violations. The prohibition is defined in statute (Currency Modernization
Act Title II), not just in contract — which means violations are not just a contract
breach but a regulatory violation subject to civil penalty.

### Concentration Risk

**Risk:** Despite the 20% anti-concentration cap, market consolidation among FIP
institutions produces effective oligopoly over the account distribution network —
4–5 large institutions covering 70%+ of accounts.

**Mitigation:** The 20% cap applies to national account share; DED can set lower
caps for individual Federal Reserve districts if concentration is occurring
regionally. Credit union and CDFI preference in bid evaluation actively counters
the large-bank advantage. DED's annual concentration analysis (through NEID) flags
emerging concentration and triggers a contract re-bidding cycle for affected zones
if the cap is approached.

### Systemic Technology Failure

**Risk:** A technology failure at a large FIP institution simultaneously disrupts
eBank node operation and account access across its coverage zone.

**Mitigation:** eBank distributed ledger architecture is specifically designed to
prevent this: each node is independently operable and independently encrypted.
A system failure at the FIP institution's central IT infrastructure triggers
partition mode at affected nodes, maintaining local transaction capability. The
eBank network does not have a single point of failure because the FIP institution
does not operate a single node — it operates multiple independent nodes under the
distributed ledger protocol.

## 10. Cost Estimates

### Per-Account Revenue Rate Derivation

The per-account revenue rate is set to cover:
- Incremental servicing cost for a basic account (estimated $12–18/year for
  a low-activity account at a large institution; $18–30/year at a community bank)
- A margin sufficient to make participation commercially rational (proposed: 10–20%
  above estimated incremental cost)
- A rural coverage premium included in the rate structure (the blended rate
  across dense and rural accounts within a zone is set so the cross-subsidy works)

Proposed rate schedule:
- Dense urban zones: $18/year per active account
- Mixed suburban zones: $22/year per active account
- Rural/frontier zones: $28/year per active account
- CDFI-operated accounts (all zones): $32/year (enhanced rate reflecting
  higher per-account service cost for CDFIs' target population)

### National Cost Estimate

| Scale | Active accounts | Annual DED cost |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| Pilot (Year 1) | 2–3M | $40–80M |
| Limited (Year 2–4) | 40–60M | $880M–1.7B |
| Full (Year 5+) | 180–220M | $3.2–6.2B |

eBank co-investment (Type B, one-time buildout): $8–15B over Years 1–7, shared
50/50 on average between DED and FIP institutions. Net DED share: $4–7.5B.

Quality withhold amount held in escrow: 10% of annual per-account revenue.
Expected payout rate (performance above threshold): ~85–90% in steady state.
Expected annual withhold (penalty for non-performance): ~$300–600M at full scale.

### Revenue Generated by FIP for DED Programs

The FIP network, by enabling efficient distribution, reduces the administrative
cost of each program it distributes compared to standalone distribution:

| Program | Standalone distribution cost | FIP distribution cost | Annual savings |
|---------|----------------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| NIT (30M recipients) | $90/recipient = $2.7B | $22 avg FIP rate = $660M | ~$2B/year |
| CRHP compensation | $60/payment cycle = $1.2B | Embedded in FIP rate | ~$800M/year |
| ASF dividend | $15/distribution = $3.75B (250M) | Embedded in FIP rate | ~$3B/year at Year 15 |
| Emergency stimulus (crisis) | $120/payment = $36B (300M) | FIP existing accounts: $2/credit | ~$34B/event |

The FIP network's administrative efficiency savings substantially exceed the
per-account contract cost at full deployment. The $3.2–6.2B/year in FIP contract
revenue to institutions is offset by ~$5–6B/year in reduced program distribution
costs — making the net DED cost of maintaining the FIP network approximately zero
in steady state.
