# DED Tool Analysis: Mandatory Paid Internship Floor

**Category:** Labor Mobilization / Human Capital Development
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Relevant Scenarios:** AI Unemployment (primary), Pandemic (workforce continuity), all scenarios
  requiring skilled workforce build-out
**Related Tools:** [strategic-contracts.md](strategic-contracts.md) | [stimulus-checks.md](stimulus-checks.md) | [expert-reserve-program.md](expert-reserve-program.md)
**DED Tool Rating:** Tier A — Structural; addresses entry-level labor market failure that no
  market mechanism corrects on its own

---

## 1. Mechanism

Require every private sector employer above a defined size threshold to maintain at minimum
one paid internship position for every 100 full-time-equivalent employees. The requirement
establishes a floor — a structural minimum — not a ceiling.

The mechanism addresses a specific market failure: large employers benefit from an educated,
trained workforce but increasingly externalize the cost of entry-level development onto
educational institutions, unpaid interns, and individuals willing to absorb training costs
themselves. As AI eliminates the routine entry-level roles that previously served as
on-ramps to skilled employment, this externalization accelerates. Without a floor, the
ladder disappears from the bottom.

**This is not a jobs program.** The DED does not create the internship positions directly.
It sets the minimum that qualifying employers must maintain — and enforces it through
annual reporting requirements already in place (EEO-1 filing infrastructure).

---

## 2. Policy Design

### Coverage Threshold

**Qualifying employers:** Any private sector employer with ≥100 full-time-equivalent employees.

The 100-employee threshold is the natural floor: it produces exactly 1 required position at
the minimum qualifying size (1 per 100 = 1). Below 100 employees, the administrative burden
of maintaining a formal internship program is disproportionate and the organizational capacity
to absorb a developmental role is limited.

**Exemptions:**
- **New business exemption:** Businesses incorporated within the prior 36 months are fully
  exempt. Startups in formation should not be penalized for scaling.
- **Hyper-growth exemption:** Companies growing headcount >75% year-over-year may defer
  compliance for 12 months — rapid hiring is itself creating entry-level employment faster
  than an internship requirement adds to it.
- **Financial distress exemption:** Employers in Chapter 11 bankruptcy proceedings or with
  a documented operating loss for 2 consecutive years receive a 12-month suspension.
  Enforced hardship is a failure mode, not an intent.

### Position Definition

A qualifying internship must meet all of the following:
- **Paid:** At or above $17/hour (2026 reference; indexed to CPI annually). Unpaid internships
  do not count toward compliance regardless of educational credit arrangements.
- **Minimum duration:** 10 weeks at 20+ hours per week (or equivalent: a 5-week full-time
  rotation counts; a one-day shadow does not)
- **Developmental:** The position must include a documented learning objective and a designated
  supervisor. It may not be used as permanent labor displacement.
- **Eligible workers:** Open to individuals who have not held a position at the company before.
  Existing employees in internship-style programs do not count toward compliance.

### Compliance Mechanism

**Reporting:** Annual declaration via EEO-1 supplement. Companies report:
- Total FTE headcount (determines required slots)
- Number of qualifying internship-weeks completed in the prior year
- Average compensation rate
- Occupation categories served

**Penalty for non-compliance:** $6,000 per unfilled required slot per year. A company with
500 employees that completes zero qualifying internships owes $30,000/year — less than the
cost of two required positions, but meaningful enough to make compliance cheaper than
non-compliance.

**Alternative compliance pathway — Industry Pool Contribution:**
Companies that cannot absorb interns directly (e.g., highly specialized operations with no
entry-level developmental work available) may contribute to a sector-managed industry intern
fund at 125% of the direct hiring cost per unfilled slot ($6,800/slot contributed). The fund
contracts internships with qualifying nonprofits, government agencies, national laboratories,
and DED-contracted training providers in the same sector. The internship happens in the
industry; the employer funds it without hosting it.

## 3. Scale and Job Creation Estimates

### Qualifying Workforce Baseline

| Company Size Band | Share of Private Employment | Approx. Workers |
|-------------------|-----------------------------|----------------|
| 1–99 employees (exempt) | ~32% | 41.6M |
| 100–499 employees | ~18% | 23.4M |
| 500+ employees | ~50% | 65.0M |
| **Qualifying total (100+)** | **~68%** | **~88.4M** |

*Source basis: BLS Business Employment Dynamics, private sector; ~130M total private employment.*

### Required Slots Under the Policy

88.4M qualifying FTE ÷ 100 = **884,000 required concurrent intern slots**

Adjusting for exemptions (new businesses, hyper-growth, distress): approximately **15%**
of qualifying headcount is likely exempt at any given time, reducing effective required
slots to:

88.4M × 0.85 ÷ 100 = **~751,000 required concurrent slots**

### Currently Existing Qualifying Positions

- NACE estimates ~1.5–1.8M total U.S. internships annually
- Approximately 60–65% are paid: ~900k–1.17M paid annually
- Assuming 12-week average and 2.5 rotations/year at qualifying employers:
  concurrent paid slots at qualifying employers ≈ 900k ÷ 2.5 = **~360,000 currently**
- Many of these are concentrated in large tech, finance, and consulting firms — significant
  coverage gaps exist in manufacturing, healthcare, logistics, and retail

### Net New Positions Created

| Component | Estimate |
|-----------|----------|
| Required concurrent slots | 751,000 |
| Currently existing concurrent (at qualifying firms) | ~360,000 |
| **Net new concurrent slots required** | **~390,000** |
| Annual intern experiences (at 2.5 rotations/year) | **~975,000 new per year** |
| Unpaid-to-paid conversions (currently unpaid at qualifying firms) | ~180,000–220,000 |
| **Total new paid intern experiences per year** | **~1.15–1.2 million** |

Rounded conservatively: **~1 million new paid internship experiences per year.**

For context: approximately 2 million students graduate from 4-year colleges annually;
1.5 million graduate from 2-year programs. The internship floor would create meaningful
paid work experience for roughly **20–25% of all new graduates** — entirely through
existing employer capacity, not government job creation.

### Wage Impact

- Average qualifying paid internship: $17–20/hr × 30 hrs/week × 12 weeks = **$6,120–$7,200 per placement**
- At 2.5 rotations/year per slot, annual cost per slot: **$15,300–$18,000**
- For 390,000 net new slots: **$6.0–$7.0B in new wages per year**
- For existing converted (unpaid-to-paid): additional $1.1–$1.6B in new wages
- **Total new wage creation: ~$7–8.5B per year**

For comparison: this is roughly the annual cost of 2–3 months of the Federal Pandemic
Unemployment Compensation program during COVID — but it is self-funded by employers, not
by the Treasury, and it creates a structural pipeline rather than a one-time transfer.

## 4. Historical Precedents

### The Apprenticeship Model — Germany and Switzerland

Germany's dual-system apprenticeship (Ausbildung) requires employers to participate in
training the workforce that will fill their skilled roles. Approximately 1.3 million
apprentices are in active training in Germany at any given time, covering ~330 recognized
occupational categories. Youth unemployment in Germany (6–8%) consistently runs at roughly
half the rate of countries with no equivalent structure.

The internship floor is the U.S.-adapted version of this logic: not a full apprenticeship
system (which requires occupational regulation the U.S. lacks), but a structural minimum
that embeds employer responsibility for entry-level development.

### Davis-Bacon and Prevailing Wage Standards

The principle that employers cannot use government-adjacent labor markets to compress wages
below a floor is well-established in U.S. law. The internship floor applies the same logic
to developmental labor: if you benefit from operating in the U.S. economy and employing
at scale, you bear a minimum responsibility for developing the next generation of that
workforce.

### WARN Act Reporting Infrastructure

The policy's compliance mechanism — annual reporting via EEO-1 supplement — builds directly
on existing WARN Act and EEO-1 filing infrastructure. No new reporting system is required.
The administrative burden on employers is minimal: one additional data field in an existing
annual filing.

## 5. DED Applications

### AI Unemployment — Primary Application

The internship floor directly addresses the "missing rung" problem in an AI-displaced labor
market. As AI eliminates routine entry-level positions in clerical, data entry, customer
service, and basic analysis roles, the first jobs that gave workers professional credentials
and employer networks disappear. The floor ensures that large employers maintain a formal
entry point regardless of how automated their operations become.

**AI-specific reinforcement:** The DED can condition ATC rate reductions (see
`ded/scenarios/ai-unemployment/phase-1-triage-and-stabilization.md`) on internship floor
compliance — companies paying the AI Transition Contribution that also meet or exceed the
internship floor receive a 2-percentage-point rate reduction. This creates aligned incentive:
deploy AI, pay the externality cost, AND maintain the workforce development pipeline.

**AI exposure requirement:** For companies above 500 employees in the technology sector,
DED recommends (not mandates) that at least 25% of internship rotations include a documented
AI tool literacy component. This creates the first rung of the AI literacy ladder at scale.

### Pandemic — Workforce Continuity

During a pandemic Phase 1 declaration, the internship floor can be suspended for contact-heavy
industries (food service, hospitality) while being maintained or accelerated for remote-capable
industries. This preserves the pipeline where safe and suspends the burden where impossible.
Suspension is temporary and auto-resumes at Phase 2.

### All Scenarios — CRHP and ERP Pipeline

Internship floor positions at qualifying employers are explicitly recognized as eligible for
DED credential programs:
- Interns who complete a DED-recognized skill module during their rotation receive a
  CRHP-eligible certification, creating a direct pipeline from employer internship to reserve
  enrollment
- For interns at ERP-enrolled employers (companies with Type D contract members), the
  internship can include supervised exposure to DED-priority skill domains — a pre-pipeline
  for future ERP eligibility

## 6. Targeting Variants

**Geographic targeting:** DED can recommend that companies with operations in designated
economic distress zones allocate a minimum fraction of required slots to workers from those
zones. This converts the national policy into a regional economic development tool without
changing the overall structure.

**Demographic targeting:** Companies receiving DED strategic contracts (Types A, B, C) face
an enhanced internship floor: 1 per 75 FTE rather than 1 per 100, with a documented
outreach requirement to first-generation college students and workers in ODRI Tier 1
displaced-occupation households. This links contract benefits to development obligations.

**Sector targeting:** Industries where AI displacement risk is highest (finance, legal, data
processing) receive a 1-per-75 requirement starting in Year 3 of the policy — giving them
time to design programs before the steeper floor takes effect.

## 7. Interaction Effects

**Internship floor + ATC:** Companies paying ATC earn a rate reduction for internship
compliance above floor (1 per 75 FTE or higher). Aligns the two instruments: the
companies most responsible for displacement bear both the levy cost and the development
obligation, and receive relief for meeting both.

**Internship floor + TRAL:** Interns whose household income falls below 200% of the federal
poverty line are eligible for TRAL advances during their internship rotation. Removes the
financial barrier for low-income workers who would otherwise be unable to accept a paid
internship in a distant city or during a semester requiring tuition payment.

**Internship floor + CRHP:** Interns who complete qualifying rotations at CRHP-partner
employers automatically qualify for CRHP enrollment at the subsidized fee rate. The
internship is the first step of the civilian reserve pipeline — creating the broadest
possible funnel into DED-relevant workforce development.

**Internship floor + Type B retraining contracts:** Companies holding DED Type B retraining
infrastructure contracts are required to maintain internship floor compliance — the contracts
that fund workforce development cannot be held by employers who are not themselves
developing entry-level workers.

## 8. Risks and Failure Modes

### Credential Inflation / Checkbox Compliance
**Risk:** Companies create nominal internship positions that technically qualify but provide
no meaningful development — "coffee runs" at $17/hour.

**Mitigation:** The "documented learning objective" requirement gives DOL audit authority to
review program substance. DED maintains a public registry of employer internship program
descriptions; companies with patterns of minimal programs face enhanced scrutiny. Market
pressure also mitigates this: companies that run poor programs attract fewer qualified
applicants, defeating the talent pipeline purpose.

### Geographic Mismatch
**Risk:** Required slots are created in San Francisco and New York; displaced workers are in
Ohio and Mississippi.

**Mitigation:** Remote internship eligibility (validated by 2020–2022 experience) means
geographic location of the employer and the intern can differ. Companies with headquarters
in high-cost metros can run remote internship programs drawing from lower-cost-of-living
regions. The $17/hour floor is economically meaningful across most of the country.

### Small Company Exemption Exploitation
**Risk:** Large companies restructure as networks of subsidiaries below 100 FTE to avoid
compliance.

**Mitigation:** The policy applies at the employer group level (parent + subsidiaries under
common control), matching existing IRS affiliated group definitions. Restructuring for
exemption is treated as evasion.

## 9. Cost Summary

| Stakeholder | Cost | Offset |
|-------------|------|--------|
| Qualifying employers (net new positions) | $6.0–7.0B/year in wages | Talent pipeline value; ATC rate reduction for ATC payers |
| Federal government | Near-zero (EEO-1 reporting is existing infrastructure; enforcement adds ~$50M/year) | No general appropriation required |
| Interns | Zero (paid positions) | $6.1–7.2k in wages per rotation; work experience, credential, CRHP eligibility |
| Industry pool contributors | 125% of direct hire cost per unfilled slot | No hosting burden; positions created in sector |

**The key number for policy presentation:**
~1 million new paid work experiences per year, costing the federal government near-zero,
funded entirely by the private sector employers who benefit from a trained workforce —
achieved by requiring that they maintain the minimum development function that large
employers have historically provided and are increasingly abandoning as AI reduces the
need for entry-level headcount.
