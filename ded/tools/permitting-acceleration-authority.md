# DED Tool: Permitting Acceleration Authority

**Document Type:** Tool Analysis
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Category:** Cross-Scenario Infrastructure Tool
**Related Scenarios:** Semiconductor Collapse, Rare Earth Denial, Grid Cyberattack,
  Oil Restriction, Autonomous Highway Lane Program, Pandemic (manufacturing surge)
**Related Tools:** Expert Reserve Program, Strategic contracts (Type B), Sovereign
  Compute Reserve, DPA invocation

---

## The Problem This Tool Solves

Five scenarios in the DED portfolio share a common bottleneck that is not addressed by
any existing tool: the gap between when a strategic investment is committed and when it
can legally begin operation.

| Scenario | Investment Committed | Permit Timeline | Production Gap |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Semiconductor fab (advanced node) | CHIPS Act funding awarded | 4-7 years | $5-15B/year foregone output per year of delay |
| REE processing facility | DED Type B contract signed | 5-9 years | $1-3B/year in continued import dependency |
| Domestic LPT manufacturing | DoD/DED contract awarded | 3-5 years | 12-24 month grid restoration gap per damaged transformer |
| Transmission line (AHLP corridor + grid hardening) | Utility capital plan approved | 7-12 years | 2-4 GW/year foregone clean energy capacity |
| Mountain Pass → Round Top REE expansion | Mining plan filed | 5-8 years | Each additional domestic REE site reduces CRS by ~0.05 |
| Pharmaceutical API domestic manufacturing | DED Type B co-investment | 3-6 years | Continued 90%+ import dependency during pandemic scenario |

These are not gaps in funding. They are not gaps in technical capability. They are gaps
caused by permitting processes that were designed for routine infrastructure — not for
supply chain crisis response.

**The paradox:** DED can write a Type B contract committing $2B to a rare earth processing
facility today. That facility cannot produce a gram of separated oxide until 2033 at the
earliest, not because the chemistry is uncertain or the capital is insufficient, but because
the environmental review, the state permits, the Army Corps wetlands determination, the
tribal consultation, and the local zoning approvals will take 7-9 years to work through
a system designed to process one application at a time.

The expected annual loss from permitting delay — not from supply disruption, but from the
inability to build the response — is estimated at $30-70B/year across the DED scenario
portfolio. This is the cost of the gap between "committed to build" and "permitted to build."

## Existing Reform Baseline

### What Has Already Been Attempted

The permitting delay problem is not new. Congress has attempted to address it repeatedly:

**FAST-41 (2015):** Created the Federal Permitting Improvement Steering Council (FPISC,
the "Permitting Council") under OMB. Required coordinated federal review for major
infrastructure projects; established a public permitting dashboard; set a goal (not a
mandate) of completing federal environmental review within two years. Results: meaningful
improvement for some projects; median permitting time for FAST-41-covered projects fell
from 4.5 years to 2.5 years. But "covered projects" is a narrow category and voluntary
inclusion limits reach.

**One Federal Decision (2018, extended 2021):** Executive Order requiring a single
lead agency to coordinate all federal reviews for major infrastructure; one Environmental
Impact Statement covering all agencies' concerns; 2-year target for EIS completion. Results:
valuable for projects where all federal agencies cooperate; less effective when agencies
have competing mandates (Fish and Wildlife Service ESA review often runs independently).

**Fiscal Responsibility Act (2023) — NEPA Title III:** The most significant NEPA reform
in decades. Key provisions:
- 2-year statutory deadline for Environmental Impact Statements (major actions)
- 1-year deadline for Environmental Assessments
- Page limits (150 pages for EIS; 75 pages for EA)
- Categorical exclusion expansion for routine actions
- Lead agency designation mandatory within 45 days of application

Results: Real improvement for routine infrastructure. Insufficient for the specific
problem DED faces: crisis-timeline construction of novel industrial facilities in locations
that may have never hosted that land use type before.

**CHIPS Act §9902 (2022):** Created a dedicated Commerce Department facilitation role
for semiconductor manufacturing facility permits. Commerce acts as a liaison between
applicants and permitting agencies; expedited coordination for CHIPS-funded fabs. Results:
Intel Ohio is still delayed to 2030; TSMC Arizona benefited from existing permits at the
Taiwanese-owned site; the facilitation role helps but doesn't compress timelines for
genuinely new sites.

### The Gap These Reforms Don't Close

All existing reforms share three structural limitations:

**1. They apply process improvements to the same sequential review structure.**
The FRA 2023 two-year deadline still runs through the same steps in the same order:
scoping → baseline surveys → draft EIS → public comment → final EIS → agency decision →
potential litigation. Compressing each step still doesn't address the sequential dependency.
Every step waits for the previous one. DED scenario timelines require parallel processing.

**2. They have no crisis trigger mechanism.**
Existing reforms apply the same acceleration uniformly to all covered projects. A parking
garage and a semiconductor fab get the same treatment. There is no mechanism for
a declared national economic emergency to differentiate processing timelines based on
strategic priority — the way a wartime emergency would.

**3. They don't address the pre-application data gap.**
The longest phase of permitting is often the baseline data collection phase — the 2-3 years
of ecological surveys, wetlands delineation, cultural resource surveys, and air/water
baseline monitoring required before an Environmental Impact Statement can even be written.
No existing reform funds or pre-completes this data collection for sites identified as
strategically important before a crisis application is filed.

## DED Permitting Acceleration Authority Design

### Core Instrument: National Economic Security Project Designation

**What it is:**
The Permitting Acceleration Authority (PAA) establishes a new project category —
National Economic Security Project (NESP) — that triggers a distinct permitting pathway
with statutory deadlines, concurrent review mandates, and pre-positioned baseline data.

**NESP designation criteria:**
A project qualifies for NESP designation if DED certifies that:
1. The project produces or processes a commodity on the NEID Critical Materials Register,
   or constitutes critical infrastructure identified in a DED Scenario Risk Assessment; AND
2. The project would materially reduce a Concentration Risk Score above 0.50, reduce
   an Expected Annual Loss by >$1B/year, or enable a DED Tier 1/2 scenario response
   capability that does not currently exist at sufficient scale; AND
3. The project has received or is eligible for DED Type B co-investment, a CHIPS Act
   award, a DoD strategic contract, or an equivalent federal commitment

**Who designates:** DED Secretary, with concurrence of CEQ (Council on Environmental
Quality). Designation is published in the Federal Register; subject to judicial review
but with a 30-day expedited review period before stays can be granted.

**Annual cap:** Maximum 25 NESP designations per year to prevent dilution; DED must
prioritize by CRS impact and EAL reduction per designation.

### Program 1: Pre-Positioned Environmental Baseline Program

**The problem it solves:**
Baseline data collection (ecological surveys, wetlands, cultural resources, air/water
monitoring) takes 2-3 years and must precede the EIS by statute. For a site that has
never been surveyed, this phase alone can exceed the post-FRA two-year EIS deadline.
DED cannot compress data collection that hasn't started.

**The solution:**
DED pre-funds and pre-completes baseline surveys for 50-100 sites identified by NEID
as likely locations for future critical industrial capacity, before any applicant files.
When a scenario is declared and a developer applies, the baseline data already exists.
The EIS clock starts at draft preparation, not at baseline surveys.

**Site identification methodology:**
- NEID identifies candidate sites using: existing zoning (industrial-compatible areas
  near relevant raw materials, transport, or utilities), DED CRS analysis (sites that
  would reduce geographic concentration in critical supply chains), and Expert Reserve
  Program geological/engineering assessments
- Sites are categorized by type: semiconductor fab-suitable (flat, water access, power
  grid capacity, cleanroom-compatible ground conditions); REE processing-suitable
  (chemical processing infrastructure, waste management capacity, rail/port access);
  LPT manufacturing-suitable (heavy industrial zoning, transformer-rated power supply,
  rail access for large cargo); transmission corridor-suitable (geographic routing analysis)
- Pre-surveyed sites are published in the DED Site Register — public disclosure that
  signals potential industrial development to communities and enables proactive local
  engagement before an application is filed

**What the baseline surveys cover:**
Each pre-surveyed site receives:
- Phase I and Phase II environmental site assessments
- Wetlands delineation (Army Corps jurisdictional determination; establishes Section 404
  scope before application)
- Two full years of ecological monitoring (breeding bird surveys, bat surveys,
  threatened/endangered species habitat assessment) — satisfying the FWS/NMFS baseline
  requirement
- Cultural resource survey (NHPA Section 106 Phase I and II; coordination with State
  Historic Preservation Office and relevant tribal nations)
- Air quality baseline monitoring (2-year continuous monitoring establishing pre-project
  baseline; required for EPA air permit modeling)
- Hydrological baseline (groundwater levels, surface water quality, floodplain mapping)

When an applicant files for a NESP-designated project at a pre-surveyed site, the
entire baseline phase is complete. The EIS can begin at scoping. The review timeline
compresses from 7-9 years to 2-3 years for the permitting phases.

**Cost:** $5-10M per site × 100 sites = $500M-1B over 5 years (one-time capital);
$50-80M/year for ongoing monitoring to keep baseline data current.

**Return:** One year of delay on a $10B semiconductor fab costs approximately
$800M-1.5B in foregone production. The baseline survey program for a single fab site
costs $8-12M. The cost ratio is approximately 1:100.

### Program 2: Concurrent Federal Review Mandate

**The problem it solves:**
Federal agencies currently review NESP-type projects sequentially. EPA completes its
air quality analysis; then Army Corps reviews wetlands; then Fish and Wildlife Service
completes its biological opinion; then the lead agency can finalize the EIS. Each agency
waits for the previous one's output. Total sequential time: 4-6 years even after FRA 2023.

**The solution:**
For NESP-designated projects, all federal agencies with review authority are required
to conduct their reviews concurrently, with a shared data room maintained by the lead
agency and coordinated through the DED Permitting Coordinator (a dedicated senior position
within the DED Operations Office).

**Concurrent review structure:**

| Agency | Review Type | Concurrent Deadline |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| EPA | Air quality (Clean Air Act §165); RCRA (hazardous waste if applicable) | 12 months from NESP application |
| Army Corps of Engineers | Section 404 (wetlands/waters of U.S.) | 12 months from NESP application |
| Fish and Wildlife Service | Section 7 ESA biological opinion | 15 months from NESP application |
| National Marine Fisheries Service (if applicable) | Section 7 ESA (aquatic species) | 15 months from NESP application |
| State Historic Preservation Office | NHPA Section 106 | 9 months from NESP application |
| Bureau of Land Management / Forest Service (federal land) | Right-of-way authorization | 12 months from NESP application |
| Lead agency (Commerce/DOE/DED) | NEPA EIS | 18 months from NESP application |

**Deemed-approved provision:**
If any federal agency fails to complete its required review within the statutory deadline
for a NESP-designated project:
- The agency must provide written notice of delay with specific basis; DED Secretary and
  OMB Director are notified within 48 hours
- If the delay extends beyond 90 days past the statutory deadline, the review is deemed
  complete with standard conditions unless the agency provides a specific finding of
  significant unaddressed environmental harm
- Deemed-approval with standard conditions preserves environmental protection (all standard
  mitigation measures apply) while preventing indefinite delay from agency capacity constraints

**Legal basis:**
The deemed-approved provision requires statutory authorization — this is the most legally
novel element of the PAA. Precedent exists in FAST-41's dashboard and coordination
requirements, and in the NRC's expedited review procedures for nuclear facilities.
The constitutional basis is Commerce Clause authority over facilities affecting interstate
commerce, combined with DED's statutory mandate for critical supply chain protection.

The deemed-approved provision will face legal challenge. The design anticipates this by:
(1) preserving standard environmental conditions (not eliminating environmental protection,
compressing its timeline); (2) providing explicit agency notification and opportunity to
raise specific concerns; (3) limiting the provision to NESP-designated projects only
(not a general NEPA waiver); and (4) maintaining judicial review of the designation itself.

### Program 3: Expert Reserve Program — Regulatory Track

**The problem it solves:**
Even with concurrent timelines, the bottleneck often shifts to the applicant: preparing
a technically adequate EIS, responding to agency comments, managing tribal consultation,
and navigating state permitting processes in parallel with federal review requires expertise
that most industrial companies lack in-house. A semiconductor company knows how to build
a fab; it does not necessarily know how to write a NEPA-compliant EIS for one.

**The solution:**
DED's Expert Reserve Program adds a dedicated Regulatory Track — a roster of enrolled
specialists in NEPA compliance, environmental law, tribal consultation, and state
permitting — available for deployment to NESP-designated projects.

**Regulatory Track specialties:**

| Specialty | Role in NESP Permitting | Typical Source |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------|
| NEPA specialists (EIS preparers) | Draft EIS; coordinate agency scoping; manage public comment | Retired CEQ/DOE/DoD NEPA staff; environmental consulting firms |
| Environmental attorneys (NEPA litigation) | Anticipate and respond to legal challenges; draft project-specific categorical exclusion petitions | DOJ Environment Division alumni; private environmental law firms |
| Tribal consultation coordinators | NHPA Section 106; government-to-government consultation protocols; tribal historic preservation officers | BIA alumni; tribal affairs specialists |
| Army Corps Section 404 specialists | Wetlands delineation; compensatory mitigation design; Section 404 permit applications | Corps of Engineers alumni; wetlands consulting |
| ESA Section 7 specialists | Biological assessment preparation; FWS/NMFS coordination; mitigation measure design | FWS/NMFS alumni; endangered species consulting |
| State permitting coordinators (by state) | State environmental review (CEQA, SEQRA, state equivalents); local zoning; air/water permits | State agency alumni; state environmental law specialists |

**Deployment model:**
- NESP applicants request ERP Regulatory Track deployment through DED
- DED assigns a Regulatory Coordinator (senior ERP member) to manage all permitting
  workstreams for the project
- Additional specialists deployed based on project-specific needs
- ERP members work embedded with the applicant's project team but report to DED;
  maintain their independence on environmental compliance questions
- Applicant pays ERP member compensation at cost (no profit margin for DED); DED
  subsidizes 50% of cost for projects with Type B co-investment where the applicant
  is a small or mid-size domestic producer

**Why this is better than consultants:**
Private NEPA consultants exist but have uneven quality and no special relationship with
agencies. ERP Regulatory Track members have: agency relationships (former colleagues at
EPA, Army Corps, FWS who know the preferred formats and can flag problems early);
cross-project pattern recognition (having managed ten EIS processes, they know what
agencies will object to before comment); and DED backing (when an ERP Coordinator
calls the Army Corps district office, they have institutional standing).

**ERP Regulatory Track enrollment:**
Target 200 enrolled specialists across all specialties. Compensation: $150-250/hour
retainer during standby; full deployment compensation during active assignment.
Annual standby cost: $15-25M. Deployment cost: $50-100M/year (project-funded).

### Program 4: Categorical Exclusion Expansion for NESP Projects

**The problem it solves:**
NEPA's Categorical Exclusion (CE) mechanism allows agencies to approve routine actions
without a full EIS or Environmental Assessment. Each agency maintains its own CE list.
Many activities that are genuinely routine — expanding a building at an existing industrial
facility, installing additional equipment within an already-permitted envelope — still
require EIS preparation because the CE lists haven't been updated to reflect modern
industrial operations.

**The solution:**
For NESP-designated projects at sites with completed pre-positioned baseline surveys and
prior environmental review, DED proposes a set of Categorical Exclusions applicable
to common NESP-relevant activities:

| Activity | Current Treatment | Proposed NESP CE |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| Capacity expansion at permitted semiconductor fab | Full EA or EIS (typically) | CE if within existing permitted envelope + DED certification |
| New processing equipment at existing REE facility | EA (typically) | CE if equipment type previously reviewed; DED certification |
| Substation expansion at existing transmission site | EA (typically) | CE if within existing right-of-way; DED certification |
| LPT installation at pre-surveyed substation site | Full EIS if new substation | CE if site in DED Site Register with complete baseline survey |
| Domestic pharmaceutical API manufacturing at existing GMP facility | Typically no federal nexus | CE establishes clear federal nexus and approval mechanism |

**Legal mechanism:**
Agency-specific CE expansion requires agency rulemaking — this is standard administrative
law, not novel. CEQ's unified CE provision (40 CFR §1507.3) allows agencies to establish
CEs for classes of actions. DED proposes a coordinated rulemaking across EPA, Army Corps,
DOE, and Commerce for NESP-relevant CEs; CEQ provides coordination under its NEPA
implementation authority.

### Program 5: State Permitting Compact

**The problem it solves:**
Federal permitting is only half the timeline. State environmental review (California CEQA,
New York SEQRA, Texas TCEQ, and equivalents in every state) runs independently of and
often longer than federal review. States are not bound by federal deadlines, deemed-approval
provisions, or NESP designation. A NESP project that completes federal review in 18 months
can still be held up for 5+ additional years in state permitting.

**The solution:**
DED proposes a Critical Infrastructure Permitting Compact — a voluntary interstate
agreement (modeled on the Emergency Management Assistance Compact) in which member states
commit to:

1. **Concurrent state/federal review:** State environmental review proceeds simultaneously
   with federal NEPA review; states accept federal baseline data from DED pre-positioned
   surveys to avoid duplicative data collection
2. **12-month state review deadline for NESP projects:** Member states commit to completing
   state environmental review within 12 months of NESP designation; this is aspirational
   (states cannot be federally mandated) but compact membership is the condition of priority
   for certain DED co-investments
3. **Single-window permitting:** For NESP projects, a single state agency coordinates
   all state and local permits; applicants do not navigate 6-12 state agencies independently
4. **Pre-zoning for strategic sites:** Compact member states designate DED Site Register
   locations as pre-zoned for industrial use, eliminating local zoning hearings for
   projects matching the pre-zoned use type

**Incentive for state participation:**
States that join the Compact receive:
- Priority siting for DED-co-invested NESP projects (DED prefers compact-member states
  for Type B co-investment when multiple sites are eligible)
- Access to DED pre-positioned baseline survey data for their own state planning
- Federal ERP Regulatory Track support for state-level permitting at no cost to the state
- Priority CRHP deployment to compact-member state infrastructure projects

**Political note:**
The Compact is voluntary; it does not override state sovereignty over land use. It is
structured as an economic incentive (NESP siting preference) rather than a mandate.
States with high regulatory friction (California, New York) are the highest-value
compact members but will have the most political resistance. The initial compact will
likely be signed by industrial-friendly states (Texas, Arizona, North Carolina, Ohio);
competitive pressure from NESP investment siting preference will draw additional states
over time — the same dynamic that drove most states to adopt uniform commercial codes
and environmental review timelines under prior compact models.

## Timeline Impact Analysis

### Semiconductor Fab (Illustrative)

| Phase | Without PAA | With PAA |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Site selection and pre-application | 12-24 months | 3-6 months (NEID site register + baseline data available) |
| Baseline data collection | 24-36 months | 0 months (pre-completed) |
| EIS scoping and preparation | 18-24 months | 9-12 months (concurrent agencies; ERP support) |
| Agency review and comment | 18-24 months (sequential) | 6-9 months (concurrent) |
| Final EIS and Record of Decision | 6-12 months | 3-6 months |
| State permitting | 24-48 months (parallel or serial) | 12-18 months (Compact member) |
| Local permitting | 6-18 months | 3-6 months (pre-zoned sites) |
| **Total permitting** | **7-12 years** | **2-3 years** |
| **Construction (unchanged)** | 3-4 years | 3-4 years |
| **Total: commitment to production** | **10-16 years** | **5-7 years** |

**Value of compression for semiconductor fab:**
A world-scale advanced fab produces ~$5-10B/year in output. A 5-year compression (10 years
to 5 years) captures $25-50B in production that would have been lost to delay. Against the
PAA cost attributable to that project (~$15-30M for baseline surveys + ERP deployment),
the return is approximately 1,000:1 on the project-specific cost.

### REE Processing Facility (Illustrative)

| Phase | Without PAA | With PAA |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Baseline surveys | 24-36 months | 0 months (pre-completed) |
| NEPA review | 24-36 months | 12-18 months (concurrent; CEs for processing equipment) |
| State permits | 18-36 months | 12 months (Compact) |
| Local/tribal | 12-24 months | 6-12 months (ERP tribal coordination; pre-surveyed NHPA) |
| **Total permitting** | **6-9 years** | **2-3 years** |

**Value:** Every year of delay on a domestic REE processing facility means $1-3B in
continued Chinese processing dependency. A 4-year compression = $4-12B in reduced
dependency cost plus stockpile cost savings.

### Transmission Line (Illustrative)

Transmission is the most extreme permitting case. SunZia (New Mexico-Arizona) took
17 years to permit and approve. The average interstate transmission line permitting
runs 7-12 years; FERC Order 1977 (2024) has begun to improve this but the state
coordination problem remains.

For AHLP corridor transmission and grid hardening transmission:

| Phase | Without PAA | With PAA |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Route alternatives analysis | 18-24 months | 6-9 months (NEID routing data) |
| Baseline surveys | 24-36 months | 0 months |
| NEPA/FERC EIS | 36-48 months | 12-18 months |
| State/local permits | 36-60 months | 12-18 months (Compact) |
| **Total permitting** | **9-14 years** | **3-4 years** |

**Value:** A 500-mile transmission line at 2 GW capacity foregone for 6 years = roughly
$8-16B in clean energy not delivered. The PAA baseline survey for the corridor costs ~$30-50M.

## Legal Framework and Constitutional Basis

### Authority

**Enabling authority:** DED Enabling Act creates the NESP designation authority as a
statutory basis; CEQ NEPA regulations provide the categorical exclusion mechanism;
Commerce Clause grounds federal authority over facilities affecting interstate commerce
and national security.

**Deemed-approved provision:** This is the legally novel element. The strongest precedent
is NRC's deemed-approved mechanism for nuclear facility license amendments, which has
withstood judicial review. The key constitutional requirement is that the deemed-approval
preserve substantive environmental review requirements (all standard mitigation conditions
apply) — it compresses timeline, not substance.

**Compact Clause:** Interstate compacts require congressional consent under the Compact
Clause (Art. I, §10, cl. 3) if they affect federal power or alter the balance of power
among states. The Critical Infrastructure Permitting Compact requires a simple congressional
consent provision in the DED Enabling Act; this is routine for interstate compacts affecting
federal interests.

### Litigation Risk

NESP permitting acceleration will be litigated by environmental organizations and adjacent
landowners. The design minimizes litigation vulnerability by:

1. Preserving substantive environmental review (concurrent, not eliminated)
2. Maintaining public comment periods (compressed, not removed)
3. Pre-completing baseline data (litigation is most successful when agencies cut corners
   on data; pre-positioned baseline data reduces this vulnerability)
4. Limiting NESP designation to a narrow, high-CRS category (not a general NEPA waiver)
5. Maintaining judicial review of NESP designation (litigants can challenge designation,
   not bypass it)

**Historical benchmark:** FAST-41's coordination requirements have survived legal challenge.
The Fiscal Responsibility Act's NEPA reforms survived their first year without major
successful legal challenge. The PAA's deemed-approved provision is the most aggressive
element; the others are well within existing precedent.

## Cost Summary

| Program | Capital (5-year) | Annual Operating |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Pre-positioned baseline surveys (100 sites) | $500M-1B | $50-80M (monitoring) |
| ERP Regulatory Track (200 specialists) | — | $65-125M (standby + deployment) |
| NESP designation administration (DED staff) | $30-50M | $15-25M |
| State Compact administration | $20-30M | $10-15M |
| Legal defense fund (anticipated litigation) | $50-80M | $20-30M |
| **Total capital (5-year)** | **$600M-1.16B** | |
| **Total annual operating** | | **$160-275M/year** |

**Return calculation:**
The PAA's cost of $160-275M/year is measured against the portfolio-level value of
permitting compression. Across the 25 NESP-designated projects active in a typical year:
- Average compression: 5 years per project
- Average annual value of foregone production per year of delay: $500M-3B (depending on project)
- Expected value of compression: 25 projects × 5 years × $1B average = $125B in captured production

Against $160-275M/year in operating cost, the return is approximately 500:1. Even at 10%
of the theoretical value (accounting for partial realization and litigation delay), the
return is 50:1 — among the highest of any DED tool in the portfolio.

## Political Economy

### Coalition

**In favor:** Every domestic manufacturer that has tried to site a new industrial facility.
Every utility that has tried to build transmission. Every semiconductor company that has
watched a fab take 8 years from announcement to production. The business community broadly.
Governors in industrial states who want NESP investment siting preference.

**Opposition:** Environmental organizations (any NEPA acceleration is viewed as rollback).
Adjacent landowners (particularly for transmission and mining projects). Some tribal nations
(depending on NHPA Section 106 design — the ERP tribal coordination track is the mitigation).

**Framing:** This is not a rollback of environmental protection. Environmental review still
occurs — concurrently, with pre-positioned data, and with specialists who know how to
navigate it correctly. The argument is that a 9-year permitting process is not producing
better environmental outcomes than a 3-year process with higher-quality baseline data.
The SunZia transmission line waited 17 years; the environment along its route waited too.

**Legislative vehicle:** DED Enabling Act (primary); NDAA attachment (NESP designation
for defense supply chain projects); FAST-41 reauthorization (Permitting Council reforms).
The State Compact requires standalone congressional consent provision.

**Sequencing:** The Pre-Positioned Baseline Survey Program can begin via DED administrative
action before the full NESP statute is enacted — DED can fund baseline surveys at strategic
sites under existing procurement authority. This allows the program to be operational and
demonstrating value before the full statutory framework is in place.
