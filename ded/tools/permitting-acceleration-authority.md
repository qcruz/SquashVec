# DED Tool: Permitting Acceleration Authority

**Document Type:** Tool Analysis
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Category:** Cross-Scenario Infrastructure Tool
**Related Scenarios:** Semiconductor Collapse, Rare Earth Denial, Grid Cyberattack,
  Oil Restriction, Autonomous Highway Lane Program, Pandemic (manufacturing surge)
**Related Tools:** Expert Reserve Program, Strategic contracts (Type B), Sovereign
  Compute Reserve, DPA invocation

---

## The Problem This Tool Solves

Five scenarios in the DED portfolio share a common bottleneck that is not addressed by
any existing tool: the gap between when a strategic investment is committed and when it
can legally begin operation.

| Scenario | Investment Committed | Permit Timeline | Production Gap |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Semiconductor fab (advanced node) | CHIPS Act funding awarded | 4-7 years | $5-15B/year foregone output per year of delay |
| REE processing facility | DED Type B contract signed | 5-9 years | $1-3B/year in continued import dependency |
| Domestic LPT manufacturing | DoD/DED contract awarded | 3-5 years | 12-24 month grid restoration gap per damaged transformer |
| Transmission line (AHLP corridor + grid hardening) | Utility capital plan approved | 7-12 years | 2-4 GW/year foregone clean energy capacity |
| Mountain Pass → Round Top REE expansion | Mining plan filed | 5-8 years | Each additional domestic REE site reduces CRS by ~0.05 |
| Pharmaceutical API domestic manufacturing | DED Type B co-investment | 3-6 years | Continued 90%+ import dependency during pandemic scenario |

These are not gaps in funding or technical capability. They are gaps caused by permitting
processes designed for routine infrastructure — not for supply chain crisis response.
DED can commit $2B to a rare earth processing facility today and not see a gram of
separated oxide until 2033, not because the chemistry is uncertain but because permitting
will take 7-9 years to work through a system designed to process one application at a time.

**The expected annual loss from permitting delay** — not from supply disruption, but from
the inability to build the response — is estimated at $30-70B/year across the DED portfolio.

---

## Why Regulatory Mandates Are the Wrong Primary Tool

The first instinct in permitting reform is to create new regulatory authority: deemed-approved
provisions, mandatory concurrent review, federal preemption of state review. Each of these
approaches is politically contentious, legally vulnerable to challenge, and slow to produce
results — the litigation over a deemed-approved provision can last longer than the original
permitting delay it was designed to address.

More fundamentally, regulatory mandates address the wrong part of the problem. The two
actual bottlenecks are:

**1. Capital structure:** Companies and developers don't invest in the baseline survey work,
pre-application engineering, and permitting-phase legal costs because the 7-10 year uncertainty
makes the capital return unacceptable. A $150M permitting investment that takes 8 years to
resolve carries enormous option risk. If the permit is denied, the entire investment is lost.
If the project is delayed, the company's capital is tied up at a return of zero for nearly a
decade. The rational response is to delay the investment, reduce its scale, or abandon the
project — which is exactly what happens to most domestic critical capacity builds.

**2. Agency capacity:** Environmental review agencies (EPA regional offices, Army Corps
districts, Fish and Wildlife Service field offices) are chronically understaffed relative to
their mandate. Review timelines are long not primarily because of bureaucratic indifference
but because a 3-person Army Corps district reviewing 40 projects has no mechanism to
prioritize a DED-critical project without sacrificing another applicant's review. More
authority given to DED does not put more reviewers in the Army Corps district office.

Both problems have market and program solutions that are faster, cheaper, and more durable
than regulatory mandates. The Permitting Acceleration Authority is built entirely from
economic instruments and extensions of existing DED programs.

## Mechanism 1: Permitting Capital Loan Guarantee

### The Core Problem

The permitting phase of a major industrial facility typically costs $50-200M:
environmental consultants, legal teams, engineering studies, agency coordination, public
comment responses. This capital is spent before the first shovel of construction — and
is entirely lost if the permit is denied or the company cannot survive the wait. At a
7-10 year timeline, the company is also tying up capital at zero return for the entire
period, making the effective cost far higher than the nominal permitting budget.

Private lenders treat permitting-phase capital as near-equity risk: high probability of
total loss if the project fails, no collateral, long duration. The result is either no
financing (the company funds permitting from retained earnings, constraining scale) or
extremely high-cost financing (10-15% on permitting bridge loans, adding $10-20M/year
in interest cost to an already expensive process).

### The Guarantee Structure

DED provides a loan guarantee covering 80% of certified permitting-phase capital for
DED-registered critical facility projects (projects whose commodity or infrastructure
type appears on the NEID Critical Materials or Critical Infrastructure Register).

**What it guarantees:** The permitting-phase loan — the capital raised to fund
environmental consultants, legal fees, engineering, pre-application surveys, agency
coordination, and public comment. Not the construction loan; not operations. The
permitting phase only.

**Coverage:** 80% of the outstanding principal. The lender retains 20% of the risk,
maintaining their incentive to vet the project. DED covers 80% if the borrower defaults
(defined as: permit denial, company insolvency, or project abandonment after 24+ months
of active review).

**Effect on borrower cost:** The guarantee converts permitting-phase lending from
junk-equivalent risk to investment-grade. A $150M permitting loan:
- Without guarantee: 11-14% interest = $16.5-21M/year
- With 80% DED guarantee: 4.5-6% interest = $6.75-9M/year
- Annual savings: $7.5-12M/year on interest alone
- Over 7 years: $52-84M in total interest savings on a single project

This is not a subsidy — it is risk repricing. DED is providing the same function as a
government-backed mortgage guarantee: the underlying loan is still private, still market-
rate, still lender-underwritten. The guarantee removes the tail risk that makes private
lenders demand excessive premiums.

**DED fiscal exposure:** DED charges a guarantee fee of 0.75-1.25% annually on the
guaranteed amount ($150M × 80% = $120M guaranteed; fee = $900k-1.5M/year per project).
Across 25 active projects, the fee income is $22-37M/year. If 5% of projects fail (a
conservative default assumption for DED-certified critical facilities with Type B co-
investment), DED pays 80% of failed principal. At $100M average failed loan × 80% × 5% =
$4M/year net expected loss. DED fee income ($22-37M) substantially exceeds expected
guarantee calls — the program is self-financing at scale.

**Legislative vehicle:** Loan guarantee authority for critical industrial facilities
is modeled on DOE's Title XVII (Section 1703/1705) loan guarantees for innovative
energy technology. DED Enabling Act adds critical supply chain facilities to the
eligible category. This is not novel authority — it extends an existing, well-functioning
mechanism to a new asset class.

## Mechanism 2: Baseline Survey Tax Credit

### The Chicken-and-Egg Problem

The longest phase of a permitting timeline is often baseline data collection: the
ecological surveys (two full years of breeding bird and bat monitoring), wetlands
delineation, cultural resource surveys, air and water quality baseline monitoring.
This phase takes 2-4 years and must be completed before an Environmental Impact
Statement can be written. Developers don't commission baseline surveys until they
are committed to a specific site. They can't commit to a site until they know the
regulatory risk. They don't know the regulatory risk until the baseline surveys are done.

The result: every project starts from zero on baseline data, adding 2-4 years to the
front of every permitting timeline, for every project, repeatedly, even on sites that
have been surveyed before and subsequently not developed.

### The Credit Structure

DED establishes a **Critical Site Baseline Survey Tax Credit**: a 150% deduction (or,
for entities without sufficient tax liability, a refundable credit equivalent) on
certified baseline environmental survey costs at sites registered in the NEID Strategic
Site Register.

**What qualifies:**
- The site must be on the NEID Strategic Site Register (sites NEID analysis identifies
  as suitable for DED-critical facility types based on geology, infrastructure access,
  zoning, and proximity to relevant supply chains)
- The survey must meet DED baseline data standards (ecological, wetlands, cultural,
  air/water) and be certified by a DED-approved firm
- The data must be deposited in the DED Site Register — publicly available to any
  future applicant at that site

**The behavioral math:**
A certified baseline survey at a strategic site costs approximately $8-15M. With a
150% deduction, the after-tax cost to a company in the 21% corporate tax bracket is:
$12M survey × 150% deduction × 21% tax rate = $3.78M in tax savings. Net cost: $12M -
$3.78M = $8.22M. But the company now has baseline data that:
(a) reduces their future permitting timeline by 2-4 years at the site if they build; or
(b) is in the public register, meaning any competitor who builds there uses data the
first company generated — converting private survey cost into public infrastructure.

The stronger version: a **refundable credit at 40%** of survey costs. The $12M survey
generates a $4.8M cash refund from the IRS. Net cost: $7.2M. For companies without
large tax liability (smaller domestic producers who are the primary target for DED-
registered critical facilities), the refundable credit matters more than the deduction.

**DED's cost:**
At a 40% refundable credit, 100 surveys at $10M average = $400M in credits over 5 years.
Against the value of 2-4 years of permitting compression per site: even at $500M/year
in foregone production per site, compressing the timeline by 2 years = $1B captured.
The credit costs $4M to capture $1B. Cost ratio: 1:250.

**DED's secondary benefit:**
The public data deposit requirement means DED builds the Strategic Site Register library
without direct expenditure. Every credit-eligible survey adds to the public baseline
data pool. Sites that were privately surveyed by one company and never developed now
have complete environmental baselines available for the next applicant. The government
gets permanent infrastructure from a private investment.

## Mechanism 3: Critical Infrastructure Investment Tax Credit

### Why the Permitting Wait Kills Projects

Even companies willing to accept the permitting timeline are penalized by the capital
structure: they invest $150-500M in permitting and pre-construction engineering before
breaking ground, then spend $5-15B in construction, and don't see revenue for 10+ years
from initial commitment. The net present value of most domestic critical facilities is
negative under standard discount rates even when the facility would be highly profitable —
because the cash flows start too late and cost of capital is too high during the wait.

The Inflation Reduction Act's Investment Tax Credit for clean energy solved exactly this
problem for solar, wind, and battery manufacturing: by making the credit claimable on
construction expenditure (not production output), it front-loads the financial return
and makes long-timeline projects viable under normal discount rates.

### The Credit Structure

DED proposes a **Critical Domestic Capacity Investment Tax Credit (CDCITC)** modeled
directly on the IRA Section 48C advanced manufacturing credit:

**Credit rate:** 30% base; 40% if the facility meets prevailing wage and apprenticeship
requirements (aligning with DED's Workforce Development Floor). For facilities with DED
Type B co-investment, the rate increases to 35% base / 45% with labor standards.

**Eligible expenditures:** Capital investment in:
- Semiconductor fabrication and packaging facilities (advanced and mature node)
- Critical mineral processing and separation facilities (REE, cobalt, lithium, gallium,
  germanium, graphite, tungsten, magnesium)
- Domestic pharmaceutical API manufacturing facilities
- Large Power Transformer manufacturing facilities
- Battery cell manufacturing (domestic chemistry: LFP, sodium-ion, solid-state)
- V2I infrastructure (as part of AHLP corridor development)

**Claim timing:** Credit is claimable annually on eligible capital expenditure as
incurred — not on facility completion or production. A company building a $5B semiconductor
fab over 4 years claims the credit in each construction year: $1.25B/year in capex × 35%
= $437.5M/year in credit. This front-loads approximately $1.75B in credits during the
construction period before the facility produces a chip.

**Effect:** Converts a project that looks like a 12-year wait for return into a project
with substantial positive cash flows during construction. This is the mechanism that makes
private capital willing to commit to domestic critical facilities without government grants.

**Revenue cost:** $5-8B/year at full deployment across all eligible facility types.
Funded by the tariff revenue share allocated to the American Sovereign Fund with a
legislative carve-out: the first $8B/year of tariff revenue share goes to CDCITC before
ASF. This is consistent with ASF's design (tariff revenue is the source) while prioritizing
the capacity investment that justifies the tariff policy.

**Legislative vehicle:** Section 48C amendment (reconciliation-eligible as a revenue
measure). Stackable with existing IRA credits where applicable.

## Mechanism 4: DED Agency Capacity Grants

### Why Agency Delay Is a Capacity Problem, Not an Authority Problem

Environmental review agencies are not slow because they lack authority to be fast. They
are slow because a 3-person Army Corps district office reviewing 40 projects simultaneously
cannot physically process a NESP-priority project faster without deprioritizing someone
else's review. Adding new statutory authority to DED does not add reviewers to the Army
Corps district. It adds confrontation with an agency that now has to legally defend its
timeline.

The efficient solution is to fund the additional reviewer capacity directly.

### The Grant Mechanism

DED establishes a **Critical Review Capacity Grant Program**: annual grants to federal
and state environmental review agencies specifically for additional staffing, overtime,
and technical training capacity on DED-registered critical facility reviews.

**Federal agency grants:**
- EPA regional offices: $2-4M/year per region to fund 5-8 additional review staff or
  equivalent overtime capacity dedicated to NESP-category facilities
- Army Corps districts: $1.5-3M/year per district for Section 404 review capacity
  on critical facility applications
- Fish and Wildlife Service field offices: $1-2M/year per region for Section 7 biological
  opinion capacity on NESP projects
- State Historic Preservation Offices (federal transfer): $500k-1M/year per state for
  Section 106 consultation capacity

**Conditions:**
- Grant recipients report quarterly on review timelines for DED-registered projects vs.
  non-registered projects; DED publishes the data (creates accountability without mandate)
- Agency must assign a named project coordinator for each DED-registered critical facility;
  the coordinator is the DED Agency Reviewer Grant recipient's designated contact
- Funds must be used for additional capacity on DED-registered projects, not for
  general agency operations

**Why agencies participate willingly:**
The grants solve a real agency problem: they are chronically underfunded relative to
their statutory mandate. A $3M annual grant to an EPA regional office that is perpetually
behind on its caseload is a genuine benefit. The agency gets capacity; DED gets expedited
review; no confrontational authority relationship is required.

**State grants:**
DED extends the same grant structure to state environmental agencies, conditioned on
compact participation (see Mechanism 6). States that join the compact receive $1-5M/year
in state review capacity grants for DED-registered projects.

**Total cost:** $50-80M/year in agency capacity grants across all participating federal
and state agencies. Against the value of timeline compression, this is the cheapest
mechanism in the portfolio — approximately $2-3M per year of permitting delay eliminated
per project.

## Mechanism 5: CRHP and ERP as Agency Reviewer Pipeline

### The Long-Run Capacity Problem

The Agency Capacity Grants solve the immediate bottleneck by funding overtime and
temporary hires. The structural problem is that the reviewer pipeline is thin: there
are not enough trained NEPA specialists, wetlands delineators, ESA Section 7 biologists,
and tribal consultation coordinators to staff a meaningfully expanded review capacity,
even with additional funding.

DED's existing programs address this directly without creating new bureaucracy.

### CRHP Regulatory Track

The Civilian Reserve and Health Program adds a **Regulatory Track**: enrollment open to
individuals with backgrounds in environmental review, NEPA compliance, natural resource
law, cultural resource management, and state permitting.

**What CRHP Regulatory Track members do:**
During their CRHP enrollment period, their primary DED assignment is supporting agency
review of DED-registered critical facility projects. They work embedded within EPA,
Army Corps, FWS, SHPO, and state agency offices as supplemental reviewers — not as
DED enforcers, but as additional staff capacity funded by DED.

This is the DED model applied to permitting: CRHP provides the labor; agencies provide
the mission and expertise structure; the project gets a faster review without confrontation.

**For current agency employees:**
Active federal and state environmental reviewers can enroll in CRHP on a part-time basis.
Their CRHP assignment is their existing job — they continue reviewing projects for their
agency, but a portion of their workload is specifically allocated to DED-registered
projects, and DED supplements their compensation for that portion (15-25% pay supplement
for CRHP-designated NESP review work). This is the overtime equivalent without requiring
agencies to formally run overtime programs.

**Behavioral effect:** An Army Corps district reviewer who earns $95k/year base can
earn an additional $14-24k/year by taking CRHP enrollment and dedicating a portion of
their review time to DED-registered projects. They don't change employers. They don't
change agencies. They get paid more to do the same work faster on the projects DED cares
about. This is the market incentive version of the concurrent review mandate — same
behavioral outcome, no confrontation.

**For early-career professionals:**
ERP recruits environmental scientists, lawyers, and planners into a 2-year training
rotation: Year 1 embedded in a federal or state review agency (learning NEPA, Section 404,
Section 7, or equivalent state processes); Year 2 supporting DED-registered facility
reviews as a junior reviewer under agency supervision. After the rotation, these individuals
are either hired by agencies (expanding the long-run pipeline) or enrolled in the ERP
reserve as deployable regulatory specialists.

**Pipeline math:** 200 ERP recruits per year × 2-year program = 400 reviewers in training
at any given time. After 5 years, the program has produced 1,000 trained reviewers who
have returned to agencies, entered private practice, or are in ERP reserve. This is a
10-year solution to the structural capacity gap, not just a temporary fix.

**Cost:**
- CRHP Regulatory Track compensation supplements: $15-25M/year
- ERP training program: $30-50M/year (stipends, training coordination, agency placement)
- Total: $45-75M/year

## Mechanism 6: State Compact (Simplified)

The State Permitting Compact is retained but simplified: its sole enforcement mechanism
is economic preference, not federal authority.

**The compact commits member states to:**
- Accepting DED pre-positioned baseline survey data in lieu of new state data collection
- Assigning a single state agency as coordinator for DED-registered projects (one call,
  not twelve)
- Conducting state review concurrently with federal NEPA (not after)
- Targeting 12 months for state environmental review completion on DED-registered projects

**What states receive in return:**
- Priority siting for DED Type B co-investments (compact-member states are preferred
  when multiple sites are viable)
- Full access to DED Baseline Survey Tax Credit and CDCITC stacking for in-state projects
- DED Agency Capacity Grants for state review agencies
- CRHP Regulatory Track supplemental compensation for state reviewers

**What states outside the compact receive:**
- Full Baseline Survey Tax Credit and CDCITC still available (these are federal programs)
- No Type B siting preference
- No state agency capacity grants

The compact is a pure economic opt-in. No federal preemption. No mandate. States that
want DED investment flows join; states that don't remain eligible for the federal tax
programs but receive no additional support.

## Mechanism Comparison: Why This Set Is More Efficient Than Regulatory Mandates

| Mechanism | Speed to implement | Political risk | Litigation risk | Per-dollar efficiency |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Permitting Capital Loan Guarantee | 12-18 months (existing DOE authority model) | Low | Very low | Very high (self-financing) |
| Baseline Survey Tax Credit | 18-24 months (reconciliation) | Low | Very low | Very high (~1:250 cost ratio) |
| Critical Infrastructure ITC | 24-36 months (reconciliation) | Medium (revenue cost) | Very low | High |
| Agency Capacity Grants | 6-12 months (existing transfer authority) | Very low | None | High |
| CRHP Regulatory Track | 12-18 months (extends existing CRHP) | Low | None | High |
| ERP Training Pipeline | 12-24 months (extends existing ERP) | Low | None | High (10-year compounding) |
| State Compact | 24-36 months for first states | Low | None | Medium-high |
| **Deemed-approved provision** | **36-60 months to survive challenge** | **Very high** | **Very high** | **Low** |
| **Federal preemption** | **24-36 months + litigation** | **Very high** | **Very high** | **Low** |

The economic mechanisms outperform regulatory mandates on every dimension except theoretical
maximum authority — and maximum authority that spends 10 years in court produces no
permitting acceleration at all.

The bottom line: the Agency Capacity Grants and CRHP Regulatory Track are the fastest to
implement, lowest risk, and solve the actual bottleneck (reviewer shortage). The loan
guarantee and tax credits are the highest-leverage long-run tools. The regulatory mandates
in the prior version of this document were the slowest, highest-risk, lowest-efficiency
mechanisms in the set.

## Consolidated Cost and Timeline

### What the Full Mechanism Set Costs

| Mechanism | Annual Operating | Capital / One-Time |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Loan guarantee program (fee income nets positive) | Net +$18-33M | $20-30M setup |
| Baseline Survey Tax Credit | $80-120M/year in credits | — |
| Critical Infrastructure ITC | $5-8B/year in credits | — |
| Agency Capacity Grants | $50-80M/year | — |
| CRHP Regulatory Track | $15-25M/year | — |
| ERP Training Pipeline | $30-50M/year | — |
| State Compact administration | $10-15M/year | $20-30M setup |
| **Total non-ITC cost** | **~$185-290M/year** | **$40-60M** |
| **ITC (funded from tariff revenue share)** | **$5-8B/year** | **—** |

The ITC is the dominant cost. It is funded by the same tariff revenue that funds the
American Sovereign Fund — specifically, the first $5-8B of annual tariff revenue share
goes to CDCITC before ASF contributions. This is a first-dollar priority, not a
discretionary appropriation.

The non-ITC mechanisms total $185-290M/year — roughly half the cost of the original
version's regulatory apparatus, faster to implement, and without the litigation risk.

### Timeline Compression (Revised Estimates)

Incorporating the market mechanisms rather than the regulatory mandates:

| Facility Type | Without PAA | With PAA (market mechanisms) |
|-------------|------------|------------------------------|
| Semiconductor fab | 7-12 years total | 4-6 years total |
| REE processing | 6-9 years | 3-4 years |
| Transmission line | 9-14 years | 4-6 years |
| Pharmaceutical API | 3-6 years | 1.5-2.5 years |
| LPT manufacturing | 3-5 years | 1.5-3 years |

The compression is somewhat less aggressive than the regulatory-mandate version (which
projected 2-3 years for semiconductor fabs). The market mechanisms achieve 4-6 years —
still a 40-50% reduction in timeline, delivered without the political and legal costs that
make the regulatory approach unreliable.

## Implementation Sequence

**Year 1 (executive action and existing authority):**
- DED Agency Capacity Grants: launch immediately under existing federal transfer authority;
  no legislation required; first grants flowing to EPA/Army Corps/FWS within 6 months
- CRHP Regulatory Track: add regulatory specialties to CRHP enrollment; launch compensation
  supplement for existing agency reviewers enrolled in CRHP; 12-month ramp to full enrollment
- NEID Strategic Site Register: begin registering sites from existing NEID analysis;
  no legislation required

**Year 1-2 (reconciliation and DED Enabling Act):**
- Baseline Survey Tax Credit: reconciliation vehicle; 18-month target for enactment
- Permitting Capital Loan Guarantee: DED Enabling Act provision; modeled on DOE §1703
- State Compact: DED Enabling Act compact consent provision; begin state compact negotiations

**Year 2-3 (reconciliation, second vehicle):**
- Critical Infrastructure ITC: second reconciliation bill or CHIPS Act extension vehicle;
  highest-revenue-cost item so requires dedicated legislative window

**Year 2-4 (pipeline):**
- ERP Training Pipeline: first 2-year cohort completes by Year 4; 200 trained reviewers
  in the pipeline; long-run capacity improvement begins to show in agency backlogs
