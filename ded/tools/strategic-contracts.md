# DED Tool Analysis: Strategic Contracts and Directed Subsidies

**Category:** Emergency Procurement / Financial Instruments
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Relevant Scenarios:** All scenarios — cross-cutting lever
**DED Tool Rating:** Tier S — Highest-priority tool; foundational to DED operational capacity

---

## 1. Mechanism

A DED Strategic Contract is a government agreement awarding funds, tax benefits, guaranteed
purchase commitments, or in-kind resources to a private business or individual in exchange
for maintaining a specific capability, readiness level, or developmental direction that
serves a defined national economic security objective.

This is not a grant. It is not welfare for companies. It is a **service contract**: the
recipient is paid to keep a capability alive, develop a capability the nation needs, or
orient their operations in a direction that benefits national security — on terms the
government specifies, with performance benchmarks the government audits.

The precedent is extensive:
- **Defense contracting** has sustained shipyards, aerospace manufacturers, and munitions
  producers for 80 years by ensuring demand for capabilities the military needs but the
  free market would not sustain at required scale
- **The airline industry** was sustained during COVID with $54B in payroll support
  specifically to preserve operational capacity the nation would need when travel resumed
- **The semiconductor industry** received $52B under the CHIPS Act to redirect private
  capital toward domestic fabrication the market alone would not build
- **Nuclear power plants** have received state and federal production tax credits to keep
  them operational despite uneconomic market conditions — because their closure would be
  permanent and their capacity irreplaceable
- **The OpenAI/Microsoft datacenter buildout** received federal facilitation (permitting,
  grid priority, loan guarantees) to accelerate AI infrastructure the government assessed
  as strategically necessary

The DED formalizes and generalizes this practice. Instead of ad hoc support driven by
crisis or lobbying, strategic contracts are issued against a published scenario library
with explicit national security justification, competitive solicitation, and binding
congressional oversight.

## 2. Contract Types

### Type A — Readiness Maintenance Contract
**Purpose:** Pay a company to maintain a capability it would otherwise reduce or eliminate
because market conditions do not support it.

**Structure:** Annual payment in exchange for maintaining minimum operational capacity,
staffing levels, and restart-readiness in a defined capability area.

**Examples:**
- Paying a meatpacking facility to maintain a 150% staffing roster (trained standby workers)
  so surge capacity exists in a pandemic scenario
- Subsidizing a nuclear plant's operating costs to prevent retirement
- Paying a domestic rare earth processing facility to remain operational at 30% capacity
  rather than shut down when market prices favor Chinese imports
- Maintaining a domestic shipyard's skilled workforce during peacetime troughs

**Performance benchmarks:** Quarterly readiness certifications; DoD/DED inspection rights;
minimum workforce and equipment thresholds contractually defined.

---

### Type B — Developmental Direction Contract
**Purpose:** Pay a company to develop in a direction that benefits national security but
that the company would not otherwise prioritize on commercial grounds alone.

**Structure:** Milestone-based payments tied to specific development achievements;
similar to DARPA Other Transaction Authority (OTA) contracts.

**Examples:**
- Funding a seed company to develop drought-resistant crop strains that will be licensed
  nationally (OPP-01 from value-discovery.md)
- Contracting a pharmaceutical manufacturer to maintain API production for 20 life-critical
  drugs at a domestic facility despite uneconomic production costs vs. Chinese imports
- Funding a steel mill to install grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES) production capacity
  the market does not currently demand but the grid buildout will require
- Paying a battery manufacturer to develop long-duration storage chemistry that is
  strategically important but commercially premature

**IP provisions:** Government retains a royalty-free license to any IP developed under
Type B contracts. The contractor retains commercial rights; the government retains the
right to produce or license the technology in a declared national emergency.

---

### Type C — Strategic Reserve Contract
**Purpose:** Pay a company to maintain inventory, storage, or surge capacity that functions
as a national strategic reserve without being government-owned.

**Structure:** Annual retainer for maintaining a defined stockpile or capacity level;
government has first-call rights in a declared emergency at pre-agreed pricing.

**Examples:**
- Grain elevator operators maintaining 90-day strategic reserve volumes
- Pharmaceutical distributors holding 180-day API stockpiles for life-critical drugs
- Industrial lubricant manufacturers holding strategic reserves for Tier 1 industrial facilities
- Cold storage operators in drought-vulnerable regions maintaining emergency capacity

**Advantage over government-owned reserves:** Private operators manage the inventory
efficiently (rotated, maintained, insured) without the overhead and political exposure
of government warehousing. The government pays a retainer; the operator runs the facility.

---

### Type D — Individual Expert Reserve Contract
**Purpose:** Pay a high-value individual — regardless of their civilian employment status —
a salary supplement in exchange for reserve status, drilling obligations, and emergency
mobilization availability.

**See:** `expert-reserve-program.md` for full treatment of this contract type.

## 3. Congressional Oversight Framework

This is the mechanism that separates DED strategic contracting from pork-barrel spending.
Every provision below is designed to make the political economy of contracting transparent,
competitive, and publicly accountable.

### The Joint Committee on Economic Defense (JCED)

A new joint congressional committee with jurisdiction over all DED contracts above threshold:

**Composition:** 6 members each from Senate and House; drawn from Armed Services, Commerce,
Agriculture, Energy, and Finance committees; bipartisan co-chairs; staggered 4-year terms
(insulates from election-cycle pressure).

**Jurisdiction:**
- All Type A, B, and C contracts above $50M
- All Type D (individual) contracts above $500K/year
- Any contract with a company that has received >$500M in DED contracts in the prior 5 years
- Any contract in a sector where the recipient has no prior competitive solicitation

**Non-jurisdiction (DED Secretary discretion):**
- Contracts under $50M (Type A/B/C) — Secretary authority; quarterly report to JCED
- Emergency contracts under declared DED emergency — Secretary authority; 30-day retroactive
  JCED review

---

### Approval Process

**Step 1 — DED Office Origination**
The relevant DED office (Scenario Analysis, Strategic Assets, or Research Coordination)
originates a contract proposal including:
- Scenario linkage: which scenario(s) does this contract address, at which phase?
- Capability gap: what does the nation lack without this contract?
- Competitive analysis: who else could provide this capability?
- Cost-benefit summary: estimated contract cost vs. expected national security value
- Performance benchmarks: measurable deliverables and audit schedule
- IP terms: government license provisions for any developed technology
- Duration and exit: contract term, renewal conditions, and termination provisions

**Step 2 — DED Secretary Review**
Secretary reviews for consistency with the current scenario library and strategic asset
registry. Secretary certifies that the contract addresses a documented gap — not a
lobbied preference.

**Step 3 — Open Solicitation (Type B and C)**
For developmental and reserve contracts, DED publishes a public solicitation with the
national security requirement defined but the solution open to competition. Minimum 45-day
solicitation window. All qualified bidders evaluated on technical merit, cost, and
national security value — not relationships.

**Step 4 — JCED Submission**
DED submits the contract package to JCED with:
- Full origination documentation
- Solicitation results and selection rationale
- Independent economic analysis (third-party, not DED staff)
- Conflict of interest certification from all DED staff involved in selection

**Step 5 — JCED Review (30-day standard; 72-hour emergency)**
JCED may:
- Approve as submitted
- Approve with conditions
- Request additional information (extends timeline by 15 days)
- Reject with written findings (DED may revise and resubmit; same process)

Approval requires majority vote of JCED. For contracts above $500M, full floor vote
of both chambers required.

**Step 6 — Public Disclosure**
All approved contracts are published in a public DED Contract Registry within 10 days
of JCED approval:
- Recipient name
- Contract type and value
- Scenario linkage
- Performance benchmarks
- IP terms (non-classified portions)
- Voting record of JCED members

### Anti-Corruption Provisions

**Cooling-off period:** No company that has employed a DED official in the prior 3 years
may receive a DED contract. No DED official may join a company that held a DED contract
during their tenure for 3 years after departure.

**Lobbying prohibition:** Any company or individual that has registered lobbying contacts
with DED or JCED members in the prior 2 years is ineligible for competitive solicitation.
They may respond to open solicitations but cannot initiate contract discussions.

**Geographic distribution requirement:** No single congressional district may receive
more than 8% of annual DED contract value. No single state may receive more than 20%.
DED Secretary must certify geographic distribution in annual report to Congress.

**Competition floor:** No sole-source contracts above $10M without JCED approval and
published national security justification. Sole-source above $100M requires full
floor vote.

**Clawback authority:** DED contracts include clawback provisions for:
- Failure to meet performance benchmarks
- Evidence of fraudulent certification
- Recipient company being acquired by a foreign adversary entity
- Misuse of contract funds

**GAO audit:** All contracts above $25M are subject to mandatory GAO audit at contract
midpoint and completion. GAO findings are public.

## 4. Historical Precedents

### DARPA Other Transaction Authority (OTA)
DARPA's OTA contracts are the closest existing model. OTAs allow DoD to contract with
non-traditional defense companies (startups, universities, commercial firms) without the
full FAR procurement burden. DED strategic contracts borrow this flexibility while adding
the JCED oversight layer that OTAs currently lack for large contracts.

### Tennessee Valley Authority (1933)
The TVA is the most direct historical precedent for government-directed economic development
investment. FDR created a federal corporation with authority to build dams, generate power,
and develop the regional economy of the Tennessee Valley — directing private and public
resources toward a defined economic security objective. The DED's contracting authority
is the TVA model applied nationally and across sectors.

### Defense Production Act Contracts (1950–Present)
The DPA gives the President authority to direct private companies to prioritize government
orders and accept contracts for national defense. DED strategic contracts extend this model
to economic security, not just military production, and add the competitive solicitation
and congressional oversight the DPA lacks.

### CHIPS and Science Act (2022)
$52B in semiconductor manufacturing subsidies, structured as a combination of grants
(Type B equivalent) and investment tax credits. The CHIPS Act proved that large-scale
strategic subsidy programs can be designed and administered. DED formalizes the CHIPS
model as standing permanent authority rather than one-time legislation for each sector.

## 6. Risks and Failure Modes

### Regulatory Capture
**Risk:** Over time, the companies receiving DED contracts develop relationships with
DED staff and JCED members that effectively direct contracting toward incumbents.

**Mitigation:** Rotating staff assignments; mandatory competitive solicitation; cooling-off
periods; GAO audit; public contract registry. The oversight architecture is specifically
designed for this threat.

### Misaligned Incentives (Readiness Theater)
**Risk:** Type A recipients certify readiness on paper without actually maintaining
genuine capability.

**Mitigation:** DED inspection rights with unannounced spot inspections; third-party
readiness certification; clawback for false certification; performance benchmarks
verified against actual production tests, not self-reported.

### Mission Creep
**Risk:** DED contracting expands beyond genuine national security need into general
industrial policy.

**Mitigation:** Every contract must link to a specific scenario in the published scenario
library. If the scenario doesn't exist, the contract cannot be originated. New scenarios
require the full scenario development process — they cannot be created to justify a
pre-selected contract.
