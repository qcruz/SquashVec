## The Problem: The Disappearing First Rung

The entry-level job is not merely a paycheck. It is the mechanism through which individuals
acquire the professional credentials, networks, and demonstrated competencies that determine
their trajectory across a working life. It is the first rung of the economic mobility ladder —
and it is disappearing.

Three converging forces are removing it simultaneously:

**1. AI displacement of routine entry-level work.** The occupations most exposed to AI
substitution — data entry, customer service, basic analysis, coding assistance, document
review — are disproportionately occupied by workers aged 20–35 entering the workforce.
As AI handles these tasks at a fraction of the cost, employers are not replacing the people;
they are eliminating the positions. The first rung doesn't disappear with a dramatic layoff
announcement. It disappears quietly, with each hiring cycle where the job posting simply
isn't listed.

**2. The credentialing spiral.** As entry-level positions have grown scarce, employers have
raised credential requirements for the positions that remain — requiring degrees for work that
previously required a diploma, requiring experience for jobs that previously developed it.
Workers who cannot get the first job cannot get the credential; without the credential they
cannot get the job. The spiral is self-reinforcing.

**3. The unpaid internship system.** Where entry-level paid positions have disappeared, they
have been partially replaced by unpaid internships accessible only to students who can afford
not to be paid — disproportionately from high-income families, in high-cost-of-living metros.
The National Association of Colleges and Employers (NACE) has documented that paid internship
experience translates directly to higher starting salaries and employment rates; unpaid
experience shows minimal wage benefit. The unpaid internship system does not develop workers —
it selects for financial privilege and reproduces inequality.

The result: an economy where a growing share of the workforce has no viable path from
education to employment except through personal networks and financial cushion — both of
which are distributed by the same family and class structures that workforce development is
supposed to counteract.

This is both a social failure and an economic security failure. An economy where entry-level
development is rationed by family wealth is an economy that wastes the productive potential
of the majority of its workforce — and that produces the political instability that
accompanies perceived inequality and closed mobility.

## Historical Precedents

### I. The New Deal Youth Employment Programs — Scale and ROI

The most ambitious U.S. government workforce development programs remain the New Deal
programs of the 1930s:

**Civilian Conservation Corps (1933–1942):**
In nine years, the CCC enrolled **3 million young men** — representing one-third of all
men aged 17–24 in the United States. Participants received housing, food, vocational
training, literacy education, and $30/month (equivalent to ~$650/month today), of which
$25 was sent directly to their families. Work product included 800 state parks, 13,000
miles of trails, 125,000 miles of roads, and 3 billion trees planted — infrastructure
that generates economic value today.

A 2024 study in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics* (Aizer et al.) using newly linked
administrative records found that CCC participants experienced **significantly higher
lifetime earnings** compared to comparable non-participants — the developmental benefit
persisted across an entire working life, not merely during the program period.

**National Youth Administration (1935–1943):**
The NYA employed **4.5 million young people** in part-time work while they remained in
school, providing skills training and work experience that kept youth in education and
attached to the labor force simultaneously. The NYA's model — work experience embedded in
education, not as an alternative to it — directly prefigures the work-study structure the
WDF proposes.

**The GI Bill (1944–present):**
The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944 funded the education and training of **8 million
veterans** after World War II. A Congressional analysis estimated that **for every $1 spent,
$7 returned to the national economy**. The program produced 450,000 engineers, 238,000
teachers, 91,000 scientists, 67,000 doctors, and 22,000 dentists — the professional
workforce that built the postwar American middle class and sustained U.S. economic
dominance for three decades.

The 7:1 ROI is the benchmark for workforce development investment. Every program in this
proposal is designed with that return ratio as the target.

### II. Germany: The Dual Apprenticeship System

Germany's dual system is the global reference model for structured entry-level workforce
development. Key statistics:

- **400,000 companies** offer apprenticeship positions, covering ~330 recognized occupational
  categories
- **~1.5 million apprentices** are in active training at any time
- **82% of apprentices** train at SMEs — not large corporations. The system works at small
  employer scale.
- **~50% of German school leavers** enter the dual system
- **90% completion rate** — among the highest of any structured training system
- **Youth unemployment: 7.8%** — lowest in the EU, roughly half the rate in countries
  without equivalent structures
- **Format:** 70% time at employer; 30% at vocational school (Berufsschule). Three years
  to completion; leads directly to employment in ~85% of cases.

**The key mechanism:** The German system distributes the cost of entry-level development
between employers (who pay apprentice wages and training costs), the state (which funds
the school component), and the apprentice (who accepts below-market wages during training).
All three parties have skin in the game; none bears the full cost alone.

**The critical design lesson from Germany:** The system works because it is **embedded in
employer operations as a normal function**, not treated as a charitable activity or
compliance burden. German companies recruit apprentices as part of their standard hiring
cycle. The apprentice is not a separate category — they are the entry point for the
organization's workforce pipeline.

### III. The UK Apprenticeship Levy — What Works and What Fails

The UK introduced a mandatory employer training levy in April 2017: **0.5% of annual
payroll** for all employers with a payroll exceeding £3M. Revenue is held in individual
employer digital accounts and can only be spent on approved apprenticeship training.
Unspent funds expire after 24 months.

**What worked:**
- Raised ~£2.7B annually — a substantial, stable revenue stream for workforce development
- Increased employer engagement with apprenticeship programs, particularly in sectors
  (healthcare, education) previously underrepresented
- U.S. registered apprenticeships grew independently to 680,000 active participants in
  FY2024 (114% increase since 2014), demonstrating that employer interest in structured
  training exists when conditions are right

**What failed — and why the WDF is designed differently:**
- Apprenticeship starts fell dramatically in the first year (from 117,800 to 48,000 in
  the same quarter year-over-year) — employers were confused, not prepared, and the
  system was complex
- **56% of UK apprentices were already employed at the company before starting** —
  employers used the levy to fund training for existing staff, not to create new entry
  points for young workers. The levy became a manager MBA fund, not a youth development
  pipeline.
- The levy was inflexible: funds could only be spent on formally certified apprenticeships,
  excluding many valid forms of structured work-based training
- Small employers — who provide 82% of Germany's apprenticeships — largely could not
  access the UK system effectively

**WDF design response to UK failures:**
1. The WDF requires positions **open only to individuals not previously employed at the
   company** — explicitly preventing the "rebadging existing staff" failure mode
2. The Industry Pool alternative allows levy contribution without formal apprenticeship
   administration — reducing the compliance burden for small and specialized employers
3. Phased implementation with 18 months of preparation before enforcement — giving
   employers time to design real programs

### IV. France: Universal Employer Training Obligation

France imposes the *taxe d'apprentissage* on **all employers regardless of size**: 0.68%
of gross annual wages. Of this, 0.59% funds apprenticeship programs directly; 0.09% supports
vocational education institutions. Additional contributions apply for large employers who
fall below minimum apprentice employment ratios.

**The key design insight:** France's approach treats employer contribution to workforce
development as a **standard operating cost of employment**, not a special program for
large employers. This eliminates the size-based cliff that creates inequity in other systems.

The WDF adapts this by treating the internship floor (not a levy) as the universal
obligation — achievable even by small qualifying employers at low cost — while reserving
the voluntary levy mechanism for companies that want to contribute beyond the floor.

---

### V. Singapore SkillsFuture — Continuous Development at National Scale

Singapore launched SkillsFuture in 2015 with S$1B initial government investment. Every
Singaporean adult receives a SkillsFuture Credit for approved training; employers receive
abatements for documented training hours; the government maintains a national skills
registry and career matching infrastructure.

**2024 results:**
- **555,000 training enrollments** — a record; boosted by a S$4,000 credit for citizens 40+
- More than **1 in 2 eligible Singaporeans** aged 30–75 have used their SkillsFuture Credit
- **24,000 companies** sent 241,000 employees for SSG-backed training
- **54% of career transition participants** found new employment within 6 months
- 69% reported training improved their job performance; 64% linked it to career advancement
- AI and cybersecurity training surged from 34,000 to 96,000 SSG-supported learners

**The Singapore lesson:** Government investment in training infrastructure — skills registries,
approved course frameworks, career matching platforms — dramatically increases voluntary employer
and worker participation. The WDF's DED Credential Infrastructure (Section 5 below) is modeled
on Singapore's national skills platform.

### VI. Current U.S. Landscape — The Gap

- **Registered Apprenticeships (FY2024):** 680,000 active participants; entry wage $18/hr
  rising to $32/hr on completion — but still dominated by construction trades; most
  sectors are at <5% of their potential apprenticeship employment
- **No federal mandatory paid internship requirement exists.** The DOL FLSA framework
  permits unpaid internships under a "primary beneficiary" test; enforcement is inconsistent
- **NACE advocacy:** The National Association of Colleges and Employers has formally called
  for federal legislation requiring internships to be paid, citing the documented wage
  premium for paid vs. unpaid experience
- **Federal government itself:** The Department of State was required by legislation to
  transition all unpaid internships to paid programs — acknowledging the problem at the
  institutional level while lacking a private sector equivalent

The gap is clear: every comparable economy has some form of mandatory or strongly incentivized
employer workforce development obligation. The United States has a patchwork of voluntary
programs, tax credits, and grant-funded initiatives that together serve a fraction of the
population that structured development would reach.

## The Workforce Development Floor (WDF) Program

### Mission

Every large employer operating in the United States economy benefits from the educated,
credentialed, network-connected workforce that public education, prior generations of
employers, and government programs produced. The WDF establishes that employers above a
defined threshold bear a minimum responsibility for maintaining the pipeline that delivers
new workers into that workforce — not as charity, but as a condition of operating at scale
in the U.S. economy.

**The floor is not the ceiling.** Employers that exceed it strengthen their own talent
pipeline and benefit from the DED employer partnership programs. The floor is the minimum
below which responsible operation at scale is not acceptable.

---

### Core Components

#### Component 1: The Employer Development Obligation (EDO)

**Requirement:** Any private sector employer with ≥100 FTE employees must maintain a
minimum of **1 qualifying paid development position per 100 FTE** at all times.

**Qualifying position standards:**
- Minimum $17/hr (2026 reference; CPI-indexed annually)
- Minimum 10 weeks × 20+ hours/week per rotation
- Documented learning objectives and designated supervisor
- Open only to individuals with no prior employment at the company

**Exemptions:**
- Companies operating fewer than 36 months (new business exemption)
- Companies growing headcount >75% year-over-year (hyper-growth deferral, 12 months)
- Companies in Chapter 11 proceedings or with 2+ consecutive years of operating losses
- Non-profit organizations with annual revenue <$10M

**Compliance tracking:** Annual EEO-1 supplement declaration; minimal new administrative
burden using existing reporting infrastructure.

**Non-compliance penalty:** $6,000 per unfilled required slot per year — less than the
cost of filling the position, but sufficient to make compliance economically dominant.

---

#### Component 2: Industry Development Pool (IDP)

For employers that cannot absorb development positions internally (specialized operations,
highly technical environments, or employers in regions with limited candidate pipelines),
the IDP provides an alternative compliance pathway:

**Mechanism:** Employer contributes **125% of the direct hiring cost** per unfilled required
slot to a sector-organized IDP. The pool funds qualifying positions at:
- Community colleges and technical training providers (in the employer's sector)
- National laboratory affiliates and DED-contracted training programs
- Qualifying nonprofit workforce development organizations
- CRHP service tracks and DED deployment programs

**Governance:** Each sector maintains its own IDP administered by the relevant industry
association under DOL charter. IDP funds are publicly audited annually; allocation is
reported by institution and sector.

**Why this works where UK failed:** The UK levy allowed companies to fund any "apprenticeship"
— including rebadging management training. The IDP is restricted to new-entrant development
positions in the company's sector, administered by third parties, and audited. The
position is created whether or not the contributing employer hosts it.

---

#### Component 3: DED National Development Infrastructure

The WDF is more than a floor — it is the enabling infrastructure for a national
development pipeline. The DED operates three supporting systems:

**DED Credential Registry:**
A national registry of WDF-qualifying development programs, credentialing outcomes, and
worker credential records. Modeled on Singapore's SkillsFuture platform:
- Employers register their development programs and receive DED program certification
- Participants receive a portable DED-recognized credential on completion
- Credentials are machine-readable by federal contracting systems, CRHP enrollment,
  and ERP eligibility databases — eliminating translation overhead between completion
  and employment

**DED Position Marketplace:**
An AI-powered platform connecting workers to available WDF positions, IDP-funded slots,
and CRHP development tracks:
- Workers input skills, location, and target occupation; platform matches to open positions
- Employers post positions and receive pre-screened candidates with DED credential profiles
- Reduces employer screening cost; reduces worker search time; maximizes utilization of
  required positions

**DED Employer Partnership Program:**
Companies that exceed the EDO floor receive DED partnership status with tangible benefits:
- Priority consideration in DED strategic contract solicitations (Type A, B, C)
- Enhanced ATC rate reduction (for ATC-paying employers): additional 2% reduction per
  tier above floor (1:75 = 2% reduction; 1:50 = 4% reduction)
- Public DED Partner certification — a reputational signal that the company invests in
  workforce development above minimum requirements
- Access to DED workforce intelligence: quarterly ODRI data and labor market projections
  for planning purposes

### Program Economics at Scale

#### Qualifying Employer Landscape

| Tier | Company Size | Estimated U.S. Employers | Estimated FTE in Scope |
|------|-------------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| Tier 1 | 100–499 FTE | ~90,000 companies | 23.4M workers |
| Tier 2 | 500–4,999 FTE | ~20,000 companies | 28.0M workers |
| Tier 3 | 5,000+ FTE | ~2,000 companies | 37.0M workers |
| **Total qualifying** | **100+ FTE** | **~112,000 companies** | **~88.4M workers** |

After exemptions (~15% of qualifying headcount): **~75M FTE in active scope**.

#### Required Position Estimates

| Component | Calculation | Result |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Required concurrent slots | 75M ÷ 100 | **750,000** |
| Currently existing concurrent paid slots at qualifying firms | ~360,000 (estimated) | — |
| **Net new concurrent slots required** | 750k − 360k | **~390,000** |
| Annual new intern experiences (2.5 rotations/year per slot) | 390k × 2.5 | **~975,000** |
| Unpaid-to-paid conversions at qualifying firms | ~200,000 | — |
| **Total new paid experiences per year** | — | **~1.15–1.2 million** |

#### New Wage Creation

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Average qualifying position wage | $17–20/hr × 30 hrs/week × 12 weeks = $6,120–$7,200 per rotation |
| Annual cost per slot (2.5 rotations) | $15,300–$18,000 |
| New wages from net new slots (390k) | **$6.0–$7.0B/year** |
| New wages from unpaid-to-paid conversions | **$1.2–$1.6B/year** |
| **Total new private-sector wage creation** | **~$7–8.5B/year** |
| Federal government direct cost | **Near-zero** (EEO-1 infrastructure; ~$50M enforcement) |

**For comparison:** The 2009 ARRA stimulus disbursed $787B over two years — one-time.
The WDF creates $7–8.5B in **recurring annual private-sector wages** at near-zero federal
cost, indefinitely.

#### Projected 10-Year Impact

Assuming the WDF reaches full implementation in Year 3 and operates at steady state thereafter:

| Metric | 10-Year Total |
|--------|--------------|
| New paid development experiences created | ~10–12 million |
| New wage income generated | ~$70–85B |
| Workers entering CRHP pipeline via WDF credential | ~2–3 million |
| Workers with documented paid work experience leading to higher-wage employment | ~6–8 million |
| Estimated lifetime earnings premium per beneficiary (based on NACE paid internship premium data) | $50,000–$120,000 |
| **Estimated aggregate lifetime earnings uplift** | **$300B–$960B** |

At the lower bound of the earnings uplift estimate, the WDF produces a **35–40:1 return
on the federal government's near-zero investment** — approaching the 7:1 GI Bill ROI
benchmark on an order-of-magnitude basis because the cost is borne by employers, not
the Treasury.

## DED Integration — How the WDF Amplifies Every Other Program

### WDF + AI Unemployment Scenario

The WDF is the structural response to the "missing first rung" problem that AI displacement
accelerates. As AI eliminates routine entry-level roles:

- **WDF prevents the elimination from becoming permanent:** Even as AI handles routine tasks,
  large employers must maintain entry-level development positions. The AI tools get deployed;
  the human development function is preserved.
- **ATC linkage:** Companies paying the AI Transition Contribution that also maintain WDF
  compliance at 1:75 or better receive a 2-point ATC rate reduction. Companies paying ATC
  that fall below WDF compliance face a 2-point rate increase. This creates a direct fiscal
  connection between AI deployment, displacement funding, and workforce development obligation.
- **The flywheel integration:** WDF positions feed into the AI retraining flywheel — interns
  get AI literacy exposure, earn DED credentials, enter CRHP, and become eligible for the
  higher-tier employment pathways.

See `ded/scenarios/ai-unemployment/phase-1-triage-and-stabilization.md`.

### WDF + CRHP

Every WDF position is a potential CRHP enrollment pathway:

- Workers who complete a WDF qualifying position and earn a DED credential are automatically
  eligible for CRHP enrollment at the subsidized fee rate (no additional means test)
- WDF positions at CRHP-partner employers include a CRHP orientation module as standard —
  the development position is the first introduction to the civilian reserve concept
- CRHP service tracks are recognized as WDF-qualifying positions when hosted at qualifying
  employers — meaning CRHP infrastructure and WDF infrastructure reinforce each other

The practical effect: every major employer's WDF program is a CRHP feeder. At steady state,
the WDF creates 1 million+ new CRHP-eligible workers annually — dramatically expanding the
reserve pool without any separate recruitment effort.

See `ded/proposal/civilian-reserve-health/overview.md`.

### WDF + Strategic Contracts

DED strategic contract solicitations (Types A, B, C) include WDF compliance as a selection
criterion:

- **Minimum requirement:** All Type A/B/C contract holders must maintain EDO compliance
- **Enhanced floor for contract holders:** Companies holding DED contracts above $50M are
  held to the 1:75 enhanced floor (not just 1:100)
- **Type B training contracts:** DED Type B contracts with community colleges and training
  providers are partly funded through IDP pool revenue — creating a self-reinforcing cycle
  where employer contributions fund the training infrastructure that trains the workforce
  the DED scenario plans require

See `ded/tools/strategic-contracts.md`.

### WDF + ERP / Accelerators

At the upper end of the development pipeline, WDF positions create the talent pool that
eventually feeds ERP:

- Workers who complete multiple WDF-qualifying positions and accumulate DED credentials
  become visible to ERP recruiting systems
- Accelerator programs can recruit from the DED Credential Registry — identifying workers
  who have demonstrated relevant competencies through documented WDF positions
- The WDF is the bottom of the funnel; ERP and Accelerators are the top; the DED National
  Development Infrastructure connects them

See `ded/tools/expert-reserve-program.md`.

### WDF + Pandemic and Other Crisis Scenarios

**Pandemic:** In Phase 1, contact-requiring WDF positions in food service and retail may
be suspended; remote-capable positions must continue or migrate to remote. The WDF creates
a maintained talent pipeline for the Phase 2 cross-training programs that keep essential
sectors staffed — participants already have DED credentials and CRHP relationships.

**Grid Cyberattack / Semiconductor Collapse:** The WDF's required training domains (which
DED recommends but does not mandate) include cybersecurity, grid operations, and precision
manufacturing — the same domains where the DED scenario response plans face the most severe
workforce gaps. A consistent WDF program at major utilities, defense contractors, and
semiconductor firms produces a pre-trained workforce for surge response.

## Standalone Operation — WDF Without DED

The WDF is designed to function as a standalone federal program even if the DED is not
established. In standalone mode:

**Administering agency:** Department of Labor (Office of Apprenticeship + Wage and Hour Division)
**Reporting authority:** EEO-1 supplement filed with EEOC (existing infrastructure)
**IDP governance:** Sector trade associations under DOL charter (existing model — Job Corps,
WIOA industry partnerships)
**Credential registry:** Operated by Department of Education (existing Credential Engine
and Competency-Based Education Network infrastructure)

In standalone mode, the WDF creates the same ~1 million annual paid development experiences
and $7–8.5B in wages — without the DED integration benefits of CRHP pipeline, ATC linkage,
and Accelerator feeder. Standalone WDF is still a net positive at near-zero federal cost.

The DED integration multiplies the value significantly: it connects the development floor
to the reserve pipeline, the economic stabilization tools, and the national security
workforce. But the floor itself does not require the DED to function.

## Legislative Design

### Enabling Legislation

The WDF requires a single enabling act — the **Workforce Development Floor Act** — amending:
- **Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA):** Add Section 14(d): qualifying employer development
  obligation, position standards, and non-compliance penalty
- **Equal Employment Opportunity Act (EEO):** Amend EEO-1 reporting requirements to
  include WDF compliance declaration
- **Internal Revenue Code § 45S:** Expand existing employer-provided paid family leave
  credit to cover IDP contributions — making pool contributions partially tax-creditable
  and reducing the effective cost of the alternative compliance pathway

### Political Framing — Multi-Audience Positioning

The WDF should be presented differently to different audiences:

**To business/economic audiences:**
"A talent pipeline investment that every major employer already benefits from but that
the market systematically underprovides. The WDF captures the externality — ensuring
that employers who depend on a trained workforce contribute to producing it."

**To labor/progressive audiences:**
"A structural end to the unpaid internship system that gates career entry on family wealth.
Every required position is paid at or above living wage. Every worker who completes one
gets a portable federal credential that opens the next door."

**To fiscal conservative audiences:**
"Near-zero federal cost. Funded entirely by the private sector. Seven-to-one historical
ROI benchmark from the GI Bill. The government sets the floor; the market funds it."

**To national security audiences:**
"The DED's reserve programs require a workforce pipeline. The WDF creates it. Every
CRHP member, every Accelerator participant, every Type D expert reserve contract holder
entered the pipeline somewhere. The WDF is where the pipeline begins."

## Implementation Phasing

### Phase 0 — Design and Infrastructure (Year 0–1, pre-enforcement)
- DOL publishes program regulations; final rule on position qualification standards
- DED (or DOL standalone) builds Credential Registry and Position Marketplace platforms
- IDP sector pool charters established with trade associations
- EEO-1 reporting system updated
- Employer education campaign: 18-month advance notice before first compliance year

### Phase 1 — Soft Launch (Year 1–2)
- Enforcement begins for Tier 3 employers (5,000+ FTE) only
- Non-compliance penalty applies; IDP pathway available
- DED Employer Partnership certification opens
- Annual reporting cycle established; public compliance registry published

### Phase 2 — Full Implementation (Year 3)
- Enforcement expands to Tier 1 and Tier 2 employers (100–4,999 FTE)
- ATC linkage activates (for ATC-paying employers)
- CRHP pipeline integration operational
- Enhanced floor (1:75) activates for DED strategic contract holders

### Phase 3 — Evaluation and Adjustment (Year 5+)
- GAO audit of program outcomes: positions created, wages paid, credential completion
  rates, employment outcomes for WDF participants vs. control cohort
- Congress reviews floor level (consider moving to 1:75 universal by Year 7 based on outcomes)
- International comparison: how does U.S. WDF performance compare to Germany and Singapore?

## Risk Analysis

### Employer Resistance
**Risk:** Large employer lobbying effort to delay or weaken the floor.
**Mitigation:** The floor is genuinely low. A 10,000-employee company must maintain 100
intern positions — a standard large employer already runs programs at this scale. The
companies that resist most strongly are those who have been most aggressive at eliminating
entry-level positions. The political framing — what did you do with those jobs? — is
difficult to defend publicly.

### Quality Floor, Not Quality Guarantee
**Risk:** Employers create technically-qualifying but developmentally empty positions.
**Mitigation:** DOL audit authority; DED Credential Registry outcome data (credentialed
completion rates by employer); public ranking of employer programs. Employers with
low credential completion rates face enhanced scrutiny. Market pressure: workers share
program quality information; employers with poor programs struggle to fill positions.

### Coverage Gap — Small Employers
**Risk:** 32% of the private sector workforce is at companies under 100 FTE, entirely
exempt. Entry-level development at small employers remains unpaid or absent.
**Mitigation:** The IDP pools, funded partly by large employer contributions, fund positions
at small employers indirectly. Small employers can voluntarily register as IDP hosting
organizations and receive pre-screened WDF candidates without the compliance burden.
The long-run solution is the German model: embed development culture at all employer sizes
through incentive rather than mandate.

## Summary — The Case in One Page

The United States is about to go through the largest workforce transition since mechanized
agriculture displaced farm workers in the early 20th century. AI will displace millions of
workers from routine occupations faster than labor markets can absorb them. The entry-level
positions that historically served as transition points are disappearing first.

The Workforce Development Floor does three things:
1. **Establishes a minimum:** Large employers must maintain a development function. The
   entry-level position is not optional at scale. This is not a new concept — Germany has
   done it for decades, France taxes it universally, and the United States built its
   postwar middle class on a version of it (the GI Bill).
2. **Creates a pipeline:** ~1 million new paid development experiences per year, generating
   $7–8.5B in wages, funded by the employers who benefit. Near-zero federal cost. The
   historical benchmark return on workforce development investment is 7:1.
3. **Connects to national readiness:** In the DED framework, every WDF completion is a
   potential CRHP enrollment. Every CRHP enrollment is a potential reserve deployment.
   Every reserve deployment is a force multiplier for the national response capability.
   The entry-level development floor is not just workforce policy — it is the foundation
   of the civilian reserve infrastructure.
