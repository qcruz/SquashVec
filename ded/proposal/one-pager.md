# Department of Economic Defense
## Proposal for Establishment — Executive One-Pager

---

## The Case

The United States has no standing institution whose sole mandate is the economic security
of the nation. When the 1973 oil embargo crippled the economy, the Strategic Petroleum
Reserve was created — after the crisis. When the 2008 financial collapse destroyed $19
trillion in household wealth, the CFPB and Dodd-Frank were written — after the crisis.
When COVID-19 cost the nation an estimated $16 trillion in economic output, supply chains
were rebuilt and PPE stockpiles replenished — after the crisis.

The pattern is consistent: the United States diagnoses, funds, and prepares for the last
disruption. The Department of Economic Defense is built to end that pattern.

The precedent is well-established. The Federal Reserve was created in 1913 not after a
crisis was underway but to prevent the next one. DARPA was stood up in 1958 to ensure
the nation would never again be caught off-guard by a strategic technological development.
The Department of Defense itself was created in 1947 to unify fragmented military capacity
under a single coordinating authority. In each case, the founding logic was the same:
a standing institution with a focused mission outperforms improvised response. The DED
applies that logic to economic security.

---

## Mission

**To protect, strengthen, and advance the economic security of the United States and its
citizens through continuous analysis, strategic investment, and coordinated preparedness
across all sectors of the national economy.**

---

## Core Objectives

**1. Ensure preparedness and response readiness to any potential economic disruption.**
Maintain continuously updated contingency plans, strategic reserves, and pre-authorized
response tools across all identified disruption scenarios. No economic crisis should find
the United States without a plan already in motion.

**2. Research and strengthen key economic supply chains and assets.**
Conduct ongoing dependency analysis across every sector of the national economy. Identify
single points of failure. Invest in domestic capacity, strategic stockpiles, and redundant
systems. Ensure that the assets the nation depends on are known, mapped, and protected.

**3. Ensure economic security and mobility for every U.S. citizen.**
Design and maintain the policy tools, income infrastructure, and workforce programs that
allow every American to withstand economic disruption and participate fully in economic
recovery. Security at the national level must translate to security at the household level.

---

## Framework

The DED operates a continuous planning cycle across all critical economic systems:

| Step | Function |
|------|----------|
| **Identify** | Determine which systems are critical and currently vulnerable |
| **Map** | Build the full dependency graph of each system |
| **Substitute** | Establish what replaces each dependency if it fails |
| **Preserve** | Maintain dormant capacity and restart-ready contingency plans |
| **Transition** | Develop and test pre-built emergency conversion playbooks |
| **Simulate** | Conduct regular stress tests and scenario exercises — not only during crises |

This cycle runs in peacetime. Plans exist before they are needed. Every scenario the department
tracks has a corresponding phase response, a pre-authorized tool set, and a designated
coordinating authority — ready to execute on declaration, not after deliberation.

## Organizational Structure

**Secretary of Economic Defense** — Cabinet-level. Coordinates across DoD, Treasury,
Commerce, USDA, Energy, and HHS. Chairs the National Economic Security Council.
Reports directly to the President.

**Office of Scenario Analysis and Preparedness** — Maintains the full scenario library.
Owns the continuous planning cycle. Leads annual simulation exercises with federal agencies,
state governments, and private sector partners. Issues the National Economic Preparedness
Index.

**Office of Strategic Assets and Supply Chains** — Maintains the Strategic Asset Registry.
Monitors supply chain dependencies in real time. Recommends strategic investments, reserve
contracts, and critical infrastructure hardening to the Secretary and Congress.

**Office of Citizen Economic Security** — Designs, maintains, and administers the policy
tool library: direct transfer programs, income infrastructure, workforce retraining systems,
and healthcare access programs. Ensures that national-level response plans have household-level
delivery mechanisms.

**Office of Research and National Laboratory Coordination** — Coordinates with the 17 DOE
national laboratories, DARPA, ARPA-E, NIH/BARDA, and federal research agencies. Translates
research output into deployable contingency solutions. Manages IP strategy and technology
transfer programs.

**National Economic Intelligence Division** — Monitors leading indicators across all scenario
categories. Maintains the status tracking system for critical economic data: energy reserves,
crop yields, infrastructure health, workforce metrics. Issues early warning to the Secretary
when scenario trigger thresholds are approached.

## The Defense Budget Argument

Economic disruption is not only a civilian concern. The Department of Defense spends
approximately **$20B/year on energy**, **$5B/year on food service and rations**, and
trillions on weapons platforms whose production costs are directly tied to energy,
water, steel, semiconductor, and specialty material prices. Every sustained commodity
price shock is simultaneously a defense readiness cost shock.

The 2021–2022 energy price spike increased DoD energy costs by an estimated **$3–5B**
in a single year. The 2021–2022 semiconductor shortage delayed multiple weapons program
deliveries and reduced readiness. The COVID pandemic disrupted military supply chains
across 40+ categories of equipment and consumables.

A department that maintains stable, competitive costs for the economy's critical inputs
is a force multiplier for the defense budget — not a competitor for it. **DED's annual
budget is a hedge against defense procurement cost inflation.**

---

## Scale and Cost

The DED is not a large bureaucracy. Its value is in coordination, analysis, and pre-positioning
— not in direct operation of economic systems. The department's standing budget is estimated
at **$60–80 billion per year** in steady state, covering:

- Strategic contracts maintaining critical industrial and supply chain capacity
- National laboratory coordination and directed research programs
- Civilian Reserve (CRHP) administration and infrastructure deployment programs
- Scenario simulation exercises and economic intelligence operations
- Administrative functions

For context, this is approximately equal to the current annual federal highway budget — and
it is a fraction of the cost of a single unmitigated crisis. The 2020 COVID response cost
the federal government **$5.2 trillion**. One avoided Tier 2 disruption over a decade
returns the department's full investment many times over.

## Historical Precedent for Establishment

Every major standing federal institution was created in response to a demonstrated gap
between what the nation faced and what it had built to face it:

| Institution | Year | Triggering Gap |
|-------------|------|---------------|
| Federal Reserve | 1913 | Panic of 1907 — no lender of last resort |
| DoD (unified) | 1947 | WWII — fragmented military command structure |
| DARPA | 1958 | Sputnik — no standing advanced research authority |
| Strategic Petroleum Reserve | 1975 | 1973 oil embargo — no national energy buffer |
| FEMA | 1979 | Repeated uncoordinated disaster responses |
| CFPB | 2010 | 2008 financial crisis — no consumer financial protection |

Each of these institutions was opposed at founding as unnecessary, expensive, or an
overreach. Each is now considered foundational. The United States did not have a unified
military command until World War II proved the cost of not having one. It did not have a
standing energy reserve until an oil embargo proved the cost of not having one.

The question the DED poses is not whether economic disruptions will occur. They will.
The question is whether the nation will again pay the full price of improvised response —
or whether it will build, in advance, the institution that prevents that cost.
