## Purpose of This Document

This is the founding charter document. The one-pager makes the case for the DED.
This document defines exactly what the DED is legally authorized to do, what it is
prohibited from doing, how it is structured, how it is funded, and how its powers
are constrained against capture.

The intended reader is a congressional staffer, OMB analyst, or constitutional law
reviewer asking: "What law creates this agency, what does it specifically authorize,
what does it prohibit, and how does it resist the pathologies that afflict every
prior government institution of this kind?"

## 1. Legal Authority and Enabling Legislation

### 1.1 The Enabling Act — Economic Security Act of [Year]

The DED is established by original legislation: the **Economic Security Act**, which
creates the department as a cabinet-level agency within the executive branch, defines
its mandate, grants enumerated authorities, and establishes the oversight framework.

The act does not merely reorganize existing agencies. It creates new authority in
areas where no single agency currently has jurisdiction — specifically, the authority
to manage the *intersection* between economic sectors in preparation for cross-sector
disruption events. FEMA manages disasters after they occur. Treasury manages financial
stability. USDA manages agricultural markets. Commerce manages trade. None has a mandate
to model and pre-position responses to the compounding interaction between oil restriction,
food price, and labor market disruption simultaneously. That gap is the DED's jurisdiction.

### 1.2 Inherited and Extended Statutory Authorities

The DED enabling act incorporates, extends, or modifies authority from the following
existing statutes:

**Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. § 4501 et seq.)**
The DPA grants the President emergency authority to require private sector actors to
prioritize contracts for national defense, to allocate materials, and to expand productive
capacity. DPA Title I authorities (priority ratings, allocation orders) and Title III
authorities (expansion of productive capacity and supply) are currently delegated across
multiple agencies (DoD, HHS, DHS) with no coordinating authority.

*DED modification:* The enabling act consolidates DPA Title III implementation authority
under the DED for non-military applications. DED becomes the lead DPA coordinator for
supply chain and economic resilience programs, with DoD retaining exclusive authority for
military procurement applications. This eliminates the current gap where Title III
programs are fragmented across agencies with no cross-sector view.

**Bayh-Dole Act (35 U.S.C. § 200–212)**
Bayh-Dole governs IP rights from federally funded research, allowing contractors to retain
patent rights on government-funded inventions subject to a government use license and
march-in rights. The act's march-in authority (§ 203) has never been exercised in its
40-year history despite clear statutory grounds.

*DED modification:* The enabling act designates the DED as the lead agency for march-in
right determinations in cases where (a) the licensed technology is relevant to a DED
scenario priority area and (b) the licensee is not making the invention available to the
public on reasonable terms. DED also gains authority to establish the IP Asset Register
under § 209 of the modified act, classifying government-owned IP by strategic value tier
for potential licensing as a trade negotiation instrument.

**Trade Expansion Act § 232 (19 U.S.C. § 1862)**
Section 232 authorizes the President to impose trade restrictions on goods whose import
threatens national security. Current authority is held by Commerce (investigation) and the
President (action), with no standing analytical body maintaining continuous threat assessments.

*DED modification:* DED is designated as the standing analytical authority for § 232
determinations, replacing ad-hoc investigations. The NEID (see § 2.5) maintains continuous
monitoring of import concentration in DED-priority sectors. A DED-initiated § 232 finding
triggers expedited review and activates the corresponding scenario response tools.

**International Emergency Economic Powers Act (50 U.S.C. § 1701–1707)**
IEEPA grants the President broad authority to regulate international transactions in a
declared national emergency. Currently administered through OFAC (Treasury) with no
economic preparedness dimension.

*DED modification:* DED is added as a co-administrator of IEEPA applications relevant
to supply chain, technology transfer, and trade instrument deployments. DED-recommended
IEEPA activations require concurrent notification to the JCED (see § 5.2). Financial
sanctions under IEEPA remain at Treasury; trade and investment restrictions tied to DED
scenario priorities are DED-co-administered.

**National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. § 1601–1651)**
The National Emergencies Act establishes the framework for presidential emergency declarations
and the statutory authorities that activate on declaration. DED scenarios require a tiered
declaration framework that activates specific pre-authorized response packages without
requiring new legislation during a crisis.

*DED modification:* The enabling act adds an "Economic Security Emergency" category to the
National Emergencies Act framework, with three severity tiers and defined automatic activations
for each (see § 4 below).

### 1.3 New Authorities Created by the Enabling Act

The following authorities do not currently exist in U.S. law and must be created by the
DED enabling legislation:

**DED Strategic Contract Authority**
Authority to enter long-term (5–20 year) strategic contracts with private sector entities
for capacity preservation, R&D investment, and supply chain resilience — separate from and
not subject to the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR). Strategic contracts are not
procurement contracts; they are incentive agreements. The enabling act defines the four
contract types (A/B/C/D — see `tools/strategic-contracts.md`) and the JCED oversight
framework that governs them.

Justification for FAR exemption: FAR is designed for competitive procurement of defined
deliverables. DED strategic contracts are relational agreements for maintaining capability
and generating knowledge — more analogous to DARPA OTA agreements than to DOD contracts.
The DED contract framework inherits OTA's flexibility with a stronger public interest
constraint: contracts must be tied to published scenario priorities and are subject to
mandatory JCED review at scale.

**CRHP Reserve Designation**
Authority to designate civilian Civilian Reserve Health Program enrollees as a reserve
component for the purposes of rapid mobilization for infrastructure construction, repair,
and crisis response functions. This requires explicit congressional authorization to create
a civilian reserve corps with defined mobilization rights, compensation structure, and
benefits — distinct from the military reserves or the National Guard.

The CRHP designation grants DED authority to administer the program and direct deployment
in declared economic emergencies. It does not grant DED command authority over enrollees
in defense contexts — that remains with DoD.

**DED Economic Emergency Declaration Authority**
Authority for the DED Secretary (for Tier 1 events) or the President on DED recommendation
(for Tier 2/3 events) to issue a tiered economic emergency declaration that activates
pre-authorized response packages from the DED scenario library. See § 4 for the full
declaration framework.

**Joint Committee on Economic Defense (JCED)**
Statutory creation of a standing joint congressional committee with oversight authority
over the DED's contract portfolio, emergency declarations, and IP licensing activities.
The JCED is the primary accountability mechanism for DED authority. See § 5.2.

**IP Asset Register Classification Authority**
Authority to maintain a classified registry of government-owned, government-licensed, and
government-encumbered intellectual property with strategic economic value, and to authorize
licensing of that IP as part of trade negotiations, technology transfer agreements, and
compulsory licensing frameworks.

### 1.4 Explicit Prohibitions — Boundaries of DED Authority

The enabling act includes a prohibitions section with equal legislative weight to the
grants of authority. These prohibitions cannot be waived by executive order:

**No rationing authority.** The DED has no authority to ration goods, allocate goods
by command to end users, or issue consumption mandates. Rationing authority in national
emergencies remains with FEMA, USDA, and state governments under existing law.

**No price-setting authority.** The DED has no authority to set, cap, or floor prices
for any good or service. Price stabilization is pursued exclusively through financial
incentives (contracts, subsidies, tax instruments) and supply-side investment.

**No authority to compel private sector action outside contract terms.** Except under DPA
Title I priority ratings and allocation orders in declared emergencies, the DED cannot direct
private sector production, employment, or capital allocation. Participation in DED programs
is voluntary. The DED creates conditions in which the desired behavior is economically
rational — it does not mandate it.

**No intelligence collection authority.** The NEID monitors publicly available economic
data and licensed commercial data. It has no authority to collect signals intelligence,
conduct surveillance, or compel data disclosure outside of existing regulatory reporting
requirements.

**No operational authority over critical infrastructure.** The DED has no authority to
operate, commandeer, or direct the operations of critical infrastructure — energy grids,
water systems, transportation networks, communications infrastructure. DED activity in
these sectors is limited to financial contracts, investment programs, and advisory functions.

## 2. Organizational Structure

### 2.1 Leadership — Senate-Confirmed Positions

**Secretary of Economic Defense**
Cabinet-level. Appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate. Four-year term,
renewable once. Reports directly to the President. Chairs the National Economic Security
Council (NESC) — the interagency coordinating body that includes the Secretaries of
Treasury, Commerce, Defense, Agriculture, Energy, HHS, State, and the USTR.

The four-year term with renewal creates accountability without indefinite tenure.
The single-renewal cap (maximum 8 years) prevents entrenchment. The Senate-confirmation
requirement ensures bipartisan vetting.

**Deputy Secretary of Economic Defense**
Senate-confirmed. Serves as acting Secretary in absence. Oversees internal operations,
interagency coordination, and the daily management of the five offices.

**Under Secretary for Intelligence and Analysis**
Senate-confirmed. Leads the National Economic Intelligence Division (NEID). Has authority
to receive classified intelligence products from IC agencies relevant to DED scenario
priorities, and to produce classified economic threat assessments for the NSC and JCED.

### 2.2 Five Operating Offices

**Office of Scenario Analysis and Preparedness (OSAP)**
- Maintains the DED scenario library — all disruption scenarios, phase files, and response packages
- Conducts the annual National Economic Stress Test: a mandatory interagency simulation
  exercise across all Tier 1 and Tier 2 scenarios
- Issues the **National Economic Preparedness Index (NEPI)** — a public annual assessment
  of U.S. preparedness across 8 scenario categories, rated 1–10
- Coordinates with state emergency management agencies and the private sector on preparedness
- Staffing: ~800 analysts, scenario specialists, and exercise coordinators

**Office of Strategic Assets and Supply Chains (OSASC)**
- Maintains the **Strategic Asset Registry** — 70+ assets scored on the DED 4-dimension
  scoring framework (criticality, vulnerability, substitutability, DED leverage)
- Monitors supply chain dependencies in real time using NEID data
- Recommends Type A (capacity preservation) and Type C (supply chain development) contracts
  to the Secretary
- Coordinates with USTR and Commerce on critical goods import priority determinations
- Staffing: ~600 analysts, supply chain specialists, and procurement advisors

**Office of Citizen Economic Security (OCES)**
- Designs, maintains, and administers the DED policy tool library — direct transfer programs
  (TRAL, stimulus), workforce programs (CRHP, internship floor, ERP), healthcare access (CRHP health)
- Manages the Civilian Reserve Health Program (CRHP) in peacetime: enrollment, training,
  credentialing, and benefit administration
- Maintains the **Displacement Safety Account (DSA)** portable savings account infrastructure
- Maintains TRAL (Tax Return Advance Loan) systems in partnership with IRS
- Staffing: ~1,200 program administrators, benefits specialists, and workforce coordinators

**Office of Research and National Laboratory Coordination (ORNLC)**
- Coordinates with the 17 DOE national laboratories, DARPA, ARPA-E, NIH/BARDA, NSF,
  and relevant federal research agencies
- Manages the 4 DED Accelerator programs (see `tools/expert-reserve-program.md`)
- Administers the Expert Reserve Program (ERP) and Type D contract relationships
- Oversees the IP Asset Register — classifies and maintains government IP inventory
- Manages licensing negotiations in partnership with USTR when IP is a trade instrument
- Staffing: ~400 scientists, technology transfer specialists, and IP attorneys

**National Economic Intelligence Division (NEID)**
- Monitors leading indicators across all DED scenario categories
- Maintains the **Economic Threat Dashboard**: real-time tracking of energy reserves,
  crop yields, water levels, manufacturing input prices, workforce metrics, trade flows
- Issues internal early warnings when any scenario's Tier 1 trigger thresholds are approached
- Issues public quarterly reports on commodity price trajectories and supply chain stress
- Has no collection authority; relies on USDA, EIA, BLS, Census, NOAA, and licensed
  commercial data providers
- Staffing: ~500 economists, data scientists, and sector analysts

### 2.3 Career vs. Political Appointee Balance

The DED is deliberately designed to be more career-staff-dominated than the typical
cabinet agency. The institutional knowledge required to maintain a living scenario library,
manage multi-decade strategic contracts, and operate a national laboratory coordination
program cannot be rebuilt after every presidential transition.

**Political appointees:** Secretary, Deputy Secretary, Under Secretary for Intelligence and
Analysis, five Office Directors, six Deputy Office Directors = 14 total political positions.

**Career SES and staff:** All other positions — estimated 3,500–4,000 FTEs in steady state.
Senior Economists, Scenario Directors, Asset Registry Managers, and Contract Managers are
career SES positions with civil service protections.

This ratio (14 political / ~3,500+ career) is similar to the Federal Reserve model and
explicitly unlike the current DoD/State Department model where political appointees fill
several hundred positions, disrupting institutional continuity.

### 2.4 Interagency Relationships

**National Economic Security Council (NESC)**
Chaired by the DED Secretary. Includes Secretaries of Treasury, Defense, Commerce,
Agriculture, Energy, HHS, State, and the USTR. Meets quarterly in peacetime; weekly or
daily on declaration. The NESC is the decision-making forum for cross-agency DED activations.
The DED Secretary has the authority to call an emergency NESC session within 24 hours.

**Agency coordination authorities:**
- *Treasury:* DED coordinates with Treasury on TRAL administration, ARBF bond guarantee
  facilities, and any financial instrument deployed at scale. Treasury retains authority
  over monetary policy and financial system stability.
- *Commerce:* DED and Commerce share § 232 analytical responsibility. Export controls
  (Wassenaar, EAR, ITAR) remain at Commerce/State; DED advises on economic implications.
- *USDA:* DED agricultural programs (crop yield bonds, Type B irrigation contracts,
  LFP supplements) require USDA coordination. DED does not administer USDA programs —
  it designs financial instruments that complement them.
- *DoD:* The defense budget argument is central to DED's political viability. DED provides
  DoD with supply chain vulnerability assessments and Type A contract coverage for
  defense-relevant industrial capacity. DoD provides DED with intelligence on foreign
  economic threats via the Under Secretary for Intelligence.
- *Energy:* DED energy scenario work (SPR strategy, grid resilience contracts, energy
  price bond guarantees) requires coordination with DOE. DOE national laboratories report
  to DOE administratively but participate in DED Accelerator programs under Type D contracts.
- *HHS:* CRHP health benefit administration, pandemic scenario coordination, and
  workforce health monitoring.

### 2.5 Inspector General

The DED IG is appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate independently of
the Secretary. The IG has full investigative authority over all DED programs, contracts,
and personnel. By statute, the IG reports simultaneously to the Secretary and to Congress
and cannot be removed by the Secretary.

Mandatory IG audits:
- All Type A and B contracts above $500M at signing and every 5 years
- All emergency declaration activations — full audit within 90 days of declaration termination
- All IP licensing transactions above $100M
- Annual review of JCED compliance — whether the JCED approval process is being followed
  for contracts in its review threshold
- Random audit of 10% of Type C contracts annually

## 3. Budget Framework

### 3.1 Steady-State Annual Budget — $60–80B

| Function | Annual Budget | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Type A contracts (capacity preservation) | $15–20B | Long-term agreements with critical industry |
| Type B contracts (R&D + infrastructure) | $12–18B | Investment generating IP and productive capacity |
| Type C contracts (supply chain development) | $5–8B | New domestic capacity creation |
| Type D contracts (ERP, Accelerators) | $3–5B | Expert reserve and national lab programs |
| CRHP administration and deployment | $5–8B | Peacetime training, benefits; surge-funded on activation |
| OCES programs (TRAL, DSA, WDF) | $3–5B | Administered programs; TRAL is largely self-funding |
| NEID and economic intelligence | $2–3B | Data infrastructure, analytics, monitoring |
| Scenario analysis and simulations | $1–2B | Annual stress tests, scenario library maintenance |
| Administrative and overhead | $2–3B | ~3,500 FTEs + facilities |
| **Total steady-state** | **$48–72B** | |

**Emergency reserve fund:** A $50–100B contingency facility pre-appropriated by Congress,
held at Treasury, activated only on Tier 2 or Tier 3 emergency declaration. The reserve
fund is not part of the annual budget but is scored by CBO as a contingent liability
against its actuarial activation probability — similar to FDIC resolution fund scoring.

### 3.2 Revenue Offsets

The DED is not designed as a pure cost center. Three revenue streams partially offset
the standing budget:

**Type B contract IP revenue:** Government retains a royalty-bearing license on all IP
developed under Type B contracts. In steady state (Year 5+), licensing income from
commercially deployed IP is estimated at $8–15B/year. See `economics/value-discovery.md`
for the $84B investment → $31–99B/year revenue projection across 8 opportunity categories.

**IP licensing as trade instrument:** DED-administered licensing of government-owned
and government-encumbered IP generates direct revenue and trade concessions with
measurable economic value. See `economics/ip-licensing-as-trade-tool.md`.

**TRAL self-funding:** Tax Return Advance Loans are funded by advancing against future
tax refunds — the Treasury does not make a grant; it extends credit. Estimated net cost
is near zero in non-crisis years (repayment rates above 95%) with a modest net cost in
high-displacement years as some advances are forgiven under AI-displacement tracks.

**Net steady-state budget cost after offsets (Year 5+):**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross annual budget | $60–80B |
| Type B IP revenue (Year 5+) | -$8–15B |
| Trade instrument licensing | -$2–5B |
| TRAL net cost (non-crisis year) | ~$0 |
| **Net annual cost** | **$42–63B** |

### 3.3 Ten-Year Net Cost vs. Return

| Year | Gross Cost | Revenue Offsets | Net Cost | Expected Value of Averted Loss* |
|------|-----------|-----------------|----------|---------------------------------|
| 1 | $30B (ramp) | $0.5B | $29.5B | $0 (building capacity) |
| 2 | $50B | $1B | $49B | $150B (first contracts operational) |
| 3 | $65B | $3B | $62B | $400B |
| 4 | $70B | $6B | $64B | $700B |
| 5 | $75B | $10B | $65B | $1.1T |
| 6–10 | $75B/yr | $12B/yr avg | $63B/yr | $1.5–2.5T/yr |
| **10-year total** | **$680B** | **$100B** | **$580B** | **$6.3–13.2T** |

*Expected value = probability-weighted loss avoidance across all monitored scenarios.
See `economics/benefits-summary.md` for full calculation methodology.*

**The 10:1 return case:** Even at the low end of the expected value range ($6.3T over
10 years), the DED returns approximately $10 for every $1 of net cost. The $5.2T single-event
cost of COVID-19 alone would have justified the DED's full 10-year budget at the gross level.

### 3.4 Budget Constraints Against Overreach

To prevent budget creep and mission expansion:
- Annual budget is capped by statute at 0.5% of GDP in any given year
  (~$150B at 2026 GDP levels) — preventing emergency-year blank-check appropriation
- Emergency reserve fund activation requires joint presidential/JCED authorization
- IG must certify annually that contract portfolio is within statutory geographic
  distribution limits
- CBO must score the contingent liability of all outstanding guarantee programs
  (ARBF bonds, CRHP benefits) annually

## 4. Emergency Declaration Framework

### 4.1 Declaration Tiers

The DED operates on a three-tier emergency framework that activates different levels of
pre-authorized response packages:

**Tier 1 — Economic Watch**
*Authority:* DED Secretary alone.
*Criteria:* Any single scenario's NEID monitoring indicators reach Tier 1 trigger thresholds
  (e.g., PDSI drought index for 3+ major agricultural states; oil import restriction affecting
  >15% of daily consumption; AI displacement exceeding ODRI Tier 1 in 3+ metro areas).
*Effect:* Activates monitoring protocols; puts Type A contract first-call rights on standby;
  notifies JCED within 48 hours; DED Secretary briefs NESC within 7 days.
*Duration:* 90 days; renewable by Secretary. Auto-terminates if trigger conditions resolve.

**Tier 2 — Economic Emergency**
*Authority:* President on DED Secretary recommendation; Secretary notifies JCED simultaneously.
*Criteria:* Tier 1 conditions have escalated to active disruption; cross-sector cascade
  is detected by NEID; estimated economic cost to GDP exceeds 0.5% on an annualized basis.
*Effect:* Activates full pre-built Phase 1 response package for the relevant scenario;
  Type A contract first-call rights exercised; TRAL AI-displacement track or pandemic track
  activated as applicable; CRHP deployment authorization issued; ATC levy activated in AI
  unemployment scenario; DED Secretary chairs weekly NESC sessions.
*Duration:* 180 days; requires presidential renewal for extension. Congressional review
  within 30 days of declaration under JCED oversight.

**Tier 3 — Economic Crisis**
*Authority:* President; requires concurrent resolution of support from JCED within 72 hours
  (or Secretary certifies imminent irreversible harm for 72-hour pre-authorization).
*Criteria:* Tier 2 conditions have escalated; cross-sector cascade is active; estimated
  economic cost exceeds 1% of GDP on annualized basis or DoD readiness is materially impaired.
*Effect:* Emergency reserve fund activated; Phase 2 response packages authorized; all
  pre-built tool deployments within authorized budget envelopes proceed on DED Secretary
  authority; JCED meets in continuous session; weekly presidential briefings required.
*Duration:* 1 year; requires annual reauthorization by concurrent resolution of Congress.

### 4.2 What Activates on Declaration

Each scenario's phase files define the specific pre-authorized activations for Tier 1/2/3
events. The general framework:

| On Tier 1 Declaration | On Tier 2 Declaration | On Tier 3 Declaration |
|-----------------------|-----------------------|-----------------------|
| NEID full monitoring mode | Phase 1 tools deployed | Emergency reserve fund unlocked |
| Type A contract first-call rights on standby | TRAL/DSA/CRHP track activated | Phase 2 tools deployed |
| JCED notification | Type A first-call rights exercised | DPA Title III invoked if needed |
| Strategic reserve assessments | Type B priority contracts activated | CRHP mass mobilization |
| Early warning public disclosure | ATC levy / ARBF activation as applicable | Cross-agency emergency authorities |

### 4.3 Automatic Sunset and Renewal Requirements

Every declaration must include:
- A stated triggering condition and a de-escalation threshold
- A defined maximum duration before mandatory renewal
- JCED notification within 48 hours of any Tier 1 or higher declaration
- IG notification within 24 hours; IG audit begins at declaration and is due 90 days
  after termination

No declaration can extend beyond 24 months without an act of Congress. The 24-month limit
prevents permanent emergency-mode governance — the most dangerous failure mode of
emergency authority frameworks.

## 5. Constraints and Prohibitions — Anti-Capture Design

### 5.1 The Capture Problem

The DED's greatest long-term risk is not ineffectiveness but capture: the gradual
colonization of the department's contract portfolio by concentrated interests (defense
contractors, agricultural conglomerates, energy companies) whose lobbying resources dwarf
those of the diffuse public interest the DED is designed to serve.

The defense procurement system is the cautionary model. The DoD budget exceeds $800B/year
and is routinely shaped more by Congressional district employment politics and prime
contractor relationships than by actual defense requirements. The DED must be designed
from its founding statute to resist the same dynamics.

### 5.2 Joint Committee on Economic Defense (JCED)

The JCED is a standing joint committee of Congress with oversight authority over all
major DED activities. It is the institutional mechanism that prevents the DED from
becoming an unaccountable executive agency.

**Composition:**
- 12 members: 3 from each chamber's relevant committees (Senate Finance, Senate Armed
  Services; House Ways and Means; House Armed Services), with majority and minority
  representation from each
- Chair rotates between Senate and House majority annually
- Members serve 4-year terms on the committee (staggered)

**Jurisdiction — JCED approval required for:**
- Any single strategic contract above $2B
- Any IP licensing agreement above $500M
- Emergency reserve fund activations
- New scenario additions to the DED scenario library
- Any Tier 2 or Tier 3 declaration extension beyond 180 days
- DED Secretary appointment (confirmation vote)

**JCED reporting requirements from DED:**
- Quarterly: contract portfolio summary; NEID economic threat dashboard
- Annual: full audit results; contract performance review; scenario library update; NEPI
- Within 48 hours: any Tier 1 or higher declaration; any IP licensing negotiation initiated
- Within 7 days: any contract above $500M; any emergency reserve expenditure

### 5.3 Geographic Distribution Caps

From `tools/strategic-contracts.md`: the DED contract portfolio is subject to
statutory geographic distribution constraints:

- No more than **8% of total contract value** in any single congressional district
- No more than **20% of total contract value** in any single state
- No more than **35% of total contract value** in any single economic sector

These caps prevent the DED from becoming a regional economic development slush fund
channeled through a few high-lobbying-power states.

Exception process: JCED may authorize exceptions above these caps for scenarios where
the relevant infrastructure is geographically concentrated by necessity (e.g., a
Gulf Coast oil restriction response that must concentrate contracts in Louisiana and Texas).
Exceptions require JCED majority vote and public justification filing.

### 5.4 Contract Transparency Requirements

- All DED strategic contracts are listed in a **public contract registry** within 30 days
  of signing, including: contracting party, contract type, value, duration, scenario
  linkage, and expected deliverable
- Classified contracts (limited to Type D ERP components where disclosure would reveal
  strategic capability gaps) require a classified JCED filing and an unclassified public
  notice confirming the contract exists
- Contract performance is publicly reported annually: milestone achievement, cost vs.
  budget, independent evaluation summary

### 5.5 Revolving Door and Lobbying Prohibitions

**Secretary and Under Secretaries:** Cannot join any DED-contracted company or any
registered lobbyist firm representing DED-contracted companies for **5 years** post-service.

**SES contract managers:** Cannot join any company they personally oversaw a contract for
within **3 years** of departure.

**Lobbying blackout:** No registered lobbyist for a company currently under DED contract
may participate in any DED procurement advisory body, scenario advisory panel, or
JCED hearing as a paid witness.

**JCED members:** Cannot receive honoraria, speaking fees, or campaign contributions
from any DED-contracted company during their committee tenure and for 2 years post-service.

### 5.6 Scenario Linkage Requirement

Every DED contract — without exception — must include a published linkage to at least
one scenario in the DED scenario library explaining:
- Which scenario the contract supports
- Which phase of that scenario the deliverable serves
- What specific capability gap the contract addresses

Contracts without a documented scenario linkage cannot be signed. This requirement prevents
the DED from funding economically arbitrary projects wrapped in national security language —
the most common failure mode of defense-adjacent agencies.

The scenario library itself is public, maintained on a public registry updated no less
than annually, and subject to JCED approval for additions. Scenarios cannot be retroactively
added to justify contracts already signed.

### 5.7 Mandatory External Evaluation

- All Accelerator programs and Type B contracts above $1B must contract with an independent
  evaluator at program inception — not at completion
- Evaluator is selected from a DED-managed roster of qualified research universities and
  independent policy research organizations (no for-profit contractors)
- Evaluator receives access to program data and produces a public final report
- DED may not use the same evaluator for the same program type for more than 2 consecutive cycles

### 5.8 GAO Mandatory Audit Schedule

The Government Accountability Office has standing authority (not requiring congressional
request) to audit:
- The full DED contract portfolio on a rolling 5-year cycle
- Any emergency declaration and its activations within 1 year of termination
- The NEID economic intelligence function every 3 years
- JCED compliance annually

GAO findings are transmitted simultaneously to the JCED, OMB, and the DED IG.
The DED Secretary must respond in writing to all GAO findings within 90 days.

## 6. The Independence Doctrine

The DED must function across administrations with different economic philosophies.
A Democratic administration may prioritize climate transition investments; a Republican
administration may prioritize energy independence and manufacturing reshoring. Both are
legitimate DED applications. Neither should be able to repurpose the DED's contract
portfolio toward purely political objectives.

The independence doctrine is maintained through three mechanisms:

**Career staff continuity:** The 3,500+ career staff, including all SES contract managers
and scenario analysts, carry institutional knowledge across transitions. Political appointees
set direction; career staff maintain the portfolio. Scenario library changes require
JCED approval — a political appointee cannot unilaterally delete a scenario that was
added through the proper process.

**Contract term locking:** Type A and B contracts run 5–20 years. Once signed, they cannot
be cancelled without paying a substantial termination fee (at least 50% of remaining contract
value for no-fault termination). This prevents a new administration from simply canceling
the predecessor's strategic investments to reallocate to different political priorities.
The termination fee creates a fiscal deterrent; cancellation can happen but has a price.

**Scenario library as constitutional constraint:** Because every contract must link to a
public scenario, the only way to eliminate a contract class is to remove the scenario from
the library — which requires JCED approval. A scenario cannot be removed from the library
if it has active contracts. This creates a chain of accountability: scenarios → contracts →
capability; no link can be broken without the others being resolved first.
