# Department of Economic Defense — Organizational Charter and Mission

**Document Type:** Legislative and structural proposal
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Related Documents:** `ded/proposal/one-pager.md`, `ded/proposal/executive-summary.md`

## Purpose of This Document

This document defines the DED's formal organizational charter: its legal authority,
statutory mandate, internal structure, budget framework, relationship to existing agencies,
and the legislative vehicle through which it would be established.

This is the document that answers the question a congressional staffer or OMB analyst
asks first: "What law creates this agency, what does it specifically authorize, and what
does it prohibit?" The one-pager makes the case for the DED. This document defines exactly
what the DED is and is not authorized to do.

  - Trade Expansion Act § 232 — national security import restrictions

  - National Emergencies Act — emergency declaration framework
- New authorities the enabling legislation must create:
  - DED strategic contract authority (distinct from FAR procurement)
  - CRHP military reserve designation for civilian enrollees
  - DED emergency declaration authority (triggering pre-authorized response packages)
  - JCED joint congressional committee (statutory creation)
  - IP Asset Register classification authority
- Authorities the DED explicitly does NOT receive:
  - No authority to ration goods (remains with FEMA/states)
  - No authority to set prices (remains with FTC/market)
  - No authority to compel private sector action outside of contract terms

### 3. Budget Framework

Estimated annual budget in steady state: $60–80B. This needs to be broken down into:
- Standing contract portfolio (Type A/B/C) — estimated $30–40B/year
- Research coordination and national laboratory programs — $10–15B/year
- CRHP administration and deployment — $5–8B/year
- Economic intelligence and monitoring systems — $2–3B/year
- Administrative and oversight — $2–3B/year
- Emergency reserve fund (pre-authorized; activated by declaration) — $50–100B contingency

The budget argument: DED spending is largely offset by Type B contract IP revenue,
licensing income, and averted crisis costs. This document needs a 10-year budget model
showing net cost vs. projected Category A (loss avoidance) and Category B (value creation) returns.

### 5. Constraints and Prohibitions — Anti-Capture Design

*TODO: Full design*

The DED must be designed to resist the same political economy forces that turned the
defense procurement system into a pork-barrel mechanism. The key constraints:
- No authority without scenario linkage: every contract must be traceable to a
  published scenario in the DED scenario library
- JCED oversight: all major contracts require congressional committee approval
- Geographic distribution caps (from strategic-contracts.md: 8% per district, 20% per state)
- Cooling-off periods and lobbying prohibitions
- Public contract registry
- GAO mandatory audit schedule
- Term limits for DED Secretary (serve no more than 8 years; cannot move to a DED-contracted
  company for 5 years post-service)
