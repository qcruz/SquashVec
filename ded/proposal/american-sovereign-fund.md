## 1. Core Concept

### What the American Sovereign Fund Is

The **American Sovereign Fund (ASF)** is a permanently capitalized national investment
vehicle, funded by designated revenue streams from arms sales, tariffs, and taxes, that:

1. **Invests** in U.S. companies and strategic assets — with a mandate that combines
   commercial return-seeking with DED strategic sector priorities
2. **Distributes** monthly dividends to every American citizen who has held citizenship for
   18 or more years — interest and dividends earned by the fund divided equally among all
   eligible participants and paid the first of each month, beginning with the fund's first
   year of operation
3. **Compounds** over decades — the longer it operates, the larger the monthly payment,
   creating a growing permanent income supplement for every eligible American and an expanding
   capital base for DED strategic investment

The ASF is **not** a government spending program. It does not disburse grants, run agencies,
or allocate contracts through bureaucratic processes. It is an investment fund — governed by
professional asset managers with a legislated mandate, operating at arm's length from Congress
and the executive — that happens to be owned by the American public.

### What Makes the ASF Distinct from Prior Proposals

Several sovereign wealth fund proposals have circulated in U.S. policy circles. The ASF
differs from all of them in three ways:

**1. Revenue linkage — not appropriations.** The ASF is funded by dedicated revenue streams
(arms sales proceeds, tariff revenue, tax allocation) that flow automatically without annual
congressional appropriation. This insulates the fund from the budget cycle that kills every
long-horizon government investment program.

**2. Strategic investment mandate — not pure returns.** The ASF is required to allocate 25%
of its capital to DED-designated strategic sectors (semiconductors, rare earth processing,
pharmaceutical manufacturing, energy infrastructure, advanced materials). These investments
serve the national resilience function even if their commercial return is below market — the
insurance value makes up the difference. The remaining 75% is managed for maximum risk-adjusted
commercial return.

**3. Universal dividend — not government spending.** The distribution mechanism is an investment
return, not a transfer payment. This distinction is both economic (it does not expand the
deficit) and political (it is not "welfare" — it is a dividend on national assets the recipient
already owns as a citizen).

## 2. Funding Mechanisms

### 2.1 Arms Sales Revenue Share

**Mechanism:** A fixed percentage of gross U.S. defense export revenue — across both Foreign
Military Sales (FMS, government-to-government) and Direct Commercial Sales (DCS, manufacturer
direct) — is remitted to the ASF upon contract execution.

**Scale:**

| FY | FMS Volume | DCS Volume | Total Arms Sales | 5% ASF Share | 10% ASF Share |
|----|-----------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|--------------|
| 2020 | $50.8B | ~$124B | ~$175B | $8.8B | $17.5B |
| 2021 | $34.8B | ~$103B | ~$138B | $6.9B | $13.8B |
| 2022 | $53.8B | ~$100B | ~$154B | $7.7B | $15.4B |
| 2023 | $80.9B | ~$157B | **~$238B** | **$11.9B** | **$23.8B** |
| 2024 | ~$95B (est.) | ~$170B | ~$265B | $13.3B | $26.5B |

*FMS data: Defense Security Cooperation Agency annual reports. DCS data: State Department.*

**Proposed rate:** 7.5% — splitting the difference between a token contribution and a
significant levy. At FY2024 volumes, this generates **~$20B/year** and grows with arms sales.

**DED rationale — military cost linkage in reverse:** Every allied arms sale strengthens
allied military capability, reducing the cost to the U.S. of defending those allies. The ASF
share converts this geopolitical benefit into direct domestic economic benefit — ensuring that
the citizens who fund the defense industrial base receive a return on the national security
investments that make U.S. arms competitive globally.

**Political structure:** Arms manufacturers pay the 7.5% levy at contract execution, not at
delivery. The levy is treated as a cost of doing business — incorporated into contract pricing
with allies. This means allied governments are effectively co-funding the ASF through the
pricing of the arms they purchase: U.S. defense exports generate both security for allies
and capital for U.S. citizens.

**Anti-gaming provision:** The arms sales percentage applies to gross contract value, not net
margin, and includes offset arrangements, licensed production, and technology transfer
agreements at imputed market value. This prevents structuring transactions to minimize the levy.

### 2.2 Tariff Revenue Share

**Mechanism:** A fixed percentage of all U.S. customs and tariff revenue is automatically
deposited into the ASF on a monthly basis, before any congressional appropriation process.

**Scale:**

| FY | Total Tariff Revenue | 15% ASF Share | 25% ASF Share |
|----|---------------------|-------------|--------------|
| 2019 (pre-trade war) | $71B | $10.7B | $17.8B |
| 2020 | $68B | $10.2B | $17.0B |
| 2022 | $80B | $12.0B | $20.0B |
| 2023 | $80B | $12.0B | $20.0B |
| 2025 (post-escalation, est.) | $150–300B | $22.5–45B | $37.5–75B |

**Proposed rate:** 20% of tariff revenue. At current volumes: **$16–60B/year** depending on
trade policy. At the elevated tariff regime likely to persist post-2025: the upper end of
this range becomes the baseline.

**DED rationale — trade protection generates domestic capital:** Tariffs are a tax on imports
that redistributes income from consumers (who pay higher prices) to the federal government.
The ASF share converts this redistribution into capital formation — the protection premium
paid by consumers funds investment in the domestic industries that protection is meant to
strengthen. This closes the loop between trade policy intent and trade policy outcome: tariffs
on Chinese semiconductors fund investment in domestic semiconductor capacity; tariffs on
Chinese steel fund domestic steel infrastructure investment.

**Countercyclical property:** Tariff revenue is highest during trade tensions — precisely
when the U.S. most needs domestic capital formation to build strategic alternatives to the
imports being restricted. The tariff-to-ASF pipeline is automatically countercyclical
relative to import dependency: as dependency is weaponized and tariffs rise, ASF capital
accumulation accelerates.

### 2.3 Dedicated Tax Allocation

**Mechanism:** Three dedicated tax streams feed the ASF annually, separate from tariff revenue:

**Stream A — Technology Automation Tax (TAT):**
A 0.5% excise tax on enterprise software license revenue and automated transaction processing
fees for companies with >$1B in annual revenue. This is the DED's Automation Tax Credit
(ATC) mechanism, with a portion directed to the ASF rather than the general displacement
benefit fund.

*Scale:* Enterprise software market >$1B threshold: ~$800B/year in U.S. revenues.
0.5% excise = **~$4B/year** growing at ~12%/year with software market.

**Stream B — Financial Transaction Surcharge:**
A 0.01% (1 basis point) surcharge on U.S. equity and derivatives trades above $1M notional.
This is not a Robin Hood tax — it is calibrated to be invisible to retail investors and
absorb into bid-ask spreads at institutional volumes.

*Scale:* U.S. equity market average daily volume ~$500B/day → ~$125T/year.
0.01% of $125T = **~$12.5B/year.**
Derivatives add another $5–8B. Total: **~$17–20B/year.**

**Stream C — Resource Extraction Royalty Enhancement:**
An additional 3% royalty on federal land oil, gas, and mineral extraction above existing rates,
deposited directly to the ASF. This mirrors the Alaska model: resource extraction on public
land generates public capital, not just current government revenue.

*Scale:* Federal land oil/gas royalties currently ~$14B/year.
Additional 3% on extraction value (~$200B/year): **~$6B/year.**

**Stream D — Consumption and Sales Tax Dividend Loop:**
A 5% share of the federal excise tax base (alcohol, tobacco, gasoline, air travel, phone
service) plus, by negotiated revenue-sharing agreement, a 1% share of participating states'
general sales tax collections, remitted monthly to the ASF.

*Scale:* Federal excise taxes: ~$85B/year. 5% share = **~$4.3B/year.**
State sales tax collections: ~$600B/year. Participating states at 1%: **~$3–6B/year** depending
on opt-in. Full national participation: ~$6B/year.

*Rationale — the circulation loop:* ASF monthly dividends are distributed to eligible citizens
who spend them in the domestic economy. That spending generates sales and excise tax revenue.
Routing a share of that revenue back to the ASF closes the loop: the fund's own distributions
partially self-fund the next month's distributions. As the fund grows and dividends increase,
the circulation revenue grows proportionally — a built-in, automatic growth stabilizer.

*State participation incentive:* States that opt in to the 1% revenue share receive Priority
Access Agreements with DED Strategic Reserve investments targeting their jurisdictions, access
to pooled Financial Institution Partnerships procurement at federal contract rates, and early
Civilian Reserve and Health Program deployment priority. Participation is voluntary but
economically attractive.

**Stream E — Capital Gains Tax Surtax Allocation:**
An additional 0.5% surtax on net long-term capital gains above $100,000, deposited to the
ASF. Structured as a legislated add-on to the existing capital gains rate, not a replacement
of existing revenue.

*Scale:* Reported long-term capital gains in the U.S. run $1.5–2.0T/year in high-return
periods. 0.5% surtax on gains above $100k threshold (roughly top quintile of capital
gains recipients): **~$5–8B/year.**

*Rationale:* As the ASF grows and its Strategic Reserve investments appreciate, capital gains
are realized across the broader economy. The surtax routes a fraction of that wealth creation
back to the fund that helped generate it — capturing a portion of the market appreciation
that ASF-catalyzed domestic investment produces.

**Stream F — Estate and Inheritance Tax Enhancement:**
An increase of 2 percentage points on the federal estate tax rate above the current exemption
threshold ($13.6M per individual), with all additional revenue deposited to the ASF rather
than the general fund.

*Scale:* Federal estate tax currently generates ~$35B/year. A 2pp rate increase above the
existing threshold on estates above $25M: **~$8–12B/year** additional, depending on estate
size distribution.

*Rationale — generational equity:* The ASF distributes wealth to living citizens every month.
The estate surtax ensures that the concentrated wealth accumulation of one generation
contributes to the fund that benefits all citizens of the next. The ASF both builds wealth
(through dividends and strategic investment) and moderates extreme intergenerational
concentration (through the estate surtax that helps fund it).

**Combined dedicated tax stream: ~$52–72B/year, growing with software markets, resource
prices, consumer spending, capital markets, and estate values.**

## 3. Investment Mandate

### 3.1 The Two-Tranche Structure

**Tranche 1 — Strategic Reserve (25% of AUM):**
Invested exclusively in DED-designated strategic sectors. Returns are expected to be below
market (these are resilience investments, not alpha-seeking investments), but the combined
commercial return plus insurance value exceeds market-rate investments when properly accounted.

*Strategic Reserve allocation priorities (ordered):*
1. **Domestic semiconductor manufacturing** — equity stakes in U.S.-operated fabs; convertible
   debt for new advanced node capacity; minority positions in TSMC-US, Intel Foundry, Samsung US
2. **Critical minerals processing** — rare earth, lithium, cobalt, graphite processing facilities;
   allied nation processing capacity (Australia, Canada, Greenland) via Type B strategic contracts
3. **Pharmaceutical manufacturing** — API production facilities; strategic stockpile management
   companies; biologics manufacturing capacity
4. **Energy infrastructure** — advanced nuclear (SMR developers), grid modernization,
   strategic petroleum and LNG facilities, offshore wind
5. **Advanced materials** — gallium, germanium, indium, SiC production; REE-free magnet
   manufacturers; high-temperature alloy producers
6. **Defense industrial base** — equity positions in Tier 2/3 defense suppliers (not prime
   contractors) that provide critical components with no commercial alternative

*Target return:* 3.0–4.5% (insurance value makes up the gap to market rate)
*Cap per sector:* 5% of Tranche 1 AUM in any single company; 40% in any single sector

**Tranche 2 — Commercial Portfolio (75% of AUM):**
Invested for maximum risk-adjusted return using standard institutional asset management
practices. Broadly diversified across U.S. equities, bonds, real assets, and private equity.
Professional asset managers selected by competitive RFP; performance benchmarked against
S&P 500 and peer sovereign wealth funds.

*Constraints:*
- Minimum 60% U.S.-domiciled companies (not required to be U.S.-incorporated — revenue source)
- No investments in companies on OFAC sanctions lists or Chinese military-linked entity lists
- ESG screening optional (left to managers) — the mandate is return, not moral portfolio construction
- No leverage at the fund level (individual investment vehicles may use asset-level leverage)

*Target return:* 6.0–7.5% (market rate; competitive with Norway GPF and Yale Endowment)

**Blended return target: ~5.5% on total AUM.**

### 3.2 Investment Governance

**Board of Trustees:** 9 members
- 3 appointed by President (confirmed by Senate), staggered 6-year terms
- 3 appointed by Congress (1 House majority, 1 House minority, 1 Senate majority)
- 3 independent trustees selected by existing board members from a pre-qualified pool
- No more than 5 trustees from the same political party at any time
- No trustee may have been a registered lobbyist, senior government official, or campaign
  contributor above $10,000 within 4 years of appointment

**CEO / Chief Investment Officer:** Professional appointment by board; not a political appointment.
Compensation set at competitive institutional asset management rates (~$2–5M/year).
Fixed 5-year term; removal only for cause by supermajority board vote.

**Investment Committee:** 12-person committee of investment professionals; 9 external
(from pension funds, endowments, sovereign wealth peers); 3 internal (CIO direct reports).
No trustee sits on the Investment Committee — governance and investment decisions separated.

**Congressional oversight:** Annual report to JCED (Joint Committee on Economic Defense);
GAO audit every 3 years; Inspector General embedded; no line-item direction of investments
by Congress (fund is insulated from political investment direction by charter).

**Anti-politicization provisions:**
- Investment decisions may not be conditioned on companies' political donations, lobbying
  activities, or positions on legislation
- No trustee or senior staff may communicate with a member of Congress or their staff about
  specific investment decisions (only aggregate portfolio information permitted)
- The Strategic Reserve allocation is determined by the DED's NEID analysis, not political
  designation — sectors qualify by measured CRS score, not congressional earmark

## 4. Distribution Mechanism — The American Dividend

### 4.1 Eligibility

**Eligible recipients:** U.S. citizens who have held citizenship continuously for 18 or more years.

**Natural-born citizens:** Eligible beginning at age 18. First monthly payment in the month
following their 18th birthday.

**Naturalized citizens:** Eligible 18 years after the date of naturalization. Incentivizes
long-term integration and creates a meaningful distinction between citizenship and permanent
residency without creating permanent exclusion.

**Incarceration rule:** Dividend suspended during incarceration for felony convictions;
resumes upon release. The dividend is not forfeited permanently — it is deferred during
the period of incarceration.

**Residency requirement:** No residency requirement for receipt — diaspora citizens receive
the dividend. However, dividends paid to citizens resident abroad for 5+ consecutive years
are reduced by 50% (domestic residency premium).

**Ineligibility:** Citizens who formally renounce citizenship forfeit future dividends.
There is no clawback of prior dividends received before renunciation.

---

### 4.2 Monthly Dividend Calculation

The American Dividend is paid monthly, beginning in the first full calendar month after
the ASF's initial capitalization. There is no accumulation phase — the fund distributes
its earned income from day one.

**What is distributed monthly:**

```
Monthly American Dividend per Eligible Citizen =
  (ASF Yield Income for Month) / (Number of Eligible Citizens as of 1st of month)
  minus Operating expenses (target: < 0.10% of AUM annualized)

Yield Income = interest received on fixed income + dividends received on equity holdings
             + net rental and real asset income
             (approximately 2.5% of AUM annually, distributed monthly)

Capital Appreciation = unrealized and realized gains RETAINED in the fund
  (approximately 3% of AUM annually; not distributed; compounds into fund value)

New Contributions = annual revenue streams (arms, tariffs, tax allocations)
  ADDED to AUM monthly, not distributed; compounds into fund value
```

**Why this structure sustains growth:**
Interest and dividends are cash income the fund earns without selling positions. Distributing
yield while retaining capital appreciation means the fund's asset base grows continuously
from both retained appreciation and new annual contributions — while recipients receive
meaningful monthly payments from Year 1. This mirrors how endowment universities operate:
Harvard distributes ~5% of AUM annually while the fund still grows, because new gifts and
retained gains exceed distributions.

**The monthly dividend is small in the early years but immediately universal.** Every
eligible American receives their first deposit within the fund's first year of operation —

The framing is correct: not a poverty program, but a citizen dividend on national assets
owned collectively. A $0.68/month check in Year 1 is not life-changing. A $58/month check
in Year 30, arriving reliably on the first of every month for life, is meaningful income.

**Circulation effect on Streams D, E, F:**
As monthly dividends increase, downstream consumer spending increases → sales tax and
excise revenue increases → Stream D contributions increase → ASF receives more capital →
fund grows faster → next year's yield is larger. The system is self-reinforcing. At Year 20,
estimated Stream D contributions from dividend circulation alone exceed $12B/year —
partially funding the dividends that generate them.

### 4.3 Distribution Infrastructure — Financial Institution Partnership Accounts

The ASF monthly dividend is delivered through the Financial Institution Partnerships
(FIP) infrastructure. Rather than building a parallel government payment system, the ASF
leverages the geographic account network established by FIP to distribute dividends to
every eligible citizen through their existing or newly opened FIP-serviced account.

**The institutional account competition:**

Every eligible American's monthly dividend — beginning in Year 1 — represents a regular
deposit into a financial institution account. At scale:

- ~245M eligible citizens × $8/year in Year 1 = $2B in annual deposits flowing through FIP accounts
- ~245M eligible citizens × $112/year in Year 10 = $27B in annual deposits
- ~245M eligible citizens × $695/year in Year 30 = $170B in annual deposits

These deposits are predictable, recurring, and government-guaranteed. For financial
institutions, this represents an extraordinary acquisition opportunity: **millions of new
accounts with guaranteed monthly deposits, at zero marketing cost.**

Under the FIP contract structure, financial institutions compete by geographic zone for the
right to service ASF dividend accounts in their area. Competition terms include:

- **No-fee commitment:** FIP-contracted institutions may not charge monthly service fees,
  minimum balance fees, or overdraft fees on ASF dividend accounts
- **Enhanced account benefits:** Competing institutions offer ASF account holders preferential
  rates on savings products, fee waivers on additional banking services, and access to
  financial literacy tools — as inducements to win and retain the accounts
- **Account bundling:** Citizens who hold their ASF dividend account with a FIP-contracted
  institution may also access Negative Income Tax Tax Return Advance Loan distributions,
  Civilian Reserve and Health Program compensation, and other DED program payments through
  the same account — making a single FIP-serviced account the primary financial relationship
  for millions of Americans
- **Deposit minimum guarantees:** FIP contracts with ASF addendum commit the institution to
  maintain ASF dividend account holders' access to deposits within 24 hours of scheduled
  payment, with penalty provisions for delayed access

**Why institutions compete aggressively:**

A financial institution servicing 8 million ASF dividend accounts holds a guaranteed
relationship with 8 million citizens who receive regular deposits for life. Even if the
initial deposit is small ($0.68/month in Year 1), the long-term value of the account
relationship — cross-sell of savings, lending, insurance products to an account holder
who receives government-guaranteed deposits for the rest of their eligible life — is
substantial. The institution that wins urban FIP zones in Year 1 builds a structural
advantage in retail banking that compounds over 30 years.

**Payment schedule:**

Monthly dividends are calculated as of the 25th of each preceding month and deposited
to FIP accounts on the 1st of the following month. Citizens without an existing FIP
account are enrolled automatically using IRS records; a default account with the
designated FIP contractor for their geographic zone is created unless the citizen
designates a different FIP-contracted institution.

**Tax treatment:** The dividend is taxable as ordinary income for recipients above the
standard deduction threshold. For low-income recipients, it is tax-free (falls below
deduction threshold). Middle-income recipients pay marginal rates on dividend income.
*This creates a mild progressive structure without means-testing the dividend itself.*

**Integration with Negative Income Tax:** The dividend is income for purposes of the
Negative Income Tax calculation. At the proposed guarantee of $6,200 (see
`universal-basic-income.md`) and a 30% phase-out rate, a $695 annual dividend reduces
the Negative Income Tax benefit by $208 for the lowest-income recipients — meaning the
combined program delivers slightly less to the lowest-income recipients than the
Negative Income Tax alone, but creates universal stake in the fund regardless of income
level.

*Design choice:* The dividend is a **wealth-building instrument for all**, not a poverty
alleviation instrument for few. These are different policy goals and the design reflects
that distinction.

### 4.4 Eligible Vendor Restriction — Years 1–5

During the first five years of the American Dividend program, all monthly distributions
are subject to an Eligible Vendor Restriction: funds may only be spent at categories of
approved domestic vendors and services. This restriction operates identically to the
SNAP/EBT model — payments arrive in the citizen's ASF account and are available
immediately, but the account's debit function is restricted to eligible merchant categories
for the duration of the restriction period.

**Policy rationale:**

The first five years of distribution are also the years when the fund is smallest and
monthly payments are smallest (under $5/month). The economic impact of allowing unrestricted
spending during this period is minimal — but the *certainty* of domestic circulation is
valuable. During the fund's formative years, guaranteed domestic spend:

1. Protects the circulation loop: dividend spend → sales tax → Stream D → back to ASF
2. Prevents early political attacks framing the program as "giving money away to be spent
   on imports" — the restriction makes clear that ASF distributions are a domestic
   economic activation instrument, not a consumption subsidy
3. Builds merchant enrollment infrastructure: businesses that enroll as eligible vendors
   in Years 1–5 have the systems, familiarity, and political stake to defend the program
   when the restriction lifts

**Eligible vendor categories (Years 1–5):**

| Category | Eligible | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| Groceries and food (domestic chains and local) | Yes | All SNAP-eligible food vendors eligible |
| Utilities (electric, gas, water, internet) | Yes | Domestic-headquartered providers |
| Healthcare (copays, pharmacy, dental) | Yes | All licensed U.S. healthcare providers |
| Housing (rent, mortgage payment, HOA) | Yes | Direct payment to domestic landlord/servicer |
| Education (tuition, books, supplies) | Yes | Accredited U.S. institutions |
| Domestic small business (NAICS-coded) | Yes | U.S.-headquartered, <500 employees |
| Public transit / transportation | Yes | U.S. carriers |
| Large domestic retailers | Yes | U.S.-headquartered, full-price domestic goods |
| International travel | No | Restriction period only |
| Foreign-domiciled online merchants | No | Restriction period only |
| Cash withdrawal | No | No cash-out during restriction period |
| Financial products (investment, crypto) | No | Restriction period only |

**Restriction lift (Year 6):**

Beginning in Year 6, the ASF dividend account converts to an unrestricted account. Citizens
may spend their monthly dividend on any legal purchase. The FIP-serviced account operates
as a standard checking account from that point forward.

*The restriction is graduated, not cliff-edged:* In Year 5, a 50% unrestricted option is
introduced — half the monthly dividend remains restricted to eligible vendors, half is
freely spendable. This gives citizens time to adjust spending patterns before full
unrestriction in Year 6.

**Merchant enrollment and ASF Preferred Vendor Network:**

The Eligible Vendor Restriction creates an Enrolled Merchant Network — businesses that
register as ASF-eligible vendors, accept ASF debit payments, and agree to the program
terms (domestic goods sourcing verification, reporting). Enrolled merchants receive:

- Priority listing in ASF account holder-facing apps and directories
- Marketing rights: "ASF Preferred Vendor" designation
- Accelerated DED procurement consideration for ASF-enrolled small businesses (enrollment
  signals domestic sourcing compliance already on record)

After Year 5, the Enrolled Merchant Network persists as a voluntary program with benefits
for continued participation, creating a lasting directory of domestically-verified merchants
that other DED programs can reference.

## 5. Historical Precedents

**AUM (2024):** ~$1.6T — world's largest sovereign wealth fund
**Funding source:** Norwegian state's share of offshore oil revenue; ~$100B/year at peak
**Investment mandate:** Global equities (70%), bonds (25%), real estate (5%); no Norway
  domestic investment (to prevent domestic inflation from fund deployment)
**Distribution:** Fund does NOT pay direct dividends — it funds Norwegian government spending
  via the "fiscal rule": 3% of AUM per year may be used in the national budget
**Annual fiscal rule withdrawal (2024):** ~$48B into a country of 5.5M people = ~$8,700/person
  in implicit public services funded

**Lessons for ASF:**
- Revenue dedication (oil → fund) works; political agreement to not spend the money is essential
- Professional management at arm's length from government produces market-rate returns (6.3% avg)
- The fund's transparency (full public reporting of all holdings) is a political asset, not liability
- The "no domestic investment" rule is Norway-specific (small economy); the U.S. *should* invest
  domestically — that is half the point

**Key difference from ASF:** Norway's fund is a fiscal buffer (saves today's oil revenue for
tomorrow's government spending). The ASF is a **national wealth fund** (converts today's
policy revenue into citizen dividends and strategic investment). The ASF model is closer to
Alaska than Norway.

### 5.2 Alaska Permanent Fund (APF) — The U.S. Precedent

**Founded:** 1976 (constitutional amendment); first dividend paid 1982
**AUM (2024):** ~$80B
**Funding source:** 25% of oil and mineral royalties from state lands, mandated by state constitution
**Investment mandate:** Diversified (50% equities, 23% fixed income, 16% real assets, 11% private)
**Distribution:** Annual Permanent Fund Dividend (PFD) to every Alaska resident for 1+ years
**PFD history:**

| Year | Dividend | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| 1982 | $1,000 | First payment |
| 2000 | $2,072 | Peak (dot-com era returns) |
| 2008 | $2,069 | Gov. Palin energy rebate added: $3,269 total |
| 2015 | $2,072 | Last pre-raid high |
| 2016 | $1,022 | Legislature raided fund for budget gap |
| 2022 | $3,284 | Energy price windfall |
| 2023 | $1,312 | Return to formula |

**Lessons for ASF:**
- A state constitutional mandate protected the fund from political raiding for 40 years
  before the legislature eventually circumvented it — federal charter law must be more robust
- The 1-year residency requirement (Alaska) vs. 18-year citizenship requirement (ASF): Alaska's
  short residency window was gamed by workers who moved to Alaska for one year for the dividend;
  the 18-year citizenship requirement eliminates this gaming almost entirely
- Small PFDs ($1,000–2,000) create enormous political support — voters will defend a fund
  that writes them annual checks with striking intensity
- The fund consistently outperforms when managed by professionals; political interference in
  investment decisions reliably underperforms

**ASF improvement on Alaska model:**
- Federal constitutional authority (harder to raid than state statute)
- Citizenship requirement (not residency) — more durable eligibility
- Yield-only distribution (interest and dividends paid monthly; capital appreciation
  retained): Alaska distributes a fixed percentage of AUM regardless of market conditions,
  creating volatility in annual payments. The ASF distributes actual earned yield, which
  grows steadily with AUM and is less sensitive to annual market swings
- Immediate distribution from Year 1 builds political constituency before amounts are large,
  rather than requiring a silent accumulation phase during which the program can be canceled
- Revenue diversification across six streams (arms/tariffs/taxes/consumption loop/capital
  gains/estate) vs. Alaska's single oil revenue stream — dramatically lower concentration risk
- FIP account infrastructure ensures universal access without government-operated payment
  systems; Alaska mails checks, creating friction for unbanked Alaskans

### 5.3 Singapore — GIC and Temasek Holdings

**GIC (Government Investment Corporation):** Manages Singapore's foreign reserves; ~$770B AUM;
  returns consistently ~7–8%/year over 20-year rolling periods; invests globally.
**Temasek Holdings:** State investment company; ~$290B AUM; invests in domestic and international
  companies including Singapore Airlines, DBS Bank, ST Engineering.

**Key lesson:** Singapore's two-fund model (GIC for returns, Temasek for strategic) maps
directly to the ASF two-tranche design (Commercial Portfolio for returns, Strategic Reserve
for national priorities). Singapore has demonstrated that sovereign investment in domestic
champions — when done with commercial discipline — produces both financial returns and
strategic industrial development.

**Temasek's domestic returns:** Its original investments in Singapore Airlines, PSA Corporation,
Singapore Power, and Singapore Telecommunications are now globally competitive companies,
not nationalized laggards. Strategic investment with commercial governance produces better
outcomes than either pure privatization or pure state ownership.

**ASF application:** Tranche 1's investment in domestic semiconductor fabs, rare earth
processors, and pharmaceutical manufacturers follows the Temasek model — government equity
in strategic industries, managed for commercial return, with a national resilience mandate.

### 5.4 Saudi Arabia PIF and UAE ADIA — Cautionary Notes

**Saudi Public Investment Fund (PIF):** $700B AUM; increasingly directed by political
priorities (LIV Golf, $500M yacht, trophy investments); returns below peers; governance
opaque.

**Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA):** $993B AUM; consistently among top-performing
SWFs; governance insulated from political direction; long-term return focus.

**Lesson:** The PIF model (political direction) is the failure mode. The ADIA model
(professional management, insulated governance) is the success model. The ASF's governance
design (independent trustees, professional CIO, no congressional line-item direction) is
explicitly designed to produce an ADIA rather than a PIF.

## 6. DED Integration Points

### 6.1 The ASF as DED's Permanent Capital Arm

The DED's strategic contract framework (Types A/B/C/D) requires the government to make
long-duration financial commitments: purchase guarantees, loan guarantees, R&D advance
contracts. Currently, these commitments compete annually with discretionary spending in
the budget process — a design flaw that makes every DED program vulnerable to defunding
in years with budget pressure.

The ASF's Strategic Reserve tranche resolves this structurally. DED-designated strategic
sector investments made by the Strategic Reserve are:
- Not subject to annual appropriation (the capital is already committed)
- Commercially structured (equity/debt with return requirements, not grants)
- Self-sustaining (returns fund future investments without new revenue)

**Mechanism:** The DED's NEID analysis identifies a sector with CRS above 0.40 (requiring
investment). DED issues a Strategic Reserve Investment Designation (SRID) specifying the
sector, investment rationale, and return expectations. The ASF Investment Committee treats
the SRID as a mandatory allocation trigger — it must deploy within 18 months or report to
JCED with explanation. This makes DED strategic analysis directly actionable through the ASF
rather than dependent on ad hoc appropriations.

---

### 6.2 Revenue Linkage Creates Automatic DED Funding

The arms sales levy and tariff share have a compounding DED-rationale:

**Arms sales → ASF → DED strategic investment → lower cost of military readiness:**
1. U.S. sells F-35s to ally
2. 7.5% of contract value flows to ASF
3. ASF Strategic Reserve invests in domestic titanium processing (F-35 supply chain)
4. Domestic titanium processing reduces F-35 unit cost
5. Lower F-35 cost means allied nation buys more F-35s
6. More F-35 sales → more ASF contributions → more domestic investment

This is a virtuous cycle: defense exports fund domestic industrial capacity that makes
defense exports more competitive.

**Tariffs → ASF → domestic alternatives → reduced import dependency → lower CRS:**
1. U.S. imposes tariffs on Chinese rare earth magnets
2. 20% of tariff revenue flows to ASF
3. ASF Strategic Reserve invests in domestic REE processing
4. Domestic REE processing reduces import dependency
5. Lower import dependency reduces CRS
6. Lower CRS means DED can reduce tariff barrier (transition from protection to competitive position)

The tariff-to-ASF pipeline is explicitly designed to be temporary: tariffs as a tool for
building domestic capacity, not as permanent trade barriers. As domestic capacity matures,
tariff barriers can be reduced — at which point the ASF's domestic investment returns
replace the protection income.

---

### 6.3 The Dividend as Political Defense for DED Programs

Every DED program faces political economy risks: constituencies that benefit from the
status quo (importers of cheap Chinese inputs, free-trade ideologues, deficit hawks) will
lobby against the investments that reduce supply chain vulnerability. The ASF dividend
creates a countervailing constituency of overwhelming size — and it begins creating that
constituency in Year 1.

**From the first month of operation:**
- Every eligible American (235M+ people) receives a monthly deposit, however small
- Every eligible American has a direct financial stake in the ASF's continued growth and
  the DED programs that feed the Strategic Reserve
- Cutting DED strategic investment programs reduces ASF returns → reduces dividends →
  costs every American money, visibly, the following month
- This is the same political dynamic that has protected Social Security and Medicare for
  80+ years: universal benefit creates universal constituency

**The immediate distribution model is politically superior to deferred distribution:**
A program that writes everyone a check in Year 1 — even a small one — is defended from
Year 1. A program that promises checks starting in Year 15 can be canceled in Year 8
by a different political majority, because no constituency has received anything yet.
The $0.68/month first-year payment is not economically significant. Its political
significance is substantial: it is proof of concept, visible to every eligible American,
arriving in their FIP account on the first of the month.

**The dividend is DED's political moat.** It converts a program that most Americans have
no direct stake in (supply chain resilience, strategic materials investment) into a program
that every American receives a check from — beginning the month the fund opens.

### 6.4 ASF Investment and DED Scenario Response

In a declared DED economic emergency (Tier 2 or 3), the ASF's Strategic Reserve can be
partially converted to emergency operational capital:

**Emergency deployment authority:** Up to 10% of the Strategic Reserve (2.5% of total AUM)
may be deployed as emergency bridge capital for DED Type A/B/C contracts without new
congressional authorization, subject to:
- Active Tier 2+ DED emergency declaration
- JCED notification within 72 hours
- Repayment obligation from emergency response contracts within 24 months
- Trustee supermajority vote (7 of 9)

This gives the DED a **pre-authorized liquidity facility** of ~$15–50B at fund maturity,
deployable without congressional action in the first 72 hours of a crisis — the window
when speed of commitment matters most.

## 7. Constitutional and Legislative Framework

### 7.1 Constitutional Authority

**Commerce Clause (Art. I, §8, cl. 3):** Broad authority to regulate interstate and foreign
commerce; tariff revenue and arms sales proceeds are within this authority.

**Spending and Taxing Power (Art. I, §8, cl. 1):** Dedicated revenue allocations (arms levy,
tariff share, tax streams) are constitutional exercises of taxing and spending power as long
as they are tied to a general welfare purpose — national economic security clearly qualifies.

**Property Rights concern:** The citizenship dividend does not raise Fifth Amendment concerns
because it is not a taking — it is a distribution of returns on government-managed national
assets. Analogy: the federal government distributes payments from federal timber sales; this
is not a constitutional "taking from" anyone.

**Precedent:** The Alaska Permanent Fund and its dividend have been constitutionally unchallenged
in state courts for 40+ years. Federal analogs exist (federal royalty payments, tribal distributions,
Social Security as a self-funding return on contributions). The ASF dividend is legally
analogous to all of these.

---

### 7.2 Enabling Legislation — American Sovereign Fund Act

**Key provisions:**

**Section 1 — Establishment:** Creates the ASF as an independent federal instrumentality,
similar in structure to the Federal Reserve (not a traditional government agency; not a
private entity; a hybrid with specific statutory authorities and constraints).

**Section 2 — Revenue dedication:** Mandatory deposit of arms sales levy, tariff share, and
designated tax streams. Establishes that these deposits are **not subject to sequestration,
rescission, or impoundment** — the primary mechanisms by which Congress raids dedicated funds.

**Section 3 — Investment mandate:** The two-tranche structure, Strategic Reserve allocation
requirements, DED SRID mechanism, and prohibited investments.

**Section 4 — Governance:** Board structure, trustee qualifications and disqualifications,
Investment Committee structure, CIO appointment, anti-politicization provisions.

**Section 5 — Distribution formula:** Eligibility, calculation methodology, distribution
rate schedule, payment infrastructure, tax treatment.

**Section 6 — Emergency deployment:** Tier 2+ declaration authority; JCED notification;
repayment obligation; trustee supermajority requirement.

**Section 7 — Oversight:** JCED annual reporting; GAO triennial audit; Inspector General;
real-time public portfolio disclosure (all holdings above $100M published quarterly).

**Constitutional protection mechanism:** The enabling legislation includes a **self-entrenching
supermajority provision** — any amendment to Sections 1–5 (the core fund structure) requires
a 2/3 majority of both chambers. This makes political raiding of the fund structurally harder
than a simple majority vote, while stopping short of a constitutional amendment requirement.

## 9. Political Economy Analysis

### 9.1 Who Supports — and Why

**Universal constituency from Month 1:** Every eligible American citizen has financial
interest in the fund's success from the moment of first distribution. $695/year at Year 30
is not life-changing — but it is politically meaningful, visible, and universally received.
More importantly: the $8/year first-year payment creates political ownership long before
the amounts are significant. History shows that even modest universal benefits create fierce
political constituencies (Social Security's early $22/month payments; Alaska PFD's
$1,000/year). The immediate distribution model activates that dynamic from Year 1 rather
than requiring 15 years of zero-distribution patience.

**Defense industry:** Mixed — the 7.5% arms levy reduces margin, but the fund's Strategic
Reserve investment in the defense industrial base returns capital to the same companies.
Net effect on prime contractors: modest negative. Net effect on Tier 2/3 suppliers:
significant positive (ASF investment in component manufacturers currently chronically
undercapitalized).

**Domestic manufacturing:** Strongly supportive — Tranche 1 targets exactly the sectors
asking for industrial policy support. ASF is industrial policy with a return requirement.

**Free-trade advocates:** Initially resistant. Long-term neutral — the tariff-to-domestic-
capacity pipeline explicitly plans for tariff reduction once domestic alternatives mature.
The end state is competitive domestic industry, not permanent protection.

**Deficit hawks:** Supportive — the ASF is entirely self-funded from dedicated revenue
streams. It does not add to the deficit. At fund maturity it generates returns exceeding
operating costs, potentially becoming a net contributor to federal revenue.

---

### 9.2 Political Risk Factors and Mitigations

**Risk 1: Congressional raiding.** Legislature votes to use ASF capital for unrelated
spending in a budget crisis.
*Mitigation:* Sequestration immunity; supermajority amendment requirement; public
disclosure of all holdings (makes raiding visible and politically costly).

**Risk 2: Investment politicization.** Administration directs ASF to make politically
motivated investments (swing-state factories; friendly company bailouts).
*Mitigation:* CIO is professional appointment removable only for cause; Investment
Committee is independent; SRID mechanism requires DED NEID justification, not political
designation; GAO audit with public reporting.

**Risk 3: Arms sales controversy.** A controversial arms sale generates political backlash
and ASF is associated with it ("we're profiting from selling weapons to X").
*Mitigation:* ASF receives the proceeds regardless of which ally purchases — it is not
a fund that endorses specific sales. The levy structure does not create a financial
incentive for the ASF to lobby for arms sales; the State Department controls arms export
approval independently.

**Risk 4: Distribution gaming.** People seek citizenship solely for the dividend.
*Mitigation:* 18-year citizenship requirement makes this infeasible — naturalization
itself takes 5+ years; then 18 more years before first payment. 23+ years of citizenship
before first dollar.

**Risk 5: Market concentration.** ASF becomes too large and distorts U.S. equity markets.
*Mitigation:* 5% cap per company; 40% cap per sector in Strategic Reserve; Tranche 2 is
globally diversified (no more than 60% U.S.); mandatory size review at $3T, $6T, and $10T
AUM milestones with potential mandate adjustment.
