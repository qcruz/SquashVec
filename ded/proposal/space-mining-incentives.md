## 1. Strategic Rationale

### The Resource Constraint Problem

The United States faces a structural vulnerability across nearly every scenario in the DED
scenario library: critical material supply concentrated in adversarial or unstable nations.
China controls 60–98% of global production and processing for rare earth elements, gallium,
germanium, and graphite. The DRC controls cobalt. The transition to clean energy, advanced
defense systems, and semiconductor manufacturing all *increase* demand for these materials
while supply geography remains concentrated.

Every DED scenario response for rare earth denial, semiconductor collapse, and clean energy
transition ultimately hits the same ceiling: there is a finite amount of these materials in
accessible deposits on Earth, and the accessible deposits are controlled by parties who may
deny, restrict, or price-gouge access.

Space resource extraction offers the only path to effectively infinite supply of the materials
that constrain the 21st-century economy. The asteroid belt contains an estimated:
- **Platinum group metals (PGMs):** More than all known terrestrial reserves combined.
  A single 500-meter M-type (metallic) asteroid can contain more platinum than has been
  mined in all of human history.
- **Nickel and iron:** Structurally unlimited relative to Earth's accessible supply
- **Cobalt:** Near-Earth asteroids contain cobalt concentrations exceeding the richest
  terrestrial deposits
- **Water ice (Lunar and asteroid):** Splittable into hydrogen and oxygen for propellant;
  the resource that makes all other space resource extraction economically feasible

The asteroid 16 Psyche alone — a metallic body 280km across — is estimated to contain
enough iron, nickel, and precious metals to represent $10 quadrillion in material value
at current Earth prices (though bringing that supply to market would obviously change prices).

### The DED Frame: Not Science Fiction, Investment Horizon

This is not a proposal to fund science fiction. It is a proposal to apply the same
investment doctrine the DED applies everywhere else: **pre-build the financial conditions
that make the right long-run behavior rational, before a crisis makes it urgent.**

The technology exists. The legal framework exists. The commercial industry exists — and
is growing at 21.4% CAGR from a $2.05B market in 2025. What is missing is the same
thing that was missing from domestic semiconductor manufacturing before the CHIPS Act:
a government as anchor buyer and co-investor that de-risks the commercial investment
case and ensures the capability develops on a timeline aligned with national security
needs rather than purely commercial returns.

The DED's instrument set — Type B co-investment contracts, Accelerator R&D programs,
anchor buying commitments, IP licensing frameworks — is exactly the toolkit that the
space mining industry needs to move from demonstration missions to commercial scale.

## 2. Legal and Regulatory Foundation

### Existing U.S. Authority

The **U.S. Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act of 2015** (SPACE Act) established
the legal foundation for commercial space resource extraction:

- U.S. citizens engaged in commercial recovery of asteroid or space resources are **entitled
  to own, possess, transport, use, and sell** extracted resources under applicable U.S. law
- The U.S. explicitly does **not** assert sovereignty over celestial bodies — in compliance
  with the Outer Space Treaty — but asserts property rights over extracted resources
- This distinction (property rights in extracted material vs. sovereignty over the body)
  mirrors how maritime law treats fish caught in international waters: you own the fish,
  not the ocean

Several allied nations have passed compatible national legislation: Luxembourg (2017),
UAE (2020), Japan (2021). This creates a nascent allied legal framework for commercial
space resource property rights.

### Gaps the DED Addresses

**Gap 1 — No government anchor buyer for space-extracted materials.**
The private industry faces a circular problem: investors won't fund extraction missions
without committed buyers; buyers won't commit without proven extraction capability.
DED advance purchase commitments break this deadlock — the same mechanism that worked
for Operation Warp Speed vaccines.

**Gap 2 — No IP framework for government co-investment.**
Without a clear IP structure for government co-investment, space mining companies cannot
accept government funding without risking loss of their core technology assets.
DED Type B contracts address this with the same structure used for domestic semiconductor
and clean energy co-investment: government gets a royalty-free license; company retains
commercial rights.

**Gap 3 — No international coordination framework for allied resource sharing.**
Multiple allied nations are investing in space mining independently. Without coordination,
they will develop incompatible standards, compete for the same near-Earth asteroids, and
fail to build the shared infrastructure (propellant depots, transport hubs) that makes
the whole system economically viable.

## 4. DED Program Design

### 4A. Accelerator: Space Resource Extraction

**Structure:** DED establishes a dedicated Space Resource Extraction Accelerator under the
ORNLC office, modeled on the existing Accelerator framework in `tools/expert-reserve-program.md`.

**Mission:** Compress the timeline from demonstration missions to commercial-scale extraction
of priority materials by 10–15 years relative to unassisted commercial development.

**Focus areas (priority order):**
1. **Platinum group metal (PGM) extraction from M-type near-Earth asteroids** — highest
   near-term DED strategic value; directly addresses the rare earth denial scenario
2. **Water ice extraction and electrolysis (ISRU)** — propellant production makes all
   other space mining economically viable; the critical enabling infrastructure
3. **Cobalt and nickel extraction** — battery and defense applications; reduces DRC and
   Chinese refining dependency
4. **Rare earth element concentration from asteroid regolith** — longer-term; REE
   distribution in asteroids is sparser than PGMs but still strategically significant

**Type D contracts with Accelerator participants:**
- AstroForge, ispace, Asteroid Mining Corporation, Karman+ as founding members
- DED contributes $500M–1B/year in co-investment; participants contribute matching R&D
- DED retains royalty-free license on all extraction technologies developed under contract
- IP enters the Asset Register; available for licensing to allied nations as trade instrument

### 4B. Advance Purchase Commitments (APC)

**Mechanism:** DED commits to purchase a defined quantity of space-extracted PGMs,
cobalt, or nickel at a floor price, delivered to U.S. custody upon return to Earth orbit
or Earth surface, for a period of 10 years.

**Floor price:** Set at 90% of the 3-year trailing terrestrial spot price for the
equivalent material. This ensures:
- Space-extracted materials are economically competitive with terrestrial supply from day one
- Government does not pay above-market; the floor only activates if terrestrial prices crash
  due to geopolitical resolution (a good outcome that doesn't need a subsidy)
- Commercial investors have certainty about the minimum revenue of a successful mission

**Priority materials for APC:**
- Platinum: $30–33M/tonne terrestrial (2025); APC at $27–30M/tonne for first 5 years
- Cobalt: $33,000/tonne; APC at $30,000/tonne
- Nickel: $17,000/tonne; APC at $15,000/tonne

**Fiscal exposure:** Contingent. The APC only costs money if the mission succeeds and
delivers material. At current extraction mission economics, DED's APC exposure is
~$500M–2B over 10 years for the first generation of commercial deliveries — a small
premium on the insurance value of supply chain diversification for materials currently
at Chinese geopolitical risk.

### 4C. Strategic Space Resource Reserve

As space-extracted materials begin arriving, DED maintains a **Strategic Space Resource
Reserve (SSRR)** — the space analog to the Strategic Petroleum Reserve:

- Purchased under APC at floor prices; held in secure DED facilities
- Managed separately from terrestrial strategic stockpiles (allows auditing of the
  space vs. terrestrial supply diversification progress)
- Released on Tier 2 rare earth denial or semiconductor collapse declaration
- Reserve targets: 6-month supply of PGMs for DoD and semiconductor fabrication;
  3-month supply of cobalt for EV battery production

### 4D. International Space Mining Framework

**Allied coordination instrument:**
DED leads the U.S. negotiating position on an **International Space Resource Framework
(ISRF)** — a multilateral agreement among SPACE Act-compatible nations (U.S., Luxembourg,
UAE, Japan; and prospective: EU, UK, Australia) governing:

1. **Resource zone coordination:** Near-Earth asteroid assignment for commercial missions
   to prevent competitive conflict over the same targets
2. **Infrastructure sharing:** Lunar propellant depot and transport hub standards enabling
   interoperability; no allied nation builds a propellant depot that only one country's
   ships can dock with
3. **Material repatriation:** Agreement that space-extracted materials from allied missions
   are available to allied partners at contract prices during declared supply emergencies —
   the space analog to the IEA emergency oil sharing mechanism
4. **IP and technology sharing:** Framework for controlled sharing of extraction
   technology between allied programs; ITAR-compatible structure

**DED leverage:** U.S. leads in launch capability (SpaceX, ULA, Blue Origin), space law
(first mover with SPACE Act), and military space assets. The ISRF gives allied nations
legal clarity and U.S. logistical support in exchange for material-sharing commitments.

## 5. Scenario Impact — How This Reduces Severity Over Time

| Scenario | Impact of Mature Space Mining Capability | Timeline |
|----------|------------------------------------------|---------|
| **Rare Earth Denial** | PGMs, cobalt, nickel available from space supply independent of China/DRC — eliminates the leverage of terrestrial denial for these categories | 10–20 years |
| **Semiconductor Collapse** | Gallium and germanium are found in asteroid regolith in higher concentrations than most terrestrial ore; space-sourced supply reduces Chinese gallium/germanium leverage | 15–25 years |
| **Oil Restriction** | PGMs are the key catalyst in hydrogen fuel cells and electrolyzers; space-sourced platinum reduces clean energy transition cost and supply risk | 10–20 years |

**The DED investment thesis:** Every year of compressed timeline to commercial space
mining is a year of reduced exposure to the terrestrial supply vulnerabilities that
drive multiple Tier 2 and Tier 3 scenarios. The investment is not in mining per se —
it is in buying down scenario severity across the entire portfolio.

## 6. Historical Precedents

**DoD as anchor buyer for semiconductors (1960s):** DoD purchased over 60% of U.S.
semiconductor output in the early 1960s, providing the demand certainty that enabled
the industry to scale. Space mining APCs apply the identical logic.

**Operation Warp Speed (2020):** Advance purchase commitments of $10B+ across 6 vaccine
manufacturers enabled parallel development and eliminated the commercial risk that would
have serialized the process. Result: vaccines in 9 months vs. typical 10+ years.

**DARPA high-risk R&D model:** DARPA's willingness to fund technically uncertain programs
with defined national security payoff has produced GPS, the internet, stealth aircraft,
and autonomous vehicles. Space mining sits in the same risk/reward category: technically
uncertain, strategically transformative, requiring government as first-mover.

**SPR (Strategic Petroleum Reserve):** Government-held physical commodity reserve as
buffer against supply disruption. The SSRR applies the same logic to strategic minerals
with the added benefit that space supply is geopolitically independent.

## 7. Risks and Failure Modes

**Technical risk — extraction economics don't close:**
Even with improving launch costs, the energy and mission cost of reaching, extracting,
and returning materials from asteroids may not close commercially within the 10–20 year
horizon. **Mitigation:** DED investment is in the R&D and APC commitment that accelerates
the economics, not in building specific missions. If the economics don't close, the R&D
investment still produces useful technology and the APC costs nothing until materials arrive.

**Political risk — Outer Space Treaty challenge:**
Other nations (Russia, China, some developing nations) contest the legality of commercial
resource extraction under the Outer Space Treaty's "common heritage" language.
**Mitigation:** The ISRF creates a coalition of allied nations operating under a common
legal framework; the U.S. does not require UN consensus to proceed. The property-rights-
in-extracted-materials vs. sovereignty-over-bodies distinction is legally defensible and
growing in international acceptance.

**Market disruption risk — space supply crashes terrestrial prices:**
If space-extracted PGMs arrive at scale, they could crash terrestrial platinum prices,
harming allied mining nations (South Africa, Russia).
**Mitigation:** DED APC floor prices provide a buffer; ISRF can govern pace of market
introduction; allied consultation mechanisms. This is a long-term problem that is
manageable at the timescales involved.

**Concentration risk — one company dominates:**
If a single company (or a single nation's backed company) achieves dominant position in
space mining, the supply chain vulnerability simply moves from terrestrial to space.
**Mitigation:** DED Accelerator contracts with multiple companies; ISRF distributes
capability across allied nations; U.S. antitrust and procurement law applied to SSRR contracts.

## 9. Integration with DED Framework

**IP Asset Register:** All extraction technologies developed under DED Accelerator
contracts enter the IP Asset Register. Space mining IP becomes a diplomatic asset:
the U.S. can license extraction technology to allied nations in exchange for ISRF
participation, material-sharing commitments, or trade concessions in other areas.

**ERP (Expert Reserve Program):** Aerospace engineers, materials scientists, and
space systems specialists from commercial companies and national labs enrolled in the
DED Expert Reserve; available for surge deployment to critical mission phases.

**Standalone value:** Like the Workforce Development Floor and Outcome-Linked Bonds,
the space mining incentive program has standalone value independent of any crisis.
The commercial space economy is growing regardless; DED investment accelerates the
timeline and ensures the strategic materials flow to allied supply chains rather than
being captured by adversarial interests or left to develop without coordination.
