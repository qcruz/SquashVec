## The Problem With How We Talk About These Programs

Every program in the Department of Economic Defense library gets described in the language
of its category. The Negative Income Tax is described as welfare policy. The American
Sovereign Fund dividend is described as a wealth distribution program. The Civilian Reserve
and Health Program is described as a labor program. The Workforce Development Floor is
described as a business regulation.

This framing is both accurate and politically lethal. "Welfare" triggers one set of
constituencies. "Redistribution" triggers another. "Regulation" triggers a third. Each
label activates opposition from a different political coalition, and taken together they
make the Department of Economic Defense look like a liberal wish list dressed up in
national security language.

The programs are not welfare. They are not redistribution. They are not regulation in
the pejorative sense. They are the economic obligations that follow from a serious theory
of what citizenship means — and what the national economy owes to the people who comprise it.

This document makes that theory explicit.

---

## 1. What Citizenship Has Always Been

Citizenship has never been just a legal status. It has always been a contract — a set of
mutual obligations between the individual, the community, and the state.

The obligations run in both directions.

**What citizenship has historically required of individuals:**
- Pay taxes to fund collective goods
- Serve in the military when called
- Obey laws and submit to the legal system
- Participate in civic processes (jury duty, census, elections)

**What citizenship has historically provided to individuals:**
- Physical security (the military protects you)
- Legal protection (courts enforce your rights)
- Basic infrastructure (roads, water, power)
- Access to markets (contract law, currency, property rights)

This contract has been renegotiated continuously throughout American history. The Civil War
amendments expanded it. The New Deal expanded it. The GI Bill expanded it. Every expansion
followed the same logic: as the economy grew more complex, the obligations of citizenship
grew more complex on both sides of the ledger.

The GI Bill is the clearest modern example of the contract working correctly. The nation
asked soldiers to risk their lives. In return, the nation provided education, housing loans,
and healthcare. Nobody called this welfare. It was understood as the return side of an
obligation the soldier had already fulfilled.

The question the Department of Economic Defense answers is: **what does that contract look
like in an economy dominated by automation, supply chain globalization, and AI?**

## 2. The Modern Economic Obligations of Citizenship

The modern economy creates obligations that the current citizenship contract does not
adequately address. Three are most important.

### Obligation 1: Every citizen contributes to the productivity base that makes national wealth possible.

Workers produce goods and services. Consumers buy them. Taxpayers fund collective goods.
Even citizens who are temporarily unemployed, retired, or in school are part of the
economic system — they trained, they paid in, they will pay in again, or they raised
children who will. The national economy is not a product of capital and technology alone.
It is a product of every person who participates in it across their lifetime.

When that economy generates surplus — when productivity grows faster than wages, when
automation multiplies output without multiplying employment — the surplus does not arise
solely from the capital that owns the machines. It arises from the entire social
infrastructure that makes production possible: educated workers, stable communities,
functioning infrastructure, rule of law, democratic stability. These are collective
contributions that no individual firm pays for in full.

**The economic citizenship obligation that follows:** When the economy generates large
productivity surpluses — especially surpluses driven by automation that reduces the
share going to labor — a portion of that surplus belongs to the citizenship base that
made it possible. The Automation Tax Credit captures a fraction of that surplus. The
American Sovereign Fund converts a portion of national policy revenue into citizen-owned
wealth. These are not redistributions from winners to losers. They are the return payment
on a collective investment the citizenry has already made.

### Obligation 2: The economy requires a functioning, educated, healthy workforce — and cannot externalize the cost of producing one.

Every firm that employs a worker receives the benefit of twelve or more years of public
education, public health infrastructure, public safety, and public roads that got that
worker to the door. The firm pays wages for the worker's time. It does not pay for the
twenty years of public investment that made the worker employable.

This is not a moral criticism of firms. It is a description of a collective action problem:
no individual firm can profitably invest in the education and health of a workforce it does
not yet employ. The market systematically underproduces education, training, and workforce
health because the benefits are shared across all employers while the costs must be borne
by someone.

**The economic citizenship obligation that follows:** Firms that benefit from the national
workforce have an obligation to invest back into it. The Workforce Development Floor's
Employer Development Obligation — requiring firms above a threshold size to provide one
percent of their workforce in paid training experiences — is not a regulation in the
pejorative sense. It is the billing mechanism for a service the economy has been consuming
for free. The Mandatory Internship Floor and the Civilian Reserve and Health Program
complete the same logic: those who benefit from the workforce must contribute to sustaining it.

### Obligation 3: Citizens who face economic disruption caused by forces beyond their control have a claim on the economy's resources.

A worker displaced by AI automation did not fail. The economy changed under them. A farmer
ruined by a multi-year drought did not fail. The climate changed under them. A small business
destroyed by a pandemic shutdown did not fail. A public health crisis destroyed the conditions
of their operation.

In each case, the economic disruption is a collective risk — borne by individuals who cannot
individually insure against it, caused by forces that no individual could control, and
manageable only through collective action. The current system responds to these events
after they occur, with improvised programs that arrive too late at the wrong scale. The
Negative Income Tax, the Tax Return Advance Loan, and Business Survival Loans are
pre-built response infrastructure — the economic equivalent of a fire department. The
fire department does not exist to reward failure. It exists because fire is a collective
risk that cannot be individually insured against at the household level.

**The economic citizenship obligation that follows:** The economy owes citizens a floor of
support during disruptions they did not cause. This floor is not charity. It is the return
on the collective risk premium every citizen implicitly pays by participating in an
interdependent economic system. The programs that provide that floor are the insurance
payout, not the welfare check.

## 3. What the Economy Owes Citizens — The Four Floors

Taken together, the obligations above generate four specific economic floors that the
citizenship contract requires:

### Floor 1: Income Floor
Every citizen is entitled to a minimum income that prevents destitution during periods of
economic disruption. This is not a guarantee of comfortable living — it is a guarantee
that participation in the economy does not end in catastrophic personal failure from forces
beyond individual control.

*Department of Economic Defense programs that deliver this:* The Negative Income Tax provides
the permanent structural floor. The Tax Return Advance Loan provides the liquidity bridge
until the floor payment arrives. Stimulus checks provide the emergency surge. Business
Survival Loans extend the floor to small enterprises that are extensions of their owners'
economic participation.

### Floor 2: Opportunity Floor
Every citizen is entitled to a structured pathway into economic participation — not a
guarantee of a specific job, but a guarantee that entry into the economy is not purely
a matter of luck, connections, or the zip code of birth.

*Department of Economic Defense programs that deliver this:* The Mandatory Internship Floor
creates one million structured entry points per year. The Workforce Development Floor's
Employer Development Obligation creates a pipeline of paid training experiences. The
Civilian Reserve and Health Program creates a training and employment pathway for those
who choose national service. The Expert Reserve Program creates pathways for mid-career
professionals to contribute to national challenges while maintaining economic participation.

### Floor 3: Health Floor
Every citizen who contributes to the national economy — through work, through service,
through participation in the civilian reserve — is entitled to access to healthcare without
the threat of medical bankruptcy. A workforce that is medically fragile is an economically
fragile workforce. This is not a moral argument. It is a supply chain argument: health is
an input to economic production, and a supply chain that depends on a fragile input is a
fragile supply chain.

*Department of Economic Defense programs that deliver this:* The Civilian Reserve and Health
Program extends military healthcare eligibility to reserve members and their families,
funded through existing Department of Defense healthcare infrastructure rather than new
government expenditure.

### Floor 4: Wealth Floor
Every citizen who has participated in the national economy across a meaningful duration of
citizenship has a claim on a share of the national wealth that participation helped create.
This is not redistribution of wealth from one citizen to another. It is the distribution
of returns on collectively-produced national assets — the same logic as the Alaska
Permanent Fund dividend, applied nationally.

*Department of Economic Defense programs that deliver this:* The American Sovereign Fund
distributes annual dividends to citizens with eighteen or more years of citizenship. The
dividend begins small and compounds over decades, converting national policy revenue —
arms sales, tariff receipts, resource royalties — into citizen-owned capital. At full
maturity, it provides a meaningful supplement to retirement income for every eligible
American regardless of prior economic status.

## 4. Why This Is Not Left or Right

The theory of economic citizenship described here does not fit neatly into either major
American political tradition — and that is the point.

**The conservative case for economic citizenship:**

Conservatives believe in earned benefits, not unearned entitlements. The economic
citizenship framework is entirely built on earning. The Negative Income Tax was Milton
Friedman's proposal. The American Sovereign Fund dividend is earned by years of citizenship
— not distributed on the basis of need. The Civilian Reserve and Health Program ties
healthcare to service, not to poverty status. The Workforce Development Floor requires
employers to earn access to the trained workforce by contributing to its development.
Nothing in this framework is given to anyone who has not participated.

Furthermore, a citizenship-based income floor is vastly more efficient than the current
means-tested welfare apparatus. It eliminates the poverty trap created by benefit cliffs.
It reduces bureaucratic overhead by replacing dozens of programs with a few universal
instruments. It treats citizens as adults capable of allocating their own resources rather
than as clients managed by caseworkers.

**The progressive case for economic citizenship:**

Progressives believe that economic power is systemically distributed in ways that
disadvantage workers, that automation and capital accumulation without labor share is
unjust, and that healthcare and education are rights rather than luxuries. The economic
citizenship framework delivers on all of these through structural mechanisms rather than
political transfers. The Automation Tax Credit makes automation pay for displacement.
The American Sovereign Fund converts national policy assets into citizen wealth. The
health floor decouples medical security from employment status. None of this requires
a political coalition to win a budget fight every year — it is embedded in institutional
structures that outlast any administration.

**The consensus case:**

Both traditions share a deeper value: they believe that participation in American society
should be meaningful, that the system should reward contribution, and that catastrophic
failure should not be the only alternative to employment. Economic citizenship is the
institutional infrastructure that makes those shared values operational.

## 5. The Graduated Model — Citizenship as Accumulation

One of the most important design features across the Department of Economic Defense proposal
library is the **graduated model of citizenship rights**: the longer and deeper a citizen's
participation, the greater the economic claim they hold.

- At birth: you are entitled to a future, to an opportunity floor, to a functioning economy
- At working age (entry into workforce): the Internship Floor guarantees a structured pathway
- During working life: the Negative Income Tax floor is available during disruption; the
  Workforce Development Floor ensures training access; Civilian Reserve membership provides
  healthcare and employment
- After eighteen years of citizenship: the American Sovereign Fund dividend begins — a
  modest initial payment that grows with the fund across a lifetime
- At retirement: the dividend continues; combined with Social Security, it is a meaningful
  supplement; for citizens who have held citizenship since birth, the dividend at retirement
  reflects forty-plus years of compounding national investment

This is not a flat universal basic income in the conventional sense. It is a **graduated
ownership model** — your stake in the national economy grows as your participation in it
deepens. This is closer to the logic of a pension than the logic of welfare: you accumulate
a claim through participation, and you draw on it in proportion to what you have accumulated.

## 6. The Political Contract This Requires

Economic citizenship is not free. It makes demands on everyone.

**On citizens:** Participation is the entry requirement. This is not a system for passive
receipt. The income floor requires filing a tax return. The health floor under the Civilian
Reserve and Health Program requires reserve membership. The opportunity floor requires
engagement with the training and placement infrastructure. The wealth floor requires
eighteen years of continuous citizenship. The system does not penalize non-participation —
but it does not equally reward it.

**On employers:** Firms that benefit from the national workforce must contribute to it. The
Employer Development Obligation is not punitive — it is calibrated to be far cheaper than
the alternative (chronic workforce shortages, skills gaps, and the social instability that
follows long-term structural unemployment). But it is mandatory. The market will not
voluntarily solve the training coordination problem at the required scale.

**On the government:** The government must honor these floors consistently, not just when
politically convenient. This requires institutional design that insulates the programs from
annual budget fights — dedicated revenue streams (as in the American Sovereign Fund),
self-funding mechanisms (as in the Automation Tax Credit), and constitutional or
supermajority protections against raiding. A citizenship contract that can be revoked in
the next election cycle is not a contract at all.

**On the economy:** The economic system must remain dynamic enough to generate the
productivity that makes these floors affordable. Economic citizenship is not a program
for managing decline. It is a program for managing the distributional consequences of
growth — specifically, the growth driven by automation and global trade that has
increasingly concentrated gains while diffusing disruption. A stagnant economy cannot
support these floors. A growing economy cannot justify not having them.

## 7. Why the Department of Economic Defense Is the Right Institution

The theory of economic citizenship requires a standing institution to implement and defend
it. It cannot be administered through the existing patchwork of agencies because no
existing agency has the mandate to think about the entire economy simultaneously — its
risks, its distribution of gains, its structural fragilities, and its long-term investment
needs.

The Department of Economic Defense is designed to hold all of these simultaneously. Its
strategic contract framework pre-builds the supply chain resilience that makes the economy
productive. Its expert reserve and accelerator programs convert national research investment
into IP and commercial capacity. Its economic intelligence function monitors the leading
indicators that tell us when a floor is about to be tested. Its investment arm — the
American Sovereign Fund — converts national policy revenue into the citizen-owned capital
base that funds the dividend.

The Department of Economic Defense is the institution that makes economic citizenship
operational — not as a budget line item that rises and falls with political cycles, but as
a permanent feature of the American economic architecture, as durable as the Federal Reserve
and as purposeful as the original Department of Defense.

## Summary: The Economic Citizenship Framework

| Obligation | What the citizen contributes | What the economy owes in return |
|-----------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Productivity base | Work, taxes, civic participation | Share of surplus via Automation Tax Credit and American Sovereign Fund dividend |
| Workforce quality | Education, training, participation | Structured opportunity via internship floor and workforce development |
| Collective risk | Exposure to shared disruption | Income floor, health floor, business support during crises |
| Long-term participation | Years of citizenship | Graduated wealth accumulation via American Sovereign Fund dividend |
| National service | Reserve membership, expert service | Healthcare, employment pathways, professional development |

This is not a new theory. It is the American social contract, updated for an economy that
the original framers could not have imagined. The programs in the Department of Economic
Defense proposal library are not ideological innovations. They are the institutional
infrastructure required to honor a contract that has always existed but has never been
fully built.
