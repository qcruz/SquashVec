## Core Concept

[STUB — expand in future session]

Designate one lane on the Interstate Highway System as an **Autonomous-Only Lane (AOL)**
for qualifying self-driving freight vehicles, delivery vehicles, and autonomous passenger
carriers. The lane operates with:

- **Technology requirements:** SAE Level 4+ autonomy; vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I)
  communication; federally certified AV operating software
- **Access control:** RFID/transponder toll tag (similar to E-ZPass); entry only at
  designated merge points with certification verification
- **Self-funding:** Toll revenue covers lane maintenance, V2I infrastructure, and
  enforcement; surplus feeds DED Supply Chain Resilience Fund or American Sovereign Fund
- **Priority users:** Long-haul autonomous freight trucks, last-mile delivery vehicles
  operating in AV mode, certified autonomous passenger vehicles

## Central Argument

[TODO: Build out]

The U.S. freight system is a national security asset. Long-haul trucking moves approximately
70% of domestic freight by value. Driver shortages (currently 80,000+ unfilled positions),
aging infrastructure, and rising fuel costs are compressing the system's reliability and
driving up the cost of every good that moves through it — including defense procurement inputs.

Autonomous trucking technology (Waymo Via, Aurora, Kodiak, Torc, Gatik) is operationally
proven on limited highway corridors but lacks the dedicated infrastructure to operate at
national scale safely and efficiently. Mixed autonomy (AVs sharing lanes with human drivers
at variable speeds and following distances) reduces the efficiency gains that make AV
trucking commercially viable.

The Autonomous Highway Lane creates the dedicated infrastructure that unlocks commercial
AV trucking deployment — the way the Interstate Highway System itself created the
infrastructure that unlocked long-haul trucking in the 1950s.

## Key Evidence and Numbers to Build From

- U.S. Interstate Highway System: 48,440 miles; federally owned and maintained
- Current trucking freight cost: approximately $0.25-0.30/ton-mile; AV trucking projected
  at $0.10-0.15/ton-mile at scale (40-50% cost reduction)
- Trucker shortage: 80,000+ unfilled positions currently; projected 160,000 by 2030
- AV trucking companies with commercial deployments: Aurora (FedEx, Werner), Waymo Via
  (J.B. Hunt), Kodiak Robotics (U.S. Army), Gatik (Walmart, Loblaw)
- E-ZPass network covers 19 states; 40M+ transponders; proven toll collection infrastructure
- Federal Highway Administration lane construction cost: $1-3M per lane-mile; conversion
  of existing lane is significantly cheaper than new construction
- V2I infrastructure cost estimate: $50,000-150,000 per mile for roadside units, sensors,
  and communication infrastructure
- AV-only lane efficiency: platooning at 4-8 vehicle intervals increases throughput 2-3×
  vs. mixed traffic; reduces aerodynamic drag (fuel/energy savings 10-15%)

## Variants to Evaluate

### Variant A: Dedicated Conversion (Remove One Lane from Mixed Use)
Convert the rightmost lane of 4+ lane interstate segments to AV-only. Reduces mixed-traffic
capacity by one lane. Requires high AV adoption to justify capacity trade-off.

**Trade-off:** Immediate capacity loss for human drivers; requires high AV traffic volume
to be net-positive for total throughput. Best on high-freight corridors (I-10, I-80, I-40).

### Variant B: Shoulder Conversion (Convert Emergency Shoulder)
Convert the right shoulder to an AV-only lane on high-width interstate segments.
No reduction in mixed-traffic lane count.

**Trade-off:** Emergency vehicle access concerns; requires shoulder widening on many segments;
emergency pull-off accommodation; may require right-of-way acquisition.

### Variant C: New Dedicated Lane (Greenfield Addition)
Build a new dedicated AV lane alongside existing interstate infrastructure on high-volume
freight corridors. No impact on existing traffic.

**Trade-off:** Most expensive; requires new right-of-way or median expansion; most disruptive
to construct; best long-term outcome if volume justifies construction cost.

### Variant D: Designated AV Platooning Corridor (No Physical Separation)
No dedicated lane; instead, designate specific time windows (e.g., 10pm-6am) on specific
corridors where AV platoons have traffic management priority — signal coordination,
on-ramp metering, and speed harmonization through V2I infrastructure.

**Trade-off:** No construction cost; lower throughput gains; no physical exclusion of human
drivers; V2I infrastructure alone is the investment; viable as Phase 1 before physical lanes.

## Key Design Questions to Resolve

1. **Which corridors first?** High-freight interstates (I-10 Phoenix-El Paso, I-80 Nevada,
   I-40 Southwest, I-95 Northeast) or corridors with most AV operator activity?
2. **Toll rate structure:** Per-mile flat rate? Weight-based? Time-of-day pricing?
   What rate makes the lane self-funding without pricing out the AV operators it's meant
   to attract?
3. **Federal vs. state jurisdiction:** Interstates are federally funded but state-operated.
   Does the federal government mandate AOL conversion as a condition of highway funding
   (similar to 55mph speed limit mechanism)? Or offer matching grants for states that opt in?
4. **Technology certification:** Who certifies AV systems as AOL-eligible? NHTSA? A new
   DED certification framework? The certification standard determines market access and
   drives technology development.
5. **Trucking workforce transition:** The AHLP accelerates autonomous trucking deployment
   and will displace human truckers over a 10-20 year horizon. The DED's AI unemployment
   response (retraining pipeline, NIT income floor) must be explicitly linked to AHLP
   as a programmatic commitment — the government that builds the lane that displaces the
   driver must fund the driver's transition.
6. **V2I infrastructure ownership:** Federal (FHWA)? State DOTs? Private (AV operators
   pay for their own V2I in exchange for lane access)? Public-private partnership?
7. **Emergency vehicle integration:** How do emergency vehicles use or cross AOL lanes?
   Does V2I infrastructure allow human-controlled emergency vehicles to enter and clear
   the lane safely?
8. **Revenue disposition:** Does toll revenue go to FHWA (highway maintenance), the
   Highway Trust Fund, or a new DED Supply Chain Resilience fund? The self-funding logic
   is most compelling if revenue stays in transportation infrastructure.

## DED Integration Points

- **Supply chain resilience:** AOL lanes reduce freight cost and transit time for DED
  strategic materials — rare earth shipments, pharmaceutical inputs, semiconductor
  components. Faster, cheaper freight reduces the effective cost of domestic supply chain
  resilience investment.
- **Oil restriction scenario:** AV trucking (primarily electric) reduces petroleum dependence
  in freight. AOL lanes prioritize electric AV freight during an oil restriction event —
  explicitly programmed into Phase 2 response.
- **AI unemployment:** AHLP is a direct cause of trucker displacement and must be paired
  with explicit workforce transition commitments. The program cannot be designed in isolation
  from the AI unemployment response — the displacement is foreseeable and the government
  as lane-builder is directly responsible for accelerating it.
- **ASF integration:** Toll revenue surplus, after covering lane maintenance and V2I
  infrastructure, could flow to the American Sovereign Fund as a transportation
  infrastructure revenue stream — parallel to the resource royalty enhancement mechanism.

## Feasibility Pre-Assessment

| Dimension | Preliminary Rating | Key Question |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Legal / Constitutional | Moderate | Federal vs. state highway jurisdiction; NHTSA certification authority |
| Administrative | Moderate | Multi-agency (FHWA, NHTSA, DED, state DOTs); V2I standards body |
| Economic | Strong | Self-funding via tolls; freight cost reduction ROI; DoD logistics savings |
| Political | Moderate | Trucker union opposition (workforce displacement); state DOT autonomy |
| Implementation | Moderate | Variant D (time-window platooning) is fastest; Variant A/B require construction |
| International Precedent | Moderate | Netherlands platooning corridors; Singapore AV lanes; UK CAV corridor program |

**Preliminary Implementation Readiness:** Conditional
*Technology is ready for Variant D (platooning corridor, no construction). Physical lane
conversion (Variants A-C) requires AV adoption rates that will not be sufficient for
lane utilization until 2030-2035. Recommend: Variant D as Phase 1 (2026-2030) to build
V2I infrastructure and demonstrate concept; physical lane conversion as Phase 2 (2030-2035)
on corridors where Variant D demonstrates sufficient AV volume to justify dedicated capacity.*
