## Overview

The DED's proposals are technically sound and economically justified. That is necessary
but not sufficient for enactment. Every proposal creates winners and losers. Every proposal
will be opposed by organized interests that benefit from the status quo. Every proposal must
navigate a legislative process designed to give organized minorities veto power over majority-
benefit programs.

This document maps the political economy of each major DED proposal: who benefits, who is
threatened, what the organizing opposition looks like, and what coalitions can move the proposal
through Congress. It also defines the legislative vehicles, recommended sequencing, and
messaging by audience.

The single most important strategic observation: DED programs do not fit either party's
existing brand cleanly, which is a political asset rather than a liability. Programs that
belong to no party cannot be killed by a single party's opposition.

## Part 1: The Core Political Reframe

### The Left/Right Trap

Defense hawks will support "economic defense" but resist "economic safety net." Progressives
will support "shared ownership" but resist "defense-framed spending." The frame wars will
consume the proposal if they are not preemptively settled.

The solution is the contractarian citizenship frame from `ded/proposal/economic-citizenship.md`:
not left/right, but earned rights and reciprocal obligations. You contribute to the national
economy; you earn a share of the national wealth. This frame is:

- Libertarian-compatible: rights + reciprocity, not paternalism
- Conservative-compatible: earned, not given; self-funding, not transfer
- Progressive-compatible: universal access, not means-tested exclusion
- Military-compatible: the Civilian Reserve is service, not welfare

**The north star sentence:** "This is what America looks like if we apply the same logic to
economics that we apply to national defense — you invest before the crisis, not after."

### What DED Is Not

Proactively defining what DED is not prevents opponents from defining it for you:

- Not a new welfare program (all CRHP benefits are earned through enrollment and work)
- Not protectionism (DED uses market mechanisms — contracts, APCs, IP licensing — not tariffs)
- Not a jobs program (CRHP is civilian reserve training, funded as military readiness)
- Not socialism (the American Sovereign Fund invests in commercial markets; Norway's fund has
  beaten private equity benchmarks for 30 years)
- Not redistribution (the dividend is return on national assets, not transfer from taxpayers)

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**

**Swing constituencies:** Defense-oriented Democrats (Armed Services Committee); fiscal hawks
who can be shown the 8:1 ROI against crisis response spending

### American Sovereign Fund

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**
- All eligible citizens (eventually — 18-year clock creates patient constituency)
- States that want federal investment flows (75% commercial portfolio = domestic investment)
- Alaska-state precedent holders (bipartisan; PFD is Alaska's third rail)
- Fiscal conservatives who like self-funding (ASF revenue comes from arms sales levy, tariff
  share, and dedicated taxes — not income tax)

**Organized opposition:**
- Defense contractors (7.5% arms sales levy — direct cost)
- Financial industry (ASF manages large capital pool; may compete with private asset managers)
- Short-term fiscal hawks (takes 15 years to produce first dividend)

**Mitigation strategy:**
- 75% commercial portfolio managed by private asset managers under GPFG-equivalent mandate —
  the financial industry is the implementation partner, not the adversary
- The 15-year wait is the core political vulnerability; the counter-move is to fund CRHP and
  WDF (which produce immediate visible benefits) before ASF, building the constituency that will
  wait for the dividend
- Arms industry: frame levy as re-investment in the national defense industrial base through the
  Strategic Reserve tranche

**Swing constituencies:** Rural state senators (sovereign wealth fund flows into domestic
infrastructure); fiscal conservatives who understand Alaska PFD and Norway GPFG precedent

### Negative Income Tax / Income Floor

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**
- Working poor and low-income workers (direct cash benefit)
- Libertarians (Milton Friedman's design — replaces bureaucratic welfare with direct transfer)
- Unions (income floor = stronger wage bargaining position; workers can decline bad offers)
- Employers in tight labor markets (income floor reduces desperation hiring, improves match quality)
- Caregiver constituency (NIT is the payment mechanism for care economy credit)

**Organized opposition:**
- Means-tested welfare bureaucracies (SNAP, Medicaid, TANF administrators face consolidation)
- Fiscal conservatives (net cost $89-145B/year before ATC offset)
- Some progressives (unconditional nature; prefer benefit expansion over cash)
- Work-requirement advocates (NIT is unconditional for floor; this is politically contestable)

**Mitigation strategy:**
- Never say "UBI" — term is politically toxic across audiences; say "income floor" or
  "Negative Income Tax" (Friedman's term; triggers conservative credibility)
- Phase in as EITC expansion first; the legislative infrastructure already exists
- Two-track design resolves work-requirement debate: unconditional NIT floor +
  conditional Civilian Reserve premium (the premium requires enrollment)
- ATC → NIT statutory allocation (60% of ATC revenue) makes the funding source concrete

**Swing constituencies:** Libertarians (Friedman legacy is the credibility anchor); rural
working-class voters who distrust means-testing bureaucracy but are skeptical of "free money"
(framing: "this is the dividend from America's productive capacity, not charity")

**Sequencing dependency:** Must be preceded by Automation Tax Credit (ATC provides funding
source); best enacted third or fourth, after DED and CRHP create visible constituency.

### Workforce Development Floor

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**
- Young workers and students (1M paid experience slots/year)
- Labor unions (new employer-funded paid work placements)
- Employers with genuine skills gaps (structured pipeline; 35-40:1 ROI)
- Rural communities (slots can be sited anywhere, including low-employment areas)
- Bipartisan workforce development advocates (both parties run on "job training")

**Organized opposition:**
- Large employers with low-skill, high-turnover business models (compliance cost)
- Chamber of Commerce instincts (employer mandates)

**Mitigation strategy:**
- Framing is everything: "German apprenticeship model" not "employer mandate"
- Slots can be any paid experience (internship, apprenticeship, rotational program) —
  employers have maximum design flexibility
- Small business exemption threshold ($10M revenue or fewer than 50 employees)
- Certification tax credit for rapid genuine hires fully offsets cost for compliant employers
- This is the only DED program where the Chamber of Commerce can be a partner rather than
  an opponent if the implementation design is right

**Swing constituencies:** Governors and state legislators (workforce development is a state
priority; WDF slots map onto existing state apprenticeship infrastructure); trade associations
that already run apprenticeship programs (construction, manufacturing, healthcare)

**Political note:** This is the most immediately implementable program. A FLSA amendment is
the only legislative requirement. It should be the first bill introduced to establish the DED
legislative agenda.

### Civilian Reserve and Health Program

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**
- Working-class Americans without employer healthcare ($8-15k/year enrollment incentive)
- Veterans groups (the military analogy is exact; they understand the model)
- Labor unions (CRHP creates union-density infrastructure jobs with healthcare)
- Rural communities (CRHP deploys to infrastructure projects in underserved areas)
- Healthcare access advocates (FEHB-equivalent coverage for a new population)

**Organized opposition:**
- AMA and physician lobbies (expanded healthcare access = physician supply pressure)
- Private insurance industry (FEHB expansion = potential market displacement)
- Fiscal conservatives (healthcare cost is the largest budget line)

**Mitigation strategy:**
- FEHB extension for launch (Year 1-2) rather than new CRHP Health Network — uses existing
  negotiated rates; no new government healthcare program to attack
- Self-funding by Year 8 (actuarial model documented in feasibility analysis)
- CRHP is civilian reserve training funded as military readiness — this is not a healthcare
  program, it is a workforce readiness program that provides healthcare as a benefit
- Physician supply concern: CRHP deploys into shortage areas, increasing demand for existing
  physicians in underserved markets, not competing for urban physician supply

**Swing constituencies:** Red-state senators with rural healthcare shortages and defense
industrial base voters; governors in states with high uninsured rates

### Care Economy / Social Security Caregiving Credit

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**
- Every caregiver in America (sandwich generation, parents of disabled children, hospice
  caregivers, rural recovery coordinators) — conservatively 25%+ of adult Americans
- Conservative family-values constituency (the credit finally values what families have always done)
- Progressive economic-justice constituency (unpaid care work is 80% performed by women;
  the credit corrects a structural inequity)
- Seniors and disability advocates (caregiver support means more people can remain in home
  and community settings)
- Social Security solvency advocates (general fund transfer model, not Trust Fund draw)

**Organized opposition:**
- None organized; the SSA caregiving credit has no natural organized opponent
- SSA actuaries (budget concern, fully mitigated by general fund transfer mechanism)
- Some fiscal conservatives (new spending), though the 15:1 ROI argument ($20-35B cost vs.
  $470B institutional care avoided) converts most fiscal objections

**Political note:** This is the single most politically durable proposal in the entire
portfolio. It is simultaneously conservative (family values) and progressive (economic
justice), costs less than any alternative, and has no organized opposition. It should be
introduced early to build the broad constituency that subsequent programs will rely on.

**Swing constituencies:** Everyone with a parent, a child, or a chronic condition — this
constituency is universal in practice.

### Job Posting Accountability Penalty

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**
- Job seekers (enormous constituency; 100M+ job applications/year affected by ghost posting)
- Labor transparency advocates
- Small businesses that compete for talent against companies that use ghost posting for brand
  management and market intelligence

**Organized opposition:**
- Large employers with ghost-posting practices (direct compliance cost and exposure)
- Chamber of Commerce (employer compliance burden)
- Job boards and platforms (reduced listing volume = reduced revenue)

**Mitigation strategy:**
- Phase 0 (reporting only, no penalty) is achievable via DOL rulemaking — no legislation
  required, no employer compliance cost, only transparency
- Ghost job problem is well-documented in public discourse with significant media coverage;
  the political environment is favorable
- Evergreen role exemption removes the main legitimate business objection
- Good-faith waiver (Variant E) addresses rapid business condition changes
- Revenue framing: $675M-$3B/year in penalty collections at full enforcement — the program pays
  for NEID data infrastructure

**Sequencing note:** Phase 0 (reporting) should be one of the first DED-adjacent executive
actions. It requires no legislation, generates NEID data immediately, and builds the political
case for Phase 1 (penalty) by documenting the scale of the ghost job problem in federal data.

### American Prosperity Visa

**Primary beneficiary coalition:**
- Immigration reform advocates (new legal pathway)
- Fiscal conservatives (ASF contributions = net new federal investment; no welfare access
  for 18 years)
- Tech industry and high-growth sectors (Tier 2 Human Capital track captures critical skills)
- Entrepreneurs (business creation pathway)
- ASF dividend advocates (Year 10 contribution projections: $6B/year in ASF contributions)

**Organized opposition:**
- Anti-immigration factions (any new immigration pathway is opposed)
- EB-5 program defenders (American Prosperity Visa competes for the same investor constituency)

**Mitigation strategy:**
- Frame as "investment in America's sovereign wealth fund" not "immigration reform" —

  any eligible group — this is a feature for fiscal conservatives

**Sequencing dependency:** Should be enacted after ASF is established and has a visible track
record — the dividend is the product being sold; you cannot sell it before it exists.

## Part 3: Legislative Vehicle Analysis

### National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA)

The NDAA passes every year. It is the most reliable legislative vehicle for DED programs
with a national security nexus. Programs that belong in the NDAA:

| Program | NDAA Title / Section | Notes |
|---------|---------------------|-------|
| DED Charter — enabling authority | Title X (Department of Defense / Organization) or new standalone title | Requires new statutory title; possible as standalone attached to NDAA |
| Civilian Reserve and Health Program | Title X (Reserve Forces) | Direct analogy to existing reserve component authority |
| Defense Production Act modifications | Title III (Defense Industrial Base) | DPA §303 purchase commitment authority and §708 voluntary agreements |
| Expert Reserve Program | Title X + DPA Title VII | OTA authority is the legal model; direct attach |
| Supply Chain Risk Framework / National Economic Intelligence Dashboard | Title X intelligence provisions | NEID is an economic intelligence function; natural home |
| Strategic contracts — Type A/B/C/D | Title III (DPA) | Anchor buying authority already exists; this expands it |
| Space Mining Incentives | Title XVI (Space) or standalone | APC and Accelerator attach cleanly; extraction premature |
| Autonomous Highway Lane Program | Transportation attachment to NDAA or standalone | Emergency routing provisions have NDAA nexus |

**Tactical note:** The NDAA is bicameral and conference-negotiated. Programs attached to the
NDAA must survive Armed Services Committee markup in both chambers. The defense nexus must be
articulated clearly for each provision. The military cost linkage argument (every commodity
price spike is a DoD procurement cost spike) is the key to Armed Services Committee buy-in.

## Part 4: Sequencing Strategy

### The Foundational Logic

Enacting A makes B easier when: (1) A creates a visible constituency that wants B, (2) A
provides the revenue mechanism or institutional infrastructure that B requires, or (3) A
establishes the precedent that makes B's design less novel.

### Recommended Sequence

**Phase 0 — Pre-legislative groundwork (Executive action, Year 1):**

1. JPAP Phase 0 reporting requirement — DOL rulemaking; no legislation; establishes NEID
   data foundation; creates documented record of ghost job scale that drives Phase 1
2. NEID prototype — BLS/BEA/Census data-sharing MOUs; builds the intelligence infrastructure
   that every subsequent program relies on
3. DPA invocation pilot — one or two anchor buy contracts in semiconductor or REE supply
   chain using existing authority; demonstrates DED-style tool without new legislation

**Phase 1 — First legislative priorities (Year 1-2):**

4. Workforce Development Floor Act (FLSA amendment) — introduce first; broadest coalition;
   near-zero federal cost; establishes employer-government partnership infrastructure; passes
   without Democratic or Republican opposition if framed correctly
5. Care Economy Act / SSA Caregiving Credit — introduce in parallel; no organized opposition;
   broadest constituency; builds the enrollment infrastructure that CRHP will use
6. DED Charter + CRHP + Strategic Contract authority (NDAA attachment) — enables everything
   else; CRHP creates the visible civilian labor corps that makes ASF dividend politically
   comprehensible as "return on your service"

**Phase 2 — Institutional build-out (Year 2-3):**

7. American Sovereign Fund Act (standalone) — ASF is only politically viable after CRHP is
   operating and the "return on service" frame is visible; needs 2+ years of CRHP enrollment
8. JPAP Phase 1 penalty (FLSA amendment or standalone) — Phase 0 reporting data drives this;
   the documented scale makes opposition indefensible
9. Synthetic Protein Program (Farm Bill vehicle) — attaches to next Farm Bill reauthorization
10. Autonomous Highway Lane Program (FHWA reauthorization or standalone) — Variant D
    (time-window platooning) as Phase 1; full conversion in Phase 2

**Phase 3 — System completion (Year 3-5):**

11. Automation Tax Credit (reconciliation) — creates ATC revenue stream; enables NIT
12. Negative Income Tax as EITC expansion (reconciliation) — the capstone program; requires
    ATC funding, DED infrastructure, CRHP enrollment, ASF track record to be politically viable
13. American Prosperity Visa (INA amendment) — after ASF is established and has a visible
    dividend clock; the product must exist before you can sell it
14. Expert Reserve Program (NDAA) — depends on DED being fully operational; attach to NDAA
    Year 3+ when DED institutional identity is established

### Minimum Viable DED

The smallest package that creates meaningful resilience value and builds a constituency for
the rest — the package that could pass in a single Congress even in a difficult political
environment:

1. **Workforce Development Floor** (FLSA amendment) — immediate employer-funded workforce impact
2. **Care Economy SSA Caregiving Credit** (SSA amendment) — broadest constituency; self-funding
3. **DED Charter (stripped)** (NDAA attachment) — DED as an intelligence and contracting agency
   only, with no CRHP and no ASF; smaller, easier, creates the institution
4. **JPAP Phase 0** (executive action) — no legislation; immediate

This four-element package costs under $5B/year, requires no new taxation, and creates the
data infrastructure (NEID) and institutional infrastructure (DED) that every subsequent
program depends on. It is the foothold.

## Part 5: Messaging by Audience

### Messaging Matrix

| Term to Avoid | Use Instead | Reason |
|--------------|-------------|--------|
| UBI | Income floor, Negative Income Tax | "UBI" is politically toxic across audiences |
| Redistribution | Shared ownership, return on national assets | Redistribution implies taking; ownership implies earning |
| Government jobs program | Civilian Reserve training | Reserve is service; jobs program is welfare |
| Welfare | Economic floor, earned benefit | Welfare is stigmatized; floor is structural |
| Employer mandate | Workforce Development Obligation | Mandate implies coercion; obligation implies partnership |
| Penalty | Accountability requirement | Penalty antagonizes; accountability is neutral |
| Socialist | Norway model, Alaska model | Specific precedents defeat abstract labels |
| Big government | New institution (like DARPA) | DARPA framing is universally credible |
| Protectionism | Strategic resilience | Resilience is neutral; protectionism is pejorative |
| New spending | Investment with documented ROI | ROI framing converts fiscal hawks |

### Defense Hawks

**Core argument:** Every commodity cost spike is a DoD procurement cost spike. When oil
spikes, the Pentagon's fuel bill spikes. When semiconductor prices spike, weapons system
procurement costs spike. DED is a hedge against defense budget inflation — it is defense
spending that happens to benefit civilians.

**Key numbers:** $680B 10-year DED budget vs. $5.2T COVID-equivalent crisis response cost.
The ratio is 1:7.6. No defense planner would accept a weapons system that costs eight times
more per unit if you wait to buy it.

**Institutional frame:** DED is DARPA for economic threats. DARPA was created because Sputnik
demonstrated that adversaries can develop asymmetric capabilities faster than the U.S.
military can respond. The 2021 semiconductor shortage demonstrated the same thing for supply
chains. DARPA has bipartisan support; DED is DARPA's economic equivalent.

**Talking points:**
- "Supply chain concentration risk is the threat model the Pentagon identified; DED is the
  institutional response"
- "The national laboratory complex was built to ensure no adversary could hold U.S. technology
  hostage; DED extends that logic to commodities and supply chains"
- "CRHP is a civilian force multiplier — pre-trained, pre-positioned, deployable in 72 hours"

### Fiscal Conservatives

**Core argument:** DED costs $60-80B/year and prevents $500B+ crisis responses. That is
8:1 ROI minimum. Every DED program is either self-funding (ASF investments, toll revenue,
JPAP penalties) or employer-funded (Workforce Development Floor, strategic contracts). No new
income taxes are required for the core institutional package.

**Key numbers:** COVID-19 cost $5.2T in federal response spending over two years. One
well-designed pandemic preparedness program (DED Scenario: Pandemic, Phase 1) costs roughly
$15-20B. The ratio is 260:1 in favor of preparation.

**Institutional frame:** The Alaska Permanent Fund has operated since 1976 with zero federal
subsidies, no legislative appropriation, and consistent returns. It has paid $26B in dividends
to Alaskan residents. This is the model, not a new experiment.

**Talking points:**
- "DED is loss prevention, not new spending — we pay $60B/year or we pay $500B+ when the
  crisis hits without preparation"
- "The Civilian Reserve uses military funding mechanisms — this is readiness, not welfare"
- "Every program in the portfolio has a documented funding source that does not require
  raising income taxes"

## Part 6: Anti-Capture Design as Political Feature

The mission.md anti-capture provisions are not only governance — they are political selling
points that convert skeptical constituencies:

| Provision | Converts |
|-----------|---------|
| Geographic caps (no state >15% of contracts) | Rural state senators; prevents coastal concentration |
| Contract transparency (public disclosure of all DED commitments) | Good government coalition; transparency advocates |
| Revolving door restrictions (2-year cooling off for DED senior staff) | Reform coalition; anti-corruption voters |
| Scenario linkage requirement (DED can only act for documented scenario purposes) | Free-market skeptics; prevents regulatory capture |
| GAO audits (annual independent review) | Fiscal conservatives; oversight coalition |
| JCED congressional oversight (joint economic committee) | Both parties; prevents unilateral executive action |

These provisions should be introduced early and prominently in every public presentation of
DED. The question "what stops this from becoming a slush fund?" has a specific statutory answer
for every dimension of the concern. This is a legislative design achievement that most agencies
cannot match.
