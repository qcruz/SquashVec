**Document Type:** Ongoing Argument Ledger
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Status:** Living document — arguments added and refined continuously; each objection
  should be addressed before the policy is finalized; no argument is dismissed without
  a documented rebuttal or an acknowledgment that it stands

## Framing Note: UBI vs. Negative Income Tax

Most criticism of "UBI" targets a specific design: a flat universal payment to every
citizen regardless of income, funded by new taxes or deficit spending, replacing or
supplementing existing welfare programs. The DED proposal is not this design.

**The DED proposal is a Negative Income Tax (NIT):**
- Citizens below the income guarantee floor receive a supplement that brings them to
  the floor — a payment that phases out as income rises
- Citizens above the floor pay normal taxes; they receive nothing
- The guarantee floor is the UBI; the phase-out rate is the mechanism that preserves
  work incentives and limits fiscal cost
- At $6,200 guarantee floor / 30% phase-out: full benefit to zero-income recipients;
  zero benefit at ~$20,667 income; smooth gradient between

Where arguments apply specifically to flat UBI but not to the Negative Income Tax
design, this is noted. Where arguments apply to both, they are addressed for both.
The DED also layers the American Sovereign Fund monthly dividend on top of the
Negative Income Tax — that dividend is universal and flat. Both programs are analyzed
here where relevant.

## FOR: The Case for Universal Basic Income / Negative Income Tax

### F-1: Eliminates the Poverty Trap

**The argument:** Means-tested benefit programs (SNAP, Medicaid, housing vouchers, TANF)
all phase out as income rises. The phase-out schedules often overlap, creating effective
marginal tax rates above 80% for low-income workers who take a raise — for every
additional dollar earned, benefits are reduced by more than a dollar. This is the poverty
trap: the rational economic response to this structure is to stay poor.

The Negative Income Tax eliminates this by replacing multiple overlapping phase-outs with
a single, transparent phase-out rate (30% in the DED design). Every dollar earned results
in a 30-cent reduction in benefits — not an 80-cent reduction. The marginal incentive to
work is restored. A low-income worker always takes home more from working than from not
working.

**Evidence:** The Congressional Budget Office has documented effective marginal tax rates
exceeding 70–80% for families receiving multiple concurrent benefits (SNAP, ACA
subsidies, TANF, childcare assistance). Studies of program interactions consistently find
that the poverty trap reduces labor supply among the near-poor. NIT pilots (discussed in
Evidence Base section) show positive labor supply effects relative to the current system.

**Rebuttal to standard objection:** Critics argue that ending means-tested programs
harms the poorest. Under the NIT design, the poorest recipients receive the full benefit
floor ($6,200/year) — more than many currently receive through fragmented and
inconsistently administered programs — with zero phase-out cliff. The harm argument
applies to flat UBI that replaces targeted programs; it does not apply to a well-designed
NIT that preserves and regularizes existing support.

### F-2: Administrative Simplification and Overhead Reduction

**The argument:** The U.S. welfare system comprises more than 80 means-tested federal
programs administered across HHS, USDA, HUD, DOL, SSA, and dozens of state agencies.
Each program has separate eligibility rules, application processes, documentation
requirements, and administrative overhead. Administrative costs for the combined system
exceed $40B/year. Compliance costs for recipients (time, documentation, travel) are
substantial and fall entirely on the people least able to bear them.

A Negative Income Tax delivered through the existing IRS income tax filing infrastructure
reduces this to a single system. The IRS already collects the income data required to
calculate eligibility; adding a refundable credit requires new calculations, not new
agencies. Administrative overhead collapses from $40B to $5–10B/year.

**The "street-level bureaucracy" elimination:** Much of the poverty trap is maintained
not by formal policy but by administrative friction — the difficulty of maintaining
enrollment, reapplying after income changes, proving continued eligibility. People lose
SNAP benefits because they miss a renewal appointment; they lose Medicaid because they
got a raise and didn't report it in time. The NIT eliminates these friction points:
eligibility is calculated once per year at tax filing with no active maintenance required
by the recipient.

**The simplicity narrative:** "File your taxes. If your income is below the floor, you
get a check. If it's above, you don't." This is intelligible to any citizen and requires
no caseworker, no eligibility interview, no food benefit card distinct from a debit card.

### F-3: Automation-Proof Income Floor

**The argument:** The labor displacement driven by AI and automation is structurally
different from prior technology transitions. Agricultural mechanization and manufacturing
automation eliminated specific categories of physical labor but created new demand for
cognitive, service, and managerial work. AI displaces cognitive work across the
income spectrum. The historical assumption that automation always creates more jobs than
it destroys is being stress-tested for the first time in the cognitive domain.

Whether or not mass unemployment materializes, the *risk* of it materializing within a
10–20 year horizon justifies building the income floor infrastructure now, during a period
of high employment when the political will to fund it exists and the fiscal cost is
manageable. Building the NIT after automation has already disrupted the labor market
requires politically difficult emergency action. Building it now is preventive policy.

**The DED alignment:** The Automation Tax Credit and Technology Automation Tax stream
create a direct link: automation-generated productivity gains fund the income floor for
displaced workers. The company that replaces ten workers with one AI system pays into the
fund that cushions the nine. This is not punishment for innovation — it is pricing the
externality of labor displacement.

### F-4: Entrepreneurship and Economic Dynamism

**The argument:** A primary barrier to entrepreneurship among low- and middle-income
Americans is the catastrophic risk of failure. Starting a business means giving up
employment income, often losing employer-sponsored health insurance, and accepting
near-zero income during the startup phase. For someone with a mortgage, children, or
student debt, this risk is prohibitive regardless of the quality of the idea.

A guaranteed income floor changes the calculus. The floor means that failure has a bottom
— the entrepreneur will not starve, will not lose housing, will not lose access to
healthcare (if paired with Medicaid or a universal health program). The expected value of
attempting entrepreneurship rises; risk-aversion-driven inaction falls.

**Evidence:** The Stockton SEED pilot (2019–2021) found that recipients were more likely
to be employed full-time at the end of the program than controls. The interpretation: with
a floor, people take job risks (applying for better positions, starting part-time
businesses) they would not otherwise take. The guaranteed income functioned not as a
work substitute but as a risk buffer that enabled better work.

**Macro effect:** A country where 50% more people attempt entrepreneurship because the
downside risk is cushioned is a substantially more dynamic and innovative economy. The
Negative Income Tax is not a welfare program — it is an innovation policy.

### F-5: Care Work and Social Reproduction

**The argument:** The formal economy treats as "zero" an enormous quantity of economically
productive activity: childcare, elder care, disability support, household management,
community volunteering. These activities produce real value — a parent providing childcare
is producing a service that, if purchased in the market, would cost $25,000–$40,000/year
in many U.S. cities. The fact that it is unpaid does not make it unproductive.

Current welfare and tax systems penalize care work. TANF recipients with young children
are required to work paid hours to maintain eligibility. EITC requires earned income —
unpaid care does not qualify. The poverty trap for primary caregivers is therefore
particularly severe: they are penalized economically for doing work that is socially
essential.

An unconditional income guarantee solves this structurally. It does not require
categorizing care work as employment or valuing it explicitly — it simply provides a
floor that allows the caregiver to survive economically while doing the work the economy
depends on but refuses to pay for.

**See also:** `ded/proposal/care-work-tax-recognition.md` for analysis of IRS approaches
to explicitly recognizing care work in tax calculation.

### F-6: Labor Market Bargaining Power

**The argument:** The current labor market balance of power is fundamentally shaped by
the asymmetry of desperation: workers must work to eat; employers can wait to hire.
This asymmetry justifies wages below the productivity value of labor, working conditions
below what workers would accept if they had alternatives, and employer power over
scheduling, location, and terms that workers accept because the alternative is income loss.

A guaranteed income floor changes this asymmetry. Workers who can survive without a
specific job have genuine bargaining power. They can walk away from jobs with dangerous
conditions, poverty wages, or abusive management. Employers who want to attract workers
must offer compensation and conditions that workers find worth accepting.

**The objection:** "This will raise wages and hurt small businesses." This is partially
true — wages for the worst jobs will rise, because those jobs will no longer attract
workers at poverty wages. This is the point. The businesses that can only survive by
paying wages below subsistence are being subsidized by the welfare system that supports
their workers. An income floor makes that subsidy explicit and ends it.

**The efficiency argument:** Higher wages in low-wage industries reduce employee turnover,
increase worker investment in job performance, and improve service quality. The restaurant
industry's turnover rate (~75% annually) is enormously costly; a workforce that is not
desperate for any job available is a more stable, productive workforce.

### F-7: Health Outcomes and Long-Run Fiscal Return

**The argument:** Poverty is itself a health condition. Low-income Americans have
dramatically worse health outcomes than their wealthier peers across virtually every
measure: life expectancy, chronic disease prevalence, mental health, infant mortality,
disability rates. These poor outcomes are not primarily genetic — they are the documented
consequences of financial stress, food insecurity, housing instability, and inability to
access preventive care.

The fiscal return on improving health outcomes at the bottom of the income distribution
is very high. Every avoided emergency room visit, every chronic disease prevented or
delayed, every mental health hospitalization avoided saves money that otherwise would be
paid by Medicaid, Medicare, and municipal emergency services. Studies of cash transfer
programs consistently find significant improvements in healthcare utilization patterns
(more preventive, less emergency) and health outcomes.

**Cherokee Nation GSMS data:** The Great Smoky Mountains Study found that children in
families that received a cash dividend (from casino revenue — a form of UBI) had
significantly better mental health outcomes as adults, better educational attainment, and
lower rates of substance abuse. The dividend was modest ($4,000/year for a family of
four); the health effects were substantial and persisted into adulthood.

**Long-run fiscal case:** The NIT's annual cost of $600–800B must be weighed against
the long-run reduction in Medicaid expenditures, incarceration costs, disability payments,
emergency social services, and lost economic productivity from preventable poverty-related
health conditions. Full lifecycle accounting consistently shows that the net cost of well-
designed income floors is substantially lower than the gross transfer amount.

### F-8: Political Durability Through Universality

**The argument:** Every means-tested program in U.S. history has been politically
vulnerable to "welfare reform" — cuts, eligibility restrictions, time limits, work
requirements — because the constituency for the program is politically weak: poor people
who are not reliable voters and do not make large campaign donations.

Universal programs — Social Security, Medicare — have survived 80+ years of political
attack because their constituency is universal. When a politician proposes cutting Social
Security, every American over 65 and every American with parents or grandparents over 65
has a direct stake in defending it. The political math is different.

The Negative Income Tax, by phasing out rather than excluding, maintains a broad
constituency: the phase-out range ($0–$20,667) encompasses many working families who
receive partial benefit and have a stake in the program's continuation. The American
Sovereign Fund dividend reinforces this with a literally universal benefit (every eligible
citizen). Together, the two programs create a political coalition for the income floor
that approximates the Social Security coalition in durability.

---

### F-9: Dignity and Administrative Humiliation Reduction

**The argument:** Means-tested benefit programs by design require recipients to prove
their poverty, justify their need, and submit to bureaucratic scrutiny of their choices.
Drug testing for TANF recipients, food restrictions on SNAP purchases, caseworker review
of household composition — these mechanisms impose a dignity cost on recipients that has
no economic justification. They exist as political compromise, not as programmatic design.

The evidence base for these mechanisms is weak: drug testing programs consistently find
lower rates of drug use among TANF recipients than the general population; food purchase
restrictions have not been shown to improve nutrition outcomes. The primary effect is
administrative cost and recipient humiliation.

An unconditional income floor eliminates this entirely. The payment arrives because you
are a citizen below the floor — not because you have proven your worthiness to a
caseworker. This is not merely a dignity argument; humiliation has documented effects on
mental health, self-efficacy, and long-run economic outcomes. Programs that treat
recipients as untrustworthy produce recipients who internalize distrust of formal
institutions and are harder to serve through those institutions in the future.

## AGAINST: The Case Against / Critical Objections

### A-1: Fiscal Cost — The Primary Objection

**The objection:** A Negative Income Tax guaranteeing $6,200/year to all Americans below
the phase-out threshold would cost an estimated $600–800B/year — larger than the entire
current discretionary federal budget. This is not a marginal program cost; it is a
fundamental restructuring of federal expenditure at a scale that has no precedent outside
wartime mobilization.

**The strongest version of this objection:** Even replacing the existing means-tested
welfare system in its entirety ($700–900B/year across all programs) does not fully fund
the NIT at a meaningful guarantee level. A $6,200 floor for all current low-income
Americans (roughly 40 million households) costs ~$248B in gross transfers — but the NIT
phases out across a much wider income band, meaning tens of millions of working-class
households in the phase-out range also receive partial benefits. Full accounting puts
gross cost at $800B+, while the welfare programs being replaced fund perhaps $400B of
that. The gap requires new revenue.

**The DED rebuttal — partial:**
The NIT is not intended to be revenue-neutral from existing programs. The DED funding
stack — Penny Transaction Tax, Financial Transaction Surcharge, Technology Automation Tax,
American Sovereign Fund returns, and the full funding catalog — provides new revenue
streams that grow over time. The NIT is not funded by replacing food stamps; it is funded
by taxing financial transactions and automation productivity. The program becomes self-
financing as the American Sovereign Fund matures (~Year 4–5 in the implementation
timeline). But the transition period (Years 0–4) requires deficit financing of $200–400B/
year — a real cost that must be honestly acknowledged.

**Outstanding:** Full lifecycle fiscal accounting needed. The objection stands as a
serious constraint on launch-year cost. The DED response is phased implementation: NIT
at partial floor initially ($3,000), scaling to full floor ($6,200) as funding streams
mature. This requires a separate implementation phasing document.

### A-2: Inflation — Does Giving Everyone Money Just Raise Prices?

**The objection:** If every citizen below the income floor receives a cash transfer,
aggregate demand increases. With supply unchanged, prices rise — particularly for goods
consumed primarily by low-income households (food, low-end housing, basic services).
The real value of the transfer is eroded by the price increase it causes, potentially
leaving recipients no better off in real terms while the fiscal cost is entirely real.

**The strongest version:** This is particularly acute for housing, where supply is
constrained by zoning and construction costs. An increase in housing purchasing power
for the bottom third of the income distribution, with no corresponding increase in
housing supply, produces higher rents — effectively a wealth transfer to landlords funded
by the NIT budget.

**The DED rebuttal:**
*For goods with elastic supply (food, manufactured goods, services):* Inflation risk is
low. Producers respond to increased demand by increasing supply. The U.S. food system
has enormous excess production capacity; SNAP recipients eating more does not meaningfully
increase food prices.

*For goods with inelastic supply (housing, urban land):* The inflation concern is real
and specific. The correct response is not to withhold the income floor but to simultaneously
address supply constraints through DED infrastructure programs and zoning reform. The NIT
paired with housing supply expansion is the right policy package; the NIT alone in a
supply-constrained market does partly transfer value to landlords.

*Scale consideration:* The NIT transfer is approximately 2–3% of GDP. The Federal
Reserve's inflation models suggest that a demand shock of this scale, phased in over 5
years, is within the range of manageable inflation pressure — particularly if the revenue
sources are themselves deflationary (taxing financial transactions does not directly
increase money in circulation; it redirects existing flows).

**Outstanding:** Needs a dedicated inflation modeling section with supply-side pairing
requirements by category. Housing is the critical vulnerability; document the supply-side
complement required.

### A-3: Work Disincentive — Will People Stop Working?

**The objection:** If survival is guaranteed without working, some people will choose
not to work. The economic literature on income effects and labor supply predicts that
unearned income reduces work — this is not a moral claim, it is a standard economic
prediction that applies to all income, including investment returns and inheritances.
A large guaranteed income will reduce aggregate labor supply.

**The strongest version:** The substitution and income effects are different in magnitude.
For primary earners in households near the poverty line, the income effect (reduced need
to work) may dominate. For secondary earners (often women with young children), the
opportunity to exit the labor force may be attractive enough that a meaningful reduction
in labor supply occurs. Labor shortages in low-wage sectors could follow.

**The DED rebuttal:**
*The evidence from pilots is mixed but does not confirm the feared collapse:* The
Manitoba Mincome experiment (1974–79) found significant reduction in work hours for
one group only: new mothers and teenagers (who stayed in school longer). Employment
among primary earners was essentially unchanged. The Finnish UBI pilot (2017–18) found
improved wellbeing and no significant reduction in employment. The Stockton SEED pilot
found increased full-time employment among recipients. The Kenya GiveDirectly multi-year
program found long-run positive effects on economic activity.

*The Negative Income Tax design specifically addresses this:* The 30% phase-out rate
means that working always pays. A zero-income recipient who earns $1,000 loses $300 in
NIT benefits, netting $700. Compare this to TANF + SNAP + Medicaid cliff effects where
earning $1,000 can cost $1,500 in lost benefits. The NIT is the only design that
guarantees a positive marginal return to work at all income levels in the phase-out range.

*Labor shortages in low-wage jobs are a design feature, not a bug:* If the NIT causes
workers to exit the worst-paying, worst-condition jobs in sufficient numbers that those
jobs must raise wages or automate, the economy has become more efficient. The labor
shortage in awful jobs is the mechanism by which the NIT improves labor market outcomes,
not evidence of the program's failure.

**Outstanding:** Need a dedicated section on secondary earner and caregiver labor supply
effects, which are the most empirically supported reduction and also the most socially
ambiguous (a mother leaving the labor market to care for children is a labor supply
reduction; it is also care work provision, which has its own economic value).

### A-4: Immigration Pressure

**The objection:** A guaranteed income for U.S. citizens creates a significant financial
incentive for immigration — legal and unauthorized — as people from lower-income countries
seek access to the benefit. Even if the benefit is restricted to citizens, the existence
of a large permanent income floor creates pressure to become a citizen, increasing
immigration. More practically, the political debate around immigration becomes even more
charged: "open borders mean open welfare rolls."

**The strongest version:** In a world where the NIT pays $6,200/year unconditionally
to all citizens, citizenship has a calculable monetary value beyond voting rights. This
changes the political economy of immigration and naturalization in ways that are difficult
to predict. At the extreme (politically unlikely but worth modeling), immigration pressure
could expand the eligible population faster than the program's revenue base, eroding
per-capita benefits over time.

**The DED rebuttal:**
The NIT is restricted to citizens — not legal residents, not green card holders, not
visa holders. The path from undocumented to citizen averages 15+ years currently; the
program does not change that path. Immigration pressure on citizenship is driven by many
factors (safety, family, opportunity); the marginal effect of a $6,200/year benefit on
migration decisions is real but not the dominant driver.

*The political management:* The citizenship restriction is both the policy design and
the political message. The NIT is explicitly a dividend of citizenship, not a global
poverty program. The same framing that makes the American Sovereign Fund dividend
politically durable (it's your money as a citizen, not welfare for anyone who shows up)
applies here.

**Outstanding:** Model the immigration effect at different NIT guarantee levels. The
effect at $6,200/year is probably modest; at $20,000/year it would be substantial. This
sets a ceiling on long-run benefit levels that must be understood.

### A-5: Replaces Better-Targeted Programs

**The objection:** A universal income floor is less efficient than targeted programs. The
same $800B that funds a $6,200 floor for all low-income households could fund $40,000/
year for the 20 million most deeply impoverished Americans — or $80,000/year for the
10 million in extreme poverty. Universality is wasteful; targeting the neediest with
larger transfers produces more poverty reduction per dollar spent.

**The strongest version:** Disabled Americans, elderly Americans, single parents of young
children, and homeless individuals all have needs that far exceed a flat income floor.
The NIT does not address them differentially. A person who requires $50,000/year in
personal care assistance receives $6,200 and is left dramatically short. Universal income
floors are not a substitute for disability programs, housing vouchers, or targeted
services — but if the political dynamic makes them substitutes in practice (Congress
replaces targeted programs with the NIT and calls it done), the most vulnerable are
harmed.

**The DED rebuttal:**
The NIT is designed as a floor, not a ceiling. It does not preclude targeted programs
for specific populations (disability, housing, mental health). The DED design explicitly
retains Medicaid, disability benefits, and targeted housing assistance alongside the NIT.
The question is whether Congress will honor this in practice or use the NIT as a vehicle
to eliminate other programs. This is a political risk, not an inherent design flaw —
and it is mitigated by designing the NIT as an addition to, rather than replacement for,
the programs serving the most vulnerable.

The efficiency objection has a fatal assumption: that a highly targeted program actually
reaches the people it targets. The evidence on means-tested program take-up is poor.
Only 50–80% of eligible households actually enroll in most programs, due to administrative
barriers, stigma, and complexity. A universal NIT with automatic enrollment has near-100%
take-up by design. The "inefficient universality" that distributes $6,200 to 50 million
households with near-100% take-up may produce more actual poverty reduction than the
"efficient targeting" that reaches 65% of the people it intends to help.

### A-6: Inflation of Benefit — Program Doesn't Keep Pace With Cost of Living

**The objection:** A $6,200 floor sounds meaningful today but may be inadequate in 10
years if the program is not indexed to inflation. Political pressure to cut rather than
expand the benefit during budget crises could leave the floor below subsistence. Alaska's
Permanent Fund Dividend was raided for budget needs; the same will happen federally.

**The DED rebuttal:**
The NIT is indexed to CPI as a design requirement in the enabling legislation — the
guarantee floor adjusts annually with inflation, not through legislative action. The
sequestration immunity and supermajority amendment requirement (borrowed from the ASF
structure) protect the floor from budget raids. But the concern is legitimate: legislative
protection is imperfect. The long-run answer is the ASF dividend, which grows with the
economy rather than just keeping pace with inflation.

### A-7: "People Will Waste It"

**The objection:** Unconditional cash transfers will be wasted on alcohol, drugs, or
frivolous consumption by some recipients. Targeted programs (SNAP, housing vouchers,
childcare subsidies) ensure that transfers are used for necessities. Giving poor people
cash assumes a rationality and financial discipline that empirical evidence does not
universally support.

**This is the weakest objection in the catalog.** The evidence base refutes it directly.

**Evidence:**
- GiveDirectly's multi-country cash transfer research consistently finds that recipients
  spend cash transfers on food, housing, and productive assets — not alcohol and tobacco
- The alcohol and tobacco consumption rate among cash transfer recipients in controlled
  studies is equal to or lower than control groups
- The UK's "cash first" homelessness experiment (2021) gave rough sleepers £3,000 with
  no conditions: 18 months later, participants spent 36% less on alcohol and drugs than
  before and had significantly better housing and employment outcomes
- The behavioral economics case for paternalistic targeting assumes that poor people
  have worse decision-making than the general population — an assumption not supported
  by evidence and laden with class bias

**Acknowledge the outliers:** Some recipients will make poor choices. Some recipients
of Social Security, Medicare, home mortgage interest deductions, and 401(k) tax
preferences also make poor choices. The argument for restricting transfers to "good"
uses applies consistently across all government transfers or to none of them. Restricting
only cash transfers to the poor while leaving middle-class transfers unrestricted is not
a principled policy position.

### A-8: Doesn't Address Structural Inequality

**The objection:** An income floor addresses income poverty but not wealth inequality,
which is the more fundamental driver of intergenerational disadvantage. A family with
zero income but substantial assets is not in poverty in any meaningful sense; a family
with moderate income but massive debt and zero assets may be deeply precarious despite
being above the NIT phase-out threshold. The NIT does nothing for the wealth gap.

**The DED rebuttal — partial:**
This objection is correct. The NIT is an income floor, not a wealth redistribution
mechanism. The American Sovereign Fund dividend partially addresses wealth accumulation
by giving every citizen a growing equity stake in a national investment vehicle —
over 30 years, every eligible American accumulates a meaningful ASF "account" in the
form of future dividend claims. But this does not address the wealth gap between existing
asset holders and non-holders.

The objection identifies a real gap in the DED program suite. The correct response is
not to reject the NIT but to recognize that it addresses one dimension (income precarity)
while other instruments (estate tax enhancement, capital gains surtax, potential wealth
tax — see funding catalog B-6) address the wealth dimension. No single program addresses
all dimensions of inequality; the question is whether the portfolio does.

**Outstanding:** A dedicated wealth inequality analysis is needed. The NIT is necessary
but not sufficient for economic equity.

---

### A-9: International Competitiveness

**The objection:** A guaranteed income floor raises reservation wages (the minimum wage
a worker will accept to take a job). Higher reservation wages mean higher labor costs for
U.S. employers. Higher labor costs reduce competitiveness in global markets, particularly
for manufacturing, where U.S. wages are already high relative to competitors in Asia and
Latin America.

**The DED rebuttal:**
The competitiveness objection proves too much: taken to its logical conclusion, the U.S.
should eliminate the minimum wage, end overtime pay requirements, and remove all worker
protections to maximize competitiveness. This is not a serious policy position.

The DED design addresses this directly: automation is the correct response to high-wage
labor costs, not wage suppression. The Technology Automation Tax and strategic investment
in manufacturing automation through the American Sovereign Fund Strategic Reserve are
specifically designed to maintain manufacturing output with high-wage labor by
substituting capital for the labor cost differential. U.S. manufacturing competitiveness
is maintained through productivity, not through poverty wages.

Additionally: in traded goods sectors (manufacturing), the competitiveness concern is
real but limited — the U.S. is already uncompetitive on pure labor cost against Vietnam
and Bangladesh, regardless of whether the NIT exists. In non-traded sectors (healthcare,
construction, food service, retail), the competitiveness concern is irrelevant — these
jobs cannot be offshored. The NIT affects both equally but the policy relevance of
competitiveness applies to only one.

---

### A-10: Cliff Effects at the Phase-Out Boundary

**The objection:** The NIT phase-out creates a new cliff: at the phase-out income of
~$20,667, a worker goes from receiving a partial NIT benefit to receiving nothing. While
less severe than current program cliffs (where losing Medicaid can cost thousands at a
specific income dollar), the NIT cliff is still a behavioral distortion.

**The DED rebuttal:**
This is technically true and is a design challenge to manage carefully. At a 30% phase-
out rate, the cliff at $20,667 represents the final marginal dollar of a benefit that has
been declining at 30 cents per dollar earned since the first dollar of income. By the
time the benefit reaches zero, it is a very small amount — $0 at $20,667 means the last
dollar of benefit was $0.30 at $20,666. This is a gradual slope, not a cliff.

The DED design uses a smooth phase-out function (not a step function) specifically to
prevent cliff effects. The larger concern is the interaction with other programs:
if Medicaid eligibility is set at 138% of the poverty line and the NIT phase-out occurs
below that threshold, the combination is smooth. Program designers must carefully map
the interaction of the NIT phase-out against all remaining program thresholds to prevent
new cliffs from forming.

|-------|----------|-------|--------|-------------|
