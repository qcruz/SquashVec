# Civilian Reserve Health Program (CRHP)

**Proposal Type:** Structural Legislative Proposal
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Status:** Concept — requires legislative drafting, actuarial review, and DoD/HHS coordination

---

## The Problem This Solves

The United States spends more on healthcare per capita than any nation on earth (~$13,500/year
in 2025) while leaving ~25 million people uninsured and tens of millions underinsured. Every
major economic disruption — pandemic, job loss, natural disaster — is amplified by healthcare
financial fragility: people delay treatment because they cannot afford it, return to work sick
because they cannot afford to stay home, and face bankruptcy from medical bills that destabilize
household finances and suppress consumer participation in the broader economy.

The DED has already identified this as a compounding vulnerability: in the pandemic scenario,
healthcare financial insecurity directly undermines quarantine compliance. In the AI unemployment
scenario, displaced workers lose employer-sponsored insurance precisely when they are most
vulnerable. In the oil restriction scenario, financial stress from healthcare costs reduces
the household resilience needed to absorb energy price shocks.

The political obstacle to universal healthcare has never primarily been cost — the U.S. already
spends enough per capita that universal coverage is arithmetically feasible — it has been the
lack of a politically viable **delivery mechanism** that does not look like a government takeover
of private insurance markets.

The Civilian Reserve Health Program offers that mechanism: universal healthcare delivered
through the existing military healthcare infrastructure, funded by a service-linked enrollment
fee, phased in conservatively from the lowest-risk age cohort, and expanded only as each phase
proves financially solvent.

## Core Concept

Any U.S. civilian between the ages of **18 and 34** (initial bracket) may voluntarily enroll
in the Civilian Reserve Health Program. Enrollment provides:

1. **Full TRICARE-equivalent healthcare coverage** — same coverage available to active-duty
   military members and their families; no exclusions for pre-existing conditions; no lifetime
   caps; mental health parity

2. **Civilian Reserve status** — enrollee is designated a non-combat Civilian Reserve member;
   subject to periodic training and eligible for emergency mobilization in non-combat roles
   during federally declared national emergencies

3. **No change to civilian life under normal conditions** — enrollee retains their job, their
   residence, their civilian identity; reserve status is dormant except for training obligations
   and emergency call-up

In exchange, the enrollee:

1. **Pays an annual enrollment fee** — $600/year ($50/month) at program launch; income-scaled
   for households below 200% of federal poverty line (reduced to $150–$300/year)

2. **Fulfills a training obligation** — one 2-day training weekend per quarter (8 days/year)
   + one 5-day annual training event; total ~13 days/year; compensated at E-1 military pay
   rate for training days

3. **Accepts emergency call-up eligibility** — in a federally declared DED emergency, may be
   called to active non-combat service for up to 180 days; compensation at active-duty pay
   rate during service; civilian employer protections (USERRA) apply

## Why Military Healthcare Is the Right Vehicle

### It Already Exists at Scale

TRICARE, the military healthcare system, covers approximately 9.6 million beneficiaries
(active duty, retirees, and families). It operates a network of military treatment facilities
(MTFs), a civilian provider network (TRICARE Select), and a pharmacy system. The administrative
infrastructure is built and running.

Expanding enrollment does not require building a new system. It requires expanding the
beneficiary pool into a new eligibility category and expanding the provider network capacity
to serve them — a scaling problem, not a construction problem.

### Administrative Overhead Is Dramatically Lower

| System | Administrative Overhead |
|--------|------------------------|
| Commercial private insurance | 12–20% of premiums |
| Medicaid (federal/state) | 6–8% |
| Medicare | 2–3% |
| TRICARE | 3–5% |

Every percentage point of administrative efficiency recaptured from the commercial insurance
market is additional coverage capacity. At scale, the efficiency difference alone funds
millions of additional covered lives.

### Pre-Existing Conditions Are Already Covered

Military healthcare does not use underwriting. Active-duty members and their families receive
coverage regardless of medical history. This eliminates the political fight over pre-existing
condition exclusions that has derailed every prior reform attempt. The program simply applies
the existing military coverage model to a new eligible population.

### The Service Obligation Creates Political Legitimacy

The persistent objection to universal healthcare in the U.S. is that it is "something for
nothing" — redistribution without reciprocity. The Civilian Reserve model answers this directly:
enrollment is earned through service obligation. Enrollees are not receiving a free benefit;
they are receiving compensation (healthcare coverage) for accepting a contingent service
commitment. This framing has bipartisan appeal — it is simultaneously universal coverage and
a civic service program.

## Enrollment Structure

### Phase 1 — Initial Bracket (Ages 18–34)

**Rationale for this bracket:**
- Lowest healthcare utilization of any age group ($2,800–$4,200/year average)
- Highest training value — young adults in their physical prime for reserve mobilization
- Largest coverage gap — this group has the highest uninsured rate of any non-elderly
  adult bracket (~15–18%) because they age off parents' insurance at 26 and
  typically work in lower-wage, non-benefit-providing employment
- Lowest actuarial risk for program launch — a pool of predominantly healthy young adults
  is the most favorable risk pool for establishing solvency

**Estimated eligible population:** ~65 million U.S. adults aged 18–34
**Projected initial enrollment:** 15–25 million in years 1–3 (conservative); see solvency-model.md

**Training tracks by civilian occupation:**

| Track | Target Occupation | Training Focus | DED Scenario Value |
|-------|------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Medical Reserve | Healthcare workers, students | Triage, mass casualty, field medicine | Pandemic surge capacity |
| Infrastructure | Electricians, engineers, IT | Grid repair, comms, water systems | Grid attack, industrial |
| Logistics | Drivers, warehouse, supply chain | Emergency freight, distribution | All scenarios |
| Agricultural | Farmers, food workers | Field emergency ops, supply chain | Drought, pandemic |
| Cyber Reserve | IT, software, security | Network defense, SCADA security | Grid attack, semiconductor |
| General Reserve | All others | First aid, emergency response, disaster relief | All scenarios |

---

### Phase 2 — Expansion to Ages 35–49

**Trigger conditions for Phase 2 expansion** (see phase-in-schedule.md for full criteria):
- Phase 1 program is actuarially solvent for 3 consecutive years
- Enrollment has reached minimum 10 million participants
- DoD MTF network capacity expanded to absorb additional volume
- Independent actuarial review certifies Phase 2 is viable at proposed fee structure

**Actuarial adjustment:** Healthcare utilization increases significantly in the 35–49 bracket
($5,000–$8,500/year average). The Phase 2 fee structure is correspondingly adjusted, but
the cross-subsidy from the large healthy Phase 1 pool maintains system-wide solvency.

**Estimated eligible population added:** ~60 million U.S. adults aged 35–49

---

### Phase 3 — Expansion to Ages 50–64

**Trigger conditions:** Phase 2 solvent for 3 years; actuarial review; fee adjustment.

**Note:** This is the highest-utilization, highest-cost age group before Medicare eligibility.
Average healthcare cost $9,000–$14,000/year. This phase requires the largest cross-subsidy
from younger enrollees and is therefore the most sensitive to enrollment volume in earlier phases.

**Training obligation modified:** Ages 50–64 have a reduced training obligation (5 days/year)
focused on advisory, logistics, and skills-transfer roles rather than physical mobilization.

---

### Phase 4 — Full Adult Coverage (Ages 65+, Medicare Integration)

**This phase requires separate legislation** coordinating with Medicare. TRICARE for Life
(TFL) already provides supplemental coverage for military retirees over 65, so the model
exists. Full integration would convert CRHP into a universal adult coverage system, at which
point the program's solvency and the political case for expansion are both established.

## Fee Structure

### Standard Annual Fee

| Age Group | Annual Fee | Monthly Equivalent | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| 18–25 | $480/year | $40/month | Lowest utilization; highest uninsured rate |
| 26–34 | $720/year | $60/month | Slightly higher utilization; income typically rising |
| 35–49 (Phase 2) | $1,080/year | $90/month | Mid-utilization; cross-subsidized by Phase 1 pool |
| 50–64 (Phase 3) | $1,560/year | $130/month | Higher utilization; still well below market rate |

**Comparison to market:** A 30-year-old purchasing ACA Silver Plan pays average $450–$600/month
in 2025. CRHP at $60/month is an 85–90% cost reduction for the same individual, with better
coverage (no network surprise billing, no pre-existing condition exclusion, no lifetime cap).

### Income-Scaled Reduction

| Household Income | Fee Reduction | Effective Annual Fee (Age 26–34) |
|-----------------|---------------|----------------------------------|
| <100% FPL | 80% reduction | $144/year |
| 100–150% FPL | 60% reduction | $288/year |
| 150–200% FPL | 40% reduction | $432/year |
| 200–400% FPL | 0% | $720/year |
| >400% FPL | +20% surcharge | $864/year |

Income-scaled structure ensures the lowest-income enrollees — who disproportionately lack
coverage today — have the lowest barrier to enrollment.

### Training Day Compensation

Training days (compensated at E-1 military pay rate, ~$150/day) partially offset the fee:
13 training days × $150 = $1,950/year in compensation.

For lower-income enrollees, training compensation exceeds the annual fee — making CRHP
effectively a *net positive* cash arrangement: healthcare coverage + $1,950 in compensation
in exchange for 13 days of service and call-up eligibility.

## Training Obligation Design

### Annual Commitment
- **4 quarterly training weekends:** Saturday–Sunday, 8am–6pm; 8 training days/year
- **1 annual training event:** 5 consecutive days (typically summer); skills-intensive
- **Total:** ~13 days/year; ~104 hours
- **Location:** Nearest military installation or CRHP-designated civilian training facility
- **Scheduling flexibility:** Weekend scheduling with 90-day advance notice; hardship
  deferral available for documented work, childcare, or health conflict

### Training Content by Phase
**Years 1–2 (Foundation):**
- Emergency first aid and CPR (Red Cross equivalent, military standard)
- Basic disaster response: triage, shelter operations, supply distribution
- Physical fitness baseline (pass/fail, not performance-ranked; accommodations for disability)
- Communication protocols and chain of command in emergency operations

**Years 3+ (Specialization):**
- Enrollee selects a track (Medical, Infrastructure, Logistics, Agricultural, Cyber, General)
- Track-specific curriculum deepens each year
- Cross-training in second track available after year 3

### Emergency Call-Up
- **Trigger:** Federally declared DED national emergency or military emergency declaration
- **Notice:** 30-day advance notice in non-emergency; 72-hour notice in acute emergency
- **Duration:** Up to 180 days per deployment; 12-month minimum rest period between deployments
- **Roles:** Non-combat only — medical support, logistics, infrastructure repair, communications,
  food distribution, disaster response, cybersecurity defense
- **Pay:** Active-duty rate for the assigned rank equivalent; civilian job protected under USERRA
- **Voluntary combat waiver:** Enrollees may separately opt in to combat-eligible reserve
  status; this is not required for CRHP enrollment

## Healthcare Coverage Scope

CRHP provides TRICARE Prime equivalent coverage:

| Coverage Category | CRHP Benefit |
|------------------|-------------|
| Primary care | Covered; $0 copay at MTF; $20 at network civilian provider |
| Specialist care | Covered; $30 copay with referral |
| Emergency care | Covered; $60 copay |
| Inpatient | Covered; $0 at MTF; $250/day civilian network (max $750/year) |
| Mental health | Parity with medical coverage; telehealth included |
| Prescription drugs | Covered; $11–$46 copay by formulary tier |
| Preventive care | Fully covered; no copay |
| Pre-existing conditions | No exclusions; no waiting period |
| Dental (basic) | Included; major dental at additional premium |
| Vision | Basic exam covered; eyewear at subsidy |
| Pregnancy and maternity | Fully covered |
| Substance use disorder | Covered; parity with mental health |
| Lifetime cap | None |

**What CRHP does not include (at initial launch):**
- Long-term care (nursing home, assisted living) — separate program
- Cosmetic procedures
- Experimental treatments outside approved trials

## DED Integration

This is not incidental to the DED — it is a core resilience investment.

### Pre-Trained Civilian Reserve for Scenario Response

Every DED scenario identified a human capital constraint — there are not enough nurses,
electricians, logistics workers, or cybersecurity personnel available for rapid deployment
in a crisis. The CRHP training program is the peacetime infrastructure that builds this reserve.

At full Phase 1 enrollment (~20 million participants across 6 specialty tracks):

| Track | Estimated Enrollees | DED Scenario Deployment |
|-------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Medical Reserve | ~3M | Pandemic: hospital surge capacity, vaccination, contact tracing |
| Infrastructure | ~2M | Grid attack: transformer installation, line repair, SCADA support |
| Logistics | ~5M | All: freight management, food distribution, supply chain continuity |
| Agricultural | ~1M | Drought: emergency crop management, food system logistics |
| Cyber Reserve | ~1M | Grid/semiconductor: network defense, IRS/financial system protection |
| General Reserve | ~8M | All: disaster relief, shelter operations, first aid deployment |

This is the DED's answer to the workforce gap across every scenario. Unlike a traditional
military reserve that trains for combat, the CRHP trains specifically for the non-combat
economic emergency response that DED scenarios require.

### Healthcare Resilience as Economic Stabilizer

Every DED economic model is sensitive to healthcare financial fragility. The pandemic scenario
improvement from 45% to 55% DED mitigation is partially driven by better quarantine compliance —
which depends on workers being able to afford to stay home. CRHP removes healthcare cost as a
barrier to compliance for 20+ million Phase 1 enrollees, and eventually for the full adult
population.

The oil restriction fuel security dividend works better when recipient households have a stable
healthcare cost baseline. The AI unemployment conditional displacement transfer is more effective
when displaced workers don't face a simultaneous healthcare coverage cliff.

CRHP is, in DED terms, a force multiplier on every other tool in the library.

## Political Framing

The program is intentionally designed to be arguable from multiple political perspectives:

**Conservative framing:** It is a service-for-coverage model, not welfare. Enrollees earn
their coverage through training and call-up obligation. It expands the military reserve and
national readiness. It does not replace private insurance — it competes with it by offering
a better-value alternative.

**Progressive framing:** It achieves universal coverage regardless of pre-existing conditions,
employment status, or income. The income-scaled fee ensures the lowest-income enrollees pay
the least. The training compensation makes it net-positive for lower-income participants.

**Fiscal conservative framing:** It is phased — each expansion is only triggered by proven
solvency of the prior phase. It leverages existing military infrastructure rather than building
a new bureaucracy. The enrollment fee provides a revenue stream rather than pure government
expenditure. The long-run effect on national healthcare costs (prevention emphasis, earlier
treatment, reduced ER use) reduces total healthcare spending.

**Military readiness framing:** It expands the non-combat reserve by millions of trained
personnel with civilian skills. It builds the logistics, medical, infrastructure, and cyber
capacity the military has consistently identified as its most critical civilian-skill gaps.
The DED scenario response capacity this creates is a national security asset.

## Relationship to Existing Programs

| Program | Relationship to CRHP |
|---------|---------------------|
| ACA / Marketplace | CRHP is an alternative, not a replacement. Enrollees who prefer ACA or employer coverage can keep it. CRHP competes on value. |
| Medicaid | Coordination: low-income enrollees below 100% FPL who qualify for Medicaid can dual-enroll; CRHP becomes their primary coverage and reduces Medicaid cost |
| Medicare | Phase 4 integration; separate legislation required |
| National Guard / Army Reserve | Parallel programs; CRHP is non-combat focused; Guard/Reserve retain their distinct combat mission and benefits |
| TRICARE for Active Duty | CRHP is a new eligibility category; does not affect active duty coverage |
| VA Healthcare | Separate system for veterans; coordination on mental health and transition services |

## Next Steps for Development

1. **Actuarial review:** Independent actuarial analysis of Phase 1 enrollment scenarios,
   utilization projections, and fee solvency. See `solvency-model.md`.

2. **Legal/constitutional review:** DoD authority to create new reserve categories; Commerce
   Clause basis for enrollment; USERRA interaction; anti-discrimination provisions.

3. **DoD capacity assessment:** MTF network capacity for additional beneficiaries; civilian
   network expansion requirements; training facility identification.

4. **Legislative drafting:** Core authorization act; amendments to Title 10 (Armed Forces)
   and Title 10 USC chapters covering TRICARE.

5. **Pilot design:** Pre-legislative pilot under existing reserve authority in 2–3 states;
   measure enrollment interest, utilization patterns, training completion rates.
