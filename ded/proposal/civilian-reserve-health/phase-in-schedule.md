# CRHP Phase-In Schedule and Expansion Criteria

**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Companion:** [overview.md](overview.md) | [solvency-model.md](solvency-model.md)

---

## Design Principle

The phase-in schedule is deliberately conservative. Each expansion is gated by demonstrated
solvency, sufficient enrollment, and confirmed infrastructure capacity — not by a fixed
calendar date. No phase advances automatically. Political pressure to expand faster than the
actuarial basis supports should be resisted; the program's credibility rests on the
self-funding discipline.

The phasing also serves a second function: it builds a constituency. By the time Phase 1 is
proven and Phase 2 is proposed, 20–30M Americans have direct experience with CRHP coverage.
They are the political base for expansion. This is the same dynamics that made Medicare
politically irreversible after its first decade.

---

## Phase 0: Pre-Legislative Pilot (Years –2 to 0)

### Purpose
Validate enrollment mechanics, utilization projections, training compliance, and network
capacity before full legislative authorization.

### Authority
Can be executed under existing DoD reserve authority (Title 10 USC §12301 et seq.) and
TRICARE demonstration authority, without new legislation. Requires Secretarial waiver from
DoD and HHS.

### Scope
- 3 pilot states (recommended: Texas, Virginia, Colorado — geographic, demographic, and
  military-base diversity)
- Age 18–29 only for pilot
- 50,000–200,000 enrollees per state; voluntary, opt-in
- Full tracking: enrollment demographics, claims utilization, training completion, dropout rates

### What the Pilot Must Demonstrate
| Metric | Minimum Threshold to Proceed |
|--------|------------------------------|
| Enrollment uptake rate (% of eligible who enroll) | ≥8% in Year 1 |
| Training completion rate | ≥75% of enrolled complete annual obligation |
| Actual vs. projected claims cost | Actual ≤ 115% of projection |
| Opt-out rate after Year 1 | ≤20% |
| Network adequacy (wait times) | Primary care appointment within 14 days for ≥90% |

### Pilot Outputs
- Enrollment demand model calibrated to actual uptake
- Claims cost model validated against actual utilization
- Training logistics refined (scheduling, facility adequacy, hardship deferral rates)
- Network expansion requirements quantified
- Legislative language drafted and refined based on pilot learnings

## Phase 1: Initial Authorization (Ages 18–34, Years 1–7)

### Entry Trigger
Pilot meets all minimum thresholds + Congressional authorization enacted.

### Year 1–2: Ramp-Up
**Enrollment target:** 3–5M by end of Year 2
**Priority outreach:**
- Uninsured young adults (highest value proposition; ~7M in this age bracket)
- University and community college campuses (large, accessible, word-of-mouth friendly)
- Gig economy workers and self-employed (no employer-sponsored coverage; high interest)
- Employers with <50 employees (exempt from ACA employer mandate; CRHP is natural alternative)

**Infrastructure:**
- MTF network expansion in 10 highest-population metros
- Civilian network contracting in all 50 states
- IRS/SSA enrollment interface (federal agencies coordinate on eligibility verification)
- Prepaid debit card for training compensation disbursement to unbanked enrollees

**Training launch:**
- Track selection opens Year 2 (Year 1: foundation training only)
- First quarterly training weekends begin Month 6

---

### Year 3–5: Scale
**Enrollment target:** 15–25M by end of Year 5
**Scaling initiatives:**
- Employer CRHP contribution option: employers can contribute $X/month toward employee
  CRHP fee in lieu of sponsoring commercial group insurance; creates business incentive
  to shift employees toward CRHP (reduces employer insurance admin burden)
- Military installation expansion: all major installations hosting CRHP training weekends
- Telehealth integration: primary care and mental health via telehealth counted toward
  network access; expands effective coverage in rural areas without physical MTF expansion
- Annual marketing campaign targeting ACA marketplace comparison (CRHP saves average
  enrollee $4,800–$7,200/year vs. marketplace Silver plan)

---

### Year 5–7: Solvency Assessment
**Formal actuarial review:** Independent actuarial firm retained by DoD and HHS jointly.

**Full Phase 1 Solvency Scorecard:**

| Criterion | Target | Measured |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Enrollment | ≥10M | |
| 3-year claims cost vs. projection | Within ±10% | |
| Emergency reserve fund balance | ≥6 months claims | |
| Training completion rate (annual average) | ≥72% | |
| Network adequacy (primary care) | ≥90% within 14 days | |
| Dropout rate (annual) | ≤15% | |
| Fee revenue / total cost ratio | ≥11% | |
| DoD reserve mobilization readiness (trained enrollees) | ≥8M in assigned tracks | |

All criteria met → Phase 2 advancement approved.
Any criterion not met → remediation plan required; re-review at next annual cycle.

## Phase 2: Age Expansion to 35–49 (Years 8–15 estimated)

### Entry Trigger
Phase 1 solvency scorecard met for 3 consecutive years + Phase 2 actuarial certification
+ DoD network capacity confirmed for additional volume.

### Fee Adjustment
Phase 2 fee is set by actuarial review at program entry, not fixed in advance. The overview
document estimates $1,080/year for ages 35–49; the actual figure depends on Phase 1 utilization
data and enrollment projections at the time of expansion.

### New Provisions for Phase 2

**Employer integration deepened:**
At Phase 2 scale (~40M+ enrollees), CRHP becomes large enough to negotiate directly with
major hospital systems and specialty networks. This enables:
- Direct contracting with top 50 health systems (bypassing insurance intermediaries entirely)
- Global budget arrangements for enrolled population (fixed annual payment to health system
  per CRHP enrollee in their region; creates incentive for prevention and efficiency)
- Pharmaceutical price negotiation authority: DoD already negotiates drug prices under
  the Federal Supply Schedule; Phase 2 scale makes CRHP a major pharmaceutical purchaser

**Modified training obligation (Ages 35–49):**
- Same track structure as Phase 1
- Scheduling accommodation: 45+ day advance notice standard (vs. 30 days for Phase 1)
- Physical fitness requirement adjusted for age cohort norms; emphasis on skills over physical
- Leadership track opened: experienced Phase 1 enrollees (now in their late 30s) can qualify
  as Track Leaders, supervising and training newer Phase 2 enrollees

**Cross-phase dynamic:**
Phase 1 enrollees who age into the 35–49 bracket automatically transition to Phase 2 fee
structure at their birthday. For original Phase 1 enrollees, a 3-year grandfathered rate
(paying 90% of Phase 2 rate) cushions the transition. This is the most politically sensitive
moment in the program lifecycle — a fee increase for existing enrollees — and must be handled
with advance notice, clear communication, and a genuine value comparison (still saving $3,000–
$6,000/year vs. commercial alternatives).

## Phase 3: Age Expansion to 50–64 (Years 16–25 estimated)

### Entry Trigger
Phase 2 solvency scorecard met for 3 consecutive years + Phase 3 actuarial certification
+ supplemental funding mechanism identified and authorized.

### Supplemental Funding Mechanism Requirement

Phase 3 is the only expansion phase that likely cannot be sustained on enrollment fees alone
at a politically viable fee level. Three supplemental mechanisms are proposed; one must be
authorized before Phase 3 launches:

**Option A — Employer Healthcare Tax Credit Conversion:**
Current law allows employers to deduct group health insurance premiums. Employers who shift
employees to CRHP coverage (contributing to their enrollment fee) receive a tax credit equal
to 60% of their prior insurance premium deduction. The revenue savings from reduced commercial
insurance deductions partially offset the Phase 3 subsidy.

**Option B — DED Value Discovery Revenue Allocation:**
A portion (5–10%) of revenue from DED intellectual property licensing, platform subscriptions,
and trade agreement proceeds is dedicated to the CRHP trust fund. At value-discovery revenue
projections of $30–100B/year at full maturity, this contribution covers $1.5–10B of Phase 3
subsidy costs. This is the direct link between the DED's economic productivity and the
domestic healthcare resilience the DED's scenario analysis depends on.

**Option C — Early Medicare Buy-In:**
Adults aged 55–64 may pay the Medicare Part A+B equivalent premium (~$700/month) to buy into
Medicare early, exiting CRHP. This reduces Phase 3 CRHP enrollment for the highest-cost
segment and allows Medicare's larger risk pool to absorb them. The 55–64 population pays
the same Medicare premium they would have paid at 65, just earlier.

### Modified Training Obligation (Ages 50–64)
- 5 days/year (vs. 13 for Phase 1)
- Focus: advisory, mentorship, logistics, and skills-transfer roles
- Physical fitness: no requirement; skills assessment only
- Emergency call-up: advisory and logistics roles only; 90-day advance notice minimum
- Compensation: same E-1 rate per training day; $750/year in compensation
  (5 days × $150/day)

## Phase 4: Universal Adult Coverage (65+, Medicare Integration)

### This Phase Is a Separate Legislative Act

Phase 4 requires coordinating with Medicare, which is governed by separate statute and
administered by CMS. CRHP Phase 4 would convert the program into a true universal adult
coverage system by integrating CRHP coverage for 65+ with Medicare.

**Possible structures:**
- CRHP replaces Medicare for new retirees who have been CRHP members (TRICARE for Life
  already provides a model for this transition for military retirees)
- CRHP serves as the Medicare Advantage alternative for enrolled retirees
- TRICARE and Medicare merge into a unified federal health system under joint DoD/HHS authority

**Political prerequisite:** By Phase 4, there are likely 60–75M CRHP enrollees across
all ages. The political case for full universal coverage is made by the proven track record
of the program, not by the theoretical argument. Phase 4 is the political harvesting of
Phases 1–3.

## Key Risk Events and Contingency Plans

### Risk: Adverse Selection in Early Enrollment
**Scenario:** Uninsured enrollees have higher utilization than projected because they have
delayed care for pre-existing conditions and seek extensive treatment immediately upon enrollment.

**Probability:** Medium-High. This is the "welcome mat" effect documented in Medicaid
expansion states — newly insured populations seek care at elevated rates in Year 1–2.

**Expected magnitude:** Claims costs may be 20–35% above steady-state projections in
Years 1–2 as deferred care is addressed. This normalizes by Year 3.

**Contingency:** Emergency reserve fund sized to absorb 18 months of adverse selection costs.
Fee structure not adjusted during the adverse selection window — it would be politically
damaging to raise fees when the program is helping the most vulnerable enrollees. The reserve
absorbs the temporary spike; Year 3 actuarial review confirms return to baseline.

---

### Risk: Political Pressure to Expand Too Fast
**Scenario:** Congressional pressure to skip Phase 2 solvency requirements and open Phase 3
enrollment to gain political credit before actuarial conditions are met.

**Mitigation:** Solvency triggers are statutory (written into the authorizing legislation),
not discretionary. Phase expansion requires a joint DoD/HHS actuarial certification. Congress
can amend the law to remove the triggers — but this creates a politically visible accountability
record. The gating criteria are the program's structural defense against populism.

---

### Risk: Military Operational Conflict with CRHP Reserve
**Scenario:** DoD prioritizes active-duty and traditional reserve components; CRHP reserve
is left without training resources or mobilization authority when a DED scenario requires it.

**Mitigation:** CRHP reserve authority is established in the same legislation as the program.
A DED national emergency declaration gives CRHP mobilization authority independent of DoD
operational command. The civilian emergency roles (medical surge, infrastructure repair,
logistics) are distinct from combat command structures and can be activated by DED rather
than DoD chain of command.

---

### Risk: Training Dropout Reduces Reserve Value
**Scenario:** Enrollment is high but training completion rates are low, undermining the
reserve readiness justification for the program's DoD connection.

**Mitigation:** Training completion is an explicit Phase 1 solvency criterion (≥72%).
Hardship deferrals are generous but documented. Enrollees who miss two consecutive training
years face a coverage surcharge ($300/year) until they complete makeup training — creating
financial incentive without punitive coverage termination.
