# DED Proposal: The Economic Flywheel — System Map

**Document Type:** Systems Analysis / Program Integration Map
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Category:** Core Architecture
**DED Priority:** Foundational — without this document, the Department of Economic Defense
  looks like a collection of programs; with it, it looks like a coherent economic operating
  system with identifiable feedback loops and a defined steady state
**Related Proposals:** All — this is the integration layer above every proposal
**Related to:** `ded/proposal/economic-citizenship.md` (the philosophical foundation;
  this document is the operational architecture)

---

## Purpose

The Department of Economic Defense contains more than twenty distinct programs, tools,
and proposals. Taken individually, each has a clear rationale. Taken together, they form
something larger: a self-reinforcing economic system in which each program generates
resources, capabilities, or political support that enables the next.

This document maps that system — its feedback loops, its revenue flows, its labor flows,
its political reinforcement mechanisms, and its projected steady state at full deployment.

The central argument: the programs are not additive. They are multiplicative. A well-
designed economic system generates returns that exceed the sum of its parts, because
each component reduces the cost and increases the effectiveness of every other component.
The Department of Economic Defense is designed to be that kind of system.

---

## 1. The Core Engine: Three Interlocking Cycles

The entire Department of Economic Defense system rests on three interlocking cycles.
Everything else is either an input to these cycles, an output from them, or a mechanism
for protecting their continuity.

---

### Cycle 1: The Productivity-to-Ownership Loop

**The problem it solves:** Automation and globalization have steadily increased economic
productivity while concentrating the gains in capital. Workers contribute to productivity
growth but receive a declining share of it. The market mechanism that is supposed to
correct this — labor market competition for workers — works slowly and imperfectly when
the structural driver of displacement (automation) moves faster than workers can retrain.

**How the loop works:**

```
Automation increases productivity
        ↓
Automation Tax Credit captures fraction of productivity surplus
(tax on large enterprise software and AI deployment)
        ↓
Revenue flows to three destinations:
  40% → Negative Income Tax fund (income floor for displaced workers)
  30% → Civilian Reserve and Health Program training fund (retraining pipeline)
  30% → American Sovereign Fund (citizen wealth accumulation)
        ↓
Displaced workers receive:
  - Income floor via Negative Income Tax (stay solvent while retraining)
  - Retraining pathway via Civilian Reserve or Expert Reserve Program
  - Long-term: growing American Sovereign Fund dividend (permanent ownership stake)
        ↓
Retrained workers enter strategic sectors built by American Sovereign Fund investment
(semiconductor fabs, rare earth processing, pharmaceutical plants, energy infrastructure)
        ↓
Those sectors generate higher-productivity, higher-wage employment
        ↓
Higher-wage employment produces more taxable income
        ↓
More taxable income increases Automation Tax Credit base and general tax revenue
        ↓
Larger tax base supports larger Negative Income Tax floor and American Sovereign Fund contributions
        ↓
[Returns to top: next wave of automation displaces some workers; system catches them]
```

**The key insight:** The Automation Tax Credit is not a tax on innovation. It is a
mechanism for ensuring that the productivity gains from innovation flow to the citizenship
base that made innovation possible — the educated workforce, the public research
infrastructure, the stable society. The loop converts automation from a pure redistributive
threat into a wealth-building engine for the entire citizenry.

**Steady state:** At full deployment with a mature Automation Tax Credit and a mature
American Sovereign Fund, automation-driven productivity growth continuously replenishes
the income floor, training pipeline, and dividend fund. The system does not require
political intervention to sustain these flows — they are embedded in the revenue structure.

---

### Cycle 2: The Defense-to-Capital Loop

**The problem it solves:** The United States spends enormous resources maintaining the
defense industrial base and the geopolitical relationships that make allied arms sales
possible. The domestic citizenry bears the cost — through taxes, through the risks of
military engagement, through the economic distortions of defense spending. The benefits
of that spending accrue primarily to defense contractors and geopolitical stability
(a public good too diffuse to be politically visible).

**How the loop works:**

```
United States sells arms to allies
(Foreign Military Sales + Direct Commercial Sales: ~$265B/year in FY2024)
        ↓
7.5% arms sales levy flows to American Sovereign Fund
(~$20B/year at current volumes)
        ↓
American Sovereign Fund Strategic Reserve deploys capital to:
  - Domestic defense component manufacturers (Tier 2/3 suppliers)
  - Critical materials processing (rare earth, titanium, advanced alloys)
  - Semiconductor fabrication (defense-critical advanced chips)
        ↓
Investment in these sectors:
  - Reduces per-unit cost of defense systems (domestic inputs cheaper and more reliable)
  - Increases competitiveness of U.S. defense exports (lower cost → more sales)
  - Reduces supply chain concentration risk (less dependence on adversarial suppliers)
        ↓
More competitive exports → higher arms sales volume
        ↓
Higher arms sales volume → larger levy → more American Sovereign Fund capital
        ↓
[Returns to top: growing capital base funds deeper strategic sector investment]
```

**The military cost linkage in reverse:** Every commodity cost spike is a defense
procurement cost spike. The standard Department of Economic Defense argument runs forward:
"DED programs reduce commodity costs, which reduces the defense budget." The Defense-to-
Capital Loop runs the same argument backward: "defense exports fund the domestic industrial
capacity that keeps commodity costs down." Both directions are true simultaneously. The
loop is the proof.

**Additional output:** Allied nations that purchase U.S. arms become more dependent on
U.S. spare parts, software, training, and doctrine. This dependency creates diplomatic
leverage that the Department of Economic Defense can convert into favorable trade terms,
IP licensing agreements, and allied investment in shared strategic sectors. The
arms sale is the first transaction in a long-term economic relationship.

### Cycle 3: The Trade-to-Capacity Loop

**The problem it solves:** Tariffs are the most politically durable trade policy instrument
in American history. They are used to protect domestic industries. But they typically
function as permanent subsidies to inefficient producers rather than as temporary bridges
to competitive domestic capacity. The revenue they generate flows into the general fund
and is spent on unrelated programs. The protection and the revenue are never deliberately
connected to the capacity-building that would make the protection unnecessary.

**How the loop works:**

```
Tariffs imposed on imported goods from adversarial or unfair-practice nations
(current tariff revenue: $80–300B/year depending on trade policy regime)
        ↓
20% of tariff revenue flows to American Sovereign Fund
(~$16–60B/year)
        ↓
American Sovereign Fund Strategic Reserve deploys capital to domestic alternatives
in exactly the sectors where tariffs are highest:
  - Chinese rare earth magnets → domestic rare earth processing investment
  - Chinese semiconductors → domestic advanced fabrication investment
  - Chinese pharmaceutical ingredients → domestic active pharmaceutical ingredient
    manufacturing investment
  - Chinese steel and aluminum → domestic advanced materials investment
        ↓
Domestic alternatives develop and mature (5–15 year horizon)
        ↓
As domestic capacity becomes competitive:
  - Import dependency falls
  - Supply chain Concentration Risk Score decreases
  - Geopolitical leverage available to adversary diminishes
  - Tariff barriers can be reduced (protection no longer needed)
        ↓
Reduced tariffs lower consumer prices (recovering some of the protection cost)
        ↓
Mature domestic industries compete globally without protection
        ↓
Export revenue from competitive domestic industries flows back as tax revenue
and American Sovereign Fund returns
        ↓
[New cycle: American Sovereign Fund capital redirects to next concentration risk target]
```

**The key design feature:** The loop has an exit condition. Tariffs are not permanent.
The Trade-to-Capacity Loop is explicitly designed to make itself unnecessary: tariff
revenue funds the capacity that makes the tariff unnecessary. This converts the
standard political economy of tariffs — perpetual protection for organized lobbying
interests — into a temporary bridge to competitive domestic industry. The American
Sovereign Fund's Concentration Risk Score triggers provide the measurement that tells
the Department of Economic Defense when the exit condition is approaching.

## 2. How the Three Cycles Connect

The three cycles are not parallel — they share inputs, outputs, and capital flows.

### Shared Capital Pool: The American Sovereign Fund

All three cycles flow through the American Sovereign Fund:
- Cycle 1 (Productivity-to-Ownership): Automation Tax Credit → American Sovereign Fund
- Cycle 2 (Defense-to-Capital): Arms sales levy → American Sovereign Fund
- Cycle 3 (Trade-to-Capacity): Tariff revenue → American Sovereign Fund

The American Sovereign Fund is therefore the **central capital pool** of the entire system.
It receives contributions from three structurally different revenue sources, which means it
is diversified across economic conditions:
- Automation Tax Credit revenue is highest during periods of rapid technology deployment
- Arms sales levy is highest during periods of geopolitical tension and allied rearmament
- Tariff revenue is highest during periods of trade conflict with adversarial nations

These three conditions are not perfectly correlated. In fact, they are often triggered by
the same events (geopolitical tension simultaneously increases arms sales and trade
conflict), which means the American Sovereign Fund receives an inflow surge precisely when
the Department of Economic Defense most needs capital for strategic investment.

### Shared Labor Pool: The Civilian Reserve and Health Program

All three cycles generate demand for trained workers in strategic sectors. The Civilian
Reserve and Health Program is the labor supply mechanism that responds to this demand:

- Cycle 1: displaced workers from automation move through the retraining pipeline into
  strategic sector jobs
- Cycle 2: domestic defense component manufacturers and materials processors built by
  American Sovereign Fund investment require skilled manufacturing workers
- Cycle 3: domestic alternatives to imported goods — semiconductor fabs, pharmaceutical
  plants, rare earth processing — are capital-intensive but also labor-intensive in
  construction and operation

Civilian Reserve and Health Program members build the facilities that Cycle 2 and Cycle 3
fund. They staff them once built. They receive the dividend from the American Sovereign
Fund that owns those facilities. The labor and the capital are connected: workers build
the assets; the assets generate income; the income returns to the workers as dividend.

This is the **Build-Own-Benefit Loop**: the American Sovereign Fund deploys capital to
build strategic sector capacity; Civilian Reserve and Health Program labor constructs and
operates that capacity; American Sovereign Fund returns flow back as dividends to those
same workers as citizens. The loop converts strategic investment into worker ownership
without requiring any ideological commitment to collective ownership — it happens
automatically through the dividend mechanism.

### Shared Intelligence Layer: The National Economic Intelligence Dashboard

All three cycles depend on the same measurement infrastructure. The National Economic
Intelligence Dashboard monitors:
- Supply chain Concentration Risk Scores (triggers Cycle 3 capital deployment)
- Automation-driven displacement rates by sector and region (triggers Cycle 1 responses)
- Defense procurement cost indices (measures Cycle 2 effectiveness)
- American Sovereign Fund portfolio returns and Strategic Reserve impact (measures all three)
- Leading indicators of scenario activation (triggers emergency deployment authorities)

Without the National Economic Intelligence Dashboard, the three cycles operate blind —
capital flows without measurement, labor is deployed without demand signals, and tariff
exits are missed because no one is tracking when domestic capacity becomes competitive.
The National Economic Intelligence Dashboard is the nervous system that makes the system
self-regulating rather than politically managed.

## 3. The Supporting Programs

Around the three core cycles sit a set of programs that either protect the cycles from
disruption or extend their reach into areas the core cycles do not fully address.

### Protection Programs (Floor Mechanisms)

These programs ensure that workers and businesses caught in economic disruption do not
exit the economic system permanently — they remain available to re-enter the core cycles
when conditions improve.

**The Negative Income Tax:** Catches workers who fall below the income floor during
displacement. Prevents the permanent exit from the labor force that characterizes long-
term unemployment. Maintains purchasing power across the economy during downturns
(automatic stabilizer). Key interaction: without the Negative Income Tax, displaced
workers from Cycle 1 cannot afford to retrain; the retraining pipeline starves.

**Tax Return Advance Loans:** Provides immediate liquidity during acute disruption before
annual tax filing. Bridges the gap between disruption and the income floor. Key
interaction: without the Tax Return Advance Loan, the Negative Income Tax arrives too
late for the acute phase of a crisis; businesses fail and workers lose housing before
the annual payment arrives.

**Business Survival Loans:** Prevents small business failure during acute disruptions that
temporarily eliminate revenue. Preserves the employer base that provides entry-level
and mid-level employment. Key interaction: without business survival loans, the
Internship Floor and Workforce Development Floor lose their delivery infrastructure
(the small businesses that would otherwise host training experiences close permanently).

**Stimulus checks:** Surge instrument for acute demand collapse. Prevents deflation spiral.
Used sparingly — the Negative Income Tax handles the structural floor; stimulus checks
handle the sharp acute drop. Key interaction: during a Tier 2 or higher scenario, the
American Sovereign Fund's emergency deployment authority (10% of Strategic Reserve)
provides the capital for the surge; stimulus checks deliver it to citizens.

### Opportunity Programs (On-Ramp Mechanisms)

These programs ensure that the cycles generate entry points for workers who are not yet
in the system — particularly young workers and those historically excluded.

**Mandatory Internship Floor:** Creates one million structured entry points per year into
the formal economy. Prevents the system from becoming a mechanism that benefits only
those already inside it. Key interaction: Internship Floor graduates are the pipeline
for Civilian Reserve and Health Program recruitment; they are also the next generation
of workers in the Expert Reserve Program.

**Workforce Development Floor / Employer Development Obligation:** Requires large employers
to invest one percent of their workforce in paid training experiences. Closes the
free-rider gap where employers consume trained workers without contributing to training.
Key interaction: the Employer Development Obligation generates training supply that
supplements the Civilian Reserve and Health Program pipeline; the skills developed
align with the Strategic Reserve sector investments.

### Expansion Programs (Long-Horizon Investments)

These programs extend the reach of the core cycles into areas where the 5–15 year
investment horizon exceeds what even the American Sovereign Fund Strategic Reserve
can adequately fund — requiring dedicated program structures.

**Space Mining Incentive Program:** Addresses the long-run version of the critical
materials problem. Domestic rare earth processing (Cycle 3) reduces terrestrial
concentration risk over 5–15 years. Space-derived materials eliminate it over 20–30
years. The two programs operate on different time horizons and are complementary:
terrestrial diversification buys time; space extraction eliminates the constraint.

**Synthetic Protein Program:** Addresses the long-run version of the food security problem.
The drought scenario is the immediate concern; precision fermentation that produces
protein at 99% lower land use and 82–96% lower water use is the permanent solution.
The two approaches operate on the same logic: near-term mitigation buys time for
structural transformation.

**Expert Reserve Program and Accelerators:** Generates the intellectual property that
the American Sovereign Fund can monetize through licensing, that allied nations will
pay for in trade agreements, and that maintains U.S. technological leadership across
the strategic sectors where concentration risk is highest. Key interaction: Accelerator
programs produce spinout companies; the American Sovereign Fund Strategic Reserve is
the natural institutional investor in those spinouts, converting government R&D into
citizen-owned commercial returns.

### Political Reinforcement Mechanisms

These are the features of the overall system that make it politically durable — that
create constituencies large enough to protect the programs from political attack.

**The American Sovereign Fund dividend:** Once dividend payments begin (approximately
Year 15 after fund establishment), every eligible American citizen has a direct financial
stake in the fund's continued growth. Any proposal to cut the programs that feed the
fund — the arms sales levy, the tariff revenue share, the Automation Tax Credit
allocation — is simultaneously a proposal to cut every eligible American's annual
payment. This creates a constituency of 235 million people with a personal financial
interest in the Department of Economic Defense's continued operation. It is the same
political dynamic that has protected Social Security for ninety years, applied to the
full Department of Economic Defense program library.

**Universal benefit design:** Every major floor program (Negative Income Tax, American
Sovereign Fund dividend, health access through Civilian Reserve) is explicitly
non-means-tested. This is both economically efficient (avoids poverty traps and benefit
cliffs) and politically strategic: programs that benefit everyone are defended by
everyone. Means-tested programs are defended only by their recipients, who are by
definition a minority and often a politically marginalized one.

**The dual-mandate architecture:** Every program serves two masters. The Civilian Reserve
and Health Program serves the Department of Defense (military readiness) and the
Department of Economic Defense (civilian labor deployment and healthcare). The Expert
Reserve Program serves national R&D and workforce development. The American Sovereign
Fund serves financial return and strategic investment. Dual-mandate programs require
two separate political coalitions to defund — a structural protection against single-
issue opposition.

## 5. The Steady State

At full deployment — approximately thirty years after the American Sovereign Fund is
established, fifteen to twenty years after the core programs reach operating scale —
the Department of Economic Defense system converges to a steady state with the following
characteristics.

### Economic Characteristics

**Self-sustaining revenue:** The three core cycles generate sufficient revenue to fund
all programs without dependence on discretionary appropriation. The American Sovereign
Fund's investment returns alone exceed the annual cost of the income floor programs by
Year 25. The system is not merely self-funding; it is surplus-generating.

**Strategic sector maturity:** The American Sovereign Fund Strategic Reserve has built
competitive domestic capacity in every critical sector identified by the Concentration
Risk Score framework. The United States no longer holds critical supply chain positions
with Concentration Risk Score above 0.30 in any sector that adversarial nations can
exploit. Supply chain resilience is structural, not emergency-managed.

**Dividend as income supplement:** The American Sovereign Fund dividend is paying
approximately $720–$1,200/year per eligible citizen at Year 30, growing toward $2,000+
at Year 50. For lower-income Americans, this supplements the Negative Income Tax floor.
For middle-income Americans, it is a meaningful retirement income supplement. For all
Americans, it is a visible, annual reminder of ownership in the national economic project.

**Scenario resilience:** Every Department of Economic Defense scenario (nine identified,
more as they emerge) has pre-built response infrastructure: stockpiles, contracts,
trained labor, pre-authorized financial instruments, and an American Sovereign Fund
emergency deployment authority. No scenario requires improvised response. Political
leaders in a crisis make decisions within a pre-built architecture rather than inventing
one under pressure.

### Political Characteristics

**Broad constituency:** The American Sovereign Fund dividend creates a direct financial
constituency of 235M+ eligible citizens — the largest political constituency in American
history organized around a single government program. The Civilian Reserve and Health
Program creates a constituency of millions of reserve members and their families
analogous to the veterans' constituency that has protected the Department of Veterans
Affairs for a century. The Negative Income Tax creates a constituency of every American
who has ever needed it or expects to — which is most Americans across a lifetime.

**Bipartisan structure:** The system is designed to have components that appeal across
the political spectrum. Conservatives can support the return-on-service model of the
Civilian Reserve and Health Program, the efficiency of the Negative Income Tax vs.
means-tested welfare, and the self-funding American Sovereign Fund. Progressives can
support the income floor, the health access, the training pipeline, and the wealth
distribution. Fiscal hawks can support the self-funding architecture. Defense hawks can
support the military cost linkage.

**Institutional durability:** The American Sovereign Fund is protected by supermajority
amendment requirements. The Negative Income Tax is embedded in the tax code rather than
annual appropriations. The Civilian Reserve and Health Program is built into Department
of Defense healthcare infrastructure. These are not programs that can be zeroed out in
a budget resolution. They require deliberate institutional dismantling — which requires
political conditions that the programs' own constituencies make increasingly unlikely.

## 6. What the System Is Not

The system described here is not:

**Not a planned economy.** The Department of Economic Defense does not direct private
production, set prices, allocate goods, or tell firms what to make. It sets financial
incentives, makes government as reliable buyer or guarantor, and invests in capacity
where markets produce less than the socially optimal amount. Markets remain the
allocation mechanism throughout.

**Not a permanent expansion of government.** The Trade-to-Capacity Loop is explicitly
designed to reduce the need for tariff protection over time. The Automation Tax Credit
is calibrated to decline as retraining pipelines mature and labor market adjustment
catches up to automation. The emergency deployment authorities activate only during
declared scenarios. The steady-state footprint of the Department of Economic Defense
is designed to shrink relative to the economy as the strategic problems it addresses
are resolved.

**Not a redistribution program.** The American Sovereign Fund dividend is a return on
nationally-owned assets — closer in logic to an investment dividend than a transfer
payment. The Negative Income Tax is an efficiency gain on the existing welfare system
(same or better support at lower bureaucratic cost). The Automation Tax Credit captures
a surplus that markets were already generating but not routing to displaced workers.
No program takes wealth from one citizen and gives it to another — all programs capture
surplus from market activity and convert it to citizen benefit.

**Not a response to the last crisis.** The entire architecture is designed prospectively.
The scenarios, the stockpiles, the pre-authorized instruments, the trained labor force,
and the invested capital are all in place before the crisis arrives. The Department of
Economic Defense's success condition is invisibility: if it works perfectly, the crises
it prevents are never seen, and the public debates whether they were ever real threats.
This is the same challenge facing all insurance institutions — the argument for their
existence is hardest to make precisely when they are working best.
