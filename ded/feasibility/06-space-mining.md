**Last Updated:** 2026-05

| Dimension | Rating | Summary |

**Overall Implementation Readiness: Premature (for extraction program) / Conditional (for APC and framework program)**
*The extraction technology is too early-stage for a results-oriented government investment
program. The correct near-term program is narrower: (1) APC commitments to give commercial
companies bankable revenue certainty for their 2030-2035 demonstration missions, (2) a
U.S.-led international framework for space resource rights (analogous to UNCLOS for seabed),
and (3) a DED Accelerator for space resource technology development. The broader Strategic
Space Resource Reserve and full extraction program should be revisited after 2035 when
commercial demonstration results are available.*

## Legal / Constitutional Analysis

### SPACE Act of 2015
The U.S. Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act (SPACE Act) of 2015 grants U.S.
citizens and companies the right to own and sell resources they extract in space. It
does not grant sovereignty over celestial bodies (which would violate the Outer Space
Treaty) — it grants property rights in the extracted resources themselves. This distinction
is legally established and has not been successfully challenged.

### Outer Space Treaty Compatibility
Article II of the Outer Space Treaty (1967) prohibits national appropriation of celestial
bodies "by claim of sovereignty, by means of use or occupation, or by any other means."
The SPACE Act's resource ownership approach — ownership of extracted materials, not of
the body from which they were extracted — is generally considered OST-compatible by
international law scholars, though this interpretation is not universally agreed. The
Artemis Accords (2020), signed by 49 countries, endorse the extraction-without-sovereignty
approach, providing multilateral political support for the U.S. legal position.

### APC Authority
Advance Purchase Commitments are standard federal procurement: the government contracts
to buy a defined quantity of a product at a defined price upon delivery. Operation Warp
Speed used APCs for COVID vaccines. The NASA COTS and CRS programs used APCs for cargo
delivery to the ISS. No novel legal authority is required for the DED (or NASA/Commerce)
to issue APCs for space-extracted materials.

## Administrative Feasibility

### NASA Partnership Infrastructure
NASA's existing commercial partnership programs (COTS, CRS, Commercial Crew, Commercial
Lunar Payload Services, CLPS) provide the administrative infrastructure for government-
commercial space partnerships. The DED Space Mining Accelerator is an extension of the
CLPS model — government as anchor customer creating commercial revenue certainty — not
a new administrative structure.

Key difference from existing NASA commercial programs: NASA's programs have defined
technical deliverables (cargo delivered, crew transported). Space resource APCs have
performance-based deliverables (materials extracted and delivered to Earth orbit or
lunar surface for processing). The performance verification challenge — how do you
confirm that 1kg of platinum was actually extracted from an asteroid and not sourced
elsewhere? — requires new chain-of-custody and verification standards. This is a
solvable engineering problem but not a currently solved one.

### Technical Readiness Assessment

| Technology Component | Current TRL | Required TRL for Extraction | Gap |
|---------------------|------------|---------------------------|-----|
| Asteroid rendezvous and proximity operations | TRL 8-9 (demonstrated by Hayabusa, OSIRIS-REx) | TRL 9 | Minimal |
| Surface sample collection | TRL 7-8 (demonstrated by Hayabusa2, OSIRIS-REx) | TRL 9 | Small |
| In-situ resource extraction (ISRU) | TRL 3-5 | TRL 8 | Large |
| Material processing in zero-g | TRL 2-4 | TRL 7-8 | Large |
| Earth return of bulk materials | TRL 3-5 | TRL 8-9 | Large |
| Lunar surface operations at scale | TRL 4-6 | TRL 8-9 | Medium-Large |

The large gaps in ISRU and material processing are the binding constraints. A 5-year,
$3-5B program can fund technology development and demonstration — it cannot produce
commercial-scale extraction. The correct goal for a 2026-2035 program is TRL 6-7
demonstration, not commercial operation.

## Economic Analysis

### Near-Term Case (2026-2035)

The investment case for the near-term APC + Accelerator program is not extraction — it
is option value and market position:

- **Option value:** A $3-5B commitment now purchases a call option on U.S. leadership
  in space resource extraction when the technology matures. If the technology does not
  mature, the loss is the R&D spend. If it matures under U.S. commercial leadership,
  the U.S. has shaped the market and the legal framework.
- **Market position:** The commercial space resource industry is being built now by
  AstroForge, ispace, Karman+, and others. The anchor buyer that provides revenue
  certainty at the demonstration stage gets to shape technical standards, contractual
  norms, and intellectual property in the emerging industry.
- **DED scenario value:** Space-derived rare earths and platinum group metals with a CRS
  of 0.0 (no adversarial control) permanently solve the concentration risk problem that
  terrestrial alternatives only partially address. The expected value of this option,
  even at 10% probability of realization within 25 years, is substantial.

### Long-Term Case (2035-2050)

The EAL framework applied to rare earth concentration risk (CRS = 0.60, EAL = $30-60B/year
uninsured) implies that a permanent solution to rare earth concentration is worth
$300-600B in avoided losses over a decade. If space mining provides a meaningful fraction
of U.S. rare earth supply, the EAL falls toward zero and the concentrated risk is eliminated
permanently. The NPV of this outcome, discounted appropriately, justifies substantial
near-term investment.

However, the discount rate matters enormously. A 25-year payoff horizon at a 7% discount
rate reduces the NPV of a $300B eventual benefit to approximately $55-60B. The $3-5B
near-term investment still clears this hurdle — but the long-term case depends heavily
on timeline assumptions that are highly uncertain.

### Revenue Capture

The APC price structure (90% of terrestrial spot price for extracted materials) means the
government receives competitively priced materials while providing revenue certainty to
commercial operators. The price is not a subsidy — it is a market rate with a volume
commitment. At full commercial scale, the government purchases extracted materials that
substitute for adversarially controlled imports, eliminating the premium (military readiness
tax) currently paid for supply chain concentration risk.

## Political Analysis

### The Derision Risk
"Space mining" invites derision in the same way that "the internet" invited derision in
1992 and GPS-guided navigation invited derision in 1985. The correct political framing:
- Not "mining the moon" — "eliminating U.S. dependence on Chinese rare earth exports permanently"
- Not "space industry investment" — "the Operation Warp Speed model applied to critical materials"
- Not a long-term moonshot — "bankable revenue commitments to companies that already have
  functioning hardware and flight records"

The Operation Warp Speed comparison is the most powerful political tool. Warp Speed was:
- Advance purchase commitments for products that didn't yet exist
- Government as anchor buyer creating private investment certainty
- A defined timeline goal (vaccines by end of 2020) that focused development
- Bipartisan in execution even if contested in framing

The space mining APC program is the same structure for a different product.

### Defense Framing
The rare earth denial scenario (China restricts REE exports) is the defense entry point.
The F-35, the Javelin missile, M1A2 tanks, and virtually every advanced weapons system
contain rare earth magnets. A Chinese REE embargo is a defense readiness crisis.
Solving this through space-derived REEs is a defense investment, not a science project.
This framing is credible to the Armed Services Committees and the defense industrial base.

## Key Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|-----------|
| Technology does not mature on timeline | High | Medium | APC structure means government pays only upon delivery; R&D spend is the loss, not capital for undelivered hardware |
| China establishes dominant space mining position first | Medium | High | APC program creates U.S. commercial lead; international framework leadership is the competitive moat |
| Commercial companies fail before reaching extraction | High (for individual companies) | Low | Portfolio approach — APC commitments to 4-6 companies, not one; diversification across lunar and near-Earth asteroid targets |
| Outer Space Treaty legal challenge (Russia, China) | Medium | Medium | Artemis Accords provide multilateral cover; extraction-not-sovereignty distinction is defensible |
| "Space boondoggle" political narrative dominates | High | High | Operation Warp Speed framing; specific measurable milestones; GAO oversight; no cost-plus contracts |
