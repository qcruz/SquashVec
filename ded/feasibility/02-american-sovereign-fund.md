**Source:** `ded/proposal/american-sovereign-fund.md`
**Last Updated:** 2026-05

  requires the DED, but the fund itself does not.

**Overall Implementation Readiness: Conditional**
*The fund design is sound and the legal basis is strong. The conditional element is political:
the arms sales levy will generate defense industry opposition, and the 10-year accumulation
period before any dividends requires political commitment to long-horizon benefit. The Alaska
comparison is the key political tool — a version of this already works, in the same country.*

## Legal / Constitutional Analysis

### Revenue Streams

**Arms sales levy (7.5%):** A remittance obligation on defense export contractors is a
regulatory fee, not a tax. It falls within Congress's Commerce Clause authority to regulate
foreign commerce. Analogues: oil industry windfall profit taxes (1980, upheld); tobacco
Master Settlement Agreement payments (not a tax; contractual). The levy must be applied
uniformly across all qualifying contractors to avoid a takings challenge.

**Tariff revenue share (20%):** Appropriation of a defined fraction of customs revenue to
a dedicated fund is straightforwardly within congressional power. Customs revenue is
already appropriated annually; a mandatory set-aside before appropriation (similar to the
Social Security Trust Fund mechanism) is the established model and has not been successfully
challenged.

**Tax streams (TAT, FTS, RRRE):** All three are excise taxes within congressional taxing
power. The 0.01% Financial Transaction Surcharge is the most legally complex — the
European Union's FTT has faced legal challenges in member states — but U.S. constitutional
authority for federal excise taxes on financial transactions is not in serious doubt.

### Citizen Dividend

The dividend does not raise Fifth Amendment concerns because it is a distribution of
investment returns, not a taking from identifiable persons. The 18-year citizenship
eligibility requirement is constitutional — it mirrors Social Security's work-credit
requirement and has no suspect classification.

**Potential equal protection challenge:** The 18-year post-naturalization wait (giving
natural-born citizens a 18-year headstart vs. naturalized citizens) could be characterized
as discriminatory against naturalized citizens. Mitigation: the design should frame
eligibility as "18 years of citizenship" uniformly — a natural-born citizen must still
wait until age 18; a naturalized citizen must wait 18 years post-naturalization. Both
wait the same duration. The difference is when the clock starts, not how long it runs.
This framing has survived Alaska's residency requirement without successful equal protection
challenge.

## Administrative Feasibility

### Board and Governance
The Board of Trustees structure (9 members, staggered terms, no-majority-party rule) is
modeled on existing independent agency boards (FDIC, CFTC, FED). Appointment process is
standard. The CIO hire at competitive institutional rates ($2-5M) is within precedent
for government-sponsored investment entities (Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation CIO
is comparable).

### Investment Committee
The 12-person committee of investment professionals (9 external from peer institutions)
requires identifying qualified professionals willing to serve in a quasi-government role.
Precedent: the Social Security Advisory Board, the Public Company Accounting Oversight
Board, and numerous federal advisory committees demonstrate that high-caliber professionals
will serve in such roles. The peer SWF network (Norway, Singapore, ADIA) is a natural
recruitment pool for the external members.

### Dividend Distribution Infrastructure
The primary challenge is the unbanked population. Approximately 5-6 million U.S. citizens
lack bank accounts. The pre-loaded debit card mechanism requires:
- IRS integration for identity verification (exists — IRS sends refund cards)
- A card issuer contract (straightforward government procurement)
- USPS delivery infrastructure (existing, used for stimulus checks)

Total administrative cost of dividend distribution: estimated $50-100M/year at maturity
(less than 0.002% of fund AUM at Year 30). Operationally trivial.

### SRID Mechanism (DED Integration)
The Strategic Reserve Investment Designation requires DED CRS analysis as input. If the
DED does not exist, the Strategic Reserve allocation can be governed by a simplified
Concentration Risk threshold using Commerce and USTR data — a less sophisticated but
operational alternative. The SRID mechanism enhances the fund; it is not required for the
fund to operate.

## Economic Analysis

### Revenue Projection Credibility

**Arms sales:** FY2024 baseline of $265B is documented DSCA data. 7.5% = $19.9B.
The long-run trend is upward: U.S. allies have been increasing defense budgets since 2022
and the pipeline is multi-year. Risk: a major geopolitical de-escalation reduces arms
sales. However, U.S. defense exports are driven primarily by ally capability requirements,
not U.S. policy; even in de-escalation scenarios, the baseline is unlikely to fall below
$150-180B ($11-13.5B at 7.5%).

**Tariff revenue:** The most variable stream. At $80B (pre-2018 baseline), 20% = $16B.
At $150-300B (current elevated regime), 20% = $30-60B. The fund is designed to be
countercyclical to import dependency — tariff revenue is highest when domestic alternatives
are most needed. Long-run risk: as domestic capacity matures, tariffs decline and so does
revenue. This is the intended outcome; by then, the fund's investment returns replace
tariff inflows.

**Tax streams:** The 0.5% software excise ($4B) is the smallest stream but the most
stable and fastest-growing. The 0.01% FTS ($17-20B) is large but could face market
adaptation over time; institutional traders will work to minimize taxable transaction volume.
Conservative floor: $10B/year on FTS if significant adaptation occurs. Still viable.

### Fund Trajectory
At $60B/year contributions and 5.5% net return, the fund reaches $850B by Year 10. This
is conservative: Norway reached $1T in 2013, 17 years after its first capital transfer,
on contributions that were comparable as a fraction of the Norwegian economy.

**Stress test — what happens if returns are only 4%?**
At 4% net return and $60B contributions: Year 10 = $790B; Year 30 = $4.5T; Year 30
dividend = $590/citizen. Still meaningful; the fund is robust to below-average returns.

**Stress test — what happens if contributions fall to $40B/year?**
At $40B contributions and 5.5% return: Year 10 = $590B; Year 30 = $3.8T; Year 30
dividend = $480/citizen. The program still works; the dividend is smaller but the political
durability is intact.

### Macro Effects

**Inflation risk:** The fund does not inject money into the economy during its accumulation
phase (Years 1-10). During the distribution phase, the annual dividend ($108-720/citizen
depending on year) is a small fraction of GDP — smaller than the EITC at any given time —
and is unlikely to produce measurable inflationary pressure. At Year 30 ($720/citizen ×
252M citizens = $181B), this is approximately 0.5% of projected 2055 GDP — well within
the inflationary safety margin.

**Capital market effects:** The fund invests primarily in U.S. equities (75% of 75% of
AUM in the Commercial Portfolio's U.S. allocation). At $5.5T AUM, the fund would hold
approximately $3T in U.S. equities — comparable to the largest index funds. Concentration
caps (5% per company) prevent market-distorting stakes. The fund would be the largest
single institutional investor in the U.S. market, similar to Japan's GPIF. GPIF's scale
has not distorted Japanese equity markets; the ASF at equivalent scale should not distort
U.S. markets either.

## Political Analysis

### Coalition Map

**Supportive:**
- Universal benefit recipients (every eligible citizen from Year 15 — 235M+ people)
  once the dividend begins; this is the Social Security dynamic, but it requires
  surviving 15 years of accumulation before the political moat forms
- Manufacturing constituencies: Strategic Reserve investment in domestic industry is
  a visible economic development benefit in industrial states
- Fiscal conservatives: the fund is entirely self-funded; it does not add to the deficit;
  at maturity it generates net returns to the federal government
- Deficit hawks: this is one of the few federal programs that actually builds a national
  balance sheet asset rather than accumulating liabilities

**Opposed:**
- Defense industry (arms sales levy): reduces margins on export contracts; Lockheed,
  Raytheon, Boeing, Northrop will lobby against. Mitigation: Strategic Reserve investment
  in defense industrial base returns capital to Tier 2/3 suppliers; net is positive for
  the sector as a whole; only prime contractors face net negative
- Free-trade advocates: tariff revenue dedication implies acceptance of tariffs;
  the response is that the tariff-to-domestic-capacity pipeline is explicitly designed
  to enable tariff reduction as domestic alternatives mature
- Short-term-focused legislators: 15 years before first dividend is a long time for
  politicians who need visible wins in a 2-year election cycle

### The Alaska Political Template

The Alaska Permanent Fund was established by a Republican governor (Jay Hammond) with
bipartisan support, constitutional entrenchment, and immediate public enthusiasm. It has
been defended by every governor and legislature since, including those who have tried
to raid it and failed because of public opposition. The national version requires:

1. A champion willing to play the long game (15-year payoff horizon)
2. Constitutional or supermajority protection from the start
3. Transparent, public reporting of fund performance from Day 1
4. The word "dividend" — not "benefit," not "transfer," not "payment." Dividend.

## Key Risks

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------------|--------|-----------|
| Congressional raiding before first dividend | Medium | High | Sequestration immunity; supermajority amendment requirement; no revenue diversion authority |
| Arms sales levy kills defense export contracts | Low | Medium | Levy is priced into contracts with allies; allies' demand is not price-elastic at 7.5% |
| Investment politicization (PIF failure mode) | Medium | High | Independent CIO; Investment Committee separation; no congressional direction authority; SRID mechanism for Strategic Reserve |
| Below-market returns in Strategic Reserve | Certain | Low | Accepted; insurance value makes up the gap; Strategic Reserve is explicitly not a returns-maximizing investment |
| 15-year wait destroys political will | High | High | Transparent annual reporting; emphasize compounding; Alaska analogy; intermediate milestones (fund size, projected dividend) |
| Market concentration at $5T+ AUM | Low | Medium | 5% company cap; 40% sector cap; mandatory size review at $3T, $6T, $10T |
