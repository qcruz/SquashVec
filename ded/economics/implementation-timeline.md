**Document Type:** Master Integration and Sequencing Analysis
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Purpose:** Map all DED programs, tools, and scenario responses in chronological
  activation order; document program interactions, buildout chains, consolidation
  opportunities, and cumulative fiscal trajectory.

  implemented. Feasibility barriers are assumed solved by the time implementation
  is required. This document is a planning instrument, not a legislative schedule.

## Year 0: Legislative Foundation

*Day one of the first Congress and Administration that enacts the DED.*

**Enacted:**
- DED Enabling Act: establishes the Department of Economic Defense as a cabinet-level
  agency; defines the three-tier emergency declaration system; creates the Joint
  Congressional Economic Defense committee for oversight
- National Economic Intelligence Dashboard authorized within DED; data-sharing
  mandates with Treasury, Commerce, USDA, DoD, DHS, and Federal Reserve
- Strategic Contracts framework enacted: Type A (retainer/stockpile), Type B
  (co-investment), Type C (allied sourcing), Type D (classified accelerator)
- Expert Reserve Program established: Los Alamos model; competing-company recruitment;
  DED retains all IP from Accelerator programs
- Automation Tax Credit statutory rate: 18% excise on documented AI labor cost savings;
  statutory allocation: 60% to Negative Income Tax, 25% to American Sovereign Fund,
  15% to DED operations and Accelerator programs
- American Sovereign Fund established: 7.5% arms sales levy + 20% tariff revenue share
  + ATC surcharge; two-tranche structure (25% Strategic Reserve, 75% Commercial Portfolio)
- Permitting Acceleration Authority mechanisms: loan guarantee authority, tax credit
  schedules, agency capacity grant program
- Financial Institution Partnerships authority: DED can issue competitive contracts
  to financial institutions for account hosting, benefit distribution, and eBank
  node operation

**Interactions activated at Year 0:**
- ATC → NIT + ASF: the primary funding loop is statutory from day one
- ERP → all Accelerator programs: expert recruitment begins immediately, ahead of
  program buildout
- PAA → all physical infrastructure programs: same 6 mechanisms available to any
  DED-designated critical infrastructure project from year one

**Cumulative fiscal position (Year 0):** DED establishment cost ~$4–6B; no revenue yet.

## Year 1: First Instruments Operational

**Programs activated:**
- **NEID goes live:** Real-time monitoring of supply chain concentration risk,
  commodity prices, and economic indicators across all 9 scenario categories.
  First Concentration Risk Score reports published. This is the information
  foundation everything else builds on.
- **Automation Tax Credit collection begins:** IRS integration; first quarterly
  remittances from early-adopting large technology and logistics firms. Revenue
  ramp: $15–25B in Year 1, growing to $70–110B at full deployment by Year 5–7.
- **Negative Income Tax pilot:** 3-state pilot (proposed: Kentucky, New Mexico,
  Nevada — covering rural, southwestern, and gaming-economy populations for
  demographic diversity). Tax Return Advance Loan delivery mechanism operational.
  Pilot covers ~2M individuals; full national rollout pending data.
- **American Sovereign Fund first levy collections:** Arms sales levy and tariff
  revenue share begin flowing to ASF. Year 1 ASF inflow: estimated $40–70B.
  ASF begins deploying Strategic Reserve tranche into domestic critical sector equity.
- **Job Posting Accountability Penalty reporting:** EEO-1 supplement requiring
  job posting data; penalty revenue begins flowing to Workforce Development programs.
- **Financial Institution Partnerships first contracts awarded:** Competitive
  bidding process; contracts awarded to 8–12 large and regional banks plus
  credit union networks for NIT pilot account hosting, CRHP enrollment accounts,
  and eBank pilot node operation. Geographic coverage requirements enforced —
  contracts structured so high-density urban markets cannot be won without rural
  coverage commitments.
- **Type A strategic contracts activated:** Critical mineral stockpile retainers
  for rare earths, semiconductor inputs, pharmaceutical APIs, and energy storage
  materials. NEID concentration risk scores drive contract sizing.
- **Permitting Acceleration:** Permitting Capital Loan Guarantee operational
  (80% DED guarantee converts junk → investment grade for qualifying infrastructure).
  Critical Infrastructure ITC (30–45%) funded from first-dollar tariff revenue share.
  Agency Capacity Grants: $50–80M to EPA, Army Corps, FWS, SHPO.

**Key interaction: ATC → NIT pilot:**
ATC revenue in Year 1 is not yet sufficient to fully fund NIT at national scale,
but the statutory allocation means every dollar of ATC collected automatically funds
NIT expansion. The pilot's success data informs the national rollout parameters.

**Key interaction: Financial Institution Partnerships → eBank pilot:**
The same financial institutions awarded account hosting contracts are the preferred
operators for the 10-city eBank pilot. The FIP contracts include eBank node operation
as an optional extension with co-investment terms. Banks that take the extension receive
Type B infrastructure co-investment from DED for branch conversion to node-compliant
data center standard.

**Cumulative fiscal position (Year 1):**
- Gross cost: ~$20–30B (DED ops + NIT pilot + ASF seed + PAA + Type A contracts)
- Revenue: $15–25B ATC + $40–70B ASF levies
- Net: approximately balanced to slightly positive at Year 1, before program expansion

## Year 2: Core Programs Launch

**Programs activated:**
- **Negative Income Tax national rollout:** Full national deployment based on
  pilot data. $6,200 individual guarantee; 30% phase-out; TRAL advance delivery
  via Financial Institution Partnership banks. ~30M initial recipients.
  Replaces: SNAP, TANF, much of the administrative apparatus of means-tested welfare.
  Immediate savings: $3,200/person vs. current system × 30M recipients = $96B/year
  in consolidated program cost reduction.
- **Care Economy track opens:** NIT Care Provider designation for registered family
  caregivers; Social Security caregiving credit begins accruing. CRHP Care Coordinator
  designation. Medicaid institutional care avoided: federal share savings begin flowing.
- **Civilian Reserve and Health Program enrollment opens (ages 18–34):**
  Enrollment trigger: TRICARE-equivalent coverage in exchange for 13 days/year training
  + emergency call-up availability. $600/year enrollment fee (income-scaled).
  Financial Institution Partnership banks serve as enrollment points; CRHP accounts
  are the same accounts as NIT accounts — **first major account consolidation.**
- **eBank pilot (10 cities) operational:** Cities across 5 Federal Reserve districts
  running parallel to existing payment system. Partition mode tested in controlled
  drills. CRHP-enrolled technicians staffing node maintenance.
- **Workforce Development Floor enacted (federal contractors first):**
  Employer Development Obligation for federal contractors — 1% of workforce in
  paid developmental positions. Coverage: ~40% of large private employers immediately.
  Mandatory Internship Floor: 390,000 net new paid slots annually from year one.
- **Outcome-Linked Bonds first issuance:** Inverse crop yield bonds for drought
  risk (first instrument). Energy price bonds (second instrument, covering oil
  restriction scenario exposure). Both place commercial risk on willing market
  investors rather than federal appropriations.
- **Retraining Programs reform:** TAA expanded; WIOA consolidated with WDF
  programs; AI Retraining Flywheel pipeline begins for early AI-displaced workers.
- **Type B co-investment contracts:** Semiconductor domestic capacity (CHIPS-adjacent
  co-investment for next-generation facilities); rare earth processing (Mountain Pass
  full integration; Round Top Texas); synthetic protein bioreactor pilot facilities.

**Key interaction: NIT + Care Economy + CRHP = Tier 1 fiscal surplus:**
A NIT recipient who provides care work (Tier 1 in the Jobs KPI model) generates
+$6,637/year net fiscal surplus from Medicaid institutional care avoidance alone.
The three programs together — NIT, Care Economy track, CRHP healthcare — are more
fiscally efficient as a bundle than any one alone. A caregiver enrolled in all three
costs the federal government less than the current Medicaid + SNAP + TANF system
and delivers more economic value.

**Key interaction: WDF → CRHP overflow:**
Workers who cannot access the Workforce Development Floor pathway (not currently
employed, in low-training sectors, or geographically distant from qualifying employers)
flow into the CRHP training pipeline as the parallel pathway. WDF and CRHP are
designed as complementary on-ramps to the same labor market, not competing programs.

**Key interaction: eBank + Financial Institution Partnerships:**
FIP contracts now cover NIT accounts, CRHP accounts, and eBank node operation —
three programs, one contract framework, one account per citizen. The bank earns
per-account contract revenue on all three simultaneously, making geographic coverage
of difficult populations economically rational.

**Cumulative fiscal position (Year 2):**
- ATC revenue: $35–55B (growing)
- ASF levies: $40–70B (stable)
- NIT gross: $89B; net after program replacements: ~$0–10B (approximately neutral)
- WDF: employer-funded, minimal federal cost
- Care economy savings: $20–40B Medicaid avoidance emerging
- **Net cumulative: approximately self-financing at program level, ahead of schedule**

## Year 3: Strategic Infrastructure Buildout

**Programs activated:**
- **Synthetic Protein Program begins:** DED anchor buyer contract with 3 domestic
  precision fermentation producers (competitive procurement). 10-year advance
  purchase commitment at defined price schedule. DoD, VA, and Bureau of Prisons
  designated as first institutional buyers. Bioreactor infrastructure construction
  begins under PAA permitting acceleration.
- **Sovereign Compute Reserve operational:** DOE national laboratory allocation
  committed (5% of Tier 1 HPC capacity); DED GPU cluster co-investment at 2
  domestic vendors; 35 FEMA-region distributed edge nodes under construction.
  CRHP-trained compute operations technicians staffing nodes.
- **Rare Earth Denial Phase 1 full activation:** NEID concentration risk triggers
  for REE above 0.50. Type A retainer contracts for domestic REE processing.
  Mountain Pass full integration Type B co-investment closed. DED Accelerator
  for Fe₁₆N₂ rare-earth-free magnet program at ORNL/Ames Lab.
- **Allied Processing Network first contracts (Type C):** Lynas Australia heavy
  REE processing; Canadian cobalt refining; UK/EU gallium and germanium recovery.
  Anti-concentration provisions: no single allied country >35% of any category.
- **eBank national buildout Phase 1 begins:** Financial Institution Partnerships
  extended to all bank network operators meeting node compliance standards; Type B
  co-investment for branch-to-node conversion; USPS node agreement for rural coverage.
- **Expert Reserve Program first Accelerator cohorts active:** Across 4 program
  areas — semiconductor materials, rare earth chemistry, synthetic protein fermentation,
  and distributed ledger cryptography. Same experts advise NEID design, Sovereign
  Compute architecture, and eBank security simultaneously.

**Key interaction: PAA as universal infrastructure tool:**
The same 6 PAA mechanisms — Permitting Capital Loan Guarantee, Baseline Survey Tax
Credit, Critical Infrastructure ITC, Agency Capacity Grants, CRHP Regulatory Track,
ERP Training Pipeline — apply without modification to:
- eBank node facility construction
- Synthetic protein bioreactor construction
- Rare earth processing plant construction
- Semiconductor fab construction
- Sovereign compute data center construction
Every physical buildout program in the portfolio draws from the same permitting
acceleration toolkit. The mechanism is written once; the benefit compounds across
every program that builds something.

**Key interaction: CRHP labor pool as universal workforce:**
CRHP-enrolled workers trained in Year 2 are now deployable across:
- eBank node maintenance and operations (network operations specialty)
- Synthetic protein bioreactor facility support (lab technician specialty)
- Sovereign compute operations (data center technician specialty)
- Allied Processing Network facility support (materials handling specialty)
The same enrolled workforce is the labor backbone for every physical DED program.
The CRHP is not a jobs program for each individual scenario — it is the national
reserve labor pool that makes every scenario's workforce requirements solvable.

**Cumulative fiscal position (Year 3):**
- ATC revenue: $55–80B
- ASF corpus: ~$150–250B; Strategic Reserve yield beginning
- NIT net cost: declining as ATC coverage increases
- Synthetic Protein: $1.5–2B committed (5-year contract)
- Sovereign Compute: $350–550M/year (ongoing)
- Allied Processing: $1–2B/year Type C purchases (ongoing)
- **Net: system approaching 1:1 cost-to-offset ratio across all programs**

## Year 4: Expansion and First Dividends from Early Programs

**Programs activated:**
- **American Prosperity Visa opens:** ASF corpus now exists (Year 3 prerequisite met).
  Two-tier structure: Tier 1 ($500k ASF contribution, conditional residency); Tier 2
  ($1M+ ASF contribution, expedited permanent residency with ANSRP national security review).
  Visa fee revenue flows to ASF corpus. Year 4 cohort: ~5,000 applicants; $2.5B+
  in ASF contributions.
- **Autonomous Highway Lane Program Phase 1:** DOT rulemaking activates dedicated
  AV platoon time windows on 3 major freight corridors. Toll revenue begins flowing
  to Workforce Transition Fund for displaced truckers. CRHP-enrolled truck drivers
  in transition receive retraining support from the Workforce Development Floor pipeline.
- **CRHP expansion to ages 18–45:** Second enrollment bracket opens. Healthcare
  coverage extends to a larger population. Enrollment fee revenue growing.
  CRHP now has ~2–3M enrollees; reserve workforce is becoming material.
- **Rare Earth Denial Phase 2 begins:** Allied Processing Network at scale.
  Battery chemistry diversification: LFP federal fleet mandate. MnBi magnet DoD
  qualification. Critical Mineral Coordination Council G7 reserve framework.
- **Semiconductor domestic capacity:** Type B co-investment facilities in construction
  under PAA acceleration. ERP-designed process improvements reducing facility cost.
- **NIT approaching full funding:** ATC revenue at $70–90B covers the net NIT cost
  ($0–10B net after program replacements). From this point forward, NIT expansion
  is effectively self-funding through ATC growth.

**Key interaction: APV → ASF corpus acceleration:**
American Prosperity Visa contributions add $2.5–10B/year to the ASF corpus, accelerating
the dividend timeline by 1–2 years. The more the corpus grows, the earlier the
citizenship dividend reaches meaningful levels, which is the primary political moat
for the entire system.

**Key interaction: AHLP + CRHP + WDF = full freight transition:**
A displaced truck driver in Year 4 has access to three interlocking programs:
AHLP Workforce Transition Fund (toll-revenue funded transition payment), Civilian
Reserve and Health Program (healthcare continuity + training income), and Workforce
Development Floor retraining (employer-co-funded skill development). The programs
were designed independently but interact to cover the full lifecycle of an AI/automation
displacement event. This is the system map in practice.

## Year 5: Cashless Foundation and Space Mining Authorization

**Programs activated:**
- **Cashless Economy Migration Phase 0:** Currency Modernization Act Titles I and II
  enacted. Universal account access guarantee. Financial Institution Partnerships
  extended to cover Titles I-II implementation. Immigration firewall for account
  data statutory.
- **Space Mining Incentives authorized:** DED Accelerator (Type D) for in-situ
  resource utilization; $3–5B over 5-year period. Advance purchase commitments
  at 90% of terrestrial spot price for first commercial deliveries. International
  Space Mining Framework negotiation begins through State/USTR with DED technical support.
- **eBank Phase 1 coverage milestone check:** If coverage milestones met (85% account
  penetration, 95% county coverage, 80% unbanked penetration), Phase 1 transitions
  to Phase 2 national buildout. Target: milestone met by Year 5–6.
- **CMISS mandatory reporting:** Commercial supply chain transparency reporting
  becomes mandatory for manufacturers above $500M in U.S. annual sales (Rare Earth
  Phase 3 precondition). NEID commercial mineral dashboard goes live.
- **ASF first commercial portfolio distributions:** ASF corpus now ~$400–600B.
  Commercial portfolio generating returns; Strategic Reserve yield growing.
  First internal reinvestment cycle — returns compound into corpus.

**Key interaction: Cashless + eBank + Financial Institution Partnerships = unified infrastructure:**
At Year 5, three programs that were designed independently are operating as a single
infrastructure system:
- eBank distributed ledger = the payment rails
- Financial Institution Partnerships = the operator network
- Cashless migration = the demand-side transition plan
The Currency Modernization Act Titles I-II legitimize the eBank network legally and
guarantee universal account access — which is the precondition the cashless migration
requires. The FIP contracts ensure the banks that operate the eBank nodes also
host the NIT, CRHP, and ASF accounts. A citizen has one account, accessed at their
local bank node, that receives NIT payments, CRHP compensation, and eventually ASF
dividends — and is the same account they use to buy groceries.

  CRHP has 3–5M enrollees with 2–4 years of training. If any DED Tier 1 declaration

**Cumulative fiscal position (Year 6):**
- ATC revenue: $80–100B/year (approaching ceiling)
- ASF corpus: ~$600–900B; yield $30–60B/year
- NIT: fully funded by ATC; zero net federal cost
- Care economy Medicaid avoidance: $40–60B/year
- Synthetic Protein DoD savings: $400–800M/year (growing)
- Tax gap recovery (partial — cashless not complete): $20–40B/year from NEID-enabled enforcement

## Year 7: Cashless Phase 1 Begins

**Programs activated:**
- **Cashless Migration Phase 1 (infrastructure + voluntary adoption):**
  eBank national node network operational to Phase 1 coverage standards.
  Consumer cashless adoption credit ($50) deployed. Small merchant digital
  transition credit (40%) deployed. Distributed ledger 0.25% processing rate
  begins competing with private card networks. NIT and all federal benefit
  distributions converted to direct distributed ledger credit.
- **Currency Modernization Act Title III (privacy framework) drafted** and
  submitted for public comment. 3-year observation clock begins when enacted.
- **Rare Earth Denial Phase 3 begins:** CMIC certification for defense-grade
  NdFeB magnets. DoD procurement mandate phasing in. Fe₁₆N₂ magnet program
  approaching commercial demonstration. Technology independence Accelerators
  for DLE lithium extraction and Ga₂O₃ semiconductors.
- **Semiconductor Phase 3:** Post-EUV commercialization roadmap; domestic
  fab at leading-edge node operational; concentration risk score declining.
- **AHLP national expansion (Phase 2):** Dedicated autonomous lanes on
  Interstate system; V2I infrastructure deployed on major freight corridors.
  Toll revenue self-funding ongoing expansion. Freight cost reduction beginning
  to appear in commodity prices.

**Key interaction: Cashless Phase 1 + Tax Gap:**
As distributed ledger transaction volume grows through Phase 1 (voluntary adoption),
the tax gap recovery begins accelerating even before legal tender sunset. Businesses
that voluntarily go cashless now have complete transaction records. IRS enforcement
using NEID-integrated transaction data becomes more effective. Tax gap recovery
reaches $40–60B/year by the end of Phase 1 — before a single dollar of cash
has been made illegal.

## Years 11–14: Cashless Phase 2 and Space Mining Progress

**Key events:**
- **Cashless Phase 2 (managed decline):** Currency Modernization Act Title III
  enacted and 3-year observation period begins. Cash acceptance no longer legally
  required for private businesses. Fed reduces new currency printing to 60% of
  wear replacement. ATM network permitted to decline in fully-covered areas.
- **Space Mining first commercial demonstrations:** AstroForge or successor
  company achieves first in-space processing demonstration. DED advance purchase
  commitment triggers begin approaching activation milestones.
- **Rare Earth CRS approaches 0.35:** Allied processing network mature; domestic
  defense REE supply chain certified under CMIC. A Chinese export restriction
  would now produce a managed adjustment rather than a crisis.
- **ASF corpus: $1.5–2.5T.** Commercial portfolio returns compounding.
  Dividend projection for Year 15 now visible and credible to public.
- **Synthetic Protein cost parity with conventional beef** (projected Year 12–14):
  The government anchor buyer contract has produced the cost curve that private
  investment alone would not have funded. Commercial food manufacturers begin
  voluntary adoption. DoD food bill declining materially.
- **Grid Cyberattack scenario: Tier 2 → Tier 1 risk reduction.** eBank network
  and Sovereign Compute Reserve together mean a grid cyberattack produces a
  managed local disruption rather than national economic paralysis. The scenario
  that would have been catastrophic in Year 0 is now a recoverable event.

**Key interaction: Synthetic Protein → Drought scenario severity reduction:**
As commercial precision fermentation adoption grows, U.S. agricultural water demand
for protein production declines. This is the most important long-horizon interaction
in the portfolio: a technology investment made in Year 3 begins materially reducing
the probability and severity of the drought scenario in Years 12–15 and beyond.
The Synthetic Protein Program is simultaneously a food security program, a defense
logistics program, and a water conservation program. The cost was paid once;
the benefit compounds across multiple scenario categories indefinitely.

## Year 15: American Sovereign Fund First Dividend

**The most politically significant milestone in the DED timeline.**

- **ASF corpus: ~$2.5–4T.** The 18-year dividend clock (measured from Year 0 enactment)
  was designed to begin here. Commercial portfolio yield: $125–200B/year.
- **First annual dividend distributed** to all eligible citizens (~250M eligible adults):
  ~$400–720/citizen/year at Year 15. Small in absolute terms; enormous in political
  terms. This is the first time every American receives a direct financial return
  on the national wealth they collectively own.
- **Political moat solidified:** Every American now has a direct financial interest
  in the DED system's continuation and growth. The dividend grows every year the
  corpus grows. No future Congress can eliminate the American Sovereign Fund dividend
  without explicitly taking money away from every voter. This is the Alaska Permanent
  Fund dynamic at national scale.
- **Cashless privacy framework observation period complete:** If no systemic violations
  of the privacy framework have occurred in the 3-year window, Currency Modernization
  Act Title IV (legal tender transition) can be introduced in Congress.
- **NIT: zero net federal cost.** ATC revenue fully covers net NIT expenditure.
  The income floor — which appeared fiscally risky at Year 0 — costs the federal
  government nothing net at Year 15, because it generates employment that generates
  taxes that generate the ATC revenue that funds it. The machine pays for itself.

## Years 16–20: Legal Tender Transition

**Key events:**
- **Currency Modernization Act Title IV enacted (if privacy milestones met).**
  Legal tender sunset date announced: 5 years from enactment (Year 21 target).
  Foreign redemption window: 7 years (Year 23 target for foreign holders).
- **Cashless Phase 3 begins:** Domestic redemption campaign. High-volume exchange
  program at all eBank nodes and USPS nodes. Tax gap recovery accelerates sharply
  as informal cash economy is forced through the distributed ledger.
- **Tax gap recovery at Year 20: $80–120B/year** (approaching full cashless
  recovery). This is the single largest new federal revenue stream created by any
  DED program — larger than the Automation Tax Credit.
- **Space Mining commercial deliveries begin:** First asteroid-derived platinum
  group metals and cobalt delivered under DED advance purchase commitments.
  ASF Strategic Space Resource Reserve framework active. The CRS for rare earth
  elements begins declining from 0.35 toward the 20-year target of 0.0.
- **ASF dividend: ~$800–1,200/citizen/year and growing.**
- **CRHP universal healthcare expanding:** Age brackets opened to 18–65+.
  Healthcare coverage approaching universal. The political case for extending
  to all ages is overwhelming — the enrolled constituency is the majority of
  the voting-age population.

**Key interaction: Cashless + NEID = economic intelligence at full resolution:**
In Year 20, with cashless adoption above 90% and the distributed ledger reconciliation
feeding NEID aggregate data, the National Economic Intelligence Dashboard has
near-complete real-time visibility into the U.S. economy. Supply chain concentration
risks are detected in weeks rather than months. Commodity price movements are
tracked at transaction level. Economic disruptions are identified before they

## Consolidation Opportunities

### Consolidation 1: The Unified Citizen Economic Account

**Programs consolidated:** NIT, CRHP, ASF dividend, eBank basic account,
Tax Return Advance Loan, APV dividend eligibility

**Mechanism:** Every U.S. resident has a single distributed ledger account, opened
at enrollment in any of the above programs or at any eBank node. The account:
- Receives NIT payments (direct ledger credit)
- Receives CRHP training compensation (direct ledger credit)
- Receives ASF dividend (direct ledger credit)
- Receives Tax Return Advance Loan advances (TRAL via the same account)
- Is the payment account for all daily transactions (cashless infrastructure)
- Accumulates Social Security caregiving credit eligibility (recorded on account)

**Savings:** Eliminates separate enrollment bureaucracies for each program.
One ID verification. One account. One card. One FIP bank contract covering all.
Administrative overhead reduction: estimated $3–5B/year vs. separate systems.
The unbanked access problem is solved once, not five times.

### Consolidation 2: The Unified National Reserve System

**Programs consolidated:** ASF Strategic Reserve, SPR (Strategic Petroleum Reserve),
National Defense Stockpile, eBank physical reserves, Critical Mineral Strategic Reserve,
Space Mining reserve deposits

**Mechanism:** A single national reserve architecture under DED coordination,
with the ASF Strategic Reserve tranche as the funding vehicle and the eBank node
network as the distributed physical storage layer. Different legal governance
for different asset categories (local government controls eBank physical reserves;
DED controls critical minerals; ASF controls equity holdings) but unified inventory
management, automated replenishment triggers, and shared logistics protocols.

**Savings:** Eliminates duplicative reserve management bureaucracies. Enables
cross-category substitution (platinum group metals from space mining can flow into
the strategic reserve that previously held only terrestrial supplies). Shared CRHP
logistics workforce serves all reserve categories.

### Consolidation 3: PAA as the Single Permitting Infrastructure

**Programs consolidated:** eBank node construction, semiconductor fab permitting,
rare earth processing permitting, synthetic protein bioreactor permitting,
AHLP highway construction, Sovereign Compute data center permitting, space launch
facility permitting

**Mechanism:** All physical DED infrastructure programs draw on the same 6 PAA
mechanisms. No program-specific permitting authority is created. The Critical
Infrastructure ITC schedule is extended to cover all DED-designated projects.
The CRHP Regulatory Track provides trained reviewers across all project categories.

**Savings:** Single legislative authority rather than program-specific carve-outs.
Agency Capacity Grants to EPA, Army Corps, and others are pooled across programs.
ERP training pipeline produces reviewers who can serve multiple infrastructure
categories. Estimated permitting cost reduction: 30–50% per project across the
portfolio vs. separate permitting processes.

### Consolidation 4: ERP as the Cross-Portfolio Expert Backbone

**Programs consolidated:** NEID data science, Sovereign Compute cryptography,
eBank distributed ledger security, all four Accelerator program areas (semiconductor,
REE, protein, compute), Space Mining ISRU technology

**Mechanism:** The Expert Reserve Program's Los Alamos model — rotating experts
from industry into DED programs — is the single talent pipeline for all high-expertise
DED functions. An expert recruited for the semiconductor Accelerator is also
available to consult on the eBank cryptographic architecture. A compute expert
advising the Sovereign Compute Reserve is also available for NEID architecture
review. DED retains IP from all ERP-adjacent work, creating a compounding intellectual
property portfolio.

**Savings:** Prevents the proliferation of duplicative expert recruitment programs.
One ERP contract type covers all Accelerator needs. Expert cross-pollination between
programs produces innovation that siloed programs would not generate.

### Consolidation 5: Financial Institution Partnerships as the Universal Distribution Layer

**Programs consolidated:** NIT distribution, CRHP enrollment and compensation,
ASF dividend distribution, Tax Return Advance Loan delivery, Job Posting Penalty
compliance reporting, eBank node operation, Cashless Migration account universal access

**Mechanism:** Financial Institution Partnership contracts are structured as
omnibus service contracts covering all DED distribution needs. Banks compete for
geographic contracts (not program-specific contracts) and earn per-account revenue
on all programs served in their coverage area. One contract, one geographic
responsibility, multiple program services.

**Savings:** Eliminates program-specific distribution bureaucracies. Creates
geographic accountability — a bank that serves a region is responsible for all
DED programs in that region, preventing cherry-picking of high-value accounts.
Anti-concentration provisions (no single institution > 20% of national account base)
maintain competition. Estimated distribution cost reduction: 40–60% vs. program-
specific contracts.
