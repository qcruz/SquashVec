# DED Value Discovery Framework

**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Document Type:** Living framework + opportunity catalog

---

## The Core Reframe

Most government economic planning is defensive: identify risks, build buffers, reduce exposure.
The DED does that, but the scenario research generates a second class of output that is more
valuable and far easier to sell politically: **opportunities to create durable economic advantage**.

The mechanism is the same analytical process. When the DED maps supply chains looking for
vulnerabilities, it also finds:
- Chokepoints the U.S. could *own* rather than fear
- Capabilities it could develop at marginal cost that others would pay for indefinitely
- Structural positions in global trade that could be deliberately engineered rather than inherited

**The difference between a vulnerability and an opportunity is who controls the chokepoint.**

A world that depends on Taiwan for advanced chips is a vulnerability for the U.S.
A world that depends on the U.S. for advanced chip *designs* — while allied nations do the
manufacturing — is a revenue stream, a diplomatic lever, and a resilience mechanism simultaneously.

This document catalogs that second class of output.

---

## The Value Discovery Process

The DED runs this process on every supply chain it maps:

```
1. MAP the dependency graph
         ↓
2. IDENTIFY nodes with structural leverage potential
   (scarcity, knowledge concentration, network effects, irreversibility)
         ↓
3. ASK: Could the U.S. become the dominant supplier / IP holder / platform?
         ↓
4. MODEL the investment required and the value of that position
         ↓
5. DESIGN the agreement structure
   (access tiers, exclusivity, IP licensing, emergency production clauses)
         ↓
6. OUTPUT: A complete proposal — R&D investment + trade agreement anatomy
   → Passed to State Dept, USTR, USDA, or relevant agency for implementation
```

The final output is not a report. It is a **negotiating package**: here is what the U.S. can
offer, here is what it should receive, here is the emergency reserve clause that protects U.S.
sovereignty, and here is the suite of further terms the agreement creates leverage to negotiate.

## Opportunity Typology

DED value opportunities fall into five structural types:

### Type 1: Strategic Monopoly (Dominant Supply)
The U.S. invests to become the *dominant or exclusive* global supplier of something critical.
Allies are offered preferential access. Non-allies pay market rate or are excluded.
Emergency clause: U.S. retains full domestic production capability at any time.

**Example:** Drought-resistant seed IP for staple crops — the U.S. develops it, licenses
exclusively to food-insecure allied nations, collects royalties, and ensures they never need
to buy from a competing power.

### Type 2: Platform Control (Infrastructure as Leverage)
The U.S. builds the platform that others depend on for a critical function — financial
messaging, grid management software, biosurveillance networks, satellite navigation.
Others pay for access. Access can be tiered or conditioned on cooperation.

**Example:** GPS is the proof of concept. A next-generation climate and crop yield
prediction platform, operated by NOAA/DED, subscribed to by 60 nations, would create
the same dependency structure in a new domain.

### Type 3: IP Licensing (Manufactured Scarcity)
The U.S. funds research to create proprietary processes, genetics, or designs — and
then licenses them at tiered rates. Allies get favorable terms. Exclusivity windows create
first-mover advantage for ally economies. IP transfer limitations prevent re-export.

**Example:** Rare-earth-free magnet designs from Ames Laboratory. If successful, the
alternative chemistry is worth trillions globally. The U.S. can license it to allies
while pricing non-allied access prohibitively.

### Type 4: Standard-Setting (Invisible Platform)
The U.S. develops and propagates a technical standard that others adopt — and then
controls the certification, testing, and evolution of that standard. Becoming the
standards body is more durable than being the best manufacturer.

**Example:** Advanced nuclear reactor safety standards. If U.S. SMR designs are the
first certified, U.S. regulatory frameworks become the de facto global standard.
Every nation that wants an SMR must build to U.S. specs, use U.S.-certified components,
and be inspected by U.S.-trained certifiers.

### Type 5: Optionality Premium (Emergency Reserve Value)
The U.S. maintains idle capacity in a domain but agrees not to exercise it commercially
under normal conditions. Allies pay a premium — in trade, in diplomatic cooperation, or
in direct fees — for the assurance that U.S. production can be activated if they are cut off.

**Example:** U.S. maintains dormant phosphate processing capacity and agrees not to
compete in the global phosphate market while receiving annual "option fees" from
food-insecure allies. If their primary supplier is disrupted, the U.S. activates and
supplies at cost. The "option fee" is paid in diplomatic alignment, not cash.

### OPP-01: Advanced Crop Genetics — Niche Monopoly
**Type:** Strategic Monopoly + IP Licensing
**Scenario Origin:** Drought, Oil Restriction (fertilizer), Pandemic (food security)

**Current Situation:**
U.S. agriculture dominates commodity crops (corn, soy, wheat) in volume but competes on price.
The long-term competitive position is threatened by soil depletion, water scarcity, and
lower-cost producers. The value-add opportunity is *not* to grow more commodity corn but to
own the genetic IP that makes corn 30% more productive in water-stressed, low-input conditions.

**DED Investment:**
- Fund USDA-NIFA + land-grant university consortium (Iowa State, Purdue, UC Davis) to develop:
  - Drought-tolerant corn strains with 20-35% yield advantage in <60% normal rainfall
  - Nitrogen-use-efficient varieties (same yield at 40% less fertilizer input)
  - Disease-resistant staple varieties for tropical and sub-tropical climates (export markets)
- Dedicated elite breeding network: 12 facilities in different climate zones, continuously
  iterating toward harder environments
- Target: first-generation advanced strains in 5 years; generational improvement pipeline
- **Estimated cost:** $3–5B over 10 years (comparable to one season of crop insurance payouts)

**IP Position Developed:**
- USDA holds plant variety protection and relevant patents
- Private sector partners hold exclusive licenses with U.S. government retained rights
- Germplasm bank controlled by USDA with national security classification for lead strains

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- **Allied nations (Tier 1 access):** Exclusive rights to grow licensed varieties in their
  territory; prohibited from re-exporting germplasm; required to sell output at agreed
  floor prices to prevent dumping; pay annual IP licensing fee in cash or in-kind trade
- **U.S. emergency clause:** U.S. retains right to begin commercial production of any
  variety with 90-day notice; clause activates automatically if the licensing nation is
  sanctioned by the U.S. or enters armed conflict with a U.S. ally
- **Technology transfer limits:** Sequencing, genomic data, and breeding protocols are
  explicitly excluded from the license; the allied nation grows the seed, not the science
- **Reciprocal IP sharing clause:** Licensed nations agree to share any domestically
  developed improvements to the varieties with USDA; creates ongoing genetic improvement
  pipeline funded by allies

**Ongoing Value:**
- Licensing revenue: at $5–15/acre for varieties covering 200M acres across allied nations
  → $1–3B/year in ongoing IP revenue
- Diplomatic leverage: food security dependency is one of the most durable forms of
  alignment — allied nations that depend on U.S. seed IP for 30% yield advantage cannot
  easily shift alignment without catastrophic agricultural consequences
- Trade negotiation suite: the license agreement creates 8-12 separately negotiable terms
  (floor prices, re-export limits, IP sharing, emergency clause thresholds, sublicensing,
  third-country exclusions) — each is a chip in broader trade negotiations
- Resilience bonus: U.S. farmers benefit from the same IP through domestic licensing;
  the research investment increases U.S. agricultural resilience simultaneously

### OPP-02: Rare Earth-Free Magnet IP
**Type:** IP Licensing + Standard-Setting
**Scenario Origin:** Rare Earth Denial, Semiconductor Collapse, Oil Restriction (EV motors)

**Current Situation:**
Neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB) permanent magnets are in every EV motor, wind turbine generator,
and many defense systems. China controls ~85% of global rare earth supply and 90% of processing.
Ames National Laboratory is currently the world leader in researching rare-earth-free (REF)
alternative magnet chemistry.

**DED Investment:**
- Fund Ames REF magnet program to commercial demonstration: $1B over 5 years
  (currently funded at ~$50M/year — a 4× increase)
- Parallel program: advanced manufacturing process for REF magnets at scale (ORNL
  Manufacturing Demonstration Facility)
- Target performance: REF magnets reaching 80% of NdFeB performance at 60% of the cost

**IP Position:**
- DOE holds foundational patents on the new magnet chemistry and manufacturing process
- This is a genuine technology gap: no other nation is close to this position
- If achieved, the U.S. becomes the licensor for the magnet technology that replaces the
  Chinese-controlled rare earth supply chain

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- Allied EV and wind turbine manufacturers receive licensed manufacturing rights
- License tiered: Tier 1 (AUKUS + Five Eyes + NATO core) gets 10-year exclusivity;
  Tier 2 (other allies) gets access after exclusivity window
- Requirements: ally must use U.S.-certified manufacturing process; must not transfer
  process to non-licensed nations; must share manufacturing yield improvements with DOE
- Japan and South Korea premium arrangement: first movers get co-development credits
  (their engineers work at Ames on development) in exchange for guaranteed minimum
  annual purchase volumes
- Emergency clause: DOE retains right to open-source the IP if U.S. national security
  requires it (e.g., rapid global EV transition needed to reduce oil vulnerability)

**Ongoing Value:**
- Global magnet market is ~$25B/year and growing; a 30% IP royalty on allied production
  → $2–4B/year in peak licensing revenue
- Completely severs the Chinese rare earth leverage over U.S. allies — this is a
  geopolitical transformation, not just a product
- Enables domestic EV and wind production without China dependency
- Any nation wanting access must maintain aligned foreign policy

### OPP-03: SMR Export Platform
**Type:** Standard-Setting + IP Licensing
**Scenario Origin:** Oil Restriction, Grid Cyberattack, Financial Fragmentation

**Current Situation:**
The U.S. led global nuclear power for 40 years, then ceded market leadership to Russia (Rosatom)
and increasingly China (CGNPC). Rosatom's "nuclear diplomacy" model — building plants at
concessionary rates, providing fuel, training operators, maintaining long-term service
contracts — has given Russia durable political influence in ~30 nations.

SMRs are an opportunity to reverse this. The U.S. has 4+ SMR designs in various stages
of development (NuScale, Kairos, TerraPower, X-energy). The first nation to certify a
design at the NRC level has the globally-recognized standard.

**DED Investment:**
- Accelerate NRC certification for top 2 SMR designs: $2B federal cost-share
- Develop U.S. SMR export financing package (EXIM Bank + DFC combined facility):
  $50B standing facility for allied SMR deployment
- Establish U.S. Nuclear Fuel Service Program: U.S. enriches and delivers fuel for
  U.S.-designed reactors; establishes fuel dependency comparable to Rosatom's model
  without the coercive elements

**IP Position:**
- U.S. designs hold NRC certification; NRC certification is the global safety standard
- U.S.-enriched fuel supply gives ongoing commercial relationship with every reactor
- U.S.-trained operators and safety inspectors create professional network dependency

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- Purchasing allies receive favorable EXIM financing (2% over 30 years)
- Fuel supply agreement: U.S. provides enriched uranium for reactor lifetime at
  fixed-price escalation formulas; price for non-aligned nations is 3× aligned rate
- Technology provision: operator training, digital control systems, cybersecurity
  monitoring all provided by U.S. contractors; creates embedded presence in ally's
  energy infrastructure
- Proliferation safeguards clause: reactors include U.S.-designed fuel management
  systems that prevent weaponization; U.S. retains inspection rights
- Emergency clause: if the allied nation is threatened by an adversary, the U.S.
  guarantees fuel supply continuity; this is a security guarantee embedded in an
  energy agreement

**Ongoing Value:**
- A 1 GW SMR costs ~$3–4B to build; fuel, services, and software over 60-year life
  = $2–4B more; at 100 allied reactors, this is a $200–400B revenue stream
- Every allied nation that builds on U.S. nuclear technology maintains alignment
  incentive: sanctions would terminate fuel supply and operator support, shutting
  down a significant fraction of their power grid
- Displaces Rosatom and CGNPC from allied markets — the geopolitical value exceeds
  the commercial value
- Creates the same durable influence Rosatom has in Eastern Europe and Africa,
  but with transparent, non-coercive terms that are a genuine alternative model

### OPP-04: Climate and Crop Intelligence Platform
**Type:** Platform Control
**Scenario Origin:** Drought, Pandemic (food security), Financial Fragmentation

**Current Situation:**
NOAA, USDA, and NASA collectively operate the world's best network of climate observation,
weather modeling, and agricultural satellite data. This data is currently given away free
through public access programs. The underlying infrastructure costs ~$6B/year to operate.

**DED Value Discovery:**
What if this data were structured as a commercial intelligence service — specifically for
food and energy security decision-making — with allied governments as paying subscribers?

**DED Investment:**
- Upgrade NOAA/USDA data integration and analytics platform: $2B over 3 years
- Build agricultural intelligence API layer: field-level yield prediction, soil moisture,
  pest forecasting, 90-day harvest outlook
- Develop sovereign-grade food security dashboard: real-time global food supply chain
  visibility for allied government subscribers
- Estimated cost: $2B build + $500M/year operation (partially offset by subscriptions)

**IP Position:**
- No other nation has the satellite constellation, weather modeling infrastructure,
  and agricultural data integration the U.S. has
- This is GPS for food security: we already have the asset; the question is whether we
  treat it as a free public good or a strategic platform

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- **Allied government subscriptions:** $50–200M/year per nation depending on size;
  provides real-time food security intelligence including crop forecast, supply chain
  disruption alerts, 12-month harvest projections
- **Tiered access:** Tier 1 allies get predictive modeling and scenario analysis; Tier 2
  get current-condition data; non-allies get public data only (current situation)
- **Dependency structure:** Once a nation's agricultural and food security policy is built
  on U.S. data infrastructure, discontinuing access has immediate policy costs — creating
  the same alignment incentive as GPS
- **Emergency intelligence clause:** In the event of a food security crisis, U.S.
  provides enhanced real-time coordination support; non-aligned nations are excluded
  from emergency data sharing

**Ongoing Value:**
- At 40 allied nations paying $100M/year average → $4B/year subscription revenue
- Operationalizes existing $6B/year data infrastructure investment as a diplomatic asset
- Creates advance warning capability for U.S.: if ally's harvest is failing, DED
  knows before markets do → allows pre-positioning of food diplomacy
- Anchors ally food security planning to U.S. data → their agricultural policy is
  co-designed with U.S. intelligence inputs

### OPP-05: Pharmaceutical IP Reserve
**Type:** IP Licensing + Optionality Premium
**Scenario Origin:** Pandemic, Oil Restriction (chemical feedstocks), Financial Fragmentation

**Current Situation:**
The mRNA platform development (NIH-Moderna collaboration) demonstrated that U.S. federal
R&D can produce transformative pharmaceutical IP. The government contributed $2.5B in
research funding; Moderna's market cap peaked at $200B. The IP value capture was entirely
one-directional.

**DED Investment:**
- Fund BARDA + NIH platform programs for: (a) broad-spectrum antiviral platform;
  (b) mRNA vaccines for top 10 pandemic-risk pathogens; (c) next-generation antibiotic
  compound library for AMR
- IP structure from the start: federal retained rights + commercial licensing with
  government-controlled access tiers
- Estimated cost: $15B over 10 years (BARDA at 2× current budget)

**IP Position:**
- Broad-spectrum antiviral: a platform that works across virus families is the
  pharmaceutical equivalent of mRNA — if achieved, the IP is worth $50-100B globally
- Pre-developed mRNA vaccines for known pandemic-risk pathogens: 100-day deployment
  capability is a product the world will pay for
- AMR compound library: antimicrobial resistance is the next pandemic in slow motion;
  a novel antibiotic class is worth more than most drug approvals in history

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- Allied pharmaceutical manufacturers receive licensed manufacturing rights with
  favorable pricing in exchange for:
  - Maintaining surge manufacturing capacity for U.S. use
  - Sharing post-market safety data with FDA
  - Agreeing not to supply designated adversaries
- Developing nation access: humanitarian license at cost + 5% royalty, subject to
  not re-exporting to non-licensed nations
- Emergency clause: In a declared pandemic, U.S. invokes compulsory license for
  full domestic production; allied manufacturer licenses are suspended temporarily
  and resumed post-emergency
- Reciprocal: Allied nations that contribute clinical trial data and manufacturing
  facilities to development receive favorable second-tier pricing permanently

**Ongoing Value:**
- Pharmaceutical IP is one of the highest-margin asset classes in the global economy;
  even modest broad-spectrum antiviral IP could generate $5–20B/year
- Ensures U.S. has first access in any future pandemic: allied manufacturers produce
  for the world, but U.S. gets first 30% of output under the license terms
- Converts the COVID lesson (government funded the science, private sector captured
  the value) into a durable model where government retains appropriate IP stake

### OPP-06: Grid Control Software Standard
**Type:** Platform Control + Standard-Setting
**Scenario Origin:** Grid Cyberattack, Oil Restriction (grid integration), AI Unemployment

**Current Situation:**
U.S. power grids run a patchwork of SCADA systems, many from foreign manufacturers (ABB,
Siemens, GE-Vernova). Grid control software — the operating system of the power grid —
has no dominant U.S. platform. China has been actively promoting its own grid control
systems to developing nations.

**DED Investment:**
- Fund PNNL + Sandia + NREL to develop open-architecture, U.S.-standard grid
  operating system: covers generation dispatch, transmission management, market
  clearing, cybersecurity monitoring
- Open-source the base layer (like Linux); keep security and intelligence modules
  proprietary (like Red Hat Enterprise)
- Certify through NERC and position for IEC international standards adoption
- Estimated cost: $2B over 5 years

**IP Position:**
- U.S. controls the security and intelligence modules that sit on top of the open platform
- Any grid running the platform is observable to U.S. cybersecurity monitoring systems
  (with ally consent) — creating real-time allied grid visibility
- U.S. software updates are required for continued security certification

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- Free base platform to all nations; security modules available to allies only
- Allied grid operators receive U.S. cybersecurity monitoring support as part of
  the security module subscription
- Emergency response: if an allied grid is under attack, U.S. cyber teams can
  interface directly with the grid control system; creates a NATO-equivalent
  mutual defense clause for grid infrastructure
- Exclusion clause: nations that adopt adversary grid systems (Chinese SCADA)
  are excluded from U.S. cybersecurity monitoring; creates explicit choice

**Ongoing Value:**
- Security module subscriptions: $10–50M/nation/year for ~60 allied nations
  → $600M–3B/year
- Real-time visibility into allied grid stability as a foreign policy intelligence asset
- Makes adversary SCADA systems a credibility/alignment issue in allied procurement
- Positions U.S. to be the grid cybersecurity responder globally — building the
  institutional expertise and the relationships simultaneously

### OPP-07: Water Purification and Desalination IP
**Type:** IP Licensing + Optionality Premium
**Scenario Origin:** Drought, Pandemic (water security), Financial Fragmentation

**Current Situation:**
Israel (IDE Technologies) and Saudi Arabia (ACWA Power) are currently the world's leading
desalination technology providers. The U.S. has strong university research in membrane
technology (MIT, Yale, UCSB) but no dominant commercial platform.

Water scarcity is the defining resource constraint of the 21st century. Nations that control
water purification technology will have leverage comparable to nations that controlled oil in
the 20th century.

**DED Investment:**
- Fund NREL + MIT + UCSB consortium for next-generation desalination: targeting
  50% energy reduction vs. current reverse osmosis through electrodialysis reversal
  + solar-thermal hybrid systems
- Companion: atmospheric water generation for distributed use (portable, small-scale,
  relevant to military and disaster relief)
- Estimated cost: $3B over 8 years

**IP Position:**
- 50% energy reduction in desalination is a transformative improvement in a market
  currently constrained by energy cost
- Atmospheric water generation at scale has no current commercial equivalent

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- Water-stressed allies (MENA, Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia) receive licensed
  manufacturing and deployment rights in exchange for:
  - Political alignment on key UN votes
  - Access to strategic minerals or agricultural land for U.S. interests
  - Agreeing not to transfer technology to adversaries
- U.S. military retains independent deployment right for any atmospheric water generation
  technology globally
- Emergency water supply clause: U.S. agrees to priority deployment of mobile
  desalination units to allied nations experiencing acute water emergencies

**Ongoing Value:**
- Global water treatment market is $300B/year and growing; IP royalties on improved
  desalination could reach $5–15B/year
- Water security is becoming a migration and conflict driver; nations that can provide
  water technology are among the most strategically valuable allies
- Converts U.S. from net buyer of energy (oil) to net seller of energy-efficiency
  (water technology that reduces energy cost of desalination)

### OPP-08: Synthetic Biology for Industrial Chemicals
**Type:** Strategic Monopoly + IP Licensing
**Scenario Origin:** Oil Restriction (petrochemical feedstocks), Rare Earth Denial

**Current Situation:**
~40% of industrial chemicals currently derived from petroleum could theoretically be
produced through engineered microorganisms. This is already happening commercially at
small scale (Ginkgo Bioworks, LanzaTech, Genomatica). The U.S. leads in synthetic biology
research; the IP position is fragmented.

**DED Investment:**
- Fund DOE Joint BioEnergy Institute (JBEI) + DARPA Biological Technologies Office
  for priority compounds: ethylene, propylene, butadiene, benzene, adipic acid
  (nylon precursor), and key pharmaceutical intermediates
- Target: microorganism strains that produce each compound at >90% of petroleum
  chemistry economics from agricultural waste or CO2
- Estimated cost: $5B over 10 years

**IP Position:**
- Microorganism strains and associated bioprocesses are patentable
- U.S. is already the global leader in synthetic biology; coordinated federal IP
  strategy converts research leadership to commercial leverage
- The "operating system" of industrial bioprocessing becomes U.S.-controlled

**Agreement Anatomy:**
- Allied chemical companies receive strain licenses for priority compounds in exchange for:
  - Building production capacity that can surge to U.S. supply in emergencies
  - Sharing process improvement data with JBEI under joint IP arrangement
  - Agreeing to price U.S. market access in USD
- Non-allied nations access only lower-generation strains; the efficiency gap
  maintains competitive advantage
- Emergency clause: U.S. retains right to deploy any strain for domestic industrial
  production immediately if petroleum restriction exceeds Tier 2

**Ongoing Value:**
- Industrial chemicals are a $5T+ global market; IP on the microbial routes to key
  building blocks could generate $10–30B/year at full deployment
- Severs the global oil-chemistry dependency entirely for allied industrial sectors
- Creates a new export category: biological manufacturing IP, not physical goods

## The Agreement Anatomy Template

Every DED value discovery proposal should include a standardized agreement structure.
The following template applies across all opportunity types:

```
DED TRADE AGREEMENT ANATOMY

1. GRANT
   What the U.S. provides: [IP license / technology access / data / supply commitment]
   Geographic scope: [territory of licensed nation; no re-export]
   Duration: [typically 10-25 years with renewal options]

2. CONSIDERATION
   What the allied nation provides in return:
   a. Direct: [licensing fees / purchase minimums / royalties]
   b. Indirect: [diplomatic alignment commitments / vote patterns / basing rights /
      reciprocal IP sharing / trade concessions]

3. PERFORMANCE STANDARDS
   What the allied nation must do to maintain the license:
   [minimum production volumes / quality standards / audit rights /
    security protocols / non-transfer obligations]

4. EMERGENCY CLAUSE (non-negotiable in all agreements)
   "In the event the United States declares a national economic emergency under
   [DED Emergency Authority], the United States reserves the right to [produce
   domestically / activate dormant capacity / suspend license terms] without penalty
   and with [X days] notice. The allied nation acknowledges this right as a condition
   of the agreement."

5. EXCLUSION TRIGGERS
   Conditions that automatically suspend or terminate the agreement:
   [armed conflict with U.S. or U.S. ally / sanctions designation /
    material breach of non-transfer obligations / adoption of adversary alternatives]

6. IP INHERITANCE
   Improvements developed by the licensed nation:
   [joint ownership / mandatory sharing / right of first refusal for USDA/DOE/etc.]

7. NEGOTIATING SUITE
   Additional terms available as negotiating chips:
   [sublicensing rights / third-country extension / exclusivity period length /
    emergency clause threshold / dispute resolution forum / inspection rights]
```

The negotiating suite in item 7 is where the diplomatic value lives. Each additional term is
a separately negotiable point — a chip that can be offered or withheld in exchange for progress
on an unrelated diplomatic objective (military basing, vote in multilateral forum, bilateral
trade concession, intelligence sharing agreement).

## Value Discovery Update Process

**When to add a new opportunity:**
- A new DED scenario is mapped and reveals a structural supply dependency → ask whether
  the U.S. can flip from dependent to dominant
- A national lab research program reaches a maturity level where IP value is estimable
- A new geopolitical development changes the attractiveness of an existing opportunity

**What each opportunity entry must contain:**
1. Opportunity ID and Type (from typology)
2. Originating scenario(s)
3. Current situation (the vulnerability or gap being addressed)
4. DED investment required (cost, duration, who executes)
5. IP position developed
6. Agreement anatomy (using template above)
7. Ongoing value (revenue estimate, diplomatic leverage, resilience benefit)

**Review cycle:** Full catalog review annually; triggered review when a new scenario is added
or a national lab program reaches TRL 4+ (technology readiness, demonstrated in lab).
