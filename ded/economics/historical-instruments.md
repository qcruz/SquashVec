# Historical Analysis: Government Economic Instruments as Strategic Tools

**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Document Type:** Historical analysis — DED instrument precedent library

---

## Purpose

Every tool the DED proposes to use has been used before, by the U.S. government or by
peer nations, in some form. This document catalogs those precedents — not as justification,
but as evidence of mechanism. A tool with a documented track record is a tool whose
behavior is understood. A proposal with a historical precedent is a proposal that can
be defended in congressional testimony.

More importantly: many of these tools were used defensively, reactively, or in isolation.
The DED combines them, applies them proactively, and pre-designs the deployment conditions
so the right instrument is already structured when the moment arrives. The historical
record is the raw material. The DED framework is the synthesis.

## I. Government as Anchor Buyer — Creating Markets Through Procurement

The most powerful peacetime economic instrument a government possesses is not taxation
or regulation — it is guaranteed demand. When the government commits to buying a large
volume of something, it changes the economics of producing it. Risk is removed from
the manufacturer. Learning curves compress. Costs fall. A market becomes viable that
would not have existed otherwise.

### Semiconductors and Silicon Valley (1955–1975)

In 1960, the U.S. Department of Defense purchased approximately **60% of all integrated
circuits produced in the United States**. This was not a deliberate industrial policy; it
was procurement of components for weapons systems and the nascent space program. But the
effect was decisive: government demand gave Fairchild, Texas Instruments, and later Intel
the volume required to drive manufacturing costs down the learning curve at a rate that
made commercial viability possible.

By 1975, the commercial semiconductor market had grown to exceed military procurement.
The government's role had shifted from anchor buyer to minor customer. But the industry
that produced commercial semiconductors — and ultimately the personal computer,
the internet, and the modern digital economy — was built on the back of defense procurement
that de-risked the first decade of production.

**DED application:** Type A readiness contracts and Type B developmental contracts are
the institutionalized version of this pattern. The DED does not wait for a crisis to
become a dominant buyer. It pre-commits purchasing volume for the capabilities the nation
will need, at the scale required to make those capabilities economically viable.

### DARPA and the Creation of Entire Industries (1958–Present)

DARPA's model is the clearest historical precedent for the DED's Accelerator programs.
In 63 years, DARPA's research investments created or directly enabled:

| DARPA Program | Outcome | Economic Scale |
|---------------|---------|---------------|
| ARPANET (1969) | Internet | $5T+ global GDP contribution |
| GPS (1973–1995) | Global navigation standard | $1.4T/year in economic value (RTI, 2019) |
| Stealth aircraft (1975) | B-2, F-22 manufacturing base | Multiple platform generations |
| Speech recognition (1971) | Siri, Alexa, voice AI | $20B+ market |
| Touchscreen interfaces (1970s) | iPhone and tablet computing | $500B+ market |
| mRNA vaccine research (2010s) | COVID vaccines | $100B+ Moderna/BioNTech |

**The common mechanism in every case:**
DARPA contracted with researchers to solve a specific problem at the government's
expense, retained enough IP influence to ensure the results were deployable for national
security, and allowed commercial applications to develop freely once the core technology
was demonstrated. The government absorbed the early-stage risk that private capital would
not have financed. The private sector captured most of the commercial upside, but the
nation retained the strategic capability.

**DED application:** The Accelerator program is DARPA's model applied to economic
security rather than military technology. Where DARPA runs programs for specific
agencies, Accelerators recruit competing private sector experts, removing IP barriers
through classification, and retain government rights to the resulting technology.
See `ded/tools/expert-reserve-program.md`.

### Agricultural Price Supports as Industrial Policy (1933–Present)

The New Deal-era agricultural support programs — initially designed as emergency income
stabilization — had an unintended structural effect: they industrialized American farming.
By guaranteeing a price floor, the government removed the income uncertainty that had
prevented farm operations from investing in capital equipment. The result was mechanization,
scale, and the most productive agricultural system in history.

The Food for Peace program (PL 480, 1954) extended this: surplus production created by
domestic price supports was deployed as food aid and concessional sales to developing
nations. This created customer relationships that later converted to commercial trade —
nations that received U.S. food aid in the 1950s and 1960s became import markets for
U.S. agricultural exports in the 1970s and 1980s.

**Key mechanism:** The government used purchasing power to shape an industry, then used
the resulting surplus as a diplomatic tool, then stepped back as commercial relationships
took over.

**DED application:** The OPP-01 crop genetics program follows this exact pattern:
government funds the R&D investment, retains IP, licenses to allies at favorable terms
(creating food security dependency and diplomatic alignment), and harvests revenue as the
commercial market for advanced seed varieties matures.

### Operation Warp Speed — The Pre-Purchase Model (2020)

The most recent and directly relevant example. The U.S. government committed to purchase
vaccines **before they had been demonstrated to work** — paying manufacturers for doses
during Phase III trials, de-risking the manufacturing scale-up. The key innovation was
not scientific; it was financial. By removing the commercial risk of building manufacturing
capacity before proof of efficacy, the government accelerated deployment by 6–12 months.

The total cost: ~$18B in pre-purchase commitments. The value: the 2020 COVID GDP impact
was approximately $2.5T. The 6-month acceleration alone is worth hundreds of billions
in recovered economic output.

**DED application:** Type B developmental contracts use the same pre-purchase structure:
milestones trigger payments before the product is commercially proven. The government
absorbs development risk; the contractor provides the capability. This is not a subsidy —
it is payment for a service: bearing the risk the private market will not bear.

## II. Trade Restrictions as Affirmative Market-Shaping Tools

Trade restrictions are typically framed as defensive — protecting domestic industries
from foreign competition. But the historical record shows they have been used far more
aggressively: as tools to restructure global market dynamics in the U.S.'s favor, compel
behavioral changes from trading partners, and preserve U.S. technological leadership.

### The 1986 U.S.-Japan Semiconductor Agreement

By 1985, Japanese manufacturers had seized more than 60% of the global DRAM market.
U.S. producers were being priced out through a combination of government subsidization,
favorable financing, and aggressive export pricing. The U.S. response was not tariffs.

The 1986 agreement required Japan to:
1. **Set minimum prices** on semiconductor exports to the U.S. (removing the pricing weapon)
2. **Guarantee foreign semiconductor manufacturers** — primarily U.S. — at least **20% of
   Japan's domestic market**

The result: by 1993, the U.S. share of the Japanese semiconductor market exceeded 20%
for the first time. More importantly, the minimum pricing requirement removed the cost
competition that had driven U.S. manufacturers out of commodity memory — redirecting
U.S. investment toward microprocessors, where Intel established the dominance it holds today.

**The DED lesson:** Trade restrictions can be used not only to protect domestic industry
but to **restructure which segment of a market the U.S. competes in**. The U.S. did not
try to win the DRAM commodity battle — it extracted market access concessions and
redirected its competitive focus to a higher-value segment. This is the correct model
for DED trade negotiations: do not try to compete everywhere; instead, use trade leverage
to ensure U.S. dominance in the segments where IP value is highest.

### COCOM and Wassenaar — Export Controls as Competitive Advantage (1949–Present)

The Coordinating Committee for Multilateral Export Controls (COCOM) was established in
1949 to prevent the Soviet Union from acquiring Western technology. Its successor, the
Wassenaar Arrangement (1996), continues to coordinate export controls among 42 nations.

The mechanism is straightforward: any technology on the control list requires an
export license to sell to non-member nations. The effect is less obvious: by restricting
who can buy the most advanced technology, the U.S. ensures that only aligned nations
can incorporate it into their industrial base. Nations that want access must maintain
political alignment. Nations that lose alignment lose access — and lose the capability
gap that access provides.

**Huawei and the Entity List (2019–present):** The Commerce Department's Entity List
evolved from an administrative export compliance tool to a primary geopolitical instrument.
By designating Huawei, the U.S. cut off not just direct U.S. exports but any product
manufactured using U.S. software, technology, or equipment anywhere in the world
(the Foreign Direct Product Rule). Taiwan Semiconductor, the most important chip
manufacturer in the world, cannot supply Huawei without a U.S. license — regardless of
the fact that TSMC is not a U.S. company.

**DED application:** DED-developed IP carries equivalent leverage. Any technology
developed through a strategic contract or Accelerator program retains U.S. government
rights under the contract terms — which means the government controls who can access it.
The Wassenaar/Entity List model applies: access is a function of alignment. DED IP
agreements pre-structure this access tiering explicitly (see `ded/economics/value-discovery.md`).

### Buy American and Domestic Preference — Procurement as Industrial Policy

The Buy American Act (1933) and subsequent domestic preference requirements in federal
procurement are the most sustained use of government purchasing power as industrial policy
in U.S. history. The requirement that federal agencies buy U.S.-made goods — even at a
cost premium — functions as a guaranteed revenue floor for domestic manufacturers.

Recent applications:
- **CHIPS Act (2022):** $52B in direct subsidies plus investment tax credits for U.S.
  semiconductor manufacturing. Crucially, CHIPS recipients are prohibited from
  using the funds to expand advanced manufacturing in China for 10 years. The subsidy
  is conditioned on alignment with U.S. economic security policy.
- **Inflation Reduction Act (2022):** EV and clean energy tax credits conditioned on
  domestic content — North American vehicle assembly, U.S.-mined or processed battery
  minerals. This is domestic preference via tax code rather than procurement regulation.

**DED application:** Every DED strategic contract includes performance requirements
that function as domestic preference conditions. Type A and B contracts require domestic
production capability be maintained as a condition of payment. This is more targeted than
blanket Buy American requirements — it preserves specific capabilities rather than
subsidizing broad industrial categories.

## III. IP as Strategic Instrument — Rights, Seizure, and Licensing

### The Bayh-Dole Act and Government March-In Rights (1980)

Before 1980, IP created with federal research funding was owned by the federal government
and largely sat unused in government vaults. Universities and contractors had no incentive
to commercialize federally funded research because they could not own the resulting patents.

The Bayh-Dole Act reversed this: universities, small businesses, and contractors could
now own patents developed with federal funding. The result was the commercialization
of the U.S. biotech and tech transfer industry. Universities became licensing entities.
Startup companies formed around university research. The U.S. comparative advantage in
basic research converted to commercial output.

**The government's retained rights:** Bayh-Dole included a provision that the federal
funding agency retains a **paid-up, nonexclusive license** to practice any invention
for government purposes — and **march-in rights** under Section 203: the government
can require the patent owner to license the IP to other manufacturers if the owner
is not making it available to the public on reasonable terms.

March-in rights have never been successfully exercised. They exist as a **threat** —
and the threat is real. Pharmaceutical companies that received federal R&D funding
(NIH grants, BARDA contracts) are aware that extreme pricing could trigger a march-in
action, requiring them to license to generic manufacturers. The DED would formalize this
threat as a pre-agreed licensing structure in all strategic contracts: the government's
license terms are negotiated up front rather than litigated in an emergency.

**DED application:** All DED strategic contracts (Types A, B, C) include explicit IP
provisions: government retains a royalty-free license; the contractor retains commercial
rights; in a declared DED emergency the government can produce or require licensing
without court action. This is Bayh-Dole's march-in right, pre-contracted rather than
left to litigation.

---

### Government Use Exception — 28 U.S.C. § 1498

Less known but more powerful than march-in rights: the U.S. government has statutory
authority to use any patented invention without a license. A patent holder cannot
obtain an injunction against the government — only "reasonable compensation" in the
Court of Federal Claims. This provision has been used to:

- Procure generic drugs at government facilities without manufacturer consent
- Use proprietary software in government computer systems
- Manufacture military equipment incorporating privately held patents

The authority is effectively unlimited. The only constraint is compensation, not permission.

**DED application:** In a declared national economic emergency, the government can invoke
§1498 to compel licensing or direct production of any IP relevant to the emergency —
pharmaceutical compounds, industrial processes, software platforms, manufacturing methods.
The DED pre-documents the IP landscape for each scenario so the relevant patents and
owners are identified before activation is needed.

### WWII Patent Pools — Compelled Cross-Licensing (1917, 1942)

During World War I, the aircraft industry was paralyzed by patent disputes between the
Wright Company and Curtiss Corporation. Neither side could license the other; neither
could build a complete aircraft without infringing. The U.S. could not produce the planes
it needed for the war.

The government's solution: the Manufacturers Aircraft Association (MAA), a government-
brokered patent pool in which all aircraft manufacturers cross-licensed their patents
to each other for a flat fee. The competitive IP barrier was simply removed by government
action. The industry scaled rapidly.

This pattern repeated in World War II across multiple industries. The War Production Board
maintained the authority to compel patent licenses necessary for war production. Pharmaceutical
patents, chemical processes, and manufacturing methods were all subject to compelled licensing
when production required it.

**The key mechanism:** Competitive IP barriers destroy the concentration and collaboration
that produces the best outcomes in a national emergency. The government's power to remove
those barriers — either by threat or by action — is the same mechanism the DED Accelerator
programs use. Accelerators remove competitive IP barriers through classification and
pre-agreed government IP terms rather than compulsion. The voluntary version achieves the
same outcome with less resistance.

### Post-WWII German IP Seizure — Redistributive Use of Captured IP

After World War II, the Allied powers seized the patents of German industrial and
pharmaceutical companies under the Alien Property Act and the Trading with the Enemy Act.
I.G. Farben's chemical and pharmaceutical patents — representing decades of research in
synthetic fibers, dyes, pharmaceuticals, and industrial chemistry — were distributed to
U.S. companies at little or no cost.

The beneficiaries included DuPont, Dow Chemical, and multiple U.S. pharmaceutical firms.
The effect: the U.S. chemical and pharmaceutical industry gained a 10–15 year competitive
head start relative to rebuilding German firms. Many foundational patents in industries
that generated hundreds of billions in revenue over subsequent decades came from this
single post-war IP redistribution.

**DED lesson:** IP position is not permanent. It can be created rapidly through concentrated
investment (DARPA, Accelerators) and it can be acquired through policy (government contracts,
march-in rights, § 1498). The DED's value discovery framework treats IP creation as
deliberate and achievable — not as something that happens passively through market R&D.

### TRIPS and the Compulsory Licensing Framework (1994–Present)

The Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (TRIPS, 1994)
was largely driven by U.S. pharmaceutical and technology industry lobbying to require
WTO member nations to provide patent protection. Before TRIPS, nations like India could
legally manufacture generic versions of patented drugs — and they did, providing
affordable versions of HIV/AIDS medications to much of the developing world.

TRIPS eliminated generic manufacturing of patented drugs in most cases. But it included
a safety valve: **Article 31 compulsory licensing**, which allows any member nation to
override a patent in a public health emergency by issuing a compulsory license to
domestic manufacturers.

Nations that have used Article 31:
- **India** — forced generic licenses for HIV/AIDS drugs (2012); cancer medications (Novartis case)
- **Thailand** — issued compulsory licenses for HIV/AIDS and heart disease medications (2006–2007)
- **South Africa** — generic ARV production that ultimately served millions across Sub-Saharan Africa
- **Brazil** — efavirenz (HIV medication) compulsory license (2007) after Merck refused price negotiations

**The power dynamic this reveals:** IP protection is a diplomatic instrument that can be
tightened (TRIPS) or loosened (compulsory licensing) based on geopolitical context. The U.S.
pushed TRIPS to protect pharmaceutical revenue. It quietly tolerated compulsory licensing
by allies facing genuine health emergencies. Adversaries who needed U.S. pharmaceutical IP
found access conditioned on policy alignment.

**DED application:** The DED inverts this model as an affirmative tool: pre-design IP
licensing agreements that give allied nations compulsory license-equivalent access to DED-
developed pharmaceutical IP under health emergency conditions — turning what TRIPS treats
as an exception into a deliberate diplomatic instrument. See `ded/economics/ip-licensing-as-trade-tool.md` [STUB].

## IV. The DED Synthesis — Proactive, Coordinated, Pre-Designed

The historical record establishes that every instrument the DED proposes to use has
significant precedent. The difference between historical use and DED use is not the
instrument — it is the design intent and coordination:

| Historical Pattern | DED Version |
|-------------------|-------------|
| DARPA creates technology; commercial rights fragmented | Accelerators create technology; government IP rights pre-contracted; licensing terms pre-designed |
| Semiconductor procurement creates market; not planned | Type B contracts pre-commit volume to create markets for specific capabilities the scenario library requires |
| COCOM restricts adversary access; reactive to Cold War | DED IP agreements tier access by alignment; pre-built exclusion triggers; continuous rather than episodic |
| March-in rights exist as threat; never exercised | DED contracts pre-negotiate licensing terms; march-in equivalent pre-agreed rather than litigated |
| WWII patent pools compelled by emergency; ad hoc | Accelerator programs create voluntary cross-licensing through classification; same mechanism without compulsion |
| TRIPS creates global IP protection for U.S. pharma | DED designs selective compulsory licensing for allies as diplomatic tool; turns TRIPS exception into deliberate instrument |
| Food for Peace creates food dependency, converts to trade | OPP-01 creates seed IP dependency for allied nations; converts development aid into commercial licensing revenue |
| § 1498 authority documented but rarely used | DED scenario plans pre-identify IP landscape; § 1498 activation prepared as contingency for each scenario |

**The institutional gap this fills:**

Each of these historical precedents was executed by a different agency — DoD, DARPA,
USDA, State, USTR, Commerce — without coordination. The result was:
- IP created at DARPA was not leveraged by USTR in trade negotiations
- Government use rights under § 1498 were not pre-planned for economic security scenarios
- TRIPS negotiations did not reflect DED-equivalent economic security considerations
- Defense procurement that could have shaped strategic industries was not coordinated
  with trade policy that could have locked in the resulting competitive advantages

The DED provides the coordination layer. Not by taking authority from other agencies —
but by maintaining the analysis that ensures each agency knows what the others hold,
and what combination of their instruments can be deployed together for maximum effect.
