# DED Historical Loss Baseline

**Purpose:** Reference document of quantified economic losses from past crises, used to anchor DED scenario loss estimates.
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Update Frequency:** When new significant events occur or better retrospective data is published

All figures adjusted to approximate 2026 USD where noted. Sources cited for each entry.

---

## Methodology Note

Historical losses are used two ways:
1. **Direct analog:** A past event is directly comparable to a DED scenario (e.g., 2012 drought → Drought scenario loss estimate)
2. **Scaling anchor:** A past event is smaller than the DED scenario but provides a rate ($/day, $/percentage point of GDP) that can be scaled to the DED scenario magnitude

Loss figures include direct damage, lost output, government response costs, and where available, long-term economic impairment. Insurance payouts alone consistently undercount total economic impact by 3-5×.

## Pandemic

### COVID-19 (2020–Present)
**DED Scenario Analog:** Pandemic Tier 1–2

| Loss Category | Amount | Source |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| 10-year U.S. GDP loss (CBO baseline deviation) | $7.9T | Congressional Budget Office, 2021 |
| Total economic impact including behavioral (Fed NY) | ~$16T | Federal Reserve Bank of New York, Goodman & Mance 2021 |
| Federal direct relief spending (CARES, ARP, etc.) | ~$5.2T | Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget |
| Lost business revenue (2020–2021) | ~$3.7T | U.S. Chamber of Commerce; McKinsey |
| Healthcare system excess costs | ~$600B | Peterson-KFF Health System Tracker |
| Estimated deaths × VSL ($11.6M USDOT) | ~$9.3T (statistical) | CDC deaths × DOT Value of Statistical Life |

**Key finding:** Federal emergency spending of $5.2T was the *response* cost, not the damage. The damage (lost GDP) was ~$8-16T. DED investment that compressed the damage by half would save 2-3× the entire DED budget in the first pandemic alone.

**DED mitigation assumption basis:**
- Pre-positioned PPE (SNS rebuild): reduces healthcare worker attrition by 30-40% → 2-3 week faster containment
- Pre-built essential worker framework: reduces supply chain cascade by 40%
- Pre-built income support (paid sick leave): reduces "work while sick" behavior by 35%, lowering R0
- Combined effect modeled at 45-55% reduction in total economic impact

### 1918 Influenza Pandemic
**DED Scenario Analog:** Pandemic Tier 2–3

| Loss Category | Amount (2026$) | Source |
|--------------|---------------|--------|
| U.S. GDP reduction (1918–1919) | ~$1.1T equivalent | Barro, Ursua, Weng, 2020 (NBER); GDP-scaled |
| U.S. deaths | 675,000 | CDC estimate |
| Deaths × VSL equivalent (2026$) | ~$7.8T statistical | CDC × DOT VSL |

**Why this matters:** The 1918 pandemic occurred before modern supply chains, global trade, and just-in-time inventory. A Tier 2+ pandemic today would hit an economy far more interconnected and fragile. The 2026 equivalent of 1918 mortality at modern VSL values is ~$7.8T — before accounting for supply chain and economic multipliers.

### Colorado River Basin Multi-Year Decline (2000–Present)
**DED Scenario Analog:** Drought Tier 2 (slow-onset)

| Loss Category | Amount | Source |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| Agricultural output decline (CA + AZ + NV, 2020–2025) | ~$8B cumulative | USDA; University of Arizona Water Resources Research Center |
| Groundwater substitution costs (pumping premium) | ~$2B/year | Various state water agencies |
| Lake Mead hydropower reduction (2021–2024) | ~$500M/year | Reclamation Bureau |
| Municipal water rate increases (CO, NV, AZ, CA) | ~$3B/year across affected population | State utility filings |
| Estimated future loss (without intervention, 2026–2040) | $50–200B | University of Colorado CU Boulder; RAND 2023 |

**DED implication:** The Colorado River is a slow-motion crisis already costing $5-7B/year and accelerating. The total cost of inaction over 15 years likely exceeds $100B — and the DED-optimal intervention (aquifer management, drip irrigation mandate, crop transition) costs a fraction of that.

## Energy and Oil

### 1973–74 Arab Oil Embargo
**DED Scenario Analog:** Oil Restriction Tier 2

| Loss Category | Amount (2026$) | Source |
|--------------|---------------|--------|
| U.S. GDP decline (4.9% reduction, 1974) | ~$600B (2026$) | BLS; Federal Reserve historical |
| Real disposable income loss | ~$400B (2026$) | BLS CPI/income data, inflation-adjusted |
| Inflation spike (CPI peaked +12.3% by 1974) | Embedded in above | BLS |
| Stock market decline (S&P -48% peak to trough) | ~$2T wealth loss (2026$) | S&P historical |

**Scaling note:** 1973-74 oil embargo cut supply by ~7-8%. A 25% Tier 2 restriction in 2026 would be approximately 3× more severe on supply but would affect an economy 8× larger in nominal terms. A rough order-of-magnitude estimate of $3-5T in economic impact is defensible.

### 2021 Colonial Pipeline Attack
**DED Scenario Analog:** Grid/Infrastructure Cyberattack (partial)

| Loss Category | Amount | Source |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| Shutdown duration | 6 days | DHS / FBI investigation timeline |
| Direct economic impact | ~$4.4B | University of Illinois Infrastructure analysis |
| Southeast fuel price spike | +28 cents/gallon average (+12%) | AAA |
| Panic buying economic distortion | ~$600M additional | EIA |
| Ransom payment | $4.4M (partially recovered) | FBI |

**Key insight:** Six days of one pipeline = $4.4B. A simultaneous attack on three major energy infrastructure nodes (Colonial + major ISO + large refinery complex) could produce $50-100B in damage in the first week alone. The attack demonstrated that cyber-physical infrastructure attacks have immediate, severe, measurable economic consequences.

### 2021 Winter Storm Uri (Texas Power Failure)
**DED Scenario Analog:** Grid Cyberattack (cold weather variant), but provides cost-per-failure-hour data

| Loss Category | Amount | Source |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| Total economic damage (Texas) | $195B | Moody's Analytics |
| Physical infrastructure damage | $50B | Texas state estimate |
| Death toll | 246 (official); estimated 700+ actual | DSHS; Buzzfeed/academic analysis |
| Deaths × VSL | $2.9B–$8.1B statistical | |
| Duration | ~4 days of widespread outages | ERCOT post-event report |
| Cost per day of widespread outage | ~$49B/day (Texas alone) | Derived |

**Scaling:** ERCOT serves ~26M people. Eastern Interconnection serves ~260M. A proportional large-scale grid failure affecting the full Eastern Interconnection for 4 days would imply ~$490B in economic damage — and Eastern grid failures are potentially longer-duration than Texas's winter storm event.

### 2022 European Energy Crisis (Russia gas restriction)
**DED Scenario Analog:** Oil/Energy Restriction Tier 2–3 (for Europe)

| Loss Category | Amount | Source |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| European natural gas price peak | ~€300/MWh (10× pre-crisis) | ICE exchange data |
| EU GDP impact (2022 reduction) | ~€250-350B (-1.5 to -2% GDP) | IMF WEO; ECB |
| German industrial production decline | -3.5% manufacturing output in 2022 | Destatis |
| Estimated household energy cost increase (EU) | ~€800B cumulatively 2021–2023 | Bruegel Institute |
| U.S. energy inflation contribution (2022) | ~$300B additional consumer spending | EIA; BLS CPI |

**Lesson:** A 50% reduction in European gas supply caused 1.5-2% GDP contraction in one of the world's most advanced economies. The U.S. is less gas-dependent than Germany but more dependent on petroleum. A comparable restriction on U.S. oil supply would produce similar or larger impacts.

## Financial

### 2008–2009 Financial Crisis
**DED Scenario Analog:** Financial Fragmentation Tier 2–3 (domestic trigger)

| Loss Category | Amount | Source |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| Household wealth lost (peak to trough) | $19.2T | Federal Reserve Flow of Funds |
| U.S. GDP decline (peak to trough) | -4.3%; ~$700B at-trough | BEA |
| 10-year GDP gap (below pre-crisis trend) | ~$13T | CBO; economists' estimates |
| Federal bailout total (TARP + Fed facilities) | ~$700B TARP; ~$7.7T Fed peak exposure | SIGTARP; Fed |
| Unemployment peak | 10% (Oct 2009) | BLS |

**DED framing:** The 2008 crisis was triggered endogenously (domestic financial system failure). A financial fragmentation scenario is exogenous — external pressure on dollar reserve status or payment systems — which would be harder to contain using existing Fed/Treasury tools designed for domestic crises.

## Infrastructure

### 2003 Northeast Blackout
**DED Scenario Analog:** Grid Cyberattack Tier 1

| Loss Category | Amount (2026$) | Source |
|--------------|---------------|--------|
| Total economic loss | ~$8B (2026$) | DOE; Lawrence Berkeley National Lab |
| Duration | ~4 days (some areas 2 days) | DOE investigation |
| Affected population | 55 million | DOE |
| Cost per person-day | ~$36/person-day | Derived |

**Scaling note:** The 2003 blackout affected ~17% of U.S. population for 2-4 days. A major EHV transformer attack affecting 70% of the Eastern Interconnection (per FERC classified estimates) for 18 months would scale to $2-4T+ — and would be a physical infrastructure problem, not a switching fault, meaning it cannot be resolved in 4 days.
