# DED Fiscal Integration Analysis

**Document Type:** System-Level Budget and Revenue Analysis
**Last Updated:** 2026-05
**Category:** Economics — Fiscal Architecture
**Purpose:** Consolidates all DED program costs, revenue streams, offsets, and net fiscal
  impact across Year 1, Year 5, Year 10, and Year 30. Answers the CBO-style question:
  "What does this all cost and what are the offsets?"

---

## Overview

The DED portfolio contains fourteen major programs spanning six budget categories:
institutional infrastructure, labor and training, revenue and investment, economic floors,
market correction, and resilience operations. No single document has previously consolidated
the full fiscal picture.

This analysis presents:

1. Gross cost by program (what we spend)
2. Revenue generated by program (what flows back)
3. Crisis prevention value (what we avoid spending)
4. Net fiscal position by year (the bottom line)
5. Distribution of cost vs. self-funding across the portfolio

**Key finding:** The DED portfolio's gross cost at full implementation (Year 10) is
approximately $175-240B/year. Revenue streams and offsets reduce net federal cost to
$60-95B/year. Crisis prevention value — the avoided cost of unpreparedness — is
$380-680B/year in expected value terms. The ratio of net cost to expected-value benefit
is approximately 1:6 to 1:9.

## Section 1: Gross Cost by Program (Year 10 Steady State)

### Category A: Institutional Infrastructure

| Program | Annual Cost | Funding Source | Net Federal Cost |
|---------|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Department of Economic Defense operations | $8-12B | Congressional appropriation | $8-12B |
| National Economic Intelligence Dashboard | $1.5-2.5B | DED budget | Included above |
| Supply Chain Risk Framework operations | $0.5-1B | DED budget | Included above |
| Expert Reserve Program | $2-4B | DED budget / OTA contracts | Included above |

**Category A total: $8-12B/year (net federal; fully appropriated)**

The DED institutional budget is a fixed cost for the intelligence and contracting function.
At $10B/year average, this is roughly 1.4% of the current defense budget. The analogy is
appropriate: DARPA runs at approximately $4B/year; DED's broader mandate justifies 2-3× that
level. The JCED oversight function ensures this budget is subject to annual reauthorization.

### Category B: Labor, Training, and Workforce

| Program | Gross Annual Cost | Revenue / Offset | Net Federal Cost |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Civilian Reserve and Health Program | $35-55B | Payroll tax (CRHP workers), FEHB cost-sharing, eventual self-funding (Year 8+) | $20-35B Years 1-7; near-zero by Year 10 |
| Workforce Development Floor | Near-zero | Employers pay wages ($7-8.5B/year) | Near-zero |
| Care Economy SSA Caregiving Credit | $20-35B | General fund transfer (not Trust Fund draw); institutional care savings ($470B/year) | $20-35B (gross) |
| Retraining Programs (CRHP integration) | $3-5B | ATC revenue allocation | Near-zero |

**Category B total: $43-75B gross; $43-70B net (declining as CRHP self-funds)**

The Workforce Development Floor is the fiscal outlier — it costs the federal government
near-zero because employers pay 100% of wages for the placed workers. The $7-8.5B/year
flows from corporate balance sheets into worker paychecks, not from the federal budget.

The Care Economy credit appears expensive in gross terms ($20-35B/year) but generates
$470B/year in institutional care cost avoidance. The ratio is 13:1 minimum. No standard
CBO scoring captures this savings — it accrues to Medicaid, Medicare, and private families,
not to the federal general fund. A full social cost-benefit analysis changes the net figure
dramatically, but the legislative scoring will show the gross cost.

### Category C: Revenue and Investment

| Program | Gross Cost / Deployment | Revenue Generated | Net Federal Position |
|---------|------------------------|------------------|---------------------|
| American Sovereign Fund — contribution side | $60-137B/year (from arms levy, tariff share, ATC surcharge, royalties) | Revenue flows INTO ASF | Positive: revenue collected exceeds management cost |
| ASF — investment returns | — | 4-7% annual return on corpus; $480-$2,000+/person/year dividend by Year 30 | Off-balance-sheet (ASF is independent entity) |
| American Prosperity Visa | $200-400M (USCIS processing scale-up) | $4-6B/year in ASF contributions (Year 10) | Net positive by Year 3 |
| Outcome-Linked Bonds | $1-3B/year federal backstop | Bond coupon savings vs. conventional debt; private capital deployment ($30B+ at scale) | Near-neutral to positive |

**Category C total: Net fiscal positive at scale; primary revenue-generating category**

The ASF revenue streams are the most important fiscal design feature in the portfolio. The
arms sales levy (7.5% of $225-350B/year in U.S. weapons exports) generates $17-26B/year.
The tariff revenue share (20% of tariff collections) generates $18-25B/year at current rates.
The Automation Tax Credit surcharge generates $12-20B/year at projected AV penetration rates.
Financial transaction surcharge and resource royalties add $5-15B/year.

Total ASF inflow: $52-86B/year by Year 5; $60-137B/year by Year 10. This corpus
compounds at 4-7% annually. The federal general fund pays nothing into the ASF after the
initial enabling legislation. All revenues flow from dedicated sources.

### Category D: Economic Floors

| Program | Gross Annual Cost | ATC Revenue Offset | Net Federal Cost |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Negative Income Tax / Income Floor | $350-500B gross (all eligible recipients) | ATC 60% allocation: $12-20B/year; EITC consolidation savings: $80-100B; SNAP/TANF partial consolidation: $40-60B | $89-145B net |
| Civilian Reserve Premium (above NIT floor) | Included in CRHP above | — | Included |

**Category D total: $89-145B net (largest single cost category; capstone program)**

The NIT is the portfolio's most expensive and most consequential program. It cannot be
enacted until ATC provides funding and CRHP creates the enrollment constituency. At full
implementation it is also the most transformative: eliminating poverty-trap effects of the
existing means-tested system is estimated to improve labor market participation by 3-5
percentage points (documented in feasibility/03-negative-income-tax.md), generating
additional income tax revenue that partially offsets the gross cost.

A full dynamic scoring of the NIT — accounting for increased labor market participation,
reduced administrative overhead from means-tested program consolidation, reduced Medicaid
costs from improved baseline income, and multiplier effects on consumer spending — would
show a substantially lower net cost than the static $89-145B headline. CBO static scoring
will show the gross figure; proponents must be prepared to contest the scoring methodology.

### Category E: Market Correction

| Program | Gross Cost | Revenue | Net Federal Cost |
|---------|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Job Posting Accountability Penalty | DOL rulemaking: $50-100M one-time | $675M-$3B/year in penalty collections | Net positive by Year 2 |
| Strategic contracts (anchor buying) | $20-40B/year | Commodity value delivered; supply chain risk reduced | $5-15B net (commodity value partially offsets) |
| Synthetic Protein Program | $6-9B (5-year) | DoD/BOP/VA anchor buying generates product delivery | $3-5B net (over 5 years) |
| Space Mining Incentives (APC + Accelerator) | $3-5B (5-year) | Government IP retention; royalty potential | $2-3B net (over 5 years) |

**Category E total: $28-58B/year gross; $8-20B/year net (highly variable; crisis-dependent)**

Strategic contract costs are the most variable in the portfolio. In a non-crisis year, DED
activates Type A/B contracts for resilience maintenance at modest cost. In a Tier 2 declared
scenario, Type C/D contracts activate at substantially higher volumes. The $20-40B/year
figure represents a weighted average across scenario probabilities (from
supply-chain-concentration-risk.md: EAL of $56-190B/year in uninsured risk at zero cost
today; DED strategic contracts provide 4-20 cents per dollar of coverage).

### Category F: Resilience Operations and Scenarios

| Program | Annual Baseline Cost | Tier 1/2 Surge Cost | Notes |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|-------|
| Strategic Petroleum Reserve enhancements | $1-2B/year | $5-15B drawdown in Tier 2 | Replenishment is investment, not consumption |
| Scenario-specific deployments (pandemic, drought, grid, semiconductor) | $2-5B baseline | $30-120B surge (Tier 2) | Per-scenario cost in scenario files |
| DED Flying Car Pilot Reserve (emerging job categories) | $500M-$1B/year | — | Adaptive fund; no fixed program design |

**Category F total: $3.5-8B/year baseline; $35-135B/year in Tier 2 scenario**

Surge costs during a declared scenario are the fiscal stress test for the portfolio. In a
severe Tier 2 scenario (grid cyberattack, semiconductor collapse, oil restriction), DED
deploys $35-135B in a single year. This is large relative to baseline — but it is
substantially less than the unpreparedness alternative. The 2022 semiconductor shortage cost
the automotive industry alone an estimated $210B in lost production. DED's semiconductor
scenario budget would have cost $15-25B in preparation and $30-50B in response — a
$130-165B improvement on the unpreparedness outcome.

federal spending — in exchange for expected crisis prevention value of $380-680B/year.

### Year 30 (Mature portfolio; ASF dividend flowing; NIT normalized)

By Year 30 the fiscal picture changes materially:

- **ASF corpus:** At 5% average return on $80-137B/year contributions over 30 years, the
  ASF corpus is approximately $3.5-5.5T. Annual returns ($175-385B) fund operations,
  dividends, and a significant portion of the NIT floor.
- **CRHP:** Fully self-funding through payroll contributions of enrolled workers. Net federal
  cost near-zero.
- **NIT:** At Year 30, the ASF corpus returns are sufficiently large to contribute
  $50-150B/year toward the NIT floor. The NIT becomes increasingly self-funded through
  ASF investment income — the ownership transition model described in ubi-long-term-success.md.
- **ASF dividend:** $720+/person/year flowing to ~280M eligible Americans = $200B+/year
  in distributed dividend income. This is the largest single wealth-distribution event in
  American economic history, and it is funded by investment returns, not taxes.

**Year 30 net federal cost (general fund):** Approximately $30-50B/year for DED operations
and residual program costs — declining as ASF corpus funds increasing shares of the portfolio.

The portfolio is designed to become more self-funding over time, not more expensive. The
mechanism is the ASF corpus compounding: early years require appropriated funding; middle
years require ASF revenue + partial appropriations; mature years run primarily on ASF
investment returns with minimal general fund dependence.

## Section 3: Crisis Prevention Value

Standard fiscal analysis misses the most important number in the DED portfolio: the expected
value of crises that DED preparedness prevents or substantially mitigates.

### Documented Crisis Costs (Historical Baselines)

| Crisis | Actual Federal Cost | DED Scenario | DED Preparation Cost | Avoided Cost |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| COVID-19 (2020-2022) | $5.2T federal response | Pandemic Phase 1-3 | $15-20B/year baseline | $4-5T |
| 2021 Semiconductor Shortage | $210B automotive alone; ~$500B economy-wide | Semiconductor Collapse Phase 1-3 | $8-12B/year | $300-400B |
| 2022 EU Energy Crisis | $800B+ EU response; indirect U.S. cost est. $80-120B | Oil Restriction Scenario | $5-10B/year | $60-90B |
| 2011 Drought (Texas alone) | $7.6B agricultural loss | Drought Phase 1-3 | $3-5B/year | $5-6B per event |
| 2010 China REE Export Restriction | $1-3B direct + $20B+ industry disruption | Rare Earth Denial Phase 1-3 | $2-4B/year | $15-20B per event |
| 2003 Northeast Blackout | $6B direct; 50M people affected | Grid Cyberattack Phase 1-3 | $3-5B/year | $5-7B per event |

### Expected Annual Value of Crisis Prevention

Using historical frequency data and documented costs per event:

| Scenario | Annual Probability | Expected Annual Cost (no DED) | DED Mitigation | DED EAV |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------------|----------------|---------|
| Pandemic | 3-5%/year | $156-260B | 80% cost reduction | $125-208B |
| Oil supply disruption (severe) | 8-12%/year | $40-80B | 70% mitigation | $22-56B |
| Semiconductor shortage (major) | 10-15%/year | $50-100B | 75% mitigation | $38-75B |
| Grid cyberattack (significant) | 5-10%/year | $20-50B | 60% mitigation | $12-30B |
| Drought (major agricultural) | 15-20%/year | $15-40B | 50% mitigation | $11-30B |
| Rare earth supply disruption | 8-12%/year | $20-60B | 70% mitigation | $14-42B |
| **Total Expected Annual Value** | | | | **$222-441B/year** |

Adding the AI unemployment scenario (long-run transition cost reduction estimated at
$100-200B in avoided social disruption) brings total expected annual value to
**$322-641B/year**.

Against Year 10 net cost of $60-95B/year, this is a 4:1 to 9:1 expected return.

**Important caveat:** These are expected values. In non-crisis years, DED spends
$60-95B and prevents nothing visible. In crisis years, DED's preparation means the
crisis costs $300-500B less than it otherwise would. The investment logic is identical to
insurance and military readiness: the value is realized at the moment of crisis, not during
the calm years of premium payment.

### Who Bears Fiscal Cost

| Group | Primary Cost | Annual Amount |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Arms exporters | 7.5% ASF levy on arms sales | ~$17-26B total (industry-wide) |
| Tariff revenue (importers / consumers) | 20% ASF share of tariff collections | ~$18-25B (existing tariff revenue redirected) |

The distributional structure is intentional: costs are concentrated on industries that
directly benefit from the conditions DED manages (arms exporters benefit from U.S.
geopolitical position that DED backstops; large employers benefit from labor markets that
ghost jobs distort; AV operators benefit from the AHLP infrastructure they use). Benefits
are broadly distributed across citizens and domestic producers.

## Section 5: Fiscal Risk Analysis

### Downside Scenarios

**ASF revenue underperformance (4% return instead of 5.5%):**
Year 30 corpus is ~$3T instead of $4.5T. Dividend at Year 30 is ~$480/person/year instead
of $720+. Program remains viable and solvent — simply less generous.

**NIT cost overrun (participation higher than projected):**
The NIT is designed with a 30% phase-out rate. Higher participation is economically desirable
(the floor is working) but fiscally expensive. Risk mitigation: ATC revenue is the primary
offset; higher AV penetration = higher ATC revenue = higher NIT funding. The two are
correlated; the scenario that produces the most NIT recipients (mass automation) is also the
scenario that produces the most ATC revenue.

**Political attrition on ASF revenue streams:**
If Congress reduces the arms sales levy or tariff share, ASF corpus growth slows. Risk
mitigation: the dividend constituency (all enrolled Americans) is the ASF's political moat.
Any Congress that reduces ASF funding is cutting the dividend of the enrolled population.
The Alaska PFD has survived 40+ years of state budget crises because the constituency is
universal.

**Crisis surge cost exceeds reserves:**
A Tier 2 scenario deployment of $35-135B in a single year requires either emergency
appropriation or Strategic Reserve drawdown. The ASF's 25% Strategic Reserve tranche
($750B-$1.4T at Year 30 scale) provides the emergency liquidity without requiring
Congressional approval in a crisis. This is the design rationale for the Strategic Reserve
tranche.

### Stress Test Summary

| Stress Scenario | Year 10 Net Cost | Year 30 Dividend | Program Viability |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Base case | $60-95B/year | $720+/person | Fully viable |
| 4% ASF return | $70-105B/year | $480/person | Viable |
| $40B ASF revenue | $80-115B/year | $360/person | Viable but constrained |
| NIT +20% participation | $95-130B/year | $720+/person | Viable; ATC offsets |
| No NIT enacted | $25-40B/year | $720+/person | Reduced scope; core viable |
| Tier 2 crisis Year 5 | $60-95B + $50-100B surge | No change | Viable; ASF Strategic Reserve covers |

The portfolio is stress-tested against all major downside scenarios and remains viable in
each case. The key design features that ensure this:

1. Self-funding mechanisms are diversified (arms levy, tariff share, ATC, APV, JPAP penalty)
2. Programs are sequenced so late programs depend on early revenue streams being established
3. The ASF Strategic Reserve provides emergency liquidity without political negotiation
4. The NIT is the only program that fails gracefully (lower NIT = smaller benefit, not program
   collapse) — it can be scaled to available funding
